    Reserve Bank of India

    In the October policy meet, the Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) voted unanimously to maintain status quo on policy rate. As was the case in the last policy, accommodative stance was maintained with a 5:1 vote. MPC also reduced the inflation forecast for FY22 by 40 bps to 5.3%.

    The RBI decided to increase the quantum of the variable rate reverse repo auctions from INR 4 tn in September to INR 6 tn in early December. Liquidity absorption through the fixed overnight rate in early December is estimated to be INR 2-3 tn. Given the liquidity backdrop, RBI also decided to discontinue with GSAP purchases.

    Overall, the policy had a lesser dovish underpinning than the previous one in August. Going forward, we expect the RBI to keep the repo rate unchanged over FY22. However, we maintain our projections of a normalization in the Liquidity Adjustment Facility (LAF) corridor with a two-step hike in the reverse repo rate starting December 2021.

    Federal Reserve

    In its latest policy meeting in November, the FOMC moved forward with its tapering plans. It announced a USD 15 billion taper per month and emphasised that the pace of taper could get adjusted depending on the state of the economy. However, it continued to describe inflation as being 'transitory' and subsequently gave an unchanged guidance on policy rates. It attributed the slowing down in the economic activity and rise in inflation, to temporary 'supply-side' factors.

    In the next policy meeting in December, we expect the FOMC to maintain its QE taper schedule, while further raising concerns about inflation.

    We expect the FOMC to end QE by mid-2022 and embark on policy rate hiking cycle from Q4-2022 onwards. We see the prospect of at least one 25 bps rate hike in Q4-2022.

    Bank Of England

    In its latest policy meeting in November, the UK MPC surprised investors by maintaining status quo on policy rates and QE. It expressed concerns about the growth outlook, while maintained that the inflation overshoot could be temporary in nature. It indicated that the future course of action will remain 'data dependent'.

    In the next policy meeting in December, we expect the UK MPC to maintain status quo, but prepare markets for a possible rate hiking cycle in 2022, that will remain contingent on labour market developments.

    We expect the BoE to hike policy rates by 15-20 bps in February 2022 and by another 20-30 bps over the remainder of 2022. QE is likely to be terminated by end-2021, but the balance sheet size is likely to be kept unchanged over 2022.

    European Central Bank

    In its latest policy meeting in October, the ECB maintained status quo on expected lines and confirmed that the Pandemic Emergency Purchases Programme (PEPP) will end on schedule, by March 2022. However, it continued to describe inflation as being transitory. Hence, it will keep the monetary policy accommodative, over the 2021-2024 period.

    In the next policy meeting in December, we expect the ECB to indicate that once PEPP expires in March 2022, it will increase the size of its open-ended monthly QE programme, from EUR 20 billion to EUR 50-60 billion. Guidance on policy rates will be kept unchanged.

    We expect the ECB to end PEPP by March 2022, increase the size of its open-ended monthly QE programme and continue to provide guidance that policy rates will remain unchanged until end-2024.

    People's Bank of China

    Since the RRR cut of 50 bps that was provided in July, the PBOC has continued to inject liquidity via its OMO operations in response to ongoing concerns about the real estate sector and state of the economy.

    We expect the PBOC to keep policy accommodative by cutting the RRR by another 50 bps and continuing to inject liquidity to tackle the strain from Evergrande.

    Given the downside risks to growth, we see the PBOC maintaining a fairly accommodative framework and stance over 2022. The Central Bank is expected to continue with its liquidity infusion programme.
