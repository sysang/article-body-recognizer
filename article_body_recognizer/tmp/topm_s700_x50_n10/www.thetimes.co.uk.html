On this day

In 1695 a window tax was imposed in England and Wales, based on the number of windows in a house. Many windows were blocked up, causing health problems. The tax was repealed in 1851; in 1929 Robert Burns’s Auld Lang Syne was broadcast live from the Roosevelt Hotel in New York City, by Guy Lombardo and his Royal Canadians, popularising a New Year’s Eve tradition; in 1944, in Hungary, a provisional government, composed of communists loyal to the Soviet Union, officially declared war on Germany, its former ally; in 1999 the London Eye (Millennium Wheel) was officially opened by the prime minister, Tony Blair, but did not open to paying passengers until March 9, 2000 because of a technical problem; in 2020, at 11pm, the UK formally separated from the 27-member European Union.

Nature notes

On the seventh day of Christmas my true love sent to me . . . seven swan mussels lying on the riverbed. The largest of our six species of freshwater mussels, swan mussels might not be as glamorous as their feathered namesakes, but by feeding on plankton and filtering 40 litres of water a day, they keep rivers and lakes healthy. Though most of their life (up to 12 years) is spent in soft mud, swan mussels do have a brief period of going a-swimming. Their larvae, called glochidia, are carried by currents until they happen to meet a host fish, on to which they fix for several weeks, during which time they develop into baby molluscs.
