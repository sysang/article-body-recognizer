The ballet dancer, the Purple Wiggle and the family juggling act

Dana Stephensen thought she’d hung up her pointe shoes for good after having twins last year with Lachy Gillespie, but now she’s back on stage for the Australian Ballet’s Celebration Gala.

By Genevieve Quigley

December 12, 2021
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

Last Christmas, ballet dancer Dana Stephensen spent the day trying to escape the stifling heat in a darkened room, settling the newborn twin daughters, Lulu and Lottie, she shares with Lachlan “Lachy” Gillespie, aka the Purple Wiggle.

At the time, the overwhelmed mother of three – she also has a son Jasper, 6, from a previous relationship – thought she had hung up her pointe shoes for good. But 12 months later she is out of the newborn bubble and returning to the stage as part of the Australian Ballet’s Celebration Gala.

“Dana’s mum, Glenva, and I watched Dana dance with the girls in her tummy in her last performance before going on leave,” says Lachy. “To even think about sitting in the audience when the curtain opens to see Dana dance again, I’m just so happy for her.”

Here Dana chats to Sunday Life about getting back to the barre, juggling family life with a Wiggle, and their delightful Christmas plans.

How did expecting twins impact your work last year?Pregnancy is such a transformative time, especially for a ballet dancer. Obviously it takes you out of your normal duties, but at the Australian Ballet we’re lucky we can go on to safe duties, which I did from 11 weeks pregnant with the girls because I was pretty big! I was really sick and tired, and dancing was the only thing that made me feel less sick, because I wasn’t concentrating on it. But when we found out we were having twins, that changed things quite a lot.

In what ways did having your twin daughters impact your dancing career?My life picture changed quite quickly and my son started school this year, too. When I started to put the puzzle pieces together – with Lachy touring obviously, and ballet at this level being all or nothing – after 17 years in my career, I thought, “I think I’ve had a really good run. I’m really lucky to have come back after Jasper.” Those years were my best years dancing.

How did you feel about your “final” performance last year?I felt really happy. A farewell is not always something you get. If you retire through injury, it’s not a happy time. But I knew I had two little babies to look forward to. Weirdly, my last show was the company’s last show before everything closed [due to the pandemic]. It’s like not much has happened since then, but meanwhile everything changed for me.

What was it like coming back?It’s interesting because coming back from an injury is different to coming back from a pregnancy. From an injury you’re trying to fix something, maybe you need to look at your technique so that it doesn’t happen again. With pregnancy you’ve totally changed but nothing was actually wrong with you.

It’s different for everyone but in my experience, you just kind of start somewhere. It was very different from when I came back from Jasper. I hada role to come back to. I was back on stage nine months after him.

How long has it been this time?The girls were about seven months old when people around me said, “Why don’t you go in and have a conversation?” At this point I couldn’t even have a conversation with anyone, I was so removed from being a social person! With twins, people say you don’t leave the house. It seems extreme but it’s true. Then with COVID on top, it was such an insular bubble at home.

I was really struggling a lot of the time – it wasn’t like I was pushing the pram and chatting with everyone. I was very removed. I was like, “I know what this job is. It’s full-on. It’s days, it’s nights, it’s training, it’s touring.” Everyone said, “Don’t start there. Why not just go and talk about dancing again?”

How was this return different?As someone who trained my whole life and had goals, it was a very different thought process. I wasn’t thinking about a particular season to come back to or a role.

It was very simple. It was a way of getting back to myself, a way of giving me time as well, and space that I haven’t been able to have. It was a rebuilding of me which was separate to getting back to work. It was a way I could find that part of myself that needed nurturing.

“Lachy and I both feel incredibly lucky to do what we do, that we found our passion so early on and we get to live that out.”

Did you lose a bit of yourself in those first months with the twins?I think so. It’s not all a negative thing, it’s the nature of the process and I don’t take any of that back. It was definitely a massive shift for our whole family. I’ve been very lucky that there’s been support around me and I know it’s not that easy for every mother to have that time. For someone who’s exercised my whole life, I hadn’t given much value to how much I need it for my mental health.

What was the most difficult part about your return to work?Getting used to being in a room full of people. When I first started back, that was incredibly daunting. It wasn’t that it didn’t feel like home, but the company has moved on, the energy is different, we have a new artistic director. It wasn’t like putting on warm, fuzzy slippers. It was new and I had to find my place, and a lot of that is confidence. The mental road always takes the longest; it’s the tougher road than the physical.

How do you and Lachy juggle your busy careers and family life?If people knew all the puzzle pieces, especially over the last two years, it has been tricky. Both our families are in Brisbane, so we don’t have that local support. Both our mums have helped at different points, and my mum has been integral, especially when the girls were little [they are now 15 months old]. It’s a lot of planning. I have two really beautiful girls who help me out with the kids, especially with my son at school now.

Ballet is grace and elegance, but everyone knows we’re working really hard underneath. That’s the greatest metaphor for the reality of being a ballet dancer, especially with children. You are kicking like mad underneath to try to stay afloat. But Lachy and I both feel incredibly lucky to do what we do, that we found our passion so early on and we get to live that out. We are very supportive of each other in that way.

How did the pandemic impact you?”Silver lining” is not a nice term considering what has happened to the world in the last two years, but for our little family it has been a real blessing that Lachy has been able to be around. Not just in terms of support but he’s been able to stop and enjoy the girls, and our family time, and not be on the road all the time. He missed performing so much, he missed meeting all the kids, but the absolute silver lining was that we were together.

How does next year look for you?I promised myself I wouldn’t look too far ahead because looking at the touring component was too daunting. I never would have set foot in a studio! Next year will be different and I mean that in a really positive way. I know I’m very lucky to have my mum’s support. We just have to juggle it as best we can.

What are your plans for Christmas?We are hoping to be home with our families in Brisbane. For both of us,it’s our biggest break of the year. It’s a good time to switch off and just relax, especially after the anxieties of the last two years. Hopefully we’ll have a bitof fresh air and sun, and have people around the kids, too. Sharing your children with other people is a really beautiful part of parenting. Just being with everyone is the greatest gift.

The Australian Ballet’s Celebration Gala is on now at the Arts Centre Melbourne.
