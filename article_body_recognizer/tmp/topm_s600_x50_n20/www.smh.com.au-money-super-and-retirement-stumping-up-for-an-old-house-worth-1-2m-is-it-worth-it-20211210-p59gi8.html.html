Stumping up for an old house worth $1.2m: Is it worth it?

By George Cochrane
December 11, 2021 — 2.00pm
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

Three years ago, my wife and I and our three children moved to the NSW south coast, renting a beautiful house for $600 a week. I am aged 45 and earn $120,000 a year, while my wife earns $59,000. We want to buy a home, but the property market here starts at $1.2 million for an ordinary, old two-bedroom house. We have saved $400,000, and we can borrow $750,000, but I strongly believe that is a big loan to repay for an old house. I am handy around the house, but it is quite scary thinking about the amount of effort and hours that need to be put into it. Can you convince me that this is really the right time to buy a house? F.K.

No, I can’t. History teaches us that the best way to lose a lot of money is to buy an overpriced asset at the peak of a boom.

I know that real estate agents like to hang photos of bearded old men on the wall, captioned “he waited for house prices to fall” but I suggest you wait and see what happens when interest rates start to rise.

If mortgage rates rise to 5 per cent, which is possible, your rent equivalent would only pay off a $440,000 loan over 25 years, which is significantly less than the principal amount you have been offered to borrow now. In such a situation, I suspect many homeowners would find they have over-committed.

I am a 55-year-old a professional woman earning about $116,000 a year. I am debt free, having paid off my home. I have $245,000 in a retail super fund and $220,000 in the State Authorities Superannuation Scheme (SASS) defined-benefit scheme, to which I can no longer contribute but maximise contributions to the retail fund. I have $45,000 in savings. I intend to work full-time until at least age 67. I want to maximise my retirement savings. Should I borrow to invest? M.M.

I do not think it is a good time to borrow to invest.

The share and property markets are both nervously awaiting higher interest rates, possibly as early as 2022.

Keep saving as much as you can and invest your retail super fund in a mix of “balanced” and “environmentally aware” funds, as the latter seems to be attracting a flood of investment.

I am 49 years old, healthy, single and working in IT at a big bank, earning $235,000 a year. I’m expecting an inheritance and also thinking of extending my mortgage to the maximum amount – which would be another $525,000 at a Loan-to-Valuation Ratio of 80 per cent – to help me do one of three things: 1. Buy a business and change my job; 2. Buy an investment property; 3. Buy exchange-traded funds (ETFs). What do you think? J.M.

Properties are generally accepted as being overpriced and media reports indicate that the heat is starting to come out of the market.

ETFs are a mixed bag, with resource-oriented funds generally sinking, but those focusing on climate change and the environment doing a little better.

I’m all in favour of people running their own businesses. Our capitalist system favours it. So, that would be my choice, providing you research the sector thoroughly and analyse your personal strengths and weaknesses.

Bear in mind that most small businesses fail in the first few years.
