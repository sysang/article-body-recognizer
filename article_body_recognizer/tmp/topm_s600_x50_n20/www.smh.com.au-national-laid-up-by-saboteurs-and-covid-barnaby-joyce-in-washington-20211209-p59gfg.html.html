Laid up by saboteurs and COVID: Barnaby Joyce in Washington

Peter Hartcher
Political and international editor
December 11, 2021 — 5.00am
December 11, 2021 — 5.00am
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

For our free coronavirus pandemic coverage, learn more here.

Barnaby Joyce left the country with a clear mission in mind but now finds himself isolating in an American hotel room for 10 days. In some ways the Deputy Prime Minister’s situation is a metaphor for Australia’s.

He’s fully vaccinated yet still frustrated. Some overseas travel is possible yet it’s unreliable. You can book an air ticket any time you like, but you might never leave. False starts abound. Other countries close borders abruptly. And apply quarantine to international arrivals like Joyce.

And when you get back to Australia, you “may only enter and travel between NSW, Victoria and the ACT without quarantining”, as the Department of Home Affairs explains.

Joyce made it to London but seems to have picked up a case of COVID on the way to the United States. He’s restricted to his Washington DC hotel room till after Christmas as a result. As he summarised his situation, “vaxxed up and locked up”.

He has the consolation of staying in the Jefferson Hotel, not DC’s shabbiest. He thinks it’s misnamed. “It should be called the West Australian – perfectly safe but locked up.”

Loading

As for foreigners coming to Australia, reporters asked Scott Morrison about this on Friday. Will the international border open next week as scheduled? “Well, we’re taking more advice at the moment, but that is certainly our disposition.”

And the risk of perhaps closing the border suddenly again in the future? Finance Minister Simon Birmingham was asked what the trigger for a future closure might be. “There is always a risk into the future,” he told Sky News. “But now that we have such a highly vaccinated population, it would take something quite extraordinary to change that risk factor and see us go back to those widespread border closures that we were pursuing earlier.”

The obvious “something quite extraordinary” is the emergence of a savage new variant. It seems that Omicron is not that variant. But the virologists tell us that, so long as there are entire countries going unvaccinated, COVID will continue to mutate and circulate in the world.

Only about 7 per cent of the population of poor countries have had at least one dose of vaccination on average. Until everyone is safe, no one is safe, the World Health Organisation tells us. At the current rate, it will take years before everyone is safe.

Still, Australia could protect itself against savage future variants in the meantime so long as we have a domestic capacity to make mRNA vaccines, right? The ones that can be quickly gene-edited to cope with new variants and pumped out in mass quantities, the type that include Pfizer and Moderna?

Loading

Yes, indeed. So how’s that going? Australia’s CSL manufactured the AstraZeneca vaccine, a vector-type vaccine. But vector vaccines take much longer to brew up to mass scale and Australia has no mRNA capacity.

The then federal industry minister, Karen Andrews, said Australia would have mRNA “production lines” within a year. That was 14 months ago. And two industry ministers ago. And still no contracts have been awarded, no work started.

Two years after the virus was first identified in Wuhan, Australia’s capacity to produce mRNA vaccines to deal with it is unchanged. Zero. If a dangerous new variant should arise, requiring a new mRNA vaccine, Australia would once again be dependent on other countries to supply it.

So the risk of urgent border closures and protracted shutdowns will remain. Frustrated with the Morrison government, Victoria and NSW have launched their own state-based projects to create such capacity. Not an ideal situation for the country, obviously.

Barnaby Joyce doesn’t know which variant of COVID he has. He likens his symptoms so far to a case of the flu, nothing too serious. “If COVID wasn’t around, I’d just go to work.” Failing that, he’d like to watch the cricket.

But he’s had to cancel all his appointments in Washington. And he can’t watch the cricket, he says. His phone has a security feature which won’t allow him to download the necessary app. And American TV broadcasters aren’t big on cricket coverage.

He’s spending his time playing virtual Scrabble with his partner, Vikki Campion, watching inane American TV shows and getting frustrated listening to right-wing rant. The backbencher George Christensen, for instance, a member of Barnaby’s National Party and a backbencher in the Morrison government. Christensen this week gleefully accepted an invitation to appear on the InfoWars show hosted by American professional conspiracy theorist and provocateur Alex Jones. Among the highlights, Christensen called on people everywhere to “rise up” to oppose the Australian government. Of which he is a member.

When Jones compared Australian quarantine facilities to the Auschwitz concentration camp, Christensen gratified his host by laughing along and then playing it up: “The rest of the free world, please stand with us, please support us,” said Christensen. “Every time we see people out there protesting, whether it be in front of an embassy or elsewhere, protesting for our rights in Australia, it really does embolden the patriots, the people who are for freedom in our country to stand up.”

Loading

Barnaby Joyce rebuked Christensen on Twitter and on Thursday an ABC interviewer asked the Nationals leader whether he’d told his backbencher to “pull his head in”?

“I have spoken to George a couple of times,” said Joyce. “I have no control over him. He’s his own person. He’s a retiring member of Parliament. In fact, if I threatened to take him out of his job, you probably find you are breaking the law.” Joyce added that Christensen was only helping the Labor Party.

The Prime Minister wasn’t too happy with Christensen either: “I denounce the comments in the strongest possible terms. The Holocaust was an evil abomination. Respect for the victims requires that it never be referenced in such a trivial and insensitive manner.”

Christensen’s pathetic attention-seeking is on the extreme end, but a few others in the Coalition have shuffled themselves in the same general direction in recent months. Liberal senators Alex Antic and Gerard Rennick love indulging US anti-vax provocateurs too.

This is an admission of political failure by these characters. With 93 per cent of Australians over 16 having had at least one dose of a COVID vaccine, we know that they represent, at most, 7 per cent of the Australian electorate. Their own people have seen through their tatty attempts at grandstanding.

So they pander to a foreign fringe for validation. Even when they campaign against the public health and national reputation of their own people in the process. This is lazy, low-grade politics.

Christensen, Antic and Rennick join the failed Liberal Craig Kelly, now leader of Clive Palmer’s United Australia Party, in embracing the politics of national sabotage. All of them energised and excited by so-called social media, a force multiplier for anti-social extremism and conspiracy.

Barnaby Joyce is not most people’s cup of tea. His climate change denialism was cynical and unscientific. But he does represent a National-Country Party tradition of populist agrarian socialism. He has had his vaccinations. He doesn’t campaign against the public health advice.

And, like most of Australia, he’s frustrated with the extremists who do. Stuck in isolation, fuming over the right-wing fringe-dwellers he has to listen to while he tries to do the right thing, he just wants to do his job, watch the cricket and enjoy Christmas. By sheer happenstance, Joyce’s situation isn’t a bad approximation for the state of Australia, circa late 2021.

Loading

And his mission in travelling to the US in the first place? He is campaigning to civilise the big, US-based social media companies. Infuriated by Twitter trolls abusing his daughter, Joyce is pushing for a crackdown on vilification and anonymous hate speech. He wants the big platforms to be regulated.

As he put it to me a couple of months ago: “In a car, if I run over a person I go to jail, seatbelt or not. Online, I’m apparently indemnified. What’s the difference – breaking a leg or breaking a mind? We spend billions on mental health while they make billions in profits. I want to put the fear of God in them.” Joyce had lined up meetings with a range of US politicians to lend his outrage to their own.

As Deputy Prime Minister, he is partly responsible for some of the federal government sclerosis and chaos that continues to frustrate most of us. As a traveller frustrated to find his plans ruined through no fault of his own, someone who’ll have to face Christmas apart from family, someone who just wants the anti-vaxxers to shut up and get off social media so he can enjoy the cricket, he is not so different to most of Australia.
