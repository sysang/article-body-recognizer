The Sex and the City reboot was awful, and yes I will keep watching

Jacqueline Maley
Columnist and senior journalist
December 12, 2021 — 5.00am
December 12, 2021 — 5.00am
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

The groundbreaking television show Sex and the City was always criticised for being frivolous, and the people who watched it (largely women, obviously) didn’t care. Actually, it was more than that – the frivolity was what they came for.

It is impossible to overstate how refreshing and radical it was in the 1990s – when television with female leads barely existed – to see an honest rendering of women having fun.

The foursome of Carrie, Miranda, Charlotte and Samantha had made constant jokes about sex. They made jokes about men. They made jokes about their broken hearts, their personal inadequacies, each other, and the pretensions of New York society at that time. Their jokes were funny (the scripts of the original series still zing, more than 20 years later) and the physical comedy was marvellous.

Kim Cattrall’s libidinous Samantha Jones got most of the good physical gags, but one of my favourite scenes will always be the wind-up of Charlotte’s disastrous second wedding when the cynical wedding-phobe Miranda gets doinked on the side of the head by Charlotte’s flying bouquet, which she makes no attempt to catch. Her expression is deadpan as she decides that means it’s time to leave.

The missed bouquet is Sex and the City compressed – women don’t need men, the show told us, but it can still be funny when you collide with them.

The humour and zest were true to women’s lives, but it also showed a generation of single women a different way to be, and it celebrated female friendship.

“This uniquely influential TV program has reached and affected more Gen Xers and Millennials than Woodstock touched Boomers,” wrote Stephen Armstrong in the British Telegraph.

So, what happens when the influencers grow older? On Thursday we got to find out — the premiere of And Just Like That, the Sex and the City reboot/spin-off/desecration aired around the world. Almost every SATC fan I know expected it to be appalling (the second Sex and the City movie, a great cinematic crime, was a harbinger) but keenly watched it anyway.

The question of how the characters would address the passing of time is answered almost immediately.

The gals (minus Samantha because Cattrall opted not to return) sit down to lunch. They embark on a depressing patch of dialogue in which Charlotte (the prissy romantic) quizzes Miranda (aforementioned cynical non-romantic) on whether Miranda, who we have always known as Titian-locked, is going to dye her grey hair back to red.

“I just think the grey ages you,” Charlotte tells her, in a conversation no woman I know would have with another woman.

They are Karens in designer clothes, and not a single one of them has transitioned to sneakers, even though fashion has moved decisively to flat footwear in the past decade.

Miranda patiently explains that she is about to embark on a masters in human rights and “I don’t have to be a spicy redhead to do that”.

“We can’t just stay who we were, right?” she continues. “And there are more important issues in the world than looking young.”

Classic second-wave feminist comment (very Miranda), to which Charlotte (a third-wave feminist in her own way) replies: “Ruth Bader Ginsburg dyed her hair.”

It only gets worse when a new cast member joins the table and asks: “Oh my God, who ordered the French fries!?” – as though it is something delightfully naughty that must be owned up to. “I would have to know you a lot longer before I confessed to that,” Carrie rejoins.

This is a woman who once asked her bestie (Samantha) to yank out a stuck tampon for her. Now she is all faux-bashful about eating trans fats? The French fries conversation continues, but it’s too depressing to relay. The idea that women in their 50s would self-consciously flagellate themselves about a few calories is not just sad, it’s not true to the characters we knew.

As the episode creeps on, it feels as though the actresses have plumb forgotten how to play the characters they once inhabited. And the script-writers have them doing nonsensical things. Miranda – who once scored pot on a street corner after Carrie was famously dumped via Post-it note – goes berserk when she sees her 17-year-old son smoking a weed vape.

In another scene, Miranda announces she “draws the line at podcasts” – because what 50-something woman is down with technology so new and hip, right? Carrie appears to have mislaid her personality entirely and Charlotte cannot fathom why her teenage daughter doesn’t want to wear a floral dress from Oscar de la Renta.

Much of the commentary around the sequel is about how the women have “aged”. It’s not that time has passed, or that things have changed, or that the characters are simply older now. For women, time is marked by appearance. For a show that was heavily about appearances, this is not too shocking, perhaps, but it is still sad.

In the ’90s, Sex and the City showed single women cavorting against social expectations. Can’t we have some more of that now they are post-50?

The ways in which wokeness (and it’s called wokeness in the show) is interwoven is clumsy and tokenistic. It falls upon the new female black characters to redress the blinding whiteness of the 90s-era show, and a new queer non-binary character is supposed to make up for the thoroughly heterosexual focus of the original. The women are befuddled by the mores of this modern world, with its new pronouns and its podcasts.

They are Karens in designer clothes, and not a single one of them has transitioned to sneakers, even though fashion has moved decisively to flat footwear in the past decade.

There is so much wrong with it, but the show’s most grave sin is it has forgotten to have fun.

And yes, I will continue watching. You don’t dump old friends just because they’re going through a bad patch.
