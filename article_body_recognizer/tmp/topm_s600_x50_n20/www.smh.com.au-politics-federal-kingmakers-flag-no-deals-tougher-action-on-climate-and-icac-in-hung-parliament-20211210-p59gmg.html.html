‘Kingmakers’ flag no deals, tougher action on climate and ICAC in hung Parliament

By James Massola
December 12, 2021 — 5.00am
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

Scott Morrison and Anthony Albanese will face demands for higher emissions reduction targets, the rapid introduction of an anti-corruption commission with teeth, and donations reforms from the four crossbenchers in the event of a hung Parliament.

With the federal election due to be held no later than May 2022, and with a raft of ‘Voices of’ candidates targeting moderate Liberals in what have been traditionally safe Liberal seats, there is a growing expectation that – much like the 2016 and 2019 polls – the four members of the current crossbench could end up as ‘kingmakers’ in a minority government.

While the Greens’ Adam Bandt is all but certain to back Labor and KAP leader Bob Katter would very likely support the Coalition, The Sun-Herald and The Sunday Age spoke to Warringah MP Zali Steggall, Indi MP Helen Haines, Clark MP Andrew Wilkie, and Mayo MP Rebekha Sharkie about who they could support if the next election delivers a hung 47th Parliament.

The top line message for Mr Albanese and Mr Morrison was blunt: neither man is guaranteed support, and both will have to shift on key policy issues.

Mr Wilkie, whose Tasmanian seat was Labor-held until he won it in 2010, said that three of his four terms in Parliament had seen the government of the day with a tiny majority or in coalition and “I think there is a real chance of that” happening again.

The MP, who struck a deal with the Gillard government on pokies reform in exchange for his support – which it later dumped – said he had learned the lessons of 11 years ago and “I can’t see myself doing another deal”.

“I’m always on the look-out for gambling reform, but I’m older and wiser now. My experience of the second 18 months [of the Gillard government] is that I had at least as much influence with the government [after the deal ended].”

He lists a federal integrity commission, donations reform, media freedom laws, better whistleblower protections, truth in political advertising, a more ambitious climate policy and the government’s handling of China – “I want us to have a more independent foreign policy and to stand up for human rights” – as key priorities.

Ms Sharkie, one of three women to hold formerly Liberal seats, said she recognised her seat had been a non-Labor seat “since the dawn of time” but “I’d be tempted not to support anyone” as well.

“It’s months away, it’s a little early to presume, but I wouldn’t support anyone unless they gave clear commitments with short timelines on a federal ICAC, getting money out of politics and tangible action on climate change as what was presented at Glasgow [by the Morrison government] was a poor showing,” she said.

Ms Haines welcomed Labor’s more ambitious target of a 43 per cent emissions reduction by 2030 but said it was not sufficient.

“This year I seconded the bill for a Climate Act drafted by ... Zali Steggall that included a science-backed 60 per cent carbon emissions reduction target by 2030. That represents the target I believe is necessary for Australia to take real action on climate change.”

“As we approach an election which may result in a hung Parliament, I am thinking carefully and deeply about what factors would influence my decisions in these circumstances. I am committed to being upfront with the people of Indi about how I would approach a balance of power situation and will have more to say closer to the election.”

Ms Steggall said “it was a little to far out at this point” for her to indicate what she would do in a hung Parliament situation but “I’ll be thinking about carefully and consulting with the people of Warringah, I will be up front”.

“My priority will be legislating the climate change bill and a strong federal integrity commission.”

Kosmos Samaras, a former Labor official who is now a pollster with RedBridge group – which does work for the ACTU, Labor and the Climate 200 organisation – said Mr Morrison and Mr Albanese both had “goat’s track path to victory. It’s not a road or a highway, it’s a thin path”.

Mr Morrison, he said, was losing support to the United Australia Party and Pauline Hanson supporters on his right, to independents in the so-called “teal” seats – which include Warringah, North Sydney, Wentworth, Mackellar in Sydney and in Melbourne Kooyong, Higgins, Goldstein and Flinders – where moderate ‘Voices of’ candidates were running.

For Mr Albanese, “all the planets have to align” so he can pick up seats across Victoria, Western Australia, Queensland and possibly Tasmania and South Australia to win a majority.

“The era of landslides is over at a federal level. The electorate is fragmented enough that it’s likely whoever wins will win by a small number of seats at this stage.”

The federal Coalition currently holds the bare minimum of 76 seats in the 151-member lower house, while Labor holds 68 and needs to win eight seats to govern.
