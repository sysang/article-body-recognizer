Battle for Flinders heats up as ‘Voices of’ candidate Claire Boardman announced

By James Massola
December 11, 2021 — 4.59pm
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

A senior public servant working on Victoria’s COVID-19 response has been endorsed as the “Voices Of” candidate for the Victorian seat of Flinders, which Health Minister Greg Hunt will vacate at the next election.

Claire Boardman, the deputy public health commander for infection prevention and control in the Victorian Department of Health COVID-19 team, submitted her resignation on Friday.

Following Mr Hunt’s decision to retire after 20 years, the seat could be within striking distance though likely Liberal candidate Zoe McKenzie, an NBN non-executive director who advised Andrew Robb during his time as trade minister, is well regarded in the area too.

Ms Boardman has had two decades in the health sector and a career that has taken her to Thursday Island and Darwin. With a degree in nursing and a Masters in Public Health, she is hoping her experience as a frontline public servant will serve her well in Canberra.

She chooses her words carefully when asked why she put her hand up to run against the federal Health Minister (until Mr Hunt’s very recent decision to quit) given the tensions between Victoria and the federal government over the state’s COVID-19 response in 2020.

“Having worked in the COVID response for the last two years and having worked across various jurisdictions’ health systems, there is a definite capacity to improve health care delivery and the COVID response has highlighted system weakness,” she told The Age, ahead of her formal endorsement on Saturday.

“The system deficiencies impacted the most vulnerable groups of our community, including aged care and disadvantaged social groups. More could have been done earlier with a cohesive and comprehensive response.”

Ms Boardman said she had been considering a run at federal politics for close to 15 years. In the lead-up to the 2016 election, she nearly stood as a Nick Xenophon-backed senate candidate in the Northern Territory, but it wasn’t to be.

After five years on Thursday Island and six years in Darwin working in public health, followed by a year-long drive home via Western and South Australia, the sixth-generation local has been back home in Mt Martha for three years.

Ms Boardman joins Zoe Daniel and Monique Ryan in the Victorian seats of Goldstein and Kooyong, as well as NSW candidates Allegra Spender in Wentworth, Kylea Tink in North Sydney, Penny Ackery in Hume and Sophie Scamps in Mackellar, in seeking to replace sitting Liberals who are mostly from the party’s moderate wing.

Like these other candidates, she nominates a federal integrity commission, political donation reforms and stronger action to tackle climate change as key issues for her – and for Flinders.

“I’ve seen a lot of change in the region, to the built environment, I’ve specifically seen the changes to the sea, tide and sands, and changes to biodiversity,” she said.

“I became involved in the Voices group because there was an opportunity to make a difference. I didn’t think there was an opportunity to run as an independent, it’s a competitive space, but I was in the right place at the right time.”

“Climate change is the centrepiece. Integrity is another one. I support a federal ICAC, as well as good governance and transparency around political donations. And aged care, better service delivery to vulnerable people which the royal commission certainly highlighted.”

It was while based in Darwin that Ms Boardman got a close-up look at Canberra politics, becoming a regular visitor to the national capital for work where she witnessed “how decisions could be made, quite badly sometimes, and I was quite disappointed by some of the behaviour”.

In comments that underscored concern within Liberal ranks over contenders like Ms Boardman, on Friday Prime Minister Scott Morrison delivered a broadside at “Voices of” candidates.

“I mean, what, I don’t quite get this. So apparently they’re independents, but they’re only running against Liberals,” he said.

Ms Boardman would not say who she had voted for in the past and described herself as a “swinging voter”.

Unlike some of the “Voices” candidates, and given her experience of politics up close, Ms Boardman is cautious about committing to firm policy positions.

She would not say, for example, whether she supported a 2030 emissions reduction target of 26-28 per cent (the Liberal target), 43 per cent (Labor’s target) or 70 per cent (the Greens target).

“I would look to the evidence and take advice, I don’t have a firm figure. I would review the scientific information and make an informed decision,” she said, adding that Australia is a laggard in acting to combat climate change and should be at the vanguard.

Nor would she say whether, in the event of a hung Parliament, she would back a Liberal or Labor prime minister.

“I would look at who has the most effective policies, whoever has the most effective policies which are realistic and achievable.”

Just how competitive Flinders is became clear last week when Despi O’Connor, a first-term councillor on the Mornington Peninsula, missed out on the “Voices” nomination to Ms Boardman and promptly threw her name into the ring anyway.

The move potentially splits the independent vote and makes it that much harder for an upset victory.

While they face a difficult fight to actually unseat the incumbents, who are mostly moderate Liberal men, the “Voices of” campaigns will force money and resources to be diverted to what have until now been safe Liberal seats.

No money has been promised from Simon Holmes à Court’s multimillion-dollar Climate 200 war chest, but funds are expected to flow following her formal endorsement as a candidate on Saturday.
