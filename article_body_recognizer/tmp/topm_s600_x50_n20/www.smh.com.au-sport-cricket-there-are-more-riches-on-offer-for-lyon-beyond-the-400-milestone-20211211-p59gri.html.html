There are more riches on offer for Lyon beyond the 400 milestone

By Daniel Brettig
Updated December 11, 2021 — 4.23pmfirst published at 3.55pm
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

When Shane Warne fooled Sachin Tendulkar into padding up for an lbw at the MCG on his final Test match day of 1999, it felt briefly as though he would gobble up the five wickets he needed to pass Dennis Lillee’s Australian record that same afternoon.

As it was, Warne needed another two whole Test matches, and several months, to pass the milestone, admitting later that it had been an enormous weight on his shoulders.

“I probably tried a bit too hard through the whole Test match,” he said upon dismissing Paul Wiseman at Eden Park. “It is a bit of baggage out of the way but, by the same token, it’s a pretty proud moment.”

Something similarly heavy sat on Nathan Lyon for the past year, as what looked to be a doddle towards 400 Test wickets at the start of last summer’s India series turned into a saga that started to raise questions about his future - some from Warne, himself.

There was even a “ghost wicket” on day three of the Gabba Ashes Test, when Dawid Malan plopped back a return catch off pad and glove that Lyon, clearly a little unsure of where that next wicket was coming from, barely appealed for.

In the end, 705 days and 1330 deliveries after Lyon’s 390th victim, the 34-year-old from Young did make Malan his 400th, and in doing so achieved a couple of critical things. First and foremost, he contributed grandly to Australia’s fourth day victory march, finishing up with four of their eight wickets in the morning session.

“I’m nervous because I care. I care about everyone in that changing room, I care about the result and playing cricket and representing Australia.”

Australia’s man of the moment, Nathan Lyon

Secondly, Lyon showed how, with the right attitude and the right fields set by new captain Pat Cummins, he can return to being a genuine wicket-taking threat for Australia at home. It’s a role Lyon would naturally prefer to the placeholder for resting fast bowlers that he had felt at times last summer, and for several passages of this match.

“It hasn’t really hit me to be honest,” Lyon said. “I’ll get my phone and call my family and friends and that will probably hit a little closer to home. But it’s something I’m very proud of, there’s no doubt about that. It’s been some hard toil to get it, but very rewarding.

“I’m nervous because I care. I care about everyone in that changing room, I care about the result and playing cricket and representing Australia. I’m not nervous because I’m scared of failing. Pat was extremely calm as captain ... I think when Australia play their best cricket, we’re calm and playing with smiles on our faces and enjoying it. Pat really hit that home.”

What turned things around for Lyon? Arguably, it was the placement of a silly point fielder, Marnus Labuschagne, to nip at the heels of Malan and increase the risk of a bat-pad catch. For much of day three, former captain Ricky Ponting had called for exactly that placement from the TV studio turned commentary box back in Melbourne.

For much of the previous day, Lyon had been played comfortably off the back foot by Malan and Joe Root, neither of whom looked in any way influenced by the presence of a close-in fielder. But as England ate ever more into Australia’s lead, skipper Cummins took the more proactive option of posting Labuschagne in close.

Within a few balls, Malan advanced, failed to get to the pitch of a looping, dropping delivery, and squeezed an inside edge on to pad and straight to the helmeted Labuschagne. No DRS was required to settle the issue, and Lyon’s shoulders were suddenly lightened.

His subsequent dismissal of Ollie Pope - extracting bounce and turn out of Mitchell Starc’s helpful footmarks to draw a catch to slip - illustrated much about how threatening Lyon can be at his best. Certainly Pope, new to the crease, miscalculated the overspin.

That line, wide of the off-stump, is somewhere Lyon has occasionally hesitated to bowl. His ability to take consistent wickets hereafter may well be influenced by how much he can be encouraged to stick to it, tempting more batters to drive.

Nevertheless, figures of 4-91 were a timely reminder of how valuable Lyon can be, with gifts in fingers, shoulder, and full-body torque that had turned Ponting’s head more than a decade ago in Sri Lanka.

“It’s a magnificent milestone … A magnificent achievement,” Ponting said on Seven. “I played in his first Test, I faced him in the nets before his first game. I heaped praise on to the captain and coach at the time about: ‘You’ve got to play this guy’.

“His release was so pure and still is. You can see the seam coming out of his hand perfectly. The revs and the rotations on the ball were high. I think that’s why he’s had such great success. His release has always been really pure.”

One of Australian cricket’s best qualities has always been its constant forward momentum, as hungry, aspiring players jostle without undue deference for the places currently occupied by their seniors.

Elsewhere in Brisbane, 28-year-old wrist spinner Mitchell Swepson eyed off the batting lineup of the England Lions, having nipped out two wickets in their first innings of just 103. Swepson, possessing prodigious gifts of spin that he is allying to increasing levels of accuracy and self-belief, will continue to push Lyon.

But there may now be scope for Swepson and Lyon to finally play together: the other impactful spell on the fourth morning was bowled by young all-rounder Cameron Green, who hit the pitch hard and swung the ball enough to find Root’s precious outside edge.

Team balance affording the use of twin spin in places other than Asia is something that neither Warne nor Lyon enjoyed for long periods of their careers. It would be another way in which Lyon’s career can grow beyond 400.
