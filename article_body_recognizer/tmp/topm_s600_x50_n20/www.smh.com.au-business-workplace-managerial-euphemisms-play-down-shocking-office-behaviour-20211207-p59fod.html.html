Managerial euphemisms play down shocking office behaviour

By Jonathan Rivett
December 10, 2021 — 12.01am
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

Question:

Some really bad stuff happened at a workplace I thankfully got out of a year or so ago. I remember being shocked at what happened itself, but in some ways being more shocked by the language management used when referring to it.

What I and many of the folks I worked with thought was “disgusting”, “horrible” and “sickening”, was described by the managers who addressed it as “unacceptable” and “inappropriate”.

Those “gentle” descriptions didn’t “fit the crime” at all. I’ve noticed those words being used in my new place of work (thankfully to describe much less serious things) and I wonder why they’re so popular.

Answer:

You’re right, words like “unacceptable” and “inappropriate” are hugely fashionable in just about every field of work and area of life at the moment.

Just a couple of weeks ago, for instance, a cafe in suburban Melbourne was badly damaged by vandals who left a note threatening to burn down the shop and kill the owner if he did “what Daniel Andrews says”. Politicians responded with terms like “unacceptable” and “not on”. Just as with your experience at your previous place of work, I found such words hopelessly inadequate given the circumstances. “Not on” to describe a death threat is bordering on grotesque.

It brought to mind the language I remember teachers and principals using back in primary school. In this context, they make sense. When you’re trying to deal with a tricky incident involving five to 12-year-olds, ferocious language is best used sparingly if at all. Yes, these words could still come across as limp, even at school, but their edgelessness is useful and very often for the best when dealing with a minor scuffle or a bit of naughtiness.

In other words, it’s language that suits the circumstances.

It’s impossible to get into the minds of your past managers (or the politicians responding to the menacing cafe note), but my best guess at what they were trying to do by using this bland, pallid language was to be like a good primary school teacher. It was an attempt to calm the situation or to take some of the emotion out of an episode.

‘Only the most obsequious hangers-on are impressed by a tip-toeing response to immensely weighty events.’

What many modern-day managers and politicians have in common, at least from my point of view, is that they don’t want to be seen as even slightly reckless or over-zealous. That’s all well and good, but if the aim is to gain the trust of “stakeholders” or the public, avoiding outlandishness by lurching all the way to cold detachment will have the opposite effect. Only the most obsequious hangers-on are impressed by a tip-toeing response to immensely weighty events.

The other problem is that even if the language is fitting, sensibly measured to fit the situation, its overuse can turn it into something vaporous and futile. “Unacceptable” is a word I hear so often now and in so many different contexts and situations, it rarely has an effect on me; it’s not just feeble, it’s substanceless.

These words are popular precisely because they’re so innocuous. There’s a fairly thin line, however, between innocuous and impotent.
