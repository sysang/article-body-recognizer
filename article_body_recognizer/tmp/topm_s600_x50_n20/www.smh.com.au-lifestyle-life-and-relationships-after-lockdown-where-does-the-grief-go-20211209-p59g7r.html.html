After lockdown, where does the grief go?

By Sally Spicer
December 11, 2021 — 11.50am
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

For our free coronavirus pandemic coverage, learn more here.

When I think about death, I always picture the middle of the night. A sombre knock at the door from a sombre looking policewoman, Maybe a gentle nudge from my husband, easing me into his disclosure of a tragedy that took place while I was dreaming.

But 9.37am on a Monday? Not so much.

I had a missed call from a woman I used to work with. It’s the kind of thing that happens on the regular. Inconsequential. Maybe I’ll call back today. Maybe I won’t. Beyond my incredibly hackneyed Millennial phone anxiety, nothing felt amiss when I saw that failed connection.

I did call back and she answered within a beat.

“You know that Dave has been struggling with his mental health...” The bad news unspooled in her delicate Irish lilt, and my stomach dropped through the floor. “Sally, he erm… he didn’t make it.”

A beat. A lifetime. Gone. Dave was 39 years old. He was funny. Not just a little funny but side-splittingly funny. You could dine out on his jokes. The first time I met him he was jumping out of a green bin at work to build hype for a new office recycling program he’d started.

Dave excelled in kindness. He volunteered for a charity that taught disadvantaged children how to play basketball. He always listened intently when he asked how you were. You could trust him with your sadness, your anger, your fears and your life.

Dave had opal eyes, short sandy hair and a broad, impish grin that always made you feel like you were in on some secret joke that only the two of you shared.

So, how could Dave be dead?

Two weeks before his death, Dave asked me to a movie. We Melburnians were living through our fifth lockdown, and I was exhausted. The idea of braving city traffic for the first time in months and fighting for a park at Federation Square was too much on the day. I cancelled and I never saw him again.

Dave’s socially distanced death was not just tragic, but also deeply confusing. Trapped inside and away from others who loved him, there was no tangible proof that this thing – this ending – existed outside the four walls of my home.

Imagine an entire life capped at five people and four-square metres a piece, with masks hiding the faces of people once loved.

If a friend dies in lockdown and no one is together in mourning, did it really happen?

A funeral is a solitary lifeboat in a sea of grief. It’s a chance to comfort, commiserate and console with a community. But like much of what we thought we knew that reprieve was washed away by a global pandemic.

Without a funeral, my pilgrimage through grief has been confusingly nonlinear. A couple of weeks after Dave’s death, an avalanche of anguish left me bedridden for days. The pain festered like an open wound. As the Delta variant swallowed Sydney, the funeral was postponed until Dave’s relatives could get here. At the time of writing this, it still hasn’t happened.

We’ve all heard the stories of loved ones who pressed their faces against the invisible walls dividing Australia’s states, territories and the world, begging to be let in. We have watched, on the news, tales of the breathless tears that fall as final goodbyes are said, impossibly, through pixels on a screen.

Imagine an entire life capped at five people and four-square metres a piece, with masks hiding the faces of people once loved. Human life made small and insignificant.

It was not just Dave’s life that was made small but the grief of everyone who loved him. Without the send-off he deserved, Dave turned to ash, smoke-filled haze and forgetfulness. The day he died, I sent him a Facebook message. It felt nice to spend a brief second in a liminal state of hope that the message would flick over from sent to seen. Why did I do that?

If Dave were here, he’d tell me I’m doing great. He’d read this article – he’d really read it, not just skim it on his phone – and then laugh at how seriously I’m taking his departure from this earth. He’d pull out his harmonica and play something jaunty. Dave loved being the centre of attention. He’d smile at me. Waiting for a response.

Without anything better to offer my friend, these words are my funeral for him. Sadly, I know that I am not alone in my grief. That there are hundreds, more like thousands, across this country who were robbed of their goodbyes.

Bereft of rituals we still manage to adapt. My grief may be complicated, but I will not drown in it like I once feared I might. Dave wouldn’t want me to.
