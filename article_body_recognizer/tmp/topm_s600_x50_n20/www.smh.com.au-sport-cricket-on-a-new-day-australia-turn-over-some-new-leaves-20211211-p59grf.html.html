On a new day, Australia turn over some new leaves

By Greg Baum
December 11, 2021 — 6.43pm
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

Australia’s crushing victory at the Gabba was the latest instance of Test cricket’s new day syndrome. As the only sport in which one game is played over multiple days, the long format is uniquely susceptible to it. So often, the course of one day utterly reverses the next, with little apparent change except that all have had a night’s sleep to dwell on their virtues or sins.

On Friday afternoon, Australia took 2-200 in two sessions. Nathan Lyon didn’t take a wicket, Josh Hazlewood barely bowled at all, and David Warner was nowhere to be seen.

England’s Joe Root and Dawid Malan so enjoyed themselves that the infrequently selected Malan at one stage was moved to nod towards the Barmy Army and remark to Root on how much he had missed the heady stimulation of the Test stage.

On Saturday morning, without notice, Australia took 8-77 in one session to put a summary end to the match. England’s objective at day’s beginning was to get to, and through, the new ball, 10 overs hence. By the time it fell due, Malan, Root and Ollie Pope already were gone and Australia was careering away with the spoils. The Barmy Army were still in full voice, but it was ironic now.

Winning needs no explanation, and so the Australians made it sound as if this flip of fortunes was all a logical development that needed only their patience to evolve. “Five days is a long time,” said captain Pat Cummins, “and you can’t get too caught up in everything that happens.”

Coolly analysed, this is true. Australia played estimably better cricket than England in the match, had the three lions’ share of the luck, and won by a handsome margin. Yet, at another level, this result was all about the mind and the way it can bind and release. It was all about how tomorrow is always another day.

Sick of trying to craft a place in the Test team, Travis Head decided to play with such manic fury that he could barely remember, himself, what he had been doing out there on Thursday. It brought him 150, the man-of-the-match award, and a free pass for the rest of the series, beginning with a hometown Test next week.

Lyon was perched on 399 wickets for so long that it became a kind of national in-joke. On Friday, the 400th had looked as distant as ever. Overnight, he and the brains trust thought to press the English batsmen with a couple of close fielders. They said it was nothing much - a mere adjustment, cricket business, something they had slept on.

But what do you know? Four overs in, Malan advanced and edged into his pad, Marnus Labuschagne took the catch, Lyon had his 400th wicket, and quickly took three more. Cummins said the dressing room had begun to joke that he would have to wait until next summer for his milestone wicket. That’s the other thing about winners: they laugh last.

A 400th wicket relieved and freed Lyon. A first wicket did that for Cameron Green. A likely type since the start of last summer, he was a bowling maiden until this match. His first wicket was a top order one, his next the very best, Root.

“All the talk a few years ago was that Green was one of our next-best quicks,” said Cummins, who had barely seen him until he made the Test team, “and then he started turning out hundreds.”

Last summer, Green was still rehabilitating from stress fractures and kept some of himself back. Here, he laid out his bowling wares: pace, bounce and swing. It had a ripple effect, bringing him wickets, mitigating Hazlewood’s absence from the popping crease, and perhaps easing Green’s mind about his golden duck as he looks forward to Adelaide.

These are the luxuries an all-rounder brings. England knows it, though Ben Stokes looked well short of a gallop here, and Australia can lick their lips about it.

Debutant wicketkeeper Alex Carey cupped the first catch that came to him, immediately felt in the game, and duly caught seven more without dropping any, leaving only one lot of four byes in the debit column. To add to an already good first impression, he volunteered to open the batting in place of the ginger-ribbed Warner in the short second innings, with nothing to gain and as it happened only his wicket to lose.

“[I was] really impressed,” said Cummins, “and not surprised.” In the nature of these things, the Test gloves are now Carey’s to lose, not another’s to wrench from him.

If Head wasn’t man-of-the-match, Cummins would have been. He was the best bowler in the match, fielded with his usual zest, and looked in command as captain.

Moreover, he revealed almost papal infallibility, which can neither be studied nor taught. Nearly every bowling decision he took bore immediate fruit. The last, made on the advice of vice-captain Steve Smith, was to take the second new ball from Mitch Starc an over sooner than he had planned. Instantly, it gained him Stokes’ wicket, for the second time in the match. Whether running his fingers down the seam or clicking them, Cummins controlled this Test.

He’s not so glib as to think it will always be this way. And it’s not strictly true that winning needs no explanation. The status of Warner and Hazlewood have to be addressed, also the likelihood that England will be stiffened in Adelaide by the recall of Jimmy Anderson and perhaps Stuart Broad, and a kind of statistical certainty that their luck will change.

But Australia are in front in the Ashes again. And if the tide turns in Adelaide, not only will there be a whole new set of new days, but new nights as well.
