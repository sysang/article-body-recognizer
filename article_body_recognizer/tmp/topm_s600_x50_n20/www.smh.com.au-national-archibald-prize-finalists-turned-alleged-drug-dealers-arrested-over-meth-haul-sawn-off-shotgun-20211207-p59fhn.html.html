Archibald Prize finalists turned alleged drug dealers arrested over meth haul, sawn-off shotgun

By Tom Cowie
December 12, 2021 — 5.00am
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

Benjamin Aitken was once described as an enfant terrible of the art world, following in the footsteps of the “bad boys” of Australian painting.

The four-time finalist in the Archibald Prize, along with his friend and fellow artist, Shannon McCulloch, are considered rising stars for their portrayals of a demi-monde of substances and sleaze.

Their inspiration was rarely kept hidden. Mr Aitken has the words “drug money” tattooed on his right palm and shows off with guns online. Mr McCulloch, who recently won a $10,000 Brett Whiteley art scholarship, uses explicit narcotic motifs in his art.

This week, a court heard that the pair were allegedly doing more than experimenting with artistic ideas, after a police raid on Mr Aitken’s house uncovered a commercial quantity of ice, drug-dealing paraphernalia and a stolen sawn-off shotgun.

In the months before their arrest, Mr Aitken, 30, and Mr McCulloch, 26, were sharing in the success of both being named 2021 Archibald finalists.

Mr Aitken, also a finalist of the 2020 Sulman Prize, painted artist Gareth Samson as his entry. Mr McCulloch’s subject was a shirtless Mr Aitken with the words “f--- life” inked on his chest.

The friends described themselves as “like family” and were both at Mr Aitken’s house at 9am on October 26 when detectives knocked on the door to execute a search warrant.

Inside the Franklin Street apartment in Melbourne’s CBD, police allegedly uncovered a major drug-dealing enterprise.

During their search, police allegedly found more than 90 grams of what they believed to be MDMA but tests later showed was methamphetamine, the Melbourne Magistrates Court heard this week.

Officers also discovered a stolen shotgun, ammunition, prescription drugs, thousands of dollars in cash, a money counter and six iPhones. Ziplock bags containing cocaine and ketamine were also seized.

The pair gave “no comment” interviews after their arrest, however, the court heard that Mr Aitken had provided police with passcodes to the confiscated phones. On the devices, police allegedly found messages on the encrypted app Wickr referring to drug trafficking.

The menu for customers included ketamine, MDMA, acid, mushrooms, cocaine, cannabis and prescription medication, police said.

“They identified the drugs in plain English,” said Senior Constable Timothy Fitzpatrick from the North West Metro Regional Crime Squad.

Mr Aitken looked downcast on Wednesday as he appeared in a virtual court hearing from Port Phillip Prison wearing a green prison tracksuit.

“It has been sobering for him, both literally and figuratively,” said his barrister, Belinda Franjic.

Mr Aitken and Mr McCulloch’s friendship had spilt over into alleged drug dealing, the court heard.

“Through my analysis of Mr Aitken’s phone, there is reference to ‘Shannon from Brunswick’ collecting drugs on behalf of Mr Aitken,” Senior Constable Fitzpatrick said.

Mr McCulloch told the Art Gallery of NSW as part of his Archibald entry that he met Mr Aitken in 2014 when he lived in Perth.

“[My Archibald] portrait depicts Ben in his lounge room where he and I have sat countless times discussing everything from art, music, boxing and politics, to our personal histories, long through the night and into the morning,” he said.

On Tuesday, Mr McCulloch was selected along with four other painters aged under 30 for the Brett Whiteley travelling art scholarship, which includes a two-week artistic residency at the Shark Island Institute in NSW.

It’s unlikely he will be able to attend after he was bailed in early November ahead of a court date next month.

In a profile, art critic Ashley Crawford wrote that Mr Aitken was taking after “bad boys” like Adam Cullen, the grunge artist who died at 46 after a long battle with drugs and alcohol.

“Pray that he does not follow their lead too closely,” he wrote.

On his Instagram page, Mr Aitken holds guns in several photos and references using MDMA. One post shows a drawing by Mr McCulloch depicting Mr Aitken looking down the chamber of a sawn-off shotgun.

His tattoos include the puns “born Toulouse” (possibly after the absinthe-soaked French bohemian artist) and “flat baroque”.

Hours before they were arrested, Mr Aitken uploaded a photo to Instagram with Mr McCulloch, who he describes as the “cornerstone of my bizarre existence”.

Both Mr Aitken and Mr McCulloch were charged with trafficking a commercial quantity of methamphetamine, trafficking cocaine and ketamine, drug possession, handling stolen goods, possession of an unregistered gun and ammunition, and knowingly dealing with proceeds of crime.

The threshold for a commercial quantity of methamphetamine is 50 grams and the maximum jail term for trafficking that amount is 25 years.

Senior Constable Fitzpatrick said that he believed Mr Aitken had a drug habit.

“Through my analysis of Mr Aitken’s mobile there are conversations where he said to the other person he was high during a particular time,” he said.

As part of the raid, police allegedly found a Winchester 12-gauge shotgun, which had been stolen from its registered owner during a burglary in Lavington, NSW in 2002.

The gun’s barrel had been shortened, Senior Constable Fitzpatrick said, and 17 rounds of shotgun ammunition were also found.

The court heard that the shotgun had no fingerprints on it and when asked if the weapon could have been wiped clean, he responded: “That’s a possibility, yes.”

Mr Aitken was granted bail on Wednesday after spending six weeks in custody.

Prosecutors had argued that Mr Aitken might be a flight risk because he had connections to galleries overseas.

Ms Franjic, Mr Aitken’s lawyer, said her client came from a good family and that he had no prior criminal history.

“Clearly the offences are serious, I’m not going to submit otherwise but he’s not staring down the barrel of double figures [a 10-year plus jail term], if I can put it crudely,” she said.

Ms Franjic told the court that Mr Aitken was a very successful and accomplished artist. His body of work included exhibitions in the United States, Thailand and South Korea.

“It takes maturity to become a well-known artist, 30 is pretty young,” magistrate Ross Maxted said.

Ms Franjic responded: “He’s had no difficulty selling his artwork, he’s very much in demand.”

In granting bail, Mr Maxted acknowledged that the court backlog due to COVID-19 meant Mr Aitken would have spent a long time in custody before his matter was resolved.

“It’s incongruous for a man from a good family to be involved with a substantial amount of drugs,” he said.

As part of his bail conditions, Mr Aitken forfeited his passport and must enter a residential drug rehabilitation facility in Hoppers Crossing.

Ms Franjic asked the centre’s program co-ordinator if Mr Aitken would be able to practise art during his 13-month rehab stay.

The court heard that a two-hour art therapy session is offered to recovering addicts every Thursday.

“We can look for ways to help Ben express himself in the art industry,” the co-ordinator said.

The co-accused are due to face court again on January 18.
