Why Stephen Sondheim leaves a cryptic gap on the world’s puzzle stage

By David Astle
December 10, 2021 — 5.00pm
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

Stephen Sondheim didn’t care too much for the Bantu hartebeest. Worse than that, the late composer poured scorn on the creature, making it the scapegoat for every obscurity found in the so-called quick crossword.

Lebbek is the animal’s native name. Elsewhere, it’s kongoni. Who cares, argued Sondheim. Life is too short to memorise Serengeti ungulates, all in the name of filling a morning grid. “I say the hell with it,” he wrote in 1968, introducing his own crossword in an early issue New York magazine.

A wordsmith to his bootlaces, Sondheim was more into play than esoteric vocabulary, a love evident in his crossword’s radical style. The accompanying article said as much: How To Do A Real Crossword Puzzle – Or What’s A Four-Letter Word for “East Indian Betel Nut” and Who Cares? Time to cancel the kongoni, he cried, and discover the deeper joy of cryptics.

The genre was relatively new to US solvers, a cohort accustomed to dictionary-like clues, where East Indian betel nut – say – was catechu, in case you care. By 1968, the success of West Side Story still an earworm, the Sondheim name alone drew many lebbek-lovers to try the new puzzle.

Better still, the young librettist did the cryptic genre another favour. In America, prior to Sondheim’s rant, the playful clues were known as British-style, which did little for their appeal. Imagine calling karaoke something like Japanese auto-singing entertainment, and hoping the novelty catches on. Sondheim changed all that. The Broadway prodigy called them cryptics, ousting their Anglo association, and the craze subsequently bit.

True to form, most of Sondheim’s clues had a lyrical bent. Containers, say, were illustrated with: Crooner takes clarinet inside – good manners (8). Place REED in BING, the clue murmurs, and you make BREEDING. As for homophones: We hear the new musical is German (4). Timely and neat, the clue implies the Aquarian smash of Hair (sounds like HERR) which was wowing New York at the time.

At 38, Sondheim himself was on the cusp of writing Company – his fourth musical, and the one to see him quit from cryptic duties in 1970. Puzzledom’s loss was theatre’s gain, however, since Sweeney Todd and Into The Woods were hardly going to write themselves.

Turning the clock forward to 2012, Sondheim was in his early 80s by then, staving off jet lag in the foyer of Her Majesty’s Theatre in Melbourne. The occasion was his sophomore show, A Funny Thing Happened On The Way To The Forum. The curtain had fallen, the foyer a ruck of people. If I had any chance to meet Sondheim, I had to think fast.

The man seemed unreachable, a lion amid kongoni. In many ways, the scene echoed the double-meaning example he gave in 1968: Fight enclosure in the theatre (3). But rather than BOX my way into his presence, I plumped for a password.

“Hedonism,” I sang, and the maestro balked. He looked up. Who said that, he wanted to know, since very few in that lobby would realise his cryptic alias, his surname’s anagram. He urged his minders to let me through. Which was how I came to thank the king of American cryptics in person.

I told him a clue – Sondheim’s ultimate song! (5) – which he nimbly solved as MARIA (M + ARIA). In return, he shared an autobiographical anagram: He pens demon hits. Which wasn’t wrong, after such a stellar life in theatre. But not just theatre. Last month, when Stephen Sondheim breathed his last, he left behind a colossal gap on the world’s stage – and its puzzle page.
