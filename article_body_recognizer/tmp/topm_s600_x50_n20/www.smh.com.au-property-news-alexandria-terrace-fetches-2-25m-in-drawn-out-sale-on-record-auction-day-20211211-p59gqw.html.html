Alexandria terrace fetches $2.25m in drawn-out sale on record auction day

By Kate Burke
December 11, 2021 — 4.45pm
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

An Alexandria terrace fetched $2.25 million in a drawn-out auction on Saturday, as a sharp rise in homes for sale and a record-breaking volume of auctions allowed Sydney buyers the luxury of less competition.

With almost 1320 properties scheduled to go under the hammer in Sydney on Saturday – marking the city’s busiest ever auction day on Domain records stretching back to 1995 – buyers had more homes to choose from, and were prepared to take their time on the auction floor.

By evening, Domain Group recorded a preliminary clearance rate of 62.7 per cent from 868 reported results, while 236 auctions were withdrawn. Withdrawn auctions are counted as unsold properties when calculating the clearance rate.

The clearance rate is a leading indicator of price growth. When it is above 60 per cent, prices are likely to be rising, but once it dips below 60 per cent, prices are likely to be falling.

A young couple kicked off the bidding for the three-bedroom terrace with a study at 22A Jennings Street, with an opening offer of $1.8 million – below the $1.9 million price guide – soon topped by a bid of $1.85 million by a phone bidder.

It was slow going from there, despite five registered bidders, with the couple asking auctioneer Damien Cooley, of Cooley Auctions, if the property was on the market before placing another bid, to which he replied: “Absolutely no way ... I assume you knew that already.”

They went on to make an offer of $1.9 million, and from there the bidding gradually rose in $50,000 and $25,000 jumps to $2.2 million, as the two parties exchanged bids, with Mr Cooley knocking back multiple attempts to offer smaller raises.

A third buyer entered at $2.21 million – a local woman looking to upsize – and later walked away with the keys. However, that was only after increasing a further offer of $2.23 million to the $2.25 million reserve, via negotiations on the auction floor with selling agent Brad Gillespie of The Agency Eastern Suburbs.

The $2.25 million sale price for the 99-square-metre block was more than double the $950,000 that records show the terrace last sold for in 2012.

“It landed at the right price,” Mr Gillespie said post-auction.

The market has shifted gears in recent weeks, he said, but noted that while the quantity of buyers had reduced at auctions – down from 20-plus bidders on properties in lockdown and shortly after – quality buyers were still there.

“The market has obviously turned a corner and is softening now after such a big gain in one year, it can’t repeat that,” Mr Gillespie said, adding it was returning to a more even market for buyers and sellers.

Mr Cooley, who called 13 auctions on Saturday, said buyers had become a little more cautious as more property hit the market. Conversations about cooling prices, and possible interest rate rises, were also on buyers’ minds. But with most homes still selling, and limited new stock to hit the market until late January, he expected to see a good market in the new year.

It comes after Sydney’s median auction price for houses fell 3.6 per cent in November to a still-high $1.95 million, Domain figures show, as a post-lockdown surge in homes for sale gave buyers more choice and saw fewer bidders turning out to compete at auctions. The median is 21.9 per cent higher than it was a year earlier.

Elsewhere, big results were still achieved. In inner-city Darlinghurst, a three-bedroom penthouse with city views and a rooftop terrace sold for $3,985,000, with competition from four of the seven registered bidders pushing the price $685,000 above the reserve and almost $1 million above the $3 million price guide.

A couple, recently returned from Singapore, walked away with the keys, after outbidding their main competitor – a Central Coast buyer looking for a Sydney bolthole. The penthouse may be used as a home for their adult son.

The property at 1306/1 Francis Street, which sold through Guido Scatizzi, of BresicWhitney Darlinghurst, last traded for $1.6 million in 2008.

Mr Scatizzi said the market was still quite strong for A-grade properties, but had been pulling back a bit for other homes.

“With an apartment like this, when you’ve got the location next to Hyde Park and those views you can’t go wrong, always going to hold its values in the years to come,” he said.

In nearby Redfern, a 10-bedroom Victorian terrace – operating as a boarding house – passed in after a single bid of $2.5 million, made by a developer, after the only other registered bidder, an investor, refrained from making an offer.

Selling agent James Perlowski of Belle Property Surry Hills, was in negotiations with both parties on Saturday afternoon, and said the property at 73 Great Buckingham Street had drawn interest over the sales campaign from both mum and dad investors and those who owned multiple boarding houses, as well as some buyers looking to convert the large terrace into a single residence, subject to council approval.

In the city’s south, a first-home buyer couple from Paddington ended more than six months of house hunting after making the winning bid of $1,402,000 for a Kogarah home.

The pair outbid an investor for the three-bedroom house at 26 Barton Street, with the bidding starting at the $1.25 million price guide and two parties pushing the price $62,000 above the reserve as a third registered bidder watched on.

The 324-square-metre block sold through Michael Flevaris of McGrath Brighton-Le-Sands. Records show it last sold for $835,000 in 2014.

In Macquarie Park, a young family from the north shore who had been searching for a new home for about a year paid $1,195,000 for a two-bedroom townhouse on the market for the first time since 1980, with Russell Sheffield of Ray White Macquarie Park.

They were among seven registered buyers of whom five were active at the auction for 7/19 Fontenoy Road, which opened with a bid on the $975,000 price guide. The price went up in $25,000 jumps to start and slowed right down to $1000 raises before the fall of the gavel.

While the property was called on the market at $1.14 million, the sale price was just short of the initial $1.2 million written reserve.
