Meet the men taking their wife’s surname

The joy and “uncomfortable conversations” behind the decision of a growing number of men to throw off the shackles of an “archaic” marital tradition.

By Samantha Selinger-Morris

December 11, 2021
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

When Laura Henshaw and her fiance, Dalton Graham, decided that he would take her last name when they married, they knew they were in for some hard conversations. Turns out, they had no idea just how many – and just how hard.

“We didn’t think people would be as shocked as they were,” says Laura, 28. The co-founder of fitness company Keep It Cleaner married Dalton, 28, an advertising and marketing agency owner, at a winery in Victoria’s Yarra Valley last month.

Though numerous strangers among Laura’s more than 300,000 Instagram followers pelted her with “negative commentary” – like messages that Dalton must have wanted to separate from his birth family and that he’d “lost his masculinity” – the toughest conversation was with Dalton’s family, who, she notes, are “very traditional”.

“I think it was something they had never heard of before,” she says, adding that they had assumed that either she would take Dalton’s surname, or they would both keep their surnames. “I think it was a shock at the start, then they were able to process [it].”

The decision was Dalton’s idea, which came out of a discussion the couple had about how important it was for her to keep her surname because it’s attached to her company that has taken her years to build.

“Everyone was present on the wedding day,” Laura says, adding that her family was “really proud and excited” about her and Dalton’s choice. “So there was no bad blood. Everyone’s happy now and supportive. That’s really special. [But] it was definitely an uncomfortable conversation.”

Consider this a notice, then, to future parents-in-law, that they could be in for a whole lot of special feelings – and discomfort.

Because while the trend of men taking their wife’s surname after getting married has been reported over the past few years in Canada, Ireland and the United States, it has now firmly landed on our shores.

“It was pretty straightforward, we just decided that would be the best way to go about it,” Michael Marshall – formerly Michael Puhle – says about changing his last name after marrying Sydney publicist Jessy Marshall last month in a forest near Byron Bay.

“There isn’t really a family legacy from my name that I wanted to or needed to [keep],” explains Michael, 36, owner of a corporate coaching company, who calls the tradition of women taking their husband’s name “archaic”. “It was a bit of an anti-legacy, nothing really there at all. My father committed suicide. So there’s, like, no strong family name that comes through [associated with Puhle]. Whereas on the Marshalls there is,” he says, referring to Jessy’s large family – her father has nine siblings – who frequently get together and have a “blood is thicker than water” ethos. “So, to be able to be part of it, well, that’s a name I’d rather kind of pass through the generations than one that doesn’t have any associated to that.”

So what’s in a surname? Would it change our culture in any significant way if large numbers of men were to take their wife’s surname on marrying? (One 2018 study suggests that men who have the same level of education as their wives are more likely to change their surnames while men with less education than their wives are less likely.)

“It could start to shift our cultural understandings of the gendered power relations in marriage,” says RMIT global studies lecturer Dr Kaye Quek.

She says even though Western married women are no longer the legal property of their husbands – which is where the tradition of women taking their husband’s surname originates – “assumptions of male ownership” of women is still rife in many modern marriages.

“There’s a wealth of sociological research which shows that [domestic violence and marital rape] is linked to men’s understanding of their wives as their possession or property,” says Quek, who researches Western wedding practices from a feminist perspective.

Jessy, 32, the founder of events and PR agency Hive HQ, says although it’s a personal decision — and she respects that it mightn’t be right for everyone — she thinks if more men took their wife’s surname, as her husband did, it might lead to greater gender equality.

“Just from the perspective of gender pay gaps … I think it comes back to putting us [women] on an even playing field,” says Jessy, who grew up assuming she would take her husband’s surname when she married. “As a young girl, that’s just what you were taught,” she says, adding that she’s delighted that Micahael has taken her surname, which she was passionate about keeping because of the values she feels it stands for.

She speculates that if more couples had up-front conversations about what their last names meant to them – and which they wanted to assume after getting married – it might lead to more honest conversations that could benefit the entire relationship.

“My parents separated when I was 18, and my mum went back to her maiden name, so for me, I’m like, maybe if they sat down and talked about their relationship and their ethos and values when they went into marriage and what that means, maybe that open conversation would have helped to translate through their marriage.”

Quek cautions that there’s only so much that changing a surname can do to promote gender equality.

“If you accept the idea that naming is about power, to some extent, then it’s about trying to eliminate the power difference in gender inequality, rather than establishing it in reverse form,” she says. “[But] it’s something of a start.”

There are other, more under-the-radar complications that can come with changing surnames.

“They did have an issue that one of my nicknames comes from my [former] last name,” Michael Marshall says of his male friends. “They call me ‘P’, [so they asked] ‘Can I still call you ‘P’? They’ll probably get around the idea of calling me ‘Marshall’ and get a nickname from that.”

Future fiances might want to consider Chris Idermark-Boyd’s choice.

“We wanted to make a new family identity. We knew we were going to have kids, and we’re bringing together these two different cultures, so that was really it,” says Chris about his decision to adopt a hyphenated name that incorporated his wife’s maiden name – as did his wife, Anna Idermark-Boyd – after they married in 2018. His wife, notes Chris (formerly Boyd), is from Sweden, where it’s not uncommon for men, he says, to hyphenate their names after marriage, as he has done.

“It would feel wrong to have my father’s family name,” says Chris, a lawyer from Sydney who now lives in London, noting that his wife has few relatives, globally, and their new surname is a way of ensuring that her family line continues. “We’re really happy with the decision that we’ve got our own little identity.”
