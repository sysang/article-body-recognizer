NSW to scrap QR codes again as Covid-19 infections surge

NSW is set to scrap mandatory venue check-ins again, despite surging infections, with the Deputy Premier declaring the measure has played its part.

Despite surging infections, NSW is set to scrap mandatory QR code check-ins at venues again, with the state’s Deputy Premier declaring the measure has played its part.

The state reported another massive jump in cases today, with 21,151 infections and six deaths in the 24 hours up to 8pm last night, as new isolation requirements and close contact definitions come into place.

Now, just one week after restoring QR code check-ins following crisis talks in the lead up to Christmas, NSW is preparing to ditch them a second time.

Appearing on Sunrise this morning, Paul Toole said the measure has “played an important part in this Covid pandemic”.

“But I think we’re moving to the next phase,” the Deputy Premier went on, asked if checking-in would remain or become “obsolete”.

“What we’ve actually seen is the people of NSW have come forward, they’ve got double-vaxxed and that was the best way of protecting themselves and their community.

“We’ve all done the hard yards. That’s why we no longer need those settings around QR codes. If someone’s just had a fleeting contact with someone else in a retail store or (at) a hospitality event, they have to go and get tested. That’s why the changes that have been made are so important and critical in delivering that.

“We’re heading into the next phase. We actually have to learn to live with the virus. In NSW, again, keeping people safe is our number one priority, but moving away from QR codes is something we will start to phase out.”

Mr Toole’s comments came as the state woke up to a new, national “close contact” definition, and changed isolation rules.

Under the new definition, a close contact of a positive Covid-19 case will now be defined as “a household contact, or a household-like contact, of a confirmed case only”.

This means that an interaction at a retail store, bar, restaurant or supermarket will no longer land you in isolation – you’re only a close contact if you spent over four hours with an infected person inside a household-like setting.

The Deputy Premier said the change will make a “huge difference” for businesses hit by staff shortages and at reducing demand at PCR testing sites.

“By having a redefining of the definition of a close contact is going to make a big difference. It means someone you actually live with or spend a considerable number of hours with,” Mr Toole told Sunrise.

“What I’m hoping is this is a consistent rule, it is driven across all states and I hope Western Australia and Northern Territory also come on-board. This is about easing the pressure on the health system. It’s about reducing the queues we’re seeing at testing clinics.

“At the end of the day, it is about making sure more Aussies get back to work.”
