Science Advances

  • Volume 7
  • Issue 50
  • December 2021

ONLINE COVER Nonlinear waves, including seismic, ocean, and atmospheric waves, are studied in numerous areas of physical sciences and engineering. While physicists have shown that harmonic generation, a phenomenon widely used in laser science, occurs during nonlinear wave travel, the exact mechanism for nonlinear wave travel remains unknown. Khajehtourian and Hussein provide a theory that characterizes the nature of a nonlinear wave, through its harmonic generation, as it evolves over time. The study opens the way for understanding nonlinear waves in general and provides a framework for synthesizing complex waves such as solitons, a critical component in optical fiber communications.

R. Khajehtourian and M. I. Hussein, Ella Maru Studio

ONLINE COVER Inflammatory SINEs. This month's cover features a retinal color photograph showing age-related macular degeneration in a 75-year-old man. In the center of the retina, damage to the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) has caused enhanced visibility of the underlying choroidal blood vessels and impaired vision. Wang et al. identified DDX17, an RNA helicase, as a sensor of elevated levels of RNA transcripts from short interspersed nuclear elements (SINEs). DDX17 can initiate a noncanonical form of NLRC4-mediated inflammasome activation leading to RPE degeneration in the eye.

ONLINE COVER Takeoff and Landings. The ability for aerial robots to take off and land on a variety of irregular surfaces will broaden the range of environments where these robots could be deployed. Influenced by how birds take off, land, and grasp, Roderick et al. have developed a bird-inspired grasping system called Stereotyped Nature-inspired Aerial Grasper (SNAG). The researchers integrated SNAG into a quadcopter to demonstrate perching on tree branches of different sizes, orientations, and surface conditions. Science Translational Medicine

  • Volume 13
  • Issue 623
  • December 2021

ONLINE COVER Managing Muscle. In this image of gastrocnemius muscle from a young mouse, the long noncoding RNA Cytor has been knocked down, resulting in decreased fast twitch type IIb muscle fibers (magenta), with increased proportions of slow twitch type I muscle fibers (green). Type IIa muscle fibers are shown in red. Wohlwend et al. identified CYTOR as a long noncoding RNA with elevated expression after exercise in humans and rodents and with decreased expression with age. Cytor expression promoted myogenic differentiation in vitro, and manipulating Cytor expression in mice altered skeletal muscle mass, strength, and performance. These findings suggest that CYTOR may represent an intervenable pathway to address sarcopenia in humans.

Credit: Wohlwend et al./Science Translational Medicine
