The politics of presentsStuck for gift ideas? Mao bought Nixon two pandas

Historical giving, from holograms to potatoes

There are no free gifts. There are vouchers at the checkout, perfume samples sealed inside magazines and objects wrapped in coloured paper, but there are no free gifts.

Don’t take my word for it. A century ago, a French sociologist called Marcel Mauss studied the rules of present-giving in Polynesia and concluded that such exchanges did two things – they placed the giver and recipient in a potentially endless cycle of commitment, and established their relative positions in the social hierarchy. Giving things to people, argued Mauss, is not just an expression of human solidarity but also a subtle assertion of power. Philanthropists have their names put up over doors and in foyers; food-bank clients never request such a service.

This circuitry buzzes around most modern Christmas trees: the guilty giver who buys expensive jewellery for a neglected partner; the parent who loads their child with unwanted educational toys; the sibling who brings nothing, but donates to an irrigation scheme in the family name.

Such exchanges occur between countries, too. The Christmas tree in Trafalgar Square is an annual gift from Oslo to London, offered in recognition of Britain’s assistance to Norway during the second world war – not least in providing a home for King Haakon VII and his government in exile. “It perfectly symbolises our common effort as allies in the struggle against totalitarianism,” Norwegian state secretary Jens Frølich Holte said in 2019, emulating a solidarity straight out of Mauss.

But there’s a bit more going on here. The first official tree was shipped across the North Sea for Christmas 1947 and presented with great ceremony by the Norwegian ambassador, Per Preben Prebensen. Smaller specimens of spruce had been making the journey since 1942, however, the first one felled on the island of Hisøya by one Torpedomann Mons Klubben, and despatched to the homesick Haakon. This was the story told in Trafalgar Square in 1947 by Ambassador Prebensen: one about a loyal subject’s tribute to the king over the water. After the speech, some of the crowd spoilt the vibe by stealing the decorations.

Trafalgar Square spruces are usually around 90 years old. Most of them, therefore, grew up under the Nazi occupation of Norway. That history now seems rather distant to those who walk beneath their lit branches. It wasn’t much mentioned by people who complained about the condition of the tree sent from Oslo for Christmas 2019. State secretary Holte defended its unusual shape as “quintessentially Norwegian: unlike artificial and glitzy trees, it is honest and unpretentious”. But Londoners interviewed by the New York Times thought they detected a plot. The Norwegians, they suggested, had sent a weedy tree to punish British voters for Brexit. This year once again, the tree was mocked as sad and scrawny. Norway is not a member of the EU. But if you forget that, you might also forget older reciprocities.

Foal play The wooden horse of TroyAntiquity’s greatest gift horse was pretty rough on the Trojans – all those Achaean spears flying, King Priam kebabbed on the altar of Zeus, Cassandra proved right (imagine how annoying that would have been). The Trojan horse has been generous, though, to culture. When something nasty is secretly contained within something nice; when foes charm their way over a threshold, the horse is wheeled on to do metaphorical labour. It’s malware that penetrates the firewall; a plot, real or imagined, to inveigle radical Islam into state schools; a marketing technique that uses fine print to hide costs from the eye of the customer. (The Trojan condom, despite its penetrative work, does not fit the metaphor.)

Since the Greek sources – the “Iliad”, “Odyssey” and “Aeneid” – have a mysterious relationship with historical reality, metaphor might be the only life the wooden horse has ever known. There was a Troy that burned in the Late Bronze Age, but the vehicle of its destruction may be a garbled reference to the practice of wrapping siege-engines with damp horse-hides to stop them being set alight by the enemy’s flaming arrows. Or perhaps, as Austrian historian Fritz Schachermeyr suggested in the 1950s, the horse was the embodiment of an earthquake, a phenomenon whose legislating deity, Poseidon, sometimes took equine form. Another theory proposes that it was a battering ram – though such devices date from a later period of warfare. None of these explanations seems satisfying or necessary.

Better to corral it in a field of doubt, not least because the image has a paranoid dimension. It’s used by populist mythmakers who want voters to imagine covert alien invasions, usually by Muslims. (EU refugee policy, says Hungary’s prime minister, Viktor Orban, is “the Trojan horse of terrorism”.) The vehicles we see washed up and broken on European beaches are much more fragile.

Trunk or treat? The white elephantThe white elephant is, proverbially, a possession that is unwanted, expensive and difficult to maintain. We apply it when Boris Johnson daydreams about grand aquatic infrastructure projects, or when Olympic countries start throwing up football stadiums. It has been in the language for at least 150 years, describing theatres that remain stubbornly unprofitable and burdensome presents dispersed through newspaper small ads or church bazaars.

There’s an etymology attached: the kings of Siam, so the story goes, used the gift of a white elephant as an extravagant form of punishment. Too sacred to be put to work, these creatures did nothing but consume the resources of the beneficiary and defecate in his courtyard. This tale, however, seems to be a chimera: the record suggests that white elephants were so prized in South-East Asia that battles were fought over their ownership. Ralph Fitch, an English merchant who visited the Kingdom of Pegu in 1586, watched one of the monarch’s four albino animals having its feet washed in a silver basin, and recalled that “if any other King have one, and will not send it him, he will make warre with him for it.”

That desire had already been felt in Europe. The beast in the picture – copied from a life-drawing by Raphael – is Hanno, a white Asian elephant born in India in 1510 and transported to King Manuel I in Lisbon, who, in 1514, sent it to a delighted Pope Leo X. Pontiff and pachyderm had four wonderful years together, until Hanno developed constipation and died after being fitted with a gold-enriched suppository. Leo wrote his epitaph, and distributed preserved-elephant body parts among his bishops as if they were the relics of a saint. In 1962 some of Hanno’s bones were rediscovered by workmen fitting an air-conditioning system in the Vatican. His burial place had been forgotten, along with the original value of his pink eyes and parchment-pale skin.

Silent might SavannahIt’s sometimes a disappointment to be told in advance what you’re getting for Christmas. But when Abraham Lincoln received a telegram on December 22nd 1864 informing him what General William T. Sherman had bought him, the president was content. You can’t wrap a seaport and put it under the tree. “I beg to present you as a Christmas gift”, cabled Sherman, “the city of Savannah with 150 heavy guns plenty of ammunition also about 25,000 bales of cotton.” “My dear General Sherman,” replied an elated Lincoln. “Many, many, thanks for your Christmas gift.”

Sherman’s March to the Sea, his successful civil-war campaign to cut Confederate communication lines and crush civilian morale, was brutal. On their way to the coast from the ruins of Atlanta, his 62,000 Union soldiers uprooted railway tracks, demolished bridges and burned factories and barns. Instead of consuming their own supplies, they seized local produce and livestock, using the 1860 census data as a shopping list. Around 10,000 slaves took the opportunity to leave the fields and join the column as it moved towards Savannah. The press, which had previously criticised Sherman, declared the strategy a success. The Chicago Tribune hailed him as “our military Santa Claus”. A cartoon in Leslie’s Illustrated pictured him slipping a model of Savannah into a stocking nailed to Lincoln’s mantelpiece.

But it was the citizens of Savannah who perhaps had the strongest reason to celebrate. Sherman destroyed the dockyards and military installations, but his scorched-earth policy ended at the city limits. Most of the Union army camped on the outskirts, from where they sent in wagonloads of food for the poor, drawn by mules with twigs tied to their heads in imitation of reindeer antlers. In his diary Major Henry Hitchcock, Sherman’s chief of staff, recorded a Christmas Day service at which victorious soldiers prayed beside defeated Georgians – then turkey was served. “It was as quiet and pleasant a Christmas dinner”, wrote Hitchcock, “as one could wish.”

To the lighthouse Statue of LibertyThe most famous gift on these pages was not, strictly speaking, a gift at all. The story that France sent its great torchbearer to America in commemoration of their shared revolutionary past is a misremembering of a more fitful, unofficial and rickety history.

Lady Liberty’s sculptor, Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi, first thought of placing a giant woman-shaped lighthouse on the Suez Canal as a modern rival to the Pyramids. In 1867 he met Ismail the Magnificent, Khedive of Egypt, to discuss his plans. When these fell through, he reworked his design for America and went west to drum up interest. Not enough, unfortunately.

With hard-eyed single-mindedness, Bartholdi returned to Paris, turned his workshop into a visitor attraction and begged the French government to let him raise funds through a lottery. They declined. Rescue came in the form of the newspaper magnate Joseph Pulitzer, who began printing the names of everyone who had made a donation to the project, no matter how small, and discovered that people would donate just to see their name in print. New funds got the statue to New York, but Bartholdi was forced to abandon his plans to clad her in gold leaf.

We should also be grateful, perhaps, that another plan failed to come to fruition. In 1878 inventor Thomas Edison proposed installing the statue with a giant phonographic disc that would have allowed the Green Goddess to broadcast patriotic phrases across the bay and towards Manhattan. If you’ve ever heard the terrifying voice of an Edison talking doll, you’ll know the pain that new Americans were spared.

An eye for a lie Political gift-givingShakespeare’s plays are loud with warnings to politicians. One of the starkest comes from “Henry V”, in which the Battle of Agincourt is caused by a box of tennis balls sent to England by the French dauphin.

Did American secretary of state John Kerry remember this in January 2014 when he began talks on Syria by setting a cardboard carton in front of the Russian foreign minister Sergei Lavrov and pulling out a pair of bafflingly massive Idaho potatoes? News cameras captured the skittish weirdness of the moment – as well as Lavrov’s decision to accept the gift with good humour.

At their next meeting the following year, Lavrov replied with a bag of Russian potatoes. And in this, the smartness of Kerry’s strategy is revealed. If he’d furnished Lavrov with something fancy and expensive, Kerry would have been unable to make use of the reciprocal present. The value of a handful of root vegetables falls well below the strict $390 limit the American government sets on gifts to federal employees. Kerry, therefore, was permitted to take the potatoes home for dinner.

The $390 mark still stands. Anything above it is sent to the State Department’s Protocol Gift Unit and processed for storage in the National Archives – the final resting place of a rug from Azerbaijan bearing a photo-realistic image of the Clintons, a stuffed Tanzanian leopard and insurance-policy documents that would have paid Michelle Obama A$50,000 ($36,000) in the event of her husband being attacked by a crocodile.

Living gifts to world leaders constitute a different problem. Balkan of Gorannadraganov, the puppy handed to George W. Bush in 2006 by Bulgarian president Georgi Puranov, was put in kennels on a farm in Maryland. The camel given to French prime minister François Hollande during his visit to Mali in 2013 was placed for safe-keeping with a family in Timbuktu, who embarrassed their government by putting it in a tagine. (The Malian authorities supplied Hollande with another, which, they insisted, was “bigger and better-looking”.)

Mao Zedong, however, discovered that a living, breathing present could be the embodiment of international co-operation. The practice even acquired a name – panda diplomacy – under which, between 1957 and 1983, China distributed 24 thick-thighed, black-and-white bears among nine favoured countries. (In 1984, Deng Xiaoping changed the policy from gift to loan.) The first pair, Hsing-Hsing and Ling-Ling, cemented the triumph of Nixon’s 1972 visit to Beijing and became one of the biggest draws at the National Zoo in Washington. British prime minister Edward Heath repeated the move by acquiring Ching-Ching and Chia-Chia for London Zoo in 1974 – and in China, Heath enjoyed the kind of warm reception he rarely received at home. An unkind acquaintance suggested that “Mao was the first person he had seen in months who was actually pleased to see him.” Rumours of the time spoke of Heath visiting the zoo at night to commune with the beasts, inhaling the smell of success – and freshly cut bamboo.

Fashionably late? The Kardashian hologramThe Victorians mourned their dead by exchanging gifts of woven hair: elaborate and expensive jewellery, arranged in tight little loops. But it was often not sourced from the beloved – the hair of dead people can be unworkably brittle. Living matches had to be found. Like many acts of 19th-century devotion – the corpse-photographs, the visits to the spiritualist – the practice now produces shudders.

Kanye West’s decision to spend hundreds of thousands of dollars on a talking hologram of his late father-in-law might seem a similarly morbid aberration of taste; a ventriloquial party trick with the simulacrum of a dead man. It was a 40th-birthday surprise for his wife, Kim Kardashian, unveiled in 2020 at her week-long shindig on a private resort in the Abaco islands, which the Kardashian inner circle occupied using a daringly imaginative interpretation of the covid guidelines. In the video an image of Robert Kardashian, the attorney who defended O.J. Simpson and died of oesophageal cancer 18 years ago, congratulates his daughter on her birthday, dances to Barry Mann’s “Who Put the Bomp” with the sort of jerky waggle favoured by computer-game characters who have successfully picked some magic fruit, and – in a detail that may reveal something the recipient already knows about the giver – salutes his child for marrying “the most, most, most, most, most genius man in the whole world”.

Those of us who missed our invitation to quarantine ourselves and eat cake at Baker’s Bay Golf and Ocean Club had to be satisfied with the clips posted on Twitter, which also made it hard to sniff out the nature of the technology employed. (That, and the discretion of Kaleida, the company responsible.) But, like the sensibility that created it, this phenomenon also has a 19th-century precedent. If a theatre manager of the period wanted to make a phantom materialise on the boards he used Pepper’s Ghost, a stage trick that projected an image onto an invisible pane of glass. Holonet, the technology used to resurrect Robert Kardashian, would seem to be its descendant. A gift from the Victorians, to a family that shares their taste. ■

Matthew Sweet is a regular contributor to 1843 magazine, and a writer and broadcaster in London
