Daily chartFewer Americans are trying to buy guns

The decline spans red and blue states—but sales are still higher than in 2019

AMERICA IS the world’s most heavily armed country, with a per-capita rate of gun ownership more than twice that of the runner-up (Yemen). Gun ownership has been rising steadily for years. But demand fluctuates in response to the political mood, mass shootings and other events. Sales spiked in spring 2020, as Americans worried that covid lockdowns might presage stricter arms control and also about a broader breakdown in social order. They rose again that June, and the months following, during unrest after the killing of George Floyd, an unarmed black man, by a police officer. In 2020 and the first half of 2021 record numbers of people underwent the background checks required to purchase a gun legally. But the number of these checks dropped an average 16% per month between June and October compared with the same period last year. By November they had dropped 25%.

America has no national registry of guns. So the Federal Bureau of Investigation’s National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS) is a widely used, though imperfect, proxy for demand. Licensed gun sellers use the NICS (or state-based equivalents) to determine whether potential buyers have criminal or other records that would make them ineligible to purchase a firearm. Small Arms Analytics Forecasting (SAAF), a research consultancy, calculates that 1.7m guns were bought in November 2021, a 21% drop from the record 2.1m the November before. (These numbers are based on an adjustment of the NICS numbers that excludes checks unlikely to lead to gun sales, but which does not capture all private sales by unlicensed sellers. The true sales figures are likely therefore to be higher.)

Though sales remain elevated compared to pre-pandemic levels, declines are occurring across all states studied, each of which has its own laws governing background checks. Analysis by SAAF chief economist Jurgen Brauer and The Economist shows a median 31% drop for handguns and 20% for long guns in states won by Donald Trump in the 2020 election, and 27% for handguns and 17% for long guns in states won by Joe Biden.

Why have sales slowed? Concerns about the breakdown of law and order during the pandemic have calmed, reckons Timothy Lytton, an expert on the American gun industry at Georgia State University. The pandemic seemed to encourage first-time gun buyers. Others went shopping to show support for the Second Amendment of the constitution, which guarantees the right to bear arms—perceived by some to be under threat after the election of a Democratic president. “People are no longer marching on state capitols calling for the heads of governors,” he says, “and there has been a lowering of anxiety around the pandemic, and the election of Biden.”

In 2022 the Supreme Court will issue an opinion on whether to uphold New York’s restrictions on concealed weapons. It will be the first case related to the Second Amendment in more than a decade. Striking down the New York law might suggest greater challenges ahead for states that want to restrict the ownership of guns, explains Mr Lytton. That could actually dampen demand for guns, since future supply might seem more assured. But if the ruling goes the other way America might yet again see an increase in people looking to arm themselves.
