It's Not Too Late to Get Your Flu Shot

By Ally Hirschlag

7 days ago

Flu season is officially underway, but that doesn't mean it's too late for you to get your flu vaccine. While doctors and the CDC recommends you get vaccinated by the end of October (young children who need two doses should start the vaccination process sooner) before flu season kicks into high gear, it can still help protect you from getting sick even if you don't get it until later in the season.

The three flu seasons before the COVID-19 pandemic were particularly impactful. During the 2019/20 flu season, an estimated 38,000,000 Americans had symptomatic flu, 18,000,000 sought medical attention and 400,000 were hospitalized for it. After each flu season, however, the CDC estimates how much the flu vaccine helped prevent cases, hospitalizations and deaths related to the flu, and the numbers show getting vaccinated makes a difference. According to the estimates made for the 2017/18 flu season, for example, vaccinations helped prevent approximately 7.1 million illnesses related to flu, 3.7 million medical visits, 109,000 hospitalization and 8,000 deaths. And that's with a vaccine that was only considered approximately 38% effective.

It's difficult for scientists to predict just how severe a flu season will be in the US. They can look to the southern hemisphere's flu season to gauge how severe it might be in the northern hemisphere, because their flu season happens earlier in the year, but there are environmental, cultural and sociological factors that could change the ultimate flu impact in the northern hemisphere. A smart approach is to protect yourself as best you can for what could end up being an intense flu season. And while social distancing, washing your hands and avoiding touching your face are all great ways to keep respiratory viruses at bay, getting your flu vaccine is still considered your best defense against the flu.

The flu vaccine changes every year in order to contain flu strains that are expected to circulate during the upcoming flu season. They’re egg-based, cell-based or recombinant-based, and are designed to help protect against the three or four most common influenza viruses research suggests are likely to impact the US during the upcoming flu season. While the vaccine is typically delivered as an injection, a nasal spray vaccine that’s similarly effective is also available.

It’s important to note that once you get the flu vaccine, it won’t reach its full potency until about 14 days later, which means you’ll be more likely to get the flu before then. The overall flu vaccine coverage rate for the 2021/22 season for people 6 months and older is stated to be 58.5%, the highest coverage rate the CDC has recorded since 2010 when it began recommending everyone over 6 months old be vaccinated in the US. The longer you wait to get the flu vaccine, the more susceptible you may be be to getting sick this season.

The flu vaccine is designed to help protect you from getting sick, but since it’s not 100% effective, there’s still a chance you could contract the flu. If you do get the flu though, the vaccine has been seen to help reduce your chances of being hospitalized as a result of the flu by 40%. Perhaps more importantly though, getting vaccinated helps protect others who are particularly vulnerable, like children, the immune-compromised and people over the age of 65, from getting seriously ill.

All these factors considered, there’s no better time than the present to get your flu vaccine. If you’re unsure which type to get or want to learn more about how they work to fight strains of the flu, talk to your healthcare provider.
