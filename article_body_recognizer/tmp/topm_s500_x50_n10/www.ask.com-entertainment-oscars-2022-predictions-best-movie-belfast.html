Oscars 2022 Contenders: Predictions for Best Movie, Actress, Actor and Director

By Patricia PuentesLast Updated December 10, 2021
Follow Us:
Facebook
Twitter

When it comes to awards season, it’s never too early to start speculating, especially now that most of the big fall movie titles have been released — some of them only in a limited capacity and number of theaters — and seen by most critics.

We could still see some last-minute twists. Last year Minari and Nomadland already looked like the movies to beat at the Oscars after they premiered at Sundance and TIFF, respectively, but Promising Young Woman didn’t become a favorite contender for the Best Director and Best Original Screenplay categories until late into the season.

Here are some of the titles, filmmakers and performers leading the 2022 Oscars race so far with chances of getting coveted nominations. The 94th Academy Awards will take place on March 27, 2022. Nominations will be announced on February 8.

Oscars 2022: Best Movie Predictions

Kenneth Branagh’s black and white tale about a nine-year-old boy growing up in Belfast in the 1960s started award season as the title to beat at the Oscars. Belfast won the TIFF 2021 People’s Choice Award — and we’ve already told you how the Canadian festival has become a solid Oscar predictor. Nomadland won the same award there in 2020. 

But as the season progressed, other titles have climbed to the position of main contenders for best pic nominations. Among them are Jane Campion’s The Power of the Dog — with Benedict Cumberbatch playing a fierce rancher who torments his brother’s (Jesse Plemons) wife (Kirsten Dunst) — and Reinaldo Marcus Green’s King Richard — with Will Smith playing Richard Williams, the father of Venus (Saniyya Sidney) and Serena Williams (Demi Singleton). The movie tells the story of the teenage tennis champions as their dad and mom (Aunjanue Ellis) help them follow a meticulous plan to success. 

The Best Picture category, which this year will include 10 nominees, could also make room for more commercial fare like Denis Villeneuve’s big-screen adaptation of Dune. Relative newcomer Jeymes Samuel could also see his stylized western The Harder They Fall recognized in this category. Not for nothing, the movie boasts a contemporary soundtrack and a star-packed casting headlined by Regina King, Idris Elba, Jonathan Majors, Zazie Beetz, LaKeith Stanfield and Delroy Lindo.

Joel Coen’s black and white adaptation of the classic Shakespeare tale The Tragedy of Macbeth — starring Denzel Washington and Frances McDormand — could also find a spot among the nominated films. As is the case with Pablo Larraín’s Spencer, where he imagines what might have happened during the Christmas holidays Princess Diana (Kristen Stewart) spent at the Queen’s Sandringham Estate when her marriage with Prince Charles had already imploded.

Let’s also add to the mix one of my favorite films of 2021: CODA. Written and directed by Siân Heder, this coming-of-age story explores the options of a teenager (Emilia Jones) torn between her sense of duty to her family — she’s the CODA (child of deaf adults) of the family since both her parents and her brother are deaf — and her dream of making a career in music.

There are a few more titles that could be in contention for the Best Picture nomination and they benefit from being directed by established filmmakers. The favorite among this set is Steven Spielberg’s reimagining of the musical West Side Story. But I should also mention Guillermo Del Toro’s Nightmare Alley, Paul Thomas Anderson’s Licorice Pizza, Adam McKay’s Don’t Look Up, Ridley Scott’s House of Gucci and The Last Duel, Lin-Manuel Miranda’s tick, tick…Boom!, Aaron Sorkin’s Being the Ricardos and Pedro Almodóvar’s Parallel Mothers.

Notice the last paragraph was full of men’s names. So let me toss in a few titles written and directed by women. They haven’t been included in many favorite lists so far this year, but I would love for Céline Sciamma’s Petite Maman, Rebecca Hall’s Passing and Mia Hansen-Løve’s Bergman Island to all make it to the final list of nominations. The first title explores the idea of motherhood from the perspective of a young girl. The second delves into the idea of identity and the many ways we can pass as something or someone we are not. The third tells the story of a filmmaker who strives to be all things: a creative, lover, mother and self. 

And even though it wasn’t one of my favorite 2021 films, the other title directed by a woman with chances of making it to the Best Picture list — aside from The Power of the Dog and CODA — is Maggie Gyllenhaal’s directorial debut and adaptation of Elena Ferrante’s The Lost Daughter.

Oscars 2022: Best Director Predictions

ADVERTISEMENT

Kenneth Branagh also has his name locked in to get a Best Director nomination. Belfast is the most personal movie the Irish filmmaker has helmed in years. The film is based on his own childhood growing up in Belfast.

Jane Campion, who was nominated for directing The Piano (1993), is the other safe name on this list of possible director contenders. The filmmaker had been working on the TV series Top of the Lake most recently; this is her first film since Bright Star in 2009.

Denis Villeneuve could also receive a nomination for his work in Dune, as is the case of Reinaldo Marcus Green for King Richard, Pablo Larraín for Spencer and Joel Coen — notice he’s not co-directing with his brother Ethan on this occasion — for The Tragedy of Macbeth. I would be pleasantly surprised if Jeymes Samuel receives a nomination for his genre-bending work in The Harder They Fall. Samuel’s and Green’s nominations would also show the Academy’s commitment to diversity. So far, only six Black directors have been nominated in this category.

There are a few more established filmmakers in the list of favorites to receive a nomination and several of them are multiple award-winners or nominees. I’m talking about Steven pielberg (West Side Story), Guillermo del Toro (Nightmare Alley), Aaron Sorkin (Being the Ricardos), Adam McKay (Don’t Look Up) and Paul Thomas Anderson (Licorice Pizza).

Since last year the Academy made history by nominating two women for the first time in the Best Directing category — previously, only five women had been nominated as directors in total and never in the same year — I’m going to offer a few women’s names and hope this year the Oscars keep aiming for diversity: Emma Seligman proves funerals can be somewhat funny in Shiva Baby, Julia Ducournau terrifies in Cannes’ Palme d’Or winner Titane, and Eva Husson has a new novel adaptation for fans of Atonement (2007) with Mothering Sunday. And I’ve already told you about CODA’s Siân Heder, Rebecca Hall adapting Nella Larsen’s novel of the same name in Passing, Petite Maman’s Céline Sciamma, The Lost Daughter’s Maggie Gyllenhaal and Bergman Island’s Mia Hansen-Løve. Due to her starpower, Gyllenhaal is the one with more chances of getting her work recognized from this list. 

Oscars 2022: Best Actress Predictions

When it comes to Best Actress performances, I told you weeks ago Jessica Chastain had her name written all over a nomination thanks to her work in the biopic The Eyes of Tammy Faye. She’s still one of the front-runners, as is Kristen Stewart for her role as Princess Diana in the suffocating Spencer. And since the film is so different tonally from The Crown, there are no chances of getting her performance confused with the one from Emmy-nominated Emma Corrin, who played Diana in seasons three and four of the Netflix show.

Talking about The Crown, Emmy- and Oscar-winning actress and the Queen herself Olivia Colman could be grabbing yet another nomination for her role in The Lost Daughter. While Maggie Gyllenhaal’s adaptation of Elena Ferrante’s novel is far from perfect, Colman shows once again she can transform herself into practically any character. Here she plays a college professor and writer confronted with her past while on vacation in Greece.

Tessa Thompson could also snag a nomination for her work in Passing. Set in New York in the 1920s, the movie tells the story of two Black women who can “pass” as white. One of them, Clare (Ruth Negga), decides to live as a white woman. The other, Irene (Thompson), lives as a Black woman. The movie, which is shot in black and white, shows how different their experiences are because of it.

The other favorite actresses on this list are Penélope Cruz for her role as an independent photographer and zealous mother in Pedro Almodóvar’s soapy Parallel Mothers; Lady Gaga and her over-the-top Italian accent in House of Gucci; and Frances McDormand’s Lady Macbeth in The Tragedy of Macbeth. 

Nicole Kidman could also make it to the selected list of five nominees for her portrayal of the sitcom royal Lucille Ball in Being the Ricardos. And personally I would love it if Jodie Comer gets nominated for her role in Ridley Scott’s The Last Duel, where she plays a survivor of rape who decides to accuse her attacker despite the societal norms of the time. The movie is based on real events and set in 14th century France but it resonates eerily with present times.

Other actresses who could get a nomination for their work in this category are Rachel Zegler in West Side Story, Emilia Jones in CODA, Vicky Krieps in Bergman Island and Jennifer Hudson in the Aretha Franklin biopic Respect.

Oscars 2022: Best Actor Predictions

ADVERTISEMENT

Let’s talk about actors. There are three names that pretty much have a nomination secured: Will Smith in King Richard, Denzel Washington in The Tragedy of Macbeth and Benedict Cumberbatch in The Power of the Dog. But Smith is probably the one who’s done more of the heavy-lifting when it comes to aggressively campaigning for a statuette.

One of the remaining two slots could go to the always superb Peter Dinklage for Cyrano, directed by Joe Wright and adapted for the screen by Erica Schmidt from her musical stage adaptation of Edmond Rostand’s Cyrano de Bergerac. Dinklage also played the titular character in the theater version and proves he can act — and sing.

Then there’s the character actor Clifton Collins (Star Trek, Westworld) who could see his work in the indie Jockey recognized. The movie follows Collins as a veteran jockey in what could be his last season on the saddle. 

As other films have been more widely watched by critics and guild members, new names of possible contenders have cropped up. That’s the case for Andrew Garfield for his portrayal of playwright Jonathan Larson (Rent) in Lin-Manuel Miranda’s tick, tick…Boom! Bradley Cooper transforms himself into a dapper con artist in Guillermo del Toro’s 1940s New York-based Nightmare Alley. The Spaniard Javier Bardem speaks and sings in Cuban-accented tones as Desi Arnaz in Being the Ricardos. And Leonardo DiCaprio manages one of his best performances in years as the sneakers-with-tweed-jacket-and-glasses-wearing astronomy professor Dr. Randall Mindy in Don’t Look Up.

And while I believe Oscar Isaac’s best performance this year has been this chemistry-filled moment with Jessica Chastain on the red carpet while promoting the limited series Scenes of a Marriage, he may snag an Oscar nomination for his role in Paul Schrader’s The Card Counter.
