Abstract

A so-called COVID-19 passport or Immunity passport (IP) has been proposed to facilitate the mobility of individuals while the SARS-CoV-2 pandemic persists. A COVID-19 passport can play a key role in the control of the pandemic, specifically in areas with a high density of population, and the help of smart city technology could be very useful to successfully implement IPs. This research studies the impact of ethical judgments on user attitudes toward using vaccine passports based on a Multidimensional Ethics Scale (MES) that contains five ethical constructs: moral equity, relativism, egoism, utilitarianism, and contractualism. Regression analysis shows that MES satisfactorily explains attitude (R2 = 87.82%, p < 0.001) and that a positive evaluation in moral equity, egoism and utilitarianism is significant (p < 0.001). The objective of the passport (variable leisure) shows a significant negative moderating effect on moral equity (coefficient = −0.147, p = 0.0302) and a positive one on relativism (coefficient = 0.158, p = 0.0287). Adjustment by means of fsQCA shows that five ethical constructs satisfactorily explain both favorable and unfavorable attitudes toward IPs. Solutions explaining acceptance attain an overall consistency (cons) = 0.871 and coverage (cov) = 0.980. In the case of resistance, we found that cons = 0.979 and cov = 0.775. However, that influence is asymmetrical. To have a positive attitude toward the passport, it is a sufficient condition to attain a positive evaluation on a single ethical factor. On the other hand, when explaining resistance, and with the exception of the recipe ~utilitarianism (cons = 0.911 and cov = 0.859), explanatory prime implications require the interaction of at least two variables. Likewise, the context in which the passport is required is significant to explain rejection. View Full-Text

Author Biographies

Mario Arias-Oliva
Mario Arias-Oliva holds a Ph.D. in Marketing and Finance by Rovira i Virgili University and a Ph.D. in Information Science by Complutense University of Madrid. He is member of the International Advisory Board of the Centre for Business Information Ethics (Meiji University, Tokyo, Japan) and International Research Associate of the Centre for Computing and Social Responsibility (De Montfort University, Leicester, UK). His research filed is technology acceptance, publishing in international top journals papers about cryptocurrency acceptance, cyborg technologies and robotics. He has presented papers in more than a hundred International Conferences. Currently he is developing his academis activity in Complutense University of Madrid (Spain).
Jorge Pelegrín-Borondo
Jorge Pelegrín-Borondo holds PhD in Business by Rovira i Virgili University and Professor of Marketing in La Rioja University. He has conducted research focused on consumer behavior, technology acceptance, mobile marketing, e-commerce, and tourism. He is member of the International Advisory Board of the Centre for Business Information Ethics (Meiji University, Japan). Dr. Pelegrín-Borondo has published in different high standing international journals such as Journal of Business Ethics (2020), Computers in Human Behavior (2020), Journal of Retailing and Consumer Services (2020), Tourism Management Perspectives (2020), Journal of Wine Research (2020). He is the author of textbooks in relevant editors. He has participated in more than 50 international conferences.
Ala Ali Almahameed
Ali Almahameed holds Ph.D. from Rovira i Virgili University (Spain), a Bachelor’s Degree in Electronics Engineering and a Master’s degree in Business Administration in Jordan. His research filed is technology acceptance of emerging technologies, focusing in their recent papers in the analysis of robots impats in business and society. He is Research Associate in the Social and Business Research Laboratory (SBRL Lab) in Rovira i Virgili University (Spain). In the professional field he has over 15 years of experience as Senior Contracts Management Specialist in Aljazeera Media Network in Qatar.
Jorge de Andrés-Sánchez
