All articles published by MDPI are made immediately available worldwide under an open access license. No special permission is required to reuse all or part of the article published by MDPI, including figures and tables. For articles published under an open access Creative Common CC BY license, any part of the article may be reused without permission provided that the original article is clearly cited.

Feature Papers represent the most advanced research with significant potential for high impact in the field. Feature Papers are submitted upon individual invitation or recommendation by the scientific editors and undergo peer review prior to publication.

The Feature Paper can be either an original research article, a substantial novel research study that often involves several techniques or approaches, or a comprehensive review paper with concise and precise updates on the latest progress in the field that systematically reviews the most exciting advances in scientific literature. This type of paper provides an outlook on future directions of research or possible applications.

Editor’s Choice articles are based on recommendations by the scientific editors of MDPI journals from around the world. Editors select a small number of articles recently published in the journal that they believe will be particularly interesting to authors, or important in this field. The aim is to provide a snapshot of some of the most exciting work published in the various research areas of the journal.

Manuscript Submission Information

Manuscripts should be submitted online at www.mdpi.com by registering and logging in to this website. Once you are registered, click here to go to the submission form. Manuscripts can be submitted until the deadline. All papers will be peer-reviewed. Accepted papers will be published continuously in the journal (as soon as accepted) and will be listed together on the special issue website. Research articles, review articles as well as short communications are invited. For planned papers, a title and short abstract (about 100 words) can be sent to the Editorial Office for announcement on this website.

Submitted manuscripts should not have been published previously, nor be under consideration for publication elsewhere (except conference proceedings papers). All manuscripts are thoroughly refereed through a single-blind peer-review process. A guide for authors and other relevant information for submission of manuscripts is available on the Instructions for Authors page. Diversity is an international peer-reviewed open access monthly journal published by MDPI.

Please visit the Instructions for Authors page before submitting a manuscript. The Article Processing Charge (APC) for publication in this open access journal is 1800 CHF (Swiss Francs). Submitted papers should be well formatted and use good English. Authors may use MDPI's English editing service prior to publication or during author revisions.

Title: Feeding of an explorer Marsican brown bear (Ursus arctos marsicanus Altobello, 1921) in the Sibillini Mountains (Marche, Central Italy). Implications for environmental managemen Authors: Kevin Cianfaglione Affiliation: Université de Lorraine Nancy, France Abstract: The brown bear (U. arctos) is a species of a high variability degree, including a large number of local geographical or ecological types (Couturier, 1954). The Marsican brown bear (Ursus arctos marsicanus, Altobello 1921) is a central and southern Apennines typical subspecies (Boscagli, 1988; Boitani, 2003 and Frapporti, 2003). Nowadays the Marsican brown bear is only present as a limited and very endangered population on a smaller residual territory, mainly represented by the Abruzzo National Park (PNALM), which constitutes the permanent presence area of this bear subspecies. Its residual population is variable, estimated between 37 to 52 individuals (Ciucci et al., 2010). The study area belongs to the Monti Sibillini Massif. The territory is mainly characterized by mountainous and hilly areas, located at an altitude which ranges between 300 m a.s.l. (valley floor areas) and 2 476 m a.s.l. (Monte Vettore Mt.). The Marsican bear extinction from the Sibillini Mountains (and surrounding areas) is mainly due to direct persecutions, which happened during the mid-nineteenth century. The historical presence of the animal reflects into various toponyms still existing (eg: Valle Orsara, Grotta dell’Orso and others), deriving from stories handed down over time by local people, and by historical evidences reported over the centuries by various authors. In the last 20 years, however, few Marsican bear sightings led to the hypothesis of an occasional presence on Sibillini Mountains, marked by single explorer individuals coming from Abruzzo. We analyzed the diet of the last explorer bear, which for the first time was studied and stalked during its residence period at the Sibillini Mountains, where it lasted for 4 years. Fieldworks were taken in order to follow its feed and behaviour, by collecting and analyzing stool samples gathered during various seasons of the year. The analyses consisted of identifying the taxa by evaluating his feed (qualitative and quantitative analysis) This project represents a first study carried out on this species outside its main range (Abruzzo). It makes a contribution both to the knowledge of the Marsican bear explorers behaviour and to the diet of this bear. Various observations and environmental management strategies (particularly referred to the data obtained) are proposed with the aim of improving policies and territorial planning, willing to make the environmental conditions better in favour of the Marsican bear areal, and to foster the return of this bear in other zones of its potential area, thus increasing the chances of saving this animal from extinction.

Title: Biological interaction as a possible outcome for the extinction of Cedrus atlantica (Endl.) Carrière in the Iberian Peninsula Authors: Julio Peñas de Giles Affiliation: Departamento de Botánica, Facultad de Ciencias, Universidad de Granada, Avda. Severo Ochoa, 18071 Granada, Spain Abstract: The presence of Cedrus atlantica on the European continent, and especially the moment of its disappearance in the Iberian Peninsula, is one of the most controversial issues in recent decades regarding the successive extinction of conifers in the Western Mediterranean. This work raises the possibility that C. atlantica and Pinus nigra could have interacted in the past, mutually excluding each other in the areas where they shared a niche and where, ultimately, the one that was most competitive would remain. The overlapping of their niches in the two-dimensional ecological space has been analyzed. Also, the potential distribution of both species in the Western Mediterranean today and two past periods (Last Glacial Maximum and Mid-Holocene) has been modeled to identify their common geographic area of distribution. The species show very well differentiated niches and a distribution of their habitats segregated by continents (P. nigra in Europe and C. atlantica in Africa), which responds to differences in climatic affinities. However, the contact of their distributions in border areas suggests that C. atlantica maintained its presence in the Iberian Peninsula until recent times, and that P. nigra could have displaced it due to its higher prevalence on the continent.
