Abstract

A new approach to the construction of difference schemes of any order for the many-body problem that preserves all its algebraic integrals is proposed herein. We introduced additional variables, namely distances and reciprocal distances between bodies, and wrote down a system of differential equations with respect to the coordinates, velocities, and the additional variables. In this case, the system lost its Hamiltonian form, but all the classical integrals of motion of the many-body problem under consideration, as well as new integrals describing the relationship between the coordinates of the bodies and the additional variables are described by linear or quadratic polynomials in these new variables. Therefore, any symplectic Runge–Kutta scheme preserves these integrals exactly. The evidence for the proposed approach is given. To illustrate the theory, the results of numerical experiments for the three-body problem on a plane are presented with the choice of initial data corresponding to the motion of the bodies along a figure of eight (choreographic test).

Author Biographies

Yu Ying
Yu Ying is a candidate of the Physical and Mathematical Sciences (Peoples’ Friendship University of Russia, 2020) and an assistant professor at the School of Science, Kaili University.
Ali Baddour
Ali Baddour is a Ph.D. student at the Department of Applied Probability and Informatics of Peoples’ Friendship University of Russia (RUDN University).
Vladimir P. Gerdt
Vladimir Gerdt (1947–2021) earned his M.Sc. in Theoretical Physics from Saratov State University in 1971, completed his Ph.D. in Theoretical and Mathematical Physics at JINR in 1976 and then his D.Sc. in Mathematics and Computer Science at JINR in 1992. In 1997, he received the scientific title of Professor in Mathematics and Computer Science. After his M.Sc., Vladimir Gerdt worked at JINR until his death in January 5, 2021. He began as an engineer-programmer (1971–1975) and then worked as a junior researcher (1975–1977) at the JINR Department of Radiation Safety where he developed a software for neutron spectroscopy. In 1977, he moved to the JINR Laboratory of Computing Techniques and Automation, renamed in 2000 as the Laboratory of Information Technologies where he worked as a researcher (1977–1980) and as a senior researcher (1980–1983). Since 1983, the has been the head of the research group on computer algebra.
Leonid Sevastianov
Leonid Sevastianov received his Dr. Sc. degree in Physics–Mathematics from the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research, Dubna, Russia, in 1999, and his Ph.D. degree in Physics–Mathematics from the Peoples’ Friendship University of Russia. Now, Prof. Sevastianov is giving classes and lectures at the Department of Applied Probability and Informatics of the Peoples’ Friendship University of Russia, Moscow. and is a leading researcher of the Bogoliubov Laboratory of Theoretical Physics at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research, Dubna. His research deals with the mathematical modeling of a wide range of processes and systems of both natural and artificial origin. This includes the modeling of thin-film multi-layer diffractive optical gratings of integrated optical waveguide structures and quantum measurements modeling.
