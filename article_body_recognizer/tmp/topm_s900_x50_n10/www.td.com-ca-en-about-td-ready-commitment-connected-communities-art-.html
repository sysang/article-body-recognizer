TD Art

When the TD Art Collection was founded in 1968, it was premised on the idea that art is a catalyst for dialogue; creating conversations and enriching the lives of TD colleagues, clients and customers. Today, this remains our guiding principle. TD Art is focusing on work that enables us all to see more of the world around us. Art in the workplace can offer motivation and connection to others. This is what moves us to continue curatorial research and to search out works that inspire meaningful relationships.

We seek to create accessible art experiences for the public and expand its perception of contemporary art and the role of artists in our society. We are building a collection that emphasizes the stories of marginalized artists, and that brings issues of diaspora, race, gender and identity to the forefront. Please enjoy the works on this site, as we amplify voices and champion an inclusive present and future.

TD Gallery of Indigenous Art

The TD Gallery of Indigenous Art is a rotating exhibition space that presents work from the Corporate Art Collection representing regions across Canada. Through its various pieces, we hope to share the historical and ongoing cultural importance of Indigenous communities in Canada.

Experience the works virtually with the TD Gallery of Indigenous Art Self-Guided Tour.

Edge of a Moment, 2017 Giclée pigment print. Acquired by TD in 2018. Meryl McMaster Siksika Nation/Canadian. Born in Ottawa, 1988. By blending history and personal identity in a decidedly contemporary way, McMaster is a leading voice in a generation that is reclaiming Indigenous representation. More than just a spectacular backdrop, the setting for this image is the Head Smashed in Buffalo Jump in Alberta, a place that was, for thousands of years, a site where bison were sustainably hunted by the Plains People. After colonization, the bison were hunted to near extinction.

Souvenirs of the Self, 1991 Giclée pigment print. Acquired by TD in 2018. Jin-me Yoon Born, South Korea 1960, Canadian Yoon is an internationally renowned artist using photography and new media and performance to explore, culture, ethnicity, nationhood and history. This work aims to challenge constructs of Canadian identity. The photograph empowers the use of a panoramic and an iconic Canadian landscape – where the artist inserts herself as a "tourist" within Banff National Park, Alberta. The scale of the backdrop and the gaze of the artist engages the viewer on the complexity of assumptions, identity, citizenship and belonging.

Winter Landscape with Pink House, 1918 Oil on canvas. Acquired by TD in 1964. Lawren Harris Canadian. Born in Brantford, Canada, 1885–1970. Lawren Harris is arguably one of the best-known artist in Canada’s history. A founding member of the Group of Seven, he helped develop what we think of today as Canadian landscape painting. The landscape is recognizable, with an incredible range of colours. Within a simple composition, Harris manages to capture the mystical powers of nature. Harris’s contribution to Canadian art was to strike out and discover a painting style that wasn’t simply imitating or replicating the styles found in Europe, but, instead, unique to and reflective of Canada.

Owl of the sea

Kenojuak Ashevak C.C., O.N., artist (born 1927-2016) - Ikerrasak camp, South Baffin Island, A Companion of the Order of Canada and winner of the Governor General’s Award in Visual and Media Arts, Ashevak is perhaps the best-known Inuit artist because of her famous print The Enchanted Owl (1960), which was featured on a Canada Post stamp. She was also the first woman to become involved with the newly established printmaking shop at Cape Dorset.

Composition 1952 - Oil on canvas - 79 x 110 inches Jean-Paul Riopelle Canadian, Born in Montreal in 1923 Widely regarded as Canada’s most important modern artist, Riopelle's work was in the Abstract Expressionist style, and often compared to American artist Jackson Pollock. Riopelle became associated with the group of Canadian painters known as Les Automatistes – a style of subconscious and intuitive creation. He represented Canada at the Venice Biennale in 1954 achieving international acclaim.

Late Bloomer 2010 - Oil on canvas - 75 x 90 inches Elizabeth McIntoshr Canadian, Born in Simcoe, Ontario, in 1967 McIntosh has been dedicated to abstraction and concerns of painting for almost 25 years. The artist is interested in how a quote, an image or an historical painting can inspire new compositions that feel both familiar and strange. The work here holds robust patterns, polka dots set amidst a layered field of white and cream, the colours remind the viewer of a palette of classic colours of ice cream, allowing us to think about what is both close and foreign.

October! 2016 - Canvas, glue, acrylic fashe pigment - 72 x 72 inches Sojourner Truth Parsons Canadian, Born in Toronto in 1984 Combining elements of painting, sculptural assemblage and folk art, this Toronto based artist is interested in presenting the truth of lived experiences and borrowed histories. Working on a large scale, Parsons’ mixed media composition here includes materials such as fabric scraps, cardboard, glue and paint. This piece has a magical presence that is captivating and memorable.

Office Party 2017 - Acrylic on canvas - 60 x 72 inches Lawrence Paul Yuxweluptun Coast Salish Okanagan descent, Canadian, Born in Kamloops in 1957. Both artist and activist, Yuxweluptun masterfully merges traditional icons with contemporary representations of the environment, colonization and the human spirit. This large-scale work holds powerful imagery that asks us to think deeply about the environment, shared histories and our own everyday life. In 2016, the Museum of Anthropology, Vancouver, BC, presented Unceded Territories a retrospective exhibition of the artist's thirty-year art career.
