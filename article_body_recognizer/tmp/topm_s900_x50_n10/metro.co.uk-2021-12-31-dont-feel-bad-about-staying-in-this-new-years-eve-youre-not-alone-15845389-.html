Don’t feel bad about staying home this New Year’s Eve – you’re not alone

New Year is all about deception.

Pre-pandemic, 31 December was traditionally the night in which we would be deceived into paying large sums of money to get into a venue we could attend for a quarter of the price any other day of the year.

We tricked ourselves into thinking that the cost, the queues, the cold, the 3am taxi journey and ensuing hangover will all be worth it because we did the thing that is expected of us.

Then when 1 January arrives, we lie to ourselves that a new diet, workout regime or sleep pattern would be the making of us. We vow to become vegan and sober, adopting the positive mental attitude of Dwayne Johnson at a yoga retreat and levels of ambition second only to James Corden’s agent.

We fall into the trap of believing an incremental change in the last digit of a date line has the potential to change our destiny. But this year, our fate (or at least the next seven days) is decided by a tiny red line on a lateral flow test and has the potential to ruin all of our plans.

Which is why I’ll be staying in tonight. But it doesn’t necessarily feel like a free choice.

Calling New Year’s Eve an anticlimax isn’t a hot take. But what could previously have led to a hangover and an empty wallet, now has the potential to leave us housebound and possibly very poorly.

While it’s completely legal (in England, at least) to go out and celebrate New Year mostly unrestricted – except for places like nightclubs, unless you provide a Covid-19 status check – we are told to be ‘cautious’, as the Omicron variant continues to spread like wildfire throughout our population.

Yesterday, Covid cases reached another record high of 189,213 and the PM has urged people who go out tonight to take a lateral flow test before doing so, despite the fact that there have been huge shortages of LFTs across the country, according to pharmacists.

It was also reported yesterday that Mark Drakeford, First Minister of Wales has agreed to loan the English NHS four million Covid test kits in order to relieve the shortage. Presumably he won’t be wanting them back.

Once again, we have been left in a situation where the Tory Government have let us down with their p*ss poor planning.

At the time of writing, there were no home delivery slots available for rapid lateral flow tests on the Government website, but they suggested that you may be able to pick one up from a pharmacy.

The Welsh, on the other hand, have closed their nightclubs for New Year – with pubs and restaurants told to adhere to strict guidelines. Like a designated driver, Wales has opted to be sensible while selflessly offering to take care of her vulnerable pal.

‘Remember the importance of ventilation’ said the Prime Minister about an evening in late December. Have you ever seen a window in a nightclub?

‘Be cautious’ is a phrase that Johnson leads with, but seemingly doesn’t do by example – if his cheese and wine-filled ‘work meeting’ in No 10’s garden is anything to go by. So it’s up to us to decide the level of risk we are willing to take, without the lateral flow tests for guidance.

You are no more capable of making changes to your life at midnight tonight than you are at 3:15pm on a Tuesday in May

Which is why this year (like the last) I’ll be spending it at home.

I want to say that I’m happy about having a quiet one, but I’d just be falling into the trap of New Year self-deception. The truth is, I long for motivational speeches from drunk girls in toilet cubicles. For steaming pub windows and 2am chips.

But I’d also be lying if I said I was sad to be staying in. The reassurance of a zero-risk evening might not be tantamount to fun, but at least I won’t feel anxious. Midnight will happen 364 more times this year, so there’s plenty of room for improvement. 

It’s fashionable nowadays to declare oneself an introvert when there are no viable options to be extraverted, but I don’t think I’m either. Being simply a vert will have to do for now.

Omicron, lateral flows and Covid aside, New Year can be difficult for many. For some, it’s a milestone by which they judge their achievements and perceived failures. For others, it’s a time where the pressure to have fun supersedes the ability to enjoy oneself.

But dates and times are inconsequential measures. You are no more capable of making changes to your life at midnight tonight than you are at 3:15pm on a Tuesday in May.

However you spend your evening, don’t force yourself to have fun if you’re not, and don’t deceive yourself into misery if you’re spending it alone. You might quite like it.

With so much pressure to be ‘happy’ and ‘new’ this weekend, I shall say my goodbyes to 2021 by simply wishing you a ‘year’.
