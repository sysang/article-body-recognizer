From sober shaming to wine time: The truth about alcohol and us

For Nicolle Alderson, drinking booze is a life or death situation.

As somebody who was previously dependent on alcohol, with a struggling liver and regular seizures, she lost everything – custody of her son, her home, a university scholarship and family relationships.

‘Drinking for me was a way to escape reality and myself,’ admits the 29-year-old. ‘From a young age I had developed and used many other ways to escape, but I found alcohol was the quickest and most effective way, as I could drink to blackout and not feel or remember anything.

‘It became an addiction I depended on and the more life spiralled out of control the more I needed alcohol to block out my reality. Eventually, I was drinking all day every day.’

Then, in 2018, Nicolle finally found recovery, aged 26. 

Today, with more than three years’ sobriety under her belt, she is rebuilding her life and reconnecting with friends and family.

However, it certainly wasn’t an easy road. ‘I found it difficult to socialise with friends who weren’t in recovery themselves,’ remembers Nicolle. ‘My sobriety was my priority so I stopped hanging out with friends who drank as I couldn’t risk picking up a drink.’

Nicolle says she also found that many social activities that don’t focus on alcohol are relatively expensive – for example going to the cinema, bowling or watching sports – so it was often hard to find ‘safe’, affordable things to do with friends.

The UK’s relationship with booze is a troubled one. At a time when many people will be nursing hangovers of Christmas excess, or vowing to go sober for January – or longer – it plays a vital role in many lives.

Shockingly, ONS statistics report that it was also responsible for almost 9,000 UK deaths in 2020 alone. So why is it still so heavily ingrained in everything we do?

From our social lives to university and into our professional careers, alcohol isn’t just something we place on the fun pedestal – it’s woven into our everyday fabric.

Speaking exclusively to Metro.co.uk, Professor David Nutt, professor of neuropsychopharmacology at Imperial College London, explains why it’s so difficult for us to change our attitudes when it comes to alcohol.

‘Many centuries of use, especially by the church, and the fact that pubs are often at the heart of many local communities, are both key to our acceptance of alcohol as a cultural and social norm,’ he says.

‘Lobbying by the drinks industry has also played a role, leading to relatively lower taxation than previously, so the real price of alcohol is almost a third of what it was in the 1950s, making it easily accessible.’

In his award-winning book, Drugs Without the Hot Air, Professor Nutt makes the case that alcohol is indeed the most dangerous drug at our disposal – regardless of its legal and socially acceptable status. ‘To put the danger of alcohol into perspective, it is one of the only drugs that can kill you if you stop taking it abruptly whilst physically dependent,’ he says. ‘Making it incredibly difficult to quit without specialist support.’ 

While not everybody who drinks becomes dependent or addicted, the figures are frighteningly high (Professor Nutt told Metro.co.uk that around 10-15 per cent of drinkers will become dependent).

However, there are other problems that arise through alcohol consumption too. Alcohol Change UK’s website quotes ONS figures that show alcohol is a factor in around 39% of all violent crimes in England and 49% in Wales.

It was after a bad experience with alcohol as a 17-year-old on the LGBT scene, that made Sam Thomas decide booze wasn’t for him.

‘I remember getting very drunk very easily and being followed to the toilet,’ he recalls. ‘Thankfully, I’d locked the cubicle door and after a brief exchange, I’d managed to get away.’

A few weeks later – and after meeting his first boyfriend who didn’t drink alcohol – Sam gave up for several years.

Then, when he was 24, one of his good friends spiked his soft drink with vodka on a night out. It was done as a ‘prank’ – something Sam admits that at the time he, along with his friends, found hilarious when he became tipsy.

However, the incident marked the start of his devastating relationship with booze.

‘From that point on I continued to drink on our twice weekly nights out,’ Sam recalls. ‘A year later, my mother died and it was around this time when drinking at home began to feature.

‘It crept up from two mini bottles of wine on a Saturday night to three full bottles every night of the week by the time I was 27. I didn’t even realise I was alcohol dependent until I had several major withdrawal episodes including hallucinations, the shakes and fever.

‘After many relapses, four psychiatric hospital admissions and a diagnosis of CPTSD, which was pre-existing from childhood but exacerbated by my alcoholism, I was finally able to get on the road to recovery on 10 November 2019.’

Although alcohol is often seen as a staple ingredient for socialising and having fun, it isn’t where booze begins and ends. It also infiltrates our study and our careers. 

For Nic Johnson, who works in marketing, not joining in with an alcoholic drink at work would often feel like he was missing out on career opportunities.

‘Drinking culture was always a big part of my working life,’ he remembers. ‘Meeting clients for nights out, having a few at events…

‘It’s often hard to notice the gradual creep. At first you’re doing it to unwind before the weekend, then it creeps into Monday and you start again for the weekend on Thursday. All of a sudden you’re drinking every day.’

Nic no longer drinks, but before giving up, he noticed he was close to the recommended weekly limit – which according to the NHS is no more than 14 units a week – every day.

‘There were quite a few times when I’d turn up to a presentation or meeting with a colossal hangover because I’d been out with clients or the team the night before,’ he admits.

‘In hindsight, it was just really unprofessional. However, you often felt that if you weren’t drinking and socialising you’d be missing out on an opportunity.

‘Stopping drinking isn’t an easy choice to make,’ he adds. ‘I tried cutting back but the drinking slowly started to creep up again. Now, after a few years, I realise that being sober has much more going for it.’

With many professional events revolving around booze – from Christmas parties to client wins or hitting targets – positive psychology practitioner and workplace culture change specialist, Ruth Cooper-Dickson, says the impact on those who don’t drink can be significant. 

‘Not only can colleagues feel excluded, they can also potentially miss out on professional opportunities if they’re not sitting at the bar until late into the night at a networking event,’ she explains. ‘For example, they might not be present when a deal is discussed or a new client relationship is being nurtured.’

Ruth also says it’s important to remember that people choose not to drink for a whole host of reasons – from religious to medication to having (or being affected by) addiction problems. 

Focusing more on daytime events where the expectation to drink less is one way for workplaces to accommodate non-drinkers, says Ruth. ‘Also having a wider range of non-alcoholic drinks options is important,’ she adds. ‘All too often the only non-alcohol option is a glass of still water or a concentrated orange juice.’

According to Unison, drug and alcohol use in the workplace is also a health and safety issue that can impact not only the individual, but other colleagues and the organisation itself.

The organisation states that employers have a legal responsibility to look after their employees’ health, wellbeing and safety, and if there is a problem with alcohol or drug misuse in the workplace, this may indicate a wider problem of stress amongst the workforce.

To combat such situations the Alcohol and Drugs Service charity in Yorkshire recently launched a specific service called Generis, dedicated to supporting workplaces with this issue. 

It advises employers, particularly those in safety critical industries, how to take a no-nonsense approach to improving workplace safety and reducing the shame and stigma surrounding alcohol and drug problems.

For many of us, the glamourisation of drinking is something that begins way before our working life, with the most recent statistics from the National Union of Students revealing that 85 per cent of students agree that drinking and getting drunk is part of university culture.

With further education posing as a challenging time for young people – especially those vulnerable to addictive behaviours or those affected by family addiction – socialising and making new friends often becomes a priority.

Rites of passage – often known as hazing – are also seen as a big part of university life. But sometimes with tragic consequences. In 2016, 20-year-old Newcastle student Ed Farmer died from excessive alcohol consumption that had taken place during a uni initiation.

Former student Will is five years in recovery after his alcohol use became unmanageable.

His relationship with alcohol began at a very early age – he first got drunk aged just twelve, and as he headed off into the world of university, things ramped up a gear. 

‘Once I got to university the culture was all about hitting happy hours and “getting smashed”,’ he recalls. ’That was life as a student and absolutely standard behaviour. The attitude, both at uni and into my professional career, was go hard or go home.” 

Recovery Connections, a charity based in Teesside, recognised the problem universities face in relation to alcohol after spending time in Texas learning about the collegiate recovery programmes available to US students.

The charity has since launched the UK’s first ever collegiate recovery programme at Teesside University, where Adam Petson is campus recovery co-ordinator. ‘Student life in the UK can be much different to the one portrayed in the films,’ he explains.

‘There isn’t a mass fraternity community who can support each other, in fact it can be a very lonely place. If you’re not prepared to fit in with some of the cultural norms, like attending regular parties or sessions in the local pub, you can quickly become isolated. Isolation can be a breeding ground for addictive behaviour.’

Adam, who is also a current student at Teesside, explains that to help students he makes recovery visible. ‘I promote the service that I can provide which includes being there to listen to students and letting them know they are not alone,’ he says. ‘Growing the campus recovery community is key, and once it’s there things can happen organically. 

‘For example, one member of the group, with the support of others, has set up a sober society, to create and plan sober social activities, ensuring that nobody is left feeling alone simply because they don’t drink alcohol.’

With ambitions to encourage more programmes of its kind around the UK (Birmingham University also now has an engaging campus recovery programme in place) Recovery Connections has also launched a free, national online forum, HEART (Higher Education and Recovery Talk) for all students in the UK who might be struggling with the issue.

But while the UK clearly has a problematic relationship with booze, we are certainly not unique. According to a spokesperson at drink charity Alcohol Change, it’s not really possible to compare different countries‘ drinking patterns because there are so many varying contributing factors ranging from national policy, access to services, pricing and cultural influences.

Lucy Holmes, Director of Research and Policy at the charity adds that while there’s a common problem with alcohol, there’s no single relationship with it. ‘Even those cultures we might think of as homogenous, like university students, in fact contain many various sub cultures around drinking,’ she explains. 

‘The various types of sports fans booze differently, and people can misunderstand or make incorrect assumptions around what is ‘normal’ drinking in the UK.’

In fact, in addition to drinking alcohol being a well established social ‘norm’, it is often glorified in many aspects of popular culture – with parenthood and work also being topics that seem to go hand in hand with boozy slogans.

A quick Google search delivers hundreds of kitchen signs, greetings cards and even novelty socks emblazoned with the words ‘gin o’clock’ or ‘Prosecco time’.

Normalising the use of alcohol for relief from everyday pressures – this messaging reinforces the permission we sometimes give ourselves to drink – as though we deserve it.

While those who do drink are certainly at risk of developing habits and excesses, Lucy says that the most recent ONS stats found that 24% of UK adults reported not drinking at all.

Although these figures perhaps demonstrates that the issue doesn’t affect as many people as it might once have, for those who do drink, safe consumption can be a difficult balance to strike.

Plus, the acceptability and availability of alcohol may make it increasingly problematic for those who are vulnerable to addiction or dependency.

‘While many people cut down on alcohol during lockdown, a worrying proportion (around a quarter) drank more,’ explains Lucy. ‘We’ve also seen a very worrying increase in deaths caused by alcohol in 2020 – a 19% increase compared to 2019.’

In a bid to minimise the impact of alcohol on our lives, Alcohol Change are working to reduce the stigma of being sober, through their campaign to stop ‘sober shaming’. The charity is alsocalling on government to commit to a cross-party national alcohol strategy, to incorporate a better approach to pricing and labelling.

They are also behind the popular Dry January campaign, encouraging people to give up alcohol for the first month of the year, while similar initiatives, such as Bee Sober’s ‘The Sober Experiment’, encourage 30-days of abstinence at any time of year.

Behaviour change specialist, Shahroo Izadi, has worked in addiction and recovery for many years and says the key to sustaining sobriety is re-framing how we think about it. 

‘Rather than focus on what is bad or unhealthy about alcohol, consider what it’s doing for you,’ She explains. ‘Consider it as a solution instead of a problem. That way, if you discover that the need it fulfils is that it’s helping you to better manage stress, anxiety or boredom you can either consider ways to reduce these states or find preferable coping strategies.

‘A lot of people learn that sobriety is an opportunity to remind themselves they’re giving alcohol too much credit. 

‘Since we don’t tend to ‘check-in’ with our drinking habits throughout our lives, it’s easy to be running on the outdated belief that we ‘need’ it to feel socially confident or less stressed, for example; when in fact we are more capable of dealing with this type of discomfort than we once were. 

‘We deserve to remind ourselves at different stages of our lives of how capable we are and how good our bodies can be at sitting with difficult experiences and self-regulating.’

However, for some people, like Nicolle, the risk of picking up another drink could be devastating.

Which is why not only do we need to rethink our relationship with alcohol as a nation, we also need to start being more inclusive of the needs of non-drinkers. For Nicolle, that means offering safe and sober spaces for those who need to stay away from alcohol.

Recovery cafe, George Street Social, is one of those places. Managed by The Road to Recovery Trust, it’s been a vital part of Nicolle’s recovery. 

‘It became a second home to me,’ she admits. ‘I knew I could walk in any time, grab a free cup of tea if I had no money and spend hours there without being asked to leave.

‘I know there will always be at least one other person there at any given time who really understands the struggle of addiction.

‘I never feel alone when I’m there. In fact, it’s helped save my life.’

If you need help with a drinking problem, you can call the national Alcoholic’s Anonymous helpline on 0800 9177 650.
