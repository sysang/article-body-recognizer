WFH? Here’s why storing passwords on your browser might be a bad idea

Cyber experts have warned of the dangers of storing passwords on browsers like Chrome or Edge following the arrival of a cheap and readily-available piece of malware.

Known as Redline Stealer, it can be bought from shady areas of the internet for as little as $150 (£111).

The team at security firm AhnLab have published a warning about it after they dealt with a security breach affecting a company staffed with remote workers.

‘The company where the damage occurred provided VPN [virtual private network] service to employees who were working from home to give access to the company’s internal network, and the employees connected to the VPN on the provided laptops or their PCs,’ the researchers explained.

‘The targeted employee used the password management feature provided by the web browser to save and use the account and password for the VPN site on the web browser.

‘While doing so, the PC was infected with malware targeting account credentials, leaking accounts and passwords of various sites, which also included the VPN account of the company.’

As more people in the UK continue to work from home during the onslaught of Covid-19, these kinds of hacks are becoming more of a threat.

Given that many people use browsers to store the long list of passwords the average person accrues, it seems a likely target.

In fact, the password management feature is enabled by default on any browser built on the Chromium codebase – which includes Google Chrome and Microsoft Edge.

Redline Stealer, which first appeared on the Russian dark web, works by targeting the database file where the account and password information is stored.

It is spread through phishing tactics from hackers.

‘Redline Stealer first appeared in March 2020, and phishing emails abusing the issue of Covid-19 were used,’ the researchers wrote.

‘It is known that the malware was then distributed in various methods such as phishing emails, abusing of Google advertisements, and disguising as a photo editing program.’

The malware is far from widespread at this point, and it is likely broswer companies will adapt to make their products more secure in future updates.

While built-in browser password managers might not be as secure as services like OnePassword, which released a 40-page document describing their encryption processes, they are still far more secure than not using one at all and using the same password for everything.

If you don’t want to pay, but are willing to download something and use third-party software, then LastPass’ free tier, or Myki Password Manager, will do most of what you need.

For a relatively small price, you can upgrade to a service like 1Password, LastPass’ paid tier or Keeper Password Manager, which will have the full range of features and cross platform capabilities.
