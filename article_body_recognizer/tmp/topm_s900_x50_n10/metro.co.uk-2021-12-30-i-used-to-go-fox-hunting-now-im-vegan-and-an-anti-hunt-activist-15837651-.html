I used to go fox hunting – now I’m vegan and an anti-hunt activist

I sat on a dapple grey pony named Salty and excitedly watched the lively gathering of people in smart tweed hunting jackets, beige jodhpurs and polished black boots. 

I was 13 at the time and it was a crisp morning in December 2000, where we were joining in the annual Boxing Day hunt on the Goss Moor in Cornwall, which was one of the highlights of the rural equestrian calendar.

There was the customary sherry toast for the adults and older children, accompanied by a defiant speech from the huntmaster, who wore a red jacket. I observed the long-established ritual with fascination, remembering that my granny had also hunted here as a child.

Immediately before we set off to a round of applause and cheers, he made a bold claim to the watching crowd – including hunt saboteurs and police officers – that they could whinge all they liked, but we’d be back every single year.

The huntmaster’s confidence was misplaced, as it turned out, and the bloodsport was banned in its traditional form across England and Wales a few years later in 2004. 

There have been at least 514 successful prosecutions under the Hunting Act since then until 2019, according to the pro-hunting Countryside Alliance.

Nowadays, I cringe at the recollection of the pompous, self-aggrandising speech I once viewed with youthful admiration.

In a strange twist of fate, I’m now an anti-hunt activist and vegan too. 

You’ll often find me campaigning – peacefully, I might add – for an end to all forms of animal cruelty, whether it’s hunting, experimenting, eating or wearing our fellow living creatures.

In the last year or so, I’ve attended community days and run campaign stalls, spoken to the public, and raised money for a local billboard advertising campaign about ending factory farming.

I’m delighted that the annual Boxing Day hunt has prompted renewed calls for an outright ban on trail hunting – where hounds follow a scent across the countryside – over fears it’s a smokescreen for illegal fox killing. 

Growing up on a remote Cornish farm, it’s unsurpising my first encounter with fox hunting came at a young age. I was probably only seven or eight years old when our horse-riding instructor took the entire class to a local hunt meet one rainy autumn morning.

Our small group of primary school-aged kids had a great time jumping over fences on horseback and galloping across the fields. We barely registered that we were pursuing a fox, who would inevitably be torn to shreds by a pack of blood-thirsty hounds.

I recall watching the angry hunt saboteurs protesting at rural meets after the ban

I’d been vegetarian since the age of five, when my uncle took me on a fishing trip in the seaside village of Fowey. In horror, I watched the fish being unceremoniously hauled out of the water and flapping around, before their necks were broken, spraying blood over the boat.

I never ate seafood or meat again.

Despite this, I failed to acknowledge the pain and suffering foxes would’ve felt. Fox hunting had been presented as a proud, English tradition, so I just took for granted that it was the right and ethical thing to do.

After all, I’d always been told by family and friends that the fox was a pest and it had killed all of our chickens; dug up the tender seedlings in the garden and probably spread all sorts of nasty diseases. 

I recall watching the angry hunt saboteurs protesting at rural meets after the ban. 

My friends and I, teenagers by then, laughed at their seemingly-ridiculous antics, as they spray-painted horse boxes and fought with local farmers, inevitably getting arrested by unsmiling police officers.

We couldn’t understand why they’d give up their time only to get nicked and end up with a fine from the local magistrates’ court.

Nothing changed for me when I taught horse riding and riflery in the American Midwest throughout my early 20s.

Hunting was even more heavily entrenched into the local culture in Michigan and there was a whole industry built-up around the deer, bear and woodcock seasons.

But on my return to England in 2011 at the age of 23, I had to accompany my boss – a Cornish Liberal Democrat MP – to an RSPCA reception at the annual party conference in Glasgow. The lobbying event had been organised by the charity’s CEO, who was a county councillor near us, in the south west.

I sat quietly through the speeches, feeling relieved that I’d left my laptop with its big ‘hunting for repeal’ sticker back at the hotel. And while silently eating my vegetarian curry, I listened to the CEO talk about the challenges of enforcing the national fox hunting ban.

It was the first time I heard another rural person – who genuinely understood British farming culture – criticise fox hunting. 

He calmly outlined the pain and suffering to the animal, while pointing out that the controversial sport wasn’t the only way to maintain the struggling rural economy. I found myself staring at my colleague’s chicken curry – why did I think it was acceptable to hunt foxes, but not to eat chickens?

I tried to dismiss the unwanted thoughts from my mind, but I often found myself wondering whether this hunting issue was as clear-cut as I’d originally believed.

I even stopped wearing my leather Mulberry Bayswater handbag that had, up until then, been my pride and joy. The calfskin reminded me of the awkward truth that animals had needlessly died simply to make my life better.

Less than a year later in 2012, I heard that Cardiff University had sewn kittens’ eyelids shut during a scientific experiment. I was devastated that the animals were killed afterwards. It felt unbelievably cruel – a claim the university ‘completely rejects’ – so I made a donation to PETA who were campaigning to end animal testing. 

I remember being vaguely disappointed that PETA sent me constant mailshots about animal cruelty. But in the short period of time between the letterbox and the bin, I’d always spot an impactful photo or tag-line that put me off consuming animal products or going hunting.

The final straw came the following summer when my step-sister – a vet – argued over lunch at our family’s farm that being vegetarian is pointless if you’re an animal-lover, as cows and chickens are killed once they can no longer produce dairy and eggs. 

I stopped eating animal produce from that point onwards and made a conscious decision not to support the fox hunting movement.

On reflection, the RSPCA event marked the first time I’d witnessed a critical conversation about fox hunting that didn’t involve personal insults, shouting or violence. 

The speaker didn’t attack the people who engaged in the sport – his focus was on the animals, so I didn’t simply write his opinion off as nonsense.

Now that I’ve experienced this education-based approach first-hand, I realise how powerful it is in comparison to violent or aggressive activism such as fighting, shouting and throwing paint. 

I understand why many animal rights activists feel angry at people who hunt, as innocent creatures are being hurt and that’s not OK. However, I feel that abusive language and behaviour is likely to discourage hunters from considering the consequences of their actions, as nobody really wants to engage with people who attack them.

As a campaigner, I use a non-confrontational approach to win over the hearts and minds of people who hunt, so we can persuade them that the Government needs to extend the ban on the sport to include trail hunting too. 

Five of my family members have stopped eating meat, including my sister and two step-sisters. Happily, none of our relatives go fox or trail hunting anymore and they say it’s a ‘dying sport’.

I’ve also noticed more of my countryside friends going vegan in recent years, especially as plant-based eating becomes more common.

The rural folk who go hunting hopefully already know that animals feel sorrow, suffering, fear and happiness, so I hope that this non-confrontational tactic will encourage more people to treat the fox with the compassion and respect it deserves.
