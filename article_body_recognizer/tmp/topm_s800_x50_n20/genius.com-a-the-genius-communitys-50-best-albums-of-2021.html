If I Can’t Have Love, I Want Power

PinkPantheress, Playboi Carti Halsey all made the cut, but who came out on top?

Well, it’s certainly been a year. Going into 2021, a lot of us hoped the pandemic would wind down and some of the general ugliness in the world would start to subside. That didn’t quite happen, but at least we got some great music to keep us going through the many ups and downs of the last 12 months.

Our annual poll of the Genius community’s top albums has always provided a terrific snapshot of what’s happening in music and the greater culture at any given moment, and this year’s list is no exception. It’s filled with both enduring superstars we know by first name—Taylor, Tyler, Billie, Adele—and plenty of newcomers who came out of nowhere and blessed us with music we couldn’t live without.

Leading the way in the latter category was Olivia Rodrigo, a singer, songwriter, and actress previously known only to fans of High School Musical: The Musical: The Series. Rodrigo took over the pop conversation in January 2021 with her breakup anthem “drivers license” and kept the teen angst coming with her debut album, sour, released in May.

The Genius community’s Top 50 also makes room for K-pop stars (Aespa, Stray Kids), indie rockers (Japanese Breakfast, Lucy Dacus), Italian rappers (Caparezza), and less definable talents like PinkPantheress, the U.K. phenom who rose to fame on TikTok with snippets of songs blending pop with drum’n’bass, jungle, and garage. Despite the rise of pop-punk and experimental hip-hop (see: Playboi Carti’s Whole Lotta Red, which ranks high on this list), no single sound defined the year.

As always, our super-dedicated, hyper-knowledgeable Genius contributors voted on an initial poll, discussed revisions and replacements, and then volunteered to write about their favorite albums. The blurbs below reveal their passion for the music that made 2021 livable.

25
Taylor Swift – evermore

As the sister album to Swift’s first surprise album, folklore, evermore continues its predecessor’s vivid storytelling and notes of escapism while still standing on its own as some of her strongest work to date. Highlights include the mystical lead single, “willow;” the true-crime-inspired “no body, no crime,” with HAIM; and the wistful “coney island”, with The National—all of which contain interesting tales to unfold.

Beyond its artistry and strong lyricism, evermore has found incredible commercial success. It became Swift’s eighth consecutive #1 album on the Billboard 200 and made her the female artist with the shortest time between two #1 releases, after folklore topped the chart in July. Upon its vinyl release earlier this year, it sold 102,000 copies, setting the record for the highest vinyl debut week in the U.S. since 1991. Despite being regarded as a companion piece to folklore, evermore has forged its own legacy as an immersive experience and a worthy follow-up to Swift’s biggest project to date. —Madison Murray

24
Lucy Dacus – Home Video

Lucy Dacus has always had a knack for making her songs feel transportive, telling stories not merely through relatability but also immersion. Nowhere is this clearer than her third album Home Video, where she reanimates memories of attending high school in suburban Virginia—the campfires (“Going Going Gone”), the Vacation Bible School (“VBS”), the best friend dating someone totally not worth her time (“Christine”). It’s a chronicle of nostalgia, sure, as well as one of emotional clarity. Dacus positions her narrators at the apex of epiphany, finally stomaching the ramifications of honesty, even if they are painful. “Thumbs” sees a daughter get clean with herself about an estranged father, and “Partner in Crime” experiments with autotune as a young woman experiments with a taboo relationship. Violence subtly undergirds Home Video, blood bubbling under the tongue; it’s exactly that dark underbelly that makes this album such a satisfying swallow. —Max Zhang

23
Rauw Alejandro – VICE VERSA

While most Latin artists stick to the reggaetón sound as it increasingly gains popularity, Rauw Alejandro fearlessly displays his adaptability, craftwork, and versatility in his multifaceted sophomore full-length album VICE VERSA.Through fourteen tracks with genres varying from an inspired 80’s funk to Rauw’s signature Latin urban sound, VICE VERSA offers a memorable rollercoaster ride reinforced by multiple lead singles, such as sonically melodious “2/Catorce” and enticing “Sexo Virtual,” as well as notable summer hit “Todo De Ti.” Touching subjects of love, heartbreak, and loss and with Alejandro’s name rising amongst talented musicians, VICE VERSA is a look into how the future of Latin music should be like; filled with hard-hitting pop beats, undeniable confidence, and addicting well-produced tracks that will undoubtedly leave their thread woven in history. —@loveholic​

22
Aespa – Savage – The 1st Mini Album

K-pop is bigger than ever, and few Korean artists had as impressive a year as the four-piece girl group aespa. After the triumphant “Black Mamba” made them the fastest group to hit 100 million YouTube views, and the follow-up single, “Next Level,” became one of the biggest Korean songs of the year, the girls lived up to the hype with their first mini-album, Savage. The project further explores their AI concept—which includes avatar characters they have to rescue from the titular villain of “Black Mamba”—without alienating new listeners who aren’t familiar with their lore. Standout tracks include the infectiously confident “YEPPI YEPPI” and the Hayley Kiyoko-cowritten “자각몽 (Lucid Dream).”

Savage is sure to become the project that gets aespa on music lovers’ radars worldwide. They already performed lead single “Savage” at the Macy’s Thanksgiving Day Parade, making them the first K-pop girl group to appear at the event. The mini-album debuted at #20 on the Billboard 200, and it seems likely their next release will soar even higher. —Madison Murray​

21
Jorja Smith – Be Right Back

One of the smoothest-sounding records of the year, Jorja Smith’s Be Right Back represents one of RB’s most exciting stars excellently carrying the neo-soul torch a step further with eight immaculately woven, emotive tracks. Atop a nostalgic guitar line and especially dreamy arps, album opener “Addicted” sees an empath tussling with a narcissist for an adequate amount of attention, longing stripped open by honesty. Meditating on womanhood, place, and class, songs like “Home” and “Burn” represent a more lyrically mature Jorja, coupled with timeless vocals. And who could forget the effortlessly sexy “Bussdown,” a reggae-influenced early evening vibe featuring Shaybo that is perhaps the most slept-on song of the summer? In a messy, disorganized present, the mellowness and dynamism of Be Right Back is a salve—and a gift. —Max Zhang​

20
Caparezza – Exuvia

Italian rapper Caparezza has been widely respected for the increasingly complex concepts he has offered throughout the years. With his eighth studio album, Exuvia, he digs even deeper through a metaphorical journey in the woods.

Presented by the artist as the “realest album” of his career, Exuvia partially replaces the ironic and thought-provoking approach from previous works with a more introspective lens, which guides Caparezza through a dense series of topics, including the story of his family (“El Sendero”), the need for a fulfilling purpose (“La Scelta”), nature’s power (“Contronatura”), and imaginary conversations with death (“La Certa”)—all the way up to personal rebirth, symbolized by the title track.

Skits, easter eggs and a whole virtual experience push the main theme of self-discovery even further. In the process, the artist just might have gifted the world with his most ambitious project yet. —Pier Francesco Costa​

19
PinkPantheress – to hell with it

This year marked the emergence of elusive 20-year-old songstress pinkpantheress, whose intoxicating blend of frenzied drum’n’bass breakbeats, twinkling synth lines, and wistfully alluring vocals first appeared in small snippets on TikTok. Her viral singles, like the snugly lo-fi “Pain” and the sultry, acoustic-driven “Passion,” are endlessly replayable earworms. Short but sweet, each song features confessional lyricism and intricate production that rewards repeat listening. Unafraid to wear her influences on her sleeve, pinkpantheress evokes a wide range of genres, from U.K. jungle and garage to K-pop and dancehall. The artist has gone from recording in dorm halls at her university in London to collaborating in the studio with producers like Mura Masa; to hell with it tracks her artistic evolution in real time. —Ethan Rubenstein​

18
Little Simz – Sometimes I Might Be Introvert

U.K. rapper Little Simz is familiar with stepping outside of the box, and her fourth studio album proves just that. Sometimes I Might Be Introvert—an backronym of “SIMBI,” Simz’s nickname—explores the themes of loss, success, self-doubt, and, most evidently, her dualistic introversion. The 19-track album offers listeners a guided tour of Simz’s mind and perspectives. Each song is like a piece of art with its own unique backstory.

Songs such as “I Love You, I Hate You” and “Little Q, Pt. 2” dive deep into the connections Simz holds with her family, for better and for worse. Other tracks like “Rollin Stone” and “Standing Ovation” let Simz celebrate her accomplishments and innovation to the rap game. The album also includes several cinematic interludes, many of which feature orchestral and operatic accompaniments that further solidify Simz’s ability to blend genres and deliver a unique experience with each song she releases. —@lonland

17
Silk Sonic – An Evening With Silk Sonic

Who could’ve known at the beginning of a year that left no room for surprises, a collaborative RB album between pop star Bruno Mars and rapper Anderson .Paak would be one of the most groundbreaking releases in the following ten months. Ever since premiering the single “Leave The Door Open,” Silk Sonic has been entrancing listeners with occasional tracks until the duo ended the year with their debut project in November.

An Evening with Silk Sonic is nothing short of smooth, refreshing, nostalgic and melodic. The pair—who now, feel more like band mates—have been working on the project for over three years. The craft behind the cover and present subject matter capitalize on the vintage 1970s aesthetic, perfected to make the final product well worth the wait for fans. In short, Mars and .Paak delivered a jaw-dropping work of art that in many ways is the least surprising surprise of 2021. —Joseph Haske

16
Tyler, The Creator – Call Me If You Get Lost

There may never be enough writing to cover the evolution of Tyler, the Creator. Since his 2009 debut, BASTARD, the once-provocative-for-the-fuck-of-it teen who was as skilled at rapping as he was at spewing callous homophobia has become a sensitive, jazz-obsessed connoisseur who raps about dating men and cringes after saying the b-word. What can we say after 12 years? People grow.

CALL ME IF YOU GET LOST might read as Tyler’s attempt at marrying the best parts of his past and present selves. Tyler’s 30 now, and despite having won a Grammy for the melody-heavy Igor, he hasn’t lost his love for cold, no-bullshit bar-spitting. But there’s new perspective in these bars. It’s telling that one of the hardest lines on CMIYGL is also one of its most vulnerable. Talking about unrequited love on “CORSO,” Tyler sneers, “In the end, she picked him, I hope when they fuckin’ / She still thinking of me ’cos I’m that perfect.” Against a backdrop of gorgeously sampled beats and DJ Drama yelling his ad-libs aboard a Swiss yacht, Tyler’s built a whole new world to flex his old skills in. It’s a joy to get lost there. —Chris Ritter​

15
88rising, NIKI DJ Snake – Shang-Chi and the Legend of the Ten Rings: The Album

This is one of the best soundtracks of 2021. 88rising, working in a close collaboration with Marvel, picked the next generation of Asian and Asian American for the album accompanying the blockbuster Shang-Chi and The Legend of Ten Rings. The album features Jhené Aiko, Anderson .Paak, 21 Savage, BIBI, DPR IAN, keshi, Gen Hoshino, Zion.T, Mark Tuan, Saweetie, Audrey Nuna, Warren Hue, JJ Lin, and more. There’s variety in the tracks, yet everything fits together well in the context of the movie and the soundtrack.

One of the highlights of the album is “Diamonds + And Pearls” by DPR LIVE, DPR IAN, and peace. That song is definitely a lot—a dose of energy, a flex on the luxury, and a tribute to Asian culture. Other highlights include the empowering “Swan Song” by Saweetie and NIKI and the final track off the album, “Warriors,” by Warren Hue and Seori. Strong, ethereal, and nostalgic at the same time, it’s the perfect ending for the musical journey the album presents. —@siannve​

14
Clairo – Sling

There are albums that are brash and vibrant while there are albums that create musically-tangible comfort: Sling is the latter. Released during another excruciating year for many, Clairo’s soft-winded vocals combined with her lustrous songwriting provided a 44-minute blanket of warmth to those suffering or possibly just reflecting on the past. After a two-year stint from music releasing, the bedroom pop sensation made her arrival back with an entirely different sound than the usual Clairo-esque musicianship. With production mainly credited to Jack Antonoff, the euphoria of Sling speaks of simplistic activities including, “gathering to one corner of the woods” or “tying ribbons in hair.” But despite the seemingly lighthearted musical imagery, Clairo also touches on more heavy subjects regarding sexual harassment and mental illness within “Blouse” and “Just For Today.” Contrasting tracks like “Zinnias” to Clairo’s earlier hits such as “Pretty Girl,” her listeners can sense a maturity yet further versatility in the songwriting, style, and essence of Sling. At times, music needs to be loud and eye-opening but in this case, all that was necessary is minimalism and coherence, which Sling perfectly represented through the artistic author of Claire Cottrill. —Charity Spicer​

13
Young Thug – Punk

From Olivia Rodrigo to WILLOW, many of today’s top artists have made belligerent punk rock a driving force of 2021. But despite its title and accompanying Travis Barker-assisted Tiny Desk concert, Young Thug’s sophomore album, Punk, is not entirely hard-edged. The emotionally compelling project is filled with drumless production, acoustic guitars, and elastic vocal performances. At its core, Punk is even more sentimental and evocative than its 2017 predecessor, the “singing album” Beautiful Thugger Girls. From his iconic kimono-like dress on the cover of JEFFERY to the artwork of So Much Fun, which was inspired by the opening ceremony of the Beijing Olympics, the Atlanta rapper-singer’s artistic choices never fail to impress. This time around, the visually metamorphic cover art of Punk pays homage to Mexican surrealist painter Octavio Ocampo. Thugger’s unpredictable, unmatched, genre-bending aesthetic truly makes him a punk culture megastar. —@peaker​

12
Doja Cat – Planet Her

Following the highly acclaimed 2019’s Hot Pink, Doja Cat is unstoppable and returned with her third studio album, Planet Her. It resulted in another success as she was nominated for 8 Grammy Awards, including ‘Album of the Year’ and ‘Song of the Year’.

The 14-track space-themed album showcases all of Doja’s versatility and creativity, in which she is joined by a line-up of stars including The Weeknd, Young Thug, SZA and Ariana Grande. From the afrobeat opening track “Woman,” to the bad girl energy of “Ain’t Shit,” through the disco sound of “Kiss Me More” and the darkly seductive “Been Like This,” Doja Cat blends genres with each song capturing us by its “own personality.” Moreover, the album explores the theme of love, relationships, sex and the divine feminine—although Amala is discreet about her personal life, she reveals a more vulnerable side in a song like “Love to Dream,” where she confesses to listeners, “I got everything / Everything but real love.”

With stunning productions, vocals and visuals, Planet Her must be experienced in its entirety to enter Doja Cat’s world, a planet we would love to embark on. —Layla

11
JPEGMAFIA – LP!

LP!’s release was like Christmas and a birthday all wrapped up in one, as a streaming and deluxe “Offline” version dropped on the same day. It was just as exciting for producer, writer, mastering engineer, and rapper JPEGMAFIA, whose album release meant independence and finishing a record deal.

After All My Heroes Are Cornballs (2019) and a couple EP projects, fans might’ve assumed Peggy would slow down. But the production on LP! goes as hard or harder than on his previous releases. Hearing Peggy seamlessly rap over heavy metal on “END CREDITS!” reminds listeners that he is never afraid to pave his own road. Across the album, Peggy aims his iconic anger at targets like music industry hacks and copycat rappers, and he even calls out beef with other alt-hip-hop MCs. JPEGMAFIA keeps shocking fans with a ceiling that never stops elevating—all while using his growing platform to call out losers wherever they hide. —@wizard

10
Billie Eilish – Happier Than Ever

Billie Eilish’s first album, When We All Fall Asleep, Where Do We Go? was a success both commercially and culturally, leaving fans anticipating more. But note its title: it asks a fantastical question, one not really meant to be answered, but worth considering nonetheless. In contrast, on Happier Than Ever, she is making a statement: she will talk about what is true to her, and how she has grown during her years of youth and fame.

The result is the most honest album of her career. From the “Not My Responsibility” – “OverHeated” transition, where Billie firmly renounces those who objectify her to the enchanting choral intro of “GOLDWING” to the ever-relatable “I’d never treat me this shitty/You made me hate this city” of the album’s title track, Billie shows that while she may not be exactly where she wants to be, she knows she’s on the right track – to be there in a couple years —Babu Chatterjee

9
Arlo Parks – Collapsed In Sunbeams

Before releasing Collapsed in Sunbeams, Arlo Parks said she wanted her record to feel both “universal and hyperspecific.” With this dazzling debut, Parks proves those two feelings aren’t so far apart. Parks begins the album in spoken word, shifting from lyrical to conversational at a moment’s notice with turns like, “Making peace with our own distortions/You shouldn’t be afraid to cry in front of me.” Making that shift so seamlessly is Park’s gift. Even when she’s singing, she sounds like a friend across a couch in a quiet room, her lyrics the stuff of late-night FaceTimes and post-party wind-downs.

The hyperspecific and the universal overlap in more places than we think. It takes great artists to find where. Parks has cited Phoebe Bridgers as being particularly good at it, but some of her other influences—including Sufjan Stevens, Solange, and Adrianne Lenker—also make up the short list of songwriters who can tie small moments to the weighty emotions that often lie behind them. Add Parks to their ranks. —Chris Ritter

8
J. Cole – The Off-Season

J. Cole’s sixth studio album, The Off-Season, is a more than impressive addition to the North Carolina rapper’s extensive catalog. The LP is nothing short of braggadocious and lyrically imposing. Unexpected features from the likes of Atlanta’s 21 Savage, Lil Baby, and 6LACK were a welcome surprise the night of release. “m y . l i f e,” “p r i d e . i s . t h e . d e v i l,” and “h u n g e r . o n . h i l l s i d e” stand out as melodic performances carried by Cole’s collaboration with the featured artists.

The-Off Season marks a spiritual journey between J. Cole’s homes in North Carolina and New York, locations the rapper has frequented throughout his life. On a larger scale, however, the album represents passion originating from Jermaine’s hiatus from music. He returned with a body of work that was full of inspiration for hip-hop, and that established Cole as one of the most consistent artists of 2021. —Joseph Haske

7
Japanese Breakfast – Jubilee

Japanese Breakfast is having one hell of a year, and their third album, Jubilee, is nothing but stellar. It’s a much happier record than earlier offerings, which centered on the complex experience of losing a loved one. But this run towards joy is no less intimate than the slow dances with grief of yore; rather, it’s earnest as ever. Lead single “Be Sweet” is buoyant and flirtatious, a synth-pop track depicting a lover teasing someone to do better. “Kokomo, IN” evokes the growing pains of teenage sweethearts, and the headbanger “Savage Good Boy” is an ironic picture of love at the apocalypse. Supported by one of the year’s best guitar solos, album closer “Posing for Cars” closes with the image of a heart growing whole, burning with desire. Jubilee is exactly what it’s named after: a season of celebration, loving and bright. —​Max Zhang

6
James Blake – Friends That Break Your Heart

On his fifth album, James Blake shows a mastery of the angelic harmonies and atmospheric production he’s famous for. But the U.K. producer has toned down the experimentation and lyrical metaphors found on previous albums to make this one more accessible as he contemplates an oft-overlooked, yet important, topic: breakups with friends. Highlights include the contrast between SZA’s deeper vocals and Blake’s falsetto on “Coming Back” and the ’70s-inspired “Foot Forward”, featuring help from Atlanta-based producer Metro Boomin. “Say What You Will”, about being replaced by younger and more successful artists, is Blake’s best vocal performance on the album. The record ends on a lyrical and sonic high note, as Blake looks to the love he’s found with his partner, Jameela Jamil, to overcome the pain of lost relationships. —Kenisha Santiago​

5
Stray Kids – NOEASY

One of Korea’s hottest rising acts, Stray Kids built their career experimenting with music. NOEASY claps back at haters of their signature “noisy” style and—with its hard-hitting hip-hop tracks and colorful ballads—proves they have no musical boundaries. The lead single, “소리꾼 (Thunderous),” sets the album’s tone, pairing witty lyrics marking the group’s presence in the music industry with a traditional-Korean-inspired production. The 14-track project compiles tracks about their career, mental health, and their fans, with the production variety also flaunting their rap and vocal skills. Members Bang Chan, Changbin, and HAN, who collectively co-wrote all of the album, showcase stellar songwriting skills on tracks like the lyrically clever “CHEESE,” the angsty “좋아해서 미안 (Sorry, I Love You),” and the emotionally driven “말할 수 없는 비밀 (Secret Secret).” After their 2020 successes, NOEASY is the sophomore album listeners have been waiting for. It suggests Stray Kids are here to stay. —@PlanNine

4
Adele – 30

Adele’s 30, a letter to herself and her son, marked the singer-songwriter’s return to music after six years. An album about more than just her divorce, 30 finds Adele examining her complex feelings of being alone and independent. The album flows like a chronological diary, showcasing significant milestones in the singer’s path to learning how to balance her multiple identities of being a mother, a bold woman, and a global superstar.

Filled with self-reflective lyrics, 30 is about learning how to not blame yourself for the realities of life. On tracks like the six-minute “I Drink Wine,” Adele learns how to overcome her self-doubt and love unconditionally. The level of vulnerability displayed on the album is truly next level. Case in point: “To Be Loved,” which Adele has vowed to never perform live. On 30, Adele wants the world and her son to know that she tried and gave her best. She sounds the best she’s ever sounded, and the lyrical complexity makes 30 one of the best albums of 2021. —Saquib Syed​

3
Playboi Carti – Whole Lotta Red

Whole Lotta Red, the 24-track sophomore album from Playboi Carti, was panned by fans and critics alike upon release, but it didn’t take long for listeners to really sit down and examine the gem that was delivered. It’s an album for fans both new and old, and it blends elements from Carti’s entire discography. He remains a trap star on songs like “Rockstar Made” and “Beno!”, but he gets reflective on deeper cuts such as “Punk Monk” and “Over,” showcasing a part of himself that fans don’t often see. The real showstopper, “Sky,” exceeds expectations with its infectious beat and outlandish lyrics that we’ve all come to love. It’s the result of a two-year build-up that perfectly summarizes Carti’s appeal. On “M3tamorphosis,” he raps, “I’m in the twilight zone,” and it’s with lines like these that Carti proves he’s ahead of the industry and simply taking everyone else along for the ride. —@vintagewashingmachine​

2
Halsey – If I Can’t Have Love, I Want Power

Fans spent years begging for a “Punksey” alternative album, and Halsey gave them everything they wanted with the triumphant If I Can’t Have Love, I Want Power. With production by Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross of Nine Inch Nails, the project explores womanhood, fame, love, and family life over thrashing guitars, bubbling synths, and beats you won’t be able to get out of your head. Perhaps some of the most poignant moments are songs like the Lindsey Buckingham-assisted “Darling” and “Ya’aburnee,” which touch on Halsey’s experience with motherhood after detailing difficulties with endometriosis on 2020’s “More.” If I Can’t Have Love, I Want Power finds Halsey achieving some of their biggest dreams, both professionally and personally, while balancing home and celebrity life without missing a beat. Halsey proves you can have both love and power in a striking statement about being an ambitious and family-oriented artist. —Madison Murray​

1
Olivia Rodrigo – Sour

Even though the world got to know Olivia Rodrigo through breakup anthems like “drivers license” and “good 4 u,” her debut full-length, SOUR, proves her versatility and range over the course of 11 addictive tracks. Opener “brutal” addresses the difficulties of teenage life, while “jealousy, jealousy” is an introspective look at how social media and internet pressures affect young women. Closing track “hope ur ok” reflects on lost childhood friendship while extending support to the LGBT community.

Two songs on the record, “deja vu” and “1 step forward, 3 steps back,” have received cosigns from Rodrigo’s idol, Taylor Swift, who has been one of her biggest celebrity supporters and musical inspirations. And now, looking at the enthusiastic young crowds at Rodrigo’s shows, it’s clear that Olivia will be the kind of unapologetically vulnerable idol to the next generation that Taylor has been to her. —Madison Murray
