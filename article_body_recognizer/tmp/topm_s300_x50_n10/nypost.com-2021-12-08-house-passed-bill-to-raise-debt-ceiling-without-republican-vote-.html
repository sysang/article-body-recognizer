Republicans just needlessly handed Democrats a blank check on the debt limit

By
Dan Kowalski

December 8, 2021 7:17pm

Since President Joe Biden took office in January, the national debt has increased by $1.2 trillion. Legislation enacted since the Democrats took complete control of Washington will add at least another $2 trillion to deficits — and this does not include the gimmick-ridden Build Back Better plan being negotiated in the Senate.

Despite winning all levers of federal power in the last election and using them to add trillions to the debt, Democrats have refused to take responsibility for their actions and increase the debt limit without Republican help.

Under the scheme the House passed Tuesday, Senate Republicans may choose to provide Democrats a way to shirk their responsibility — and a blank check to do more damage.

Republican leader Mitch McConnell earlier vowed he would not allow a replay of October, when Republicans provided 11 votes to temporarily increase the debt limit. Democrats were expected to use the budget process and reconciliation to raise the debt ceiling without GOP votes.

After Majority Leader Chuck Schumer wasted the last two months on failed efforts to pass Democratic wish-list items like national early and mail-in voting, McConnell has again agreed to save the Democratic leader from himself.

The scheme works like this: The Senate will be asked to bring up for consideration the House-passed bill. The bill would stop across-the-board cuts required under budget law because of Democrats’ failure to pay for the American Rescue Plan, stop scheduled reductions in Medicare payments to doctors that had been delayed during the pandemic and establish a one-time martial-law (or same-day) procedure to increase the debt limit. There will be some perfunctory debate on the question of whether the Senate should even consider the bill, and then Schumer will ask to end debate.

Ending Senate debate on consideration takes 60 votes. At least 10 Republicans are expected to vote to end debate — likely the same bloc that voted to increase the debt limit outright in October.

Republicans who vote in favor likely will argue that doctors should not have to face reductions in Medicare payments. Yet, under the martial-law procedure, the debt-limit increase will be a separate piece of legislation passable with 50 Democratic votes.

This measure would work like the Monopoly Get Out of Jail Free card. Once the legislation is introduced in the Senate, it would immediately be available for consideration on the floor. No referral to committee, no hearings, no amendments — none of the things we learned in high-school civics. Debate would be limited to 10 hours, and then a simple majority vote would be held.

Republicans would not need to vote for the debt-limit increase, but Democrats would have to admit how much they need to add to the national debt to allow them to float trillion-dollar deficits through the next election and enact the profligate spending in the Build Back Broke bill.

We don’t yet know how much Democrats will choose to increase the debt limit. The House-passed legislation included a blank space to be “appropriately filled in with the dollar amount of the increase.”

By allowing this bill to pass, Republicans will hand Democrats a blank check for the debt-limit increase. The hike is only limited by the amount that the Democrats can coalesce around.

The boost could be enough to last through the Biden administration’s entire term. If Democrats choose to go big, expect Republicans to be shocked.

The plan is elegant in its cynicism.

Both sides think voters are stupid. Republicans can claim purity and say they didn’t vote for a debt-limit increase. Democrats can say the added debt is bipartisan since Republicans helped them establish a process to increase the limit. It is a parsing of words as bad as Speaker Nancy Pelosi’s “You have to pass the bill to know what’s in it” and President Bill Clinton’s “It depends on what the meaning of the word ‘is’ is.”

Enabling Democrats to shirk their responsibilities as elected leaders is not leadership. Now that’s it’s known Republicans will blink whenever the government’s ability to pay bills when due is in question, the debt limit is no longer a tool to force fiscal responsibility on the Democrats.

It may be time to eliminate the debt limit in its current form. It is a charade to pretend there is a limit when in a practical sense there is none. Rather than creating faux drama, Congress should figure out how to control its spending impulses before the financial markets question our ability — rather than our willingness — to pay our bills.

Dan Kowalski is a former counselor to the secretary of the Treasury and a former deputy staff director for the US Senate Budget Committee.
