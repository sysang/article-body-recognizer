    • Obi Toppin’s impressive dunk couldn’t even inspire woeful Knicks

The breakaway slam should have sparked a raging fire inside the Knicks, because it came straight from the heart of the city game. Obi Toppin grew up on the famous courts of Rucker Park and Dyckman and West 4th Street, watching the high-flying feats of playground legends the likes of his own father, Obadiah, known by the name of Dunker’s Delight.

They called young Obi “Next Generation” back then, because every baller needed a nickname in the parks. All these years later, Toppin lived up to that hype in the second quarter Wednesday night, when he got ahead of the field and made like a wide receiver running a go route down the left sideline.

The 6-foot-9 forward looked back over his shoulder at Derrick Rose, extended his left arm and pointed his finger toward the opposite goal, and carefully tracked the point guard’s perfect two-handed pass. Toppin caught the ball above the 3-point line, bounced it once, launched himself in the air and then staged his own personal dunk contest.

He passed the ball underneath his right leg in midair, from his left hand to his right, and then thundered it home to cut Indiana’s lead to 42-37. The Pacers called timeout, and Toppin’s teammates rushed onto the court like giddy high school kids to congratulate him. Obi had just pulled a page from his old man’s playbook. He had honored The Helicopter and The Destroyer and The Goat, all the great names of New York playground mythology.

Toppin had also inspired an entire team … just not his own.

Strangely enough, the Pacers scored the next eight points to take a 50-37 lead on the way to scoring 70 points in the first half. Seventy! They would take a 27-point lead before settling for a 122-102 victory that, once again, exposed this year’s Knicks as more fragile than last year’s Knicks.

“They just got whatever they wanted,” Julius Randle would say. And because they did, the Knicks fell back under .500 and back under the lowly conference teams currently seeded for the play-in tournament. The Knicks have lost four of their last five, and haven’t won two games in a row since Halloween.

Of course, they had their built-in excuses Wednesday night, if only because any NBA road team on the wrong end of a back-to-back against a rested home team has a hundred reasons to lose. Traveling overnight from San Antonio to Indianapolis isn’t exactly traveling overnight from Brooklyn to Boston, or from Philly to D.C. The Knicks didn’t pull in until 3 a.m., Evan Fournier said, and on top of that Fournier was struggling with a possible case of food poisoning after downing a bad piece of steak in a San Antonio restaurant.

So maybe that explains why the 6-7 veteran was eaten alive by rookie Chris Duarte in the shooting-guard matchup (23-7). The rest of the Knicks? They didn’t even bother to compete against a 10-16 team that hardly resembles the Reggie Miller Pacers.

They didn’t even bother to compete after Toppin executed a dunk a lot of NBA players wouldn’t have attempted on a 9-foot hoop.

Tom Thibodeau was asked if he was OK with Toppin doing what he did, since it seemed to fire up the wrong team. “Obi’s a great athlete,” he said. “He’s going to make some incredible plays, that’s who he is. … We have a number of players that can make plays that are special. As long as it’s in the context of the team framework, I’m good.”

Nobody should have any problem with the mustard Toppin put on this hot dog. He knew his sluggish, travel-weary team needed a jolt, and he did his damnedest to deliver one. And by the way, the Knicks’ official Twitter account sent out five consecutive tweets about the dunk. It would’ve looked and sounded silly if Thibodeau said he had an issue with it.

But the fact the Knicks had no positive response, other than their immediate celebration of Toppin, strongly suggests that nothing was going to rouse them from their slumber. This was a giveaway game for a team that couldn’t afford one.

On cue, Thibodeau talked afterward about his team needing to be more resilient. Randle said the Knicks needed to be “mentally tougher.” Fournier said the Pacers were “more physical, more into the game.” These confessions are becoming alarmingly routine.

In assessing his own struggling team, Pacers coach Rick Carlisle all but pulled a scene from “Hoosiers” when he said, “We’re in Indiana. This is the sanctity of the game, the sacred purity of the game. This is the land of hard work and pride, and one of the things we said heading into the year is our team needs to reflect that.”

Truth is, the Knicks need to start reflecting their own home. Obi Toppin did exactly that, and his team crumbled around him. Another bad sign in what has been a bad start.
