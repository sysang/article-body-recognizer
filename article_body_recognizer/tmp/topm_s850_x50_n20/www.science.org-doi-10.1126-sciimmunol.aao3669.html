Stromal cells in training immunity

Immune responses to pathogens are heightened in individuals previously exposed to pathogen-associated stimuli, a phenomenon known as innate immune training. Innate immune training is accomplished through metabolic reprogramming and epigenetic changes, but the underlying mechanisms are not fully understood. In this study, Wu et al. investigate the functional requirement of IL-17 receptor signaling in fibroblastic reticular cells (FRCs) in the development of protective antibody responses against gut infection and studied how innate immune training affects these responses. Mild gut inflammatory training before C. rodentium infection enhanced gut-protective antibody production and reduced colon inflammation. Inflammatory training required IL-17 signaling in FRCs and IL-10 production by B cells. These findings reveal a mechanism of innate immune training in FRCs for protection against gut infection.

Abstract

Prior experience of pathogen-associated stimuli reduces morbidity and mortality to newly encountered infections through innate immune training, which can be enhanced by childhood vaccination. Fibroblastic reticular cells (FRCs) are stromal cells in lymphoid organs that support lymphocyte localization and survival and modulate adaptive immune responses. IL-17 signaling is important for FRC metabolism and proliferation during inflammatory responses. Here, we show that FRC-intrinsic IL-17 signaling was required for protective antibody-mediated immunity to the gut bacterial pathogen Citrobacter rodentium. We asked whether prior activation of FRC through nonspecific inflammatory “training” of the gut would alter subsequent immune response to C. rodentium. Inflammatory training increased the number of activated FRC in mesenteric LN (MLN) and enhanced the antibody response to C. rodentium in an IL-17–dependent manner. FRC demonstrated cardinal features of innate immune training, including increased epigenetic markers of activation and increased metabolic response to infection. Enhanced responses were still evident 6 weeks after training. The kinetics of bacterial infection were not changed by inflammatory training, but colon inflammation was paradoxically reduced. Mechanistically, IL-10 production by activated B cells was required for colon protective effects of inflammatory training. Enhancing tissue protective B cell responses thus led to increased production of antibody and IL-10, allowing clearance of infection with reduced tissue inflammation. These data identify a new mode of immune training through FRC to modulate future adaptive responses and better preserve host health.

INTRODUCTION

Vaccination programs have greatly reduced childhood mortality, even beyond the prevalence of the specific diseases targeted. One of the mechanisms underlying this broad protective effect is innate immunological training, whereby exposure to pathogen-associated molecules activates a form of nonspecific innate memory that more effectively resists future “new” infections (1). This heightened responsiveness is accomplished through metabolic reprogramming and epigenetic modifications that persists for months after the initial challenge. Trained immunity likely contributes to the increasing resistance to infectious disease, both previously encountered and new, which occurs over time in children as they are exposed to a range of microbes, and hence, vaccination hastens this process.
Diarrheagenic pathogens are the second leading cause of infection-related deaths in children under the age of 5 years according to the World Health Organization (2, 3). Escherichia coli is a major contributor to diarrheal-related deaths (4, 5), with more than 300 million illnesses and 200,000 deaths globally each year (6). Citrobacter rodentium is widely studied as the murine equivalent to enteropathogenic E. coli and enterohemorrhagic E. coli, two pathotypes of diarrheagenic E. coli, because they share a similar colonization strategy known as attaching and effacing lesion formation (7, 8). Interleukin-17 (IL-17), interferon-γ, and IL-22 produced by T helper 17 (TH17) and innate lymphoid cells induce the production of antimicrobial peptides by intestinal epithelium and activate myeloid cells (9–11). Although effective in controlling the infection, the by-product of this proinflammatory response is transient colitis that lasts 2 to 4 weeks after infection. In contrast, antibody specific to C. rodentium protects against reinfection without causing inflammation (12, 13). This is the goal of vaccination programs, to bypass the early inflammation driven by innate and T cell immunity and invoke an antibody response that can provide protective immunity without affecting host health.
Secondary lymphoid organs provide critical support for both T and B cell activation and optimal antibody production through germinal center (GC) formation (14, 15). Fibroblastic reticular cells (FRCs) are lymph node (LN) stromal cells that produce cytokines and chemokines to support survival and function of lymphocytes and organization of LN architecture (16, 17). FRCs have been found to both promote and regulate adaptive immune responses and thus are considered active players in immune responses (18). We recently showed that IL-17 signaling is critical for metabolic reprogramming of activated FRC during experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE) and linked IL-17–dependent activation of FRC to enhanced autoantibody production (19).
In this study, we queried the functional requirement for IL-17 receptor signaling in FRCs during development of protective antibody responses to gut infection. Because metabolic reprogramming is a hallmark of immunological training in myeloid cells (1, 20), we further hypothesized that prior FRC activation through nonspecific gut inflammation could alter a subsequent immune response to gut infection with C. rodentium by enhancing C. rodentium–specific antibody. Here, we demonstrate that IL-17–dependent FRC activation promotes protective antibody responses both in adaptive memory and in nonspecific inflammatory training models. Furthermore, inflammatory training not only enhances antibody production but also reduces colon inflammation independent of C. rodentium infection burden. Last, we show that inflammatory training relies on FRC-intrinsic IL-17 signaling and IL-10 production by B cells, revealing a previously unappreciated mechanism of FRC training that modulates adaptive immunity with increased host health during infection.

RESULTS

Loss of FRC-intrinsic IL-17 signaling delays C. rodentium clearance

To determine whether IL-17 signaling in LN stromal cells plays a role in protection during infection of the gut, mice lacking the IL-17 receptor subunit IL-17RA specifically in FRCs were generated by crossing Ccl19cre mice with Il17rafl/fl mice, hereafter termed FRC∆Il17ra mice. There is no difference in basal numbers of FRC in this mouse strain (19). Control mice were Ccl19cre−Il17rafl/fl wild-type (WT) littermates (FRCCtrl). Upon infection with C. rodentium, FRC numbers were reduced in the mesenteric LNs (MLNs) draining the gut of infected FRC∆Il17ra compared with FRCCtrl mice (Fig. 1A and fig. S1A). This was expected from our prior studies on FRC numbers in FRC∆Il17ra mice in EAE and dextran sulfate sodium (DSS) colitis (19). Fecal burden of C. rodentium was similar between FRC∆Il17ra and FRCCtrl mice during the first week of infection, indicating that there was no difference in the ability of the innate response to control infection in the colon during this initial phase. However, during the second week of infection, corresponding to the adaptive immune response, FRC∆Il17ra mice showed an impaired ability to clear bacteria (Fig. 1B).
Although C. rodentium infection and associated transient colitis do not typically induce severe weight loss in WT mice, infected FRC∆Il17ra mice had a slight but significant reduction in body mass compared to controls (Fig. 1C). FRC∆Il17ra mice also had significantly shorter colons, indicating increased colitis (Fig. 1D). Gene expression analysis of the FRC∆Il17ra colon further supported an enhanced local inflammatory response compared with controls, with increased expression of Il17a and Ifng (Fig. 1E). Unexpectedly, the TH17-associated proinflammatory gene Csf2, encoding granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), was reduced in FRC∆Il17ra mouse colon, suggesting a possible defect in myeloid cell activation. However, a major product of protective myeloid cell function in C. rodentium, Il1b, was increased in the absence of FRC signaling (Fig. 1E) (21, 22). IL-22 plays important roles in C. rodentium clearance through induction of antimicrobial peptides including REGIIIγ and increased IL-18 expression in infected epithelial cells (23–26). IL-22 expression was also notably reduced, but Il18 was unchanged, and RegIIIg was increased (Fig. 1E), suggesting possible compensation for example through enhanced IL-17 signaling.
Overall, these data suggest that delayed clearance is associated with altered tissue cytokine responses and increased inflammation, but key effector molecules (such as antimicrobial peptides) continue to be induced and are perhaps increased. We therefore turned to investigate another key mediator of protective immunity in C. rodentium infection, namely, B cell activation and antibody production. There was a significant decrease in the frequency of GC B cells when FRCs could not respond to IL-17 (Fig. 1F and fig. S1B). Although immunoglobulin A (IgA) antibody responses are most strongly associated with mucosal immunity, C. rodentium–specific IgG is essential for its clearance (27, 28). Correspondingly, total IgG and C. rodentium–specific IgG were significantly reduced in serum of FRC∆Il17ra mice on day 14 after infection (Fig. 1, G and H). Fecal C. rodentium–specific IgA was similarly reduced at multiple time points after infection (Fig. 1I). Hence, delayed clearance of C. rodentium in FRC∆Il17ra mice corresponds to impaired antibody responses accompanied by perturbation of the colon cytokine response and increased inflammation.

Loss of protective immunity against C rodentium without FRC-intrinsic IL-17 signaling

Because antibodies are the basis of protective immunity to C. rodentium, we tested the susceptibility of FRC∆Il17ra mice to reinfection. Sixty days after primary infection, C. rodentium DNA could not be detected in stool for either FRCCtrl or FRC∆Il17ra mice (Fig. 2A). This indicated that the increased cytokine-driven antimicrobial response in FRC∆Il17ra mice had sufficiently compensated for reduced antibody to clear the infection. Upon rechallenge with C. rodentium, the bacteria were not detectable in most of the control mice and only in low amounts in those that developed infection, demonstrating the potency of immune memory (Fig. 2, A and B). However, all of the FRC∆Il17ra mice developed detectable C. rodentium infection by 10 days after reinfection (Fig. 2, A and B). Reinfected FRC∆Il17ra mice experienced significant weight loss and colitis (colon shortening) compared with FRCCtrl mice (Fig. 2, C and D). Because memory T cell responses are expected to peak earlier upon reinfection, we analyzed the colon on day 10 for expression of inflammatory genes. These were mostly unchanged with a trend toward increased IL-17 and IL-1β in FRC∆Il17ra mice compared with FRCCtrl mice (Fig. 2E). Csf2, encoding GM-CSF, was the only marker to show decreased expression in FRC∆Il17ra mice on day 10 after reinfection (Fig. 2E). However, both the percentage and absolute number of GC B cells in gut-draining LNs were reduced in FRC∆Il17ra mice (Fig. 2, F and G). Correspondingly, the production of C. rodentium–specific IgG was decreased in both FRC∆Il17ra mice serum and feces (Fig. 2, H and I).

Mild gut inflammation enhances FRC and antibody response to subsequent infection

DSS induces transient gut injury and inflammation and is a commonly used mouse model of colitis (29). DSS colitis increases FRC numbers in MLNs (19), lasting several weeks after DSS removal (fig. S2A). We therefore administered a low dose (1%) of DSS to induce mild gut injury as a nonspecific inflammatory immune training for a period of 7 days, followed by 1 week of rest to allow resolution of colon inflammation. Mice were then infected with C. rodentium, and the outcome was compared 14 days after infection. Mild DSS training did not cause significant weight loss in most mice (fig. S2B). However, after C. rodentium infection, MLN from trained mice had increased FRC numbers compared with MLNs from untrained mice (Fig. 3A). Similarly, total numbers of MLN cells were increased in infected mice with prior inflammatory training (Fig. 3B). The proportion of MLN CD4+ T cells were not changed under any condition (fig. S2C). In contrast, the percentage and number of total B cells and GC B cells were significantly increased in mice that were infected by C. rodentium after inflammatory training (Fig. 3, C and D, and fig. S2, D and E). Corresponding to increased GC B cells, T follicular helper (TFH) cells were increased in MLN from trained mice after infection (Fig. 3, E and F, and fig. S2F). DSS treatment alone did not induce any appreciable increase in total IgG or C. rodentium–specific IgG (Fig. 3, G and H). However, prior experience of gut inflammatory training significantly enhanced the IgG response to C. rodentium (Fig. 3, G and H). Despite the enhanced antibody response, there was no consistent change in kinetics of C. rodentium infection or clearance in mice that had experienced mild DSS colitis before infection (Fig. 3I).
C. rodentium induces transient colitis, and given that prior inflammatory training led to an increased adaptive immune response, we initially predicted that inflammatory gut training followed by infection would enhance colon inflammation and immunopathology. Instead, mice that had experienced mild DSS colitis were unexpectedly protected from C. rodentium–induced colitis as measured by colon shortening (Fig. 3J). This was further supported by reduced Il17a expression in colon tissues on day 14 after infection, whereas other inflammatory cytokines and antimicrobial peptides were unchanged by training (Fig. 3, K to Q). In accordance with the similar early infection load in trained and untrained mice, prior DSS treatment did not cause appreciable changes in inflammatory or antimicrobial immune responses in the distal colon on day 5 after infection (Fig. 3, K to Q). Together, these data demonstrate that prior inflammation leads to an altered adaptive immune response to subsequent de novo infection, resulting in enhanced B cell immunity but reduced tissue inflammation.

Evidence for trained immunity in MLN FRC after mild colitis

The enhanced protective response to a new infection after mild colitis was reminiscent of the protection provided by innate immune training. There are several hallmark features of trained immunity, particularly (i) enhanced response to de novo stimuli, (ii) longevity of the innate “memory,” (iii) epigenetic alterations that create memory, and (iv) enhanced metabolic status of trained cells. Our data, so far, support enhanced adaptive response to infection. To test for longevity, we administered DSS and rested mice for 6 weeks before infection with C. rodentium. Mice that were trained 6 weeks before infection again showed significantly increased numbers of FRC (Fig. 4A), accompanied by increased GC B cells in MLN (Fig. 4, B and C), and enhanced antibody responses to C. rodentium (Fig. 4, D and E). As before, training resulted in protection of colon tissue as measured by colon length and expression of inflammatory cytokines (Fig. 4, F to M). The reduction in inflammation was more clearly demarcated when mice were rested for 6 weeks, suggesting that residual inflammation from the mild DSS treatment was still present when rested for only 1 week. Hence, these data support the longevity of the trained response leading to enhanced humoral immunity and reduced inflammation.
We next tested for epigenetic changes in the expanded FRC population after DSS training (Fig. 4N). FRC from trained mice showed enhanced trimethylation at the fourth lysine residue of the DNA packaging protein histone H3 (H3K4me3), an epigenetic modification suggesting increased promoter activity (Fig. 4O and fig. S3A).
We have previously shown that inflammatory activation leads to enhanced glucose uptake by FRC in an IL-17–dependent manner, corresponding to enhanced function in metabolic assays (19). On the basis of this, we asked whether prior activation of FRC by DSS colitis resulted in altered glucose uptake as a measure of metabolic capacity in response to subsequent infection. Glucose uptake by FRC at 5 days after infection was significantly enhanced in infected mice that had received DSS training compared with untrained infected controls (Fig. 4, P and Q, and fig. S3B). Hence, FRC bear hallmark features of trained immunity after mild gut inflammation that corresponds to a sustained capacity to boost the immune response to a new infection.

FRC-intrinsic IL-17 signaling is required for immune training during gut inflammation

The data so far suggest that FRC activation by prior gut inflammatory training results in increased antibody responses and reduced colon inflammation when a new pathogen (C. rodentium) is encountered. However, other cell types in the colon or MLNs could also undergo immune training to contribute to the effect. We previously showed that FRC-intrinsic IL-17 signaling is required for metabolic reprogramming and proliferation of FRC (19). We therefore tested the gut inflammation-trained response in FRC∆Il17ra mice. Inflammatory training did not prevent the weight loss experienced by FRC∆Il17ra mice upon infection (fig. S4). As predicted, FRC∆Il17ra mice had significantly fewer MLN FRC compared with trained FRCCtrl mice after inflammatory training and infection (Fig. 5A). Similar defects were seen in numbers of GC B cells and antibody response to C. rodentium (Fig. 5, B to D).
FRC∆Il17ra mice have defects in C. rodentium clearance and antibody response (Fig. 1); therefore, we compared responses in untrained FRC∆Il17ra mice with trained FRC∆Il17ra mice. Inflammatory training was unable to boost the FRC or B cell response compared with untrained FRC∆Il17ra mice after infection (Fig. 5, A to D). Similarly, FRC∆Il17ra mice retained a poor capacity to clear C. rodentium infection after training (Fig. 5E). Trained FRC∆Il17ra mice had no protection from gut inflammation as measured by colon length (Fig. 5F). Inflammatory training of FRC∆Il17ra mice resulted in increased expression of inflammatory markers in colon tissue compared with untrained FRC∆Il17ra colon (Fig. 5G). Hence, these data further implicate FRC in the trained gut immunity by showing that deletion of IL-17 receptor in FRC prevented the beneficial protective outcome of mild inflammatory training and instead promoted inflammation.
Because FRC∆Il17ra mice have inherent defects in C. rodentium antibody response, we took an additional approach to test the role of IL-17–dependent FRC expansion in this model of inflammatory training. IL-17A was transiently blocked during the training period by administering neutralizing antibody at the start of DSS challenge. IL-17A blockade during DSS treatment resulted in increased weight loss (fig. S5A), as expected from prior reports that IL-17 promotes epithelial repair after injury (30, 31). To allow resolution of colon inflammation and for clearance of the administered anti–IL-17 antibody, we rested mice for 2 weeks after DSS before infecting with C. rodentium. After infection, there were no significant changes in body weight between mice that received inflammatory training in the presence or absence of anti–IL-17, and weights were not altered compared with untrained controls (fig. S5A). Colon lengths were also similar at 2 weeks after DSS, confirming resolution of overt inflammation by this time point (fig. S5B). IL-17A blockade during training prevented the increased numbers of FRC (Fig. 6A) and the enhanced B cell response to C. rodentium infection in trained mice (Fig. 6, B to D). IL-17A blockade also abrogated the tissue protective effect of training on colitis, as determined by increased colon shortening (Fig. 6E) and increased expression of inflammatory cytokines compared with mice treated with isotype control during DSS training (Fig. 6F). It was apparent that IL-17A neutralization did not completely abrogate the training effect, in contrast to IL-17RA deletion in FRCs (comparing Figs. 5 and 6). Because IL-17RA is a shared receptor with IL-17F, this suggests that there may be some additional IL-17F signaling that contributes to training of FRC, although the statistical significance of reductions observed suggest that this would be a minor contribution compared with IL-17A. Overall, these data demonstrate a critical role for IL-17 signaling, particularly in FRC, in promoting the enhanced B cell response to infection after DSS training.

Colon protection from inflammatory training is mediated through B cell IL-10

The data so far indicate that inflammatory activation of FRC results in a trained response that enhances adaptive immunity through increased TFH and B cell activation but paradoxically decreased colon inflammation during C. rodentium infection. IL-10 is a key regulatory cytokine for mucosal inflammation (32). Training either 1 or 6 weeks before infection resulted in increased colon IL-10 expression on day 14 after infection (Fig. 7, A and B), corresponding with the reduced inflammation. Colon IL-10 was reduced in trained mice when IL-17 was neutralized during training or FRC lacked IL-17RA (Fig. 7, C and D). During primary infection without training, there was no difference in colon IL-10 expression between FRC∆Il17ra and FRCCtrl mice (fig. S6A), suggesting that tissue IL-10 production is not inherently impaired in FRC∆Il17ra mice. However, there was reduced expression of IL-10 in the reinfected FRCΔIl17ra mice (Fig. 7E), again implicating an enhanced IL-10 response after an initial inflammatory event in an FRC-dependent manner. These findings thus support a role for training of FRC in an IL-17–dependent manner, leading to the increased expression of IL-10 and tissue protection in the colon.
Although regulatory T cells are a common source of IL-10 in the gut, we saw no correlation between IL-10 and forkhead box P3 (Foxp3) expression in these experiments (fig. S6, B to E). B cells can also provide an important source of regulatory IL-10 (33–36). We therefore queried whether the increased activation of B cells contributes to tissue protection after training via IL-10 production. There was an increase of class-switched IgG+ B cells in the colon lamina propria of trained mice after infection with C. rodentium (Fig. 7F), and IgG+ B cells showed the greatest expression of IL-10 among colonic lamina propria B cells (Fig. 7G and fig. S6F). We next tested the contribution of IL-10 produced by B cells by using Cd19Cre × Il10fl/fl mice, which have a B cell–intrinsic deletion of IL-10 (B cellΔIL10). Control mice were Cd19creIl10+/+ (B cellCtrl). After C. rodentium infection, IgG production was similar between B cellΔIL10 and B cellCtrl mice, and both B cell frequency and IgG were enhanced by training to an equal degree (Fig. 7H and fig. S6G), demonstrating that B cell IL-10 is not required for the antibody response. However, deletion of B cell–derived IL-10 abrogated the colon protective outcome of training on infectious colitis (Fig. 7I). Furthermore, after infection, trained B cellΔIL10 mice had reduced colon IL-10 expression compared with trained controls and similar colon IL-10 expression to untrained mice (Fig. 7J), confirming B cells as the critical source of colon IL-10 after training. The altered expression of inflammatory cytokines was similarly reversed in trained infected B cellΔIL10 mice (Fig. 7, K to M, and fig. S6, H to K). These data pinpoint the enhanced B cell IL-10 response as a major regulator of the tissue protective effects after inflammatory training in the gut.

DISCUSSION

Within a given population, the same infection can have vastly different outcomes for individuals, ranging from asymptomatic infection to severe inflammation and tissue injury. Some factors that contribute to determining these outcomes are known, including prior microbial experience through infection or vaccination leading to innate immune training. Our data have revealed a previously unknown pathway modulating adaptive immune responses through IL-17–dependent inflammatory FRC training. By enhancing B cell activation to a newly encountered infection, this form of inflammatory training through FRC boosts the protective antibody response along with B cell IL-10 production in the intestinal tissue. The result is improved host protection by clearing the infection with reduced inflammatory side effects.
IL-17 is required for B cell activation during autoimmune disease (37, 38). Although early studies suggested a possible direct role for IL-17 acting on B cells, the consensus is that the mechanism is more likely to be indirect. We previously showed that IL-17 signaling in FRC was required for optimal autoimmune antibody in a model of multiple sclerosis (19). Here, we confirmed a similar defect in antibody response to C. rodentium when FRC could not respond to IL-17. On the other hand, increased expansion and activation of FRC by a mild inflammatory stimulus before infection increased both the numbers of TFH cells and GC B cells to enhance the antibody response. This finding is reminiscent of a study showing that TFH cells have reduced entry to GC light zones in the absence of IL-17 (39) and further implicates an indirect role for IL-17 in promoting antibody responses through TFH cell activation via FRC. The molecular mechanisms by which FRC promote TFH have yet to be determined: One possibility is that IL-17–dependent proliferation of FRC is required to maintain the migratory routes of FRC to the GC because FRC are the “road” along which T cells move within lymphoid structures (40). Another non-exclusive possibility is that IL-17–stimulated FRC provide cytokine cues, such as IL-6, to promote TFH cell activation and metabolic fitness, as has been shown for CD8 T cells (41). Last, FRC are key providers of the B cell growth and survival factor B cell activating factor (BAFF), so increasing their numbers in an IL-17–dependent manner would also increase the availability of BAFF to support B cell activation, as suggested by the increased B cell frequency in trained MLN.
We had predicted that prior inflammation might exacerbate the transient colitis normally induced by C. rodentium infection in the gut, so it was a surprise to instead find reduced inflammation in infected colons after inflammatory training. However, this finding corresponds well to the clinical conundrum presented by the presence of IL-17–producing cells in both health and disease states in the gut. Despite the clear association between increased IL-17 and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), clinical trials targeting IL-17 in IBD failed because of lack of efficacy and exacerbated disease in some individuals (42). It has been posited that IL-17 promotes restoration of the epithelial barrier after gut injury, in addition to regulating microbiota populations that are more numerous in the gut compared to skin (43–45). Our data further suggest that IL-17 signaling in gut-draining LN helps to tune the adaptive immune response, particularly B cells, with a net protective outcome to inflammatory microbes through increased production of IL-10. In this regard, IL-10+ regulatory B cells exhibit potent anti-inflammatory effects in a number of autoimmune models including colitis and EAE (35, 46, 47). Specifically, IL-10+ B cells were shown to increase in the chronically inflamed colon to limit colitis progression in α T cell receptor knockout mice lacking regulatory T cells (36). Accordingly, we find that inflammation in the gut favors an enhanced regulatory B cell response to infection and implicate IL-17–dependent FRC activation in tuning gut immunoregulation.
Although we have not formally eliminated involvement of myeloid cells or innate lymphoid cells (ILC3) in our study, several lines of evidence lead us to think that these innate cells are not the primary driver of IL-17–dependent FRC effects on C. rodentium clearance in primary infection or after training. In contrast to many innate immune training models, training through DSS did not significantly alter the infection load of C. rodentium; this argues against a role for myeloid cells or innate lymphoid cells, which would be expected to more rapidly eliminate the infection. We did observe a reduction in Csf2, encoding GM-CSF, not only in the colon of FRCΔIL17RA mice during primary infection but also in WT mice after training. ILC3 are a major producer of GM-CSF during colitis, along with IL-17 and IL-22, and GM-CSF recruits and activates inflammatory macrophages for which IL-1β is a hallmark (21, 22, 48). However, mice with defective IL-17 signaling in FRC experienced enhanced expression of inflammatory cytokines that are associated with myeloid or innate lymphoid cell activation and GM-CSF signaling during C. rodentium control, including IL-1β, REGIIIγ, and IL-17 (21, 22, 48); yet, these mice had delayed clearance during primary infection. Training further enhanced expression of IL-22 and IL-17 in the absence of FRC IL-17 signaling yet was still unable to improve C. rodentium clearance. Furthermore, FRCΔIL17RA mice showed no benefit from training either in rate of infection clearance or colon inflammation, corresponding to reduced IL-10 and reduced B cell activation, supporting the importance of interactions between FRC and the adaptive immune system in this protective model.
Innate immunological training of myeloid lineage cells is typically mediated through IL-1β or lipopolysaccharide (LPS) signaling leading to metabolic and epigenetic changes that outlast the acute inflammatory response (49). It is worth noting some interesting commonalities between LPS, IL-1β, and IL-17 signaling. All three stimuli drive nuclear factor κB (NF-κB) activation as a major signaling pathway and induce feed-forward transcriptional regulators including the NF-κB coactivator NF-kappa-B inhibitor zeta (Iκbζ) and transcription factor CCAAT enhancer binding protein beta (C/EBPβ), which was recently shown to be critical for trained immunity in hematopoietic stem cells that generate myeloid cells (50). LPS and IL-1β are strong drivers of metabolic reprogramming in innate immune cells (51); we recently showed that IL-17 is critical for metabolic reprogramming in FRC and identified Iκbζ as one of the regulators of IL-17–induced metabolic change (19). Metabolic reprogramming and epigenetic modifications are intricately intertwined, and metabolites such as acetyl coenzyme A, succinate, and mevalonate influence chromatin accessibility. Rewiring of metabolic circuits thus poises innate immune cells to respond more rapidly after immune training (20, 52). IL-17 typically targets nonhematopoietic cells including fibroblast-type stromal cells and epithelia, whereas LPS and IL-1β primarily activate immune cells. Nevertheless, these shared signaling outputs may provide the common outcomes of trained immunity through metabolic and epigenetic rewiring of target cells.
There is very limited understanding of contributions of immune or inflammatory training in non-immune cells to date. However, Naik et al. (53) demonstrated that skin inflammation could speed up subsequent wound healing through “inflammatory memory” epigenetically programmed into epithelial stem cells. This form of inflammatory training required both IL-1β production and presence of RAR-related orphan receptor gamma (RORγt+) and IL-17–producing T cells (53), again suggesting interplay between immune and non-immune systems for the enhanced outcome. Here, we showed that inflammatory training through DSS-induced IL-17 results in metabolic and epigenetic changes in FRC to promote enhanced function.
In summary, this study shows that IL-17 helps to simultaneously maintain intestinal defense and promote immunoregulation by enhancing the capacity of gut-draining LNs to support antibody and IL-10 production through improved B cell activation. We therefore propose that IL-17 induced by microbiota or infections during early life could tune the balance of antibody-mediated versus proinflammatory cytokine-mediated immune responses to reduce severity of infectious disease in mucosal tissues. The ability to induce IL-17 for FRC training should thus be considered as a factor that could enhance the efficacy of vaccines or the ability of individuals to respond to a known pathogenic threat for which there is not yet a vaccine, without boosting side effects of inflammation and tissue damage.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Study design

The overall objective of this study was to determine the role of IL-17 signaling in FRC either before or during responses to infection with C. rodentium and to elucidate mechanisms that result in tissue protection after inflammatory training. This was achieved by using global IL-17– and IL-17RA–deficient mice, FRC-intrinsic deletion of IL-17RA, and antibody-mediated neutralization of IL-17 in DSS-mediated colitis and C. rodentium infection models of gut inflammation. Outcomes were changes in specific cell population numbers, phenotype, and function [measured by flow cytometry, enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA), gene expression, and body weight]. All data points generated were included in the analysis. Group sizes were based on power analysis of previous publications and pilot studies for the included experiments. Groups for antibody neutralization and DSS treatment were randomly assigned. Groups with transgenic mice were assigned controls on the basis of existing age-matched littermates. The study was unblinded. All experiments were repeated more than once, and numbers of experimental repeats are indicated in figure legends.

Mice

C57BL/6 was obtained from the Jackson Laboratory. FRCΔIl17ra mice generated by crossing Il17rafl/fl [a gift from J. Kolls (45)] with Ccl19Cre [obtained from B. Ludewig via Eur0pean Mouse Mutant Archive (EMMA) repository, Infrafrontier (54)]. B cellΔIl10 mice were generated by crossing IL-10fl/fl [provided by D. Rothstein with permission of W. Muller (55)] with CD19cre (the Jackson Laboratory). Protocols were approved by the University of Pittsburgh Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee and adhered to guidelines in the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals of the National Institutes of Health.

C. rodentium infection and fecal bacteria detection

Mice were orally gavaged with fresh cultured 109 colony-forming units (CFUs) of C. rodentium strain DBS100 (American Type Culture Collection 51459). For reinfection experiments, two doses of C. rodentium were given 60 days apart. Body weight was monitored every 3 days. For fecal culture, fresh feces were collected, weighed, and homogenized in 300 μl of phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Serial dilutions of feces homogenates were plated onto MacConkey agar (Oxoid) and incubated for 18 hours at 37°C. C. rodentium colonies were identified by their characteristic pink center surrounded by a white rim. For fecal C. rodentium polymerase chain reaction (PCR), DNA in feces was isolated with the QIAamp Fast DNA Stool Mini Kit (QIAGEN), Espb gene expression was quantified by quantitative PCR (qPCR) (SYBR Green; espB-F: 5′-ATGCCGCAGATGAGACAGTTG-3′, espB-R: 5′-CGTCAGCAGCCTTTTCAGCTA-3′) and normalized relative to total bacterial 16S rRNA (5′-ATTACCGCGGCTGCTGGC-3′, 5′-ACTCCTACGGGAGGCAGCAGT-3′).

DSS-induced colitis

Mice received 1% DSS (36,000 to 50,000 molecular weight; MP Biomedicals) in their drinking water for 7 days, followed by 7 or 14 days of distilled water without DSS. Control animals received distilled water for the entire period. Mice were monitored every 3 days for body weight and infected with C. rodentium after rest. For in vivo neutralization of IL-17, 100 μg of anti–IL-17 monoclonal antibody (clone 17F3; Bio X Cell) and an equal amount of isotype control IgG (IgG1, clone MOPC-21; Bio X Cell) were administered intraperitoneally on day 0 starting DSS.

Quantitative PCR

RNA was isolated with the RNeasy Mini Kit (QIAGEN), and cDNA was generated with Superscript III First Strand kits (Invitrogen), followed by real-time PCR (RT-PCR) using SYBR Green Master mix with ROX (Invitrogen) and the following RT2 qPCR primers from QIAGEN: Foxp3 (catalog number PPM05497F), Il1b (PPM03109F), Il10 (PPM03017C), Il17a (PPM03023A), Il18 (PPM03112B), Il22 (PPM05481A), Ifng (PPM03121A), Csf2 (PPM02990F), and Reg3g (PPM35204A) on a 7300 Real-Time instrument (Applied Biosystems). Gene expression was normalized to Gapdh (PPM02946E).

FRC preparation for flow cytometry

MLNs were incubated at 37°C in digestion medium (RPMI), deoxyribonuclease I (DNase I; 0.1 mg/ml; Invitrogen), and Liberase (0.1 mg/ml; Roche). Collected single-cell suspensions were filtered. For flow cytometry, cells were stained with Ghostdye510 viability dye (Tonbo Biosciences) in PBS, followed by labeling with CD45 (clone 30-F11, Invitrogen), CD45.2 (104, BD Biosciences), podoplanin (PDPN) (8.1.1, BioLegend), CD31 (MEC 13.3, BD Biosciences), H3K4me3 (C42D8, Cell Signaling Technology), and CountBright Absolute Counting Beads (Molecular Probes). For glucose uptake assays, 500 nM 2-(N-(7-nitrobenz-2-oxa-1,3-diazol-4-yl)amino)-2-deoxyglucose (2NBDG; Cayman Chemicals) was injected via tail vein 30 min before sacrifice.

CD4 and B cell analysis in MLN by flow cytometry

Single-cell suspensions were obtained from LNs by mechanical isolation and filtered before staining with Ghostdye510 (Tonbo Biosciences) viability dye in PBS and then surface staining in fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) buffer. For TFH staining, cells were fixed, permeabilized with eBioscience Foxp3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set, and incubated with antibodies to CXCR5 in Permeabilization Buffer with 3.3% normal rat serum overnight at 4°C. FACS antibodies and staining reagents were purchased from the following companies: CD4 (no. GK1.5, Invitrogen), B220 (no. RA3-6B2, BD Biosciences), CD19 (6D5, BioLegend), GL-7 (no. GL-7, BD Pharmingen), CD44 (IM7, eBioscience), PD1 (RMP1-30, BioLegend), CXCR5 (2G8, BD Biosciences), IL-10 (JES5-16E3, BioLegend), and IgG (Poly4053, BioLegend). Flow cytometry data were acquired on a FACS LSRFortessa (BD Biosciences) and analyzed with FlowJo software.

Lamina propria preparation and B cell IL-10 analysis

Colons were excised, splayed open, and rinsed with cold RPMI 1640 containing 3% fetal calf serum (FCS), penicillin-streptomycin (100 μg/ml), 25 mM Hepes, and 50 mM β-mercaptoethanol (β-ME). Tissue was minced and digested with RPMI 1640 containing 3% FCS, penicillin-streptomycin (100 μg/ml), 25 mM Hepes, 50 mM β-ME, 5 mM EDTA, and 1,4-dithiothreitol (145 μg/ml) for 20 min with continuous stirring in an incubator at 37°C/5% CO2. Tissue was strained and further digested by briefly agitating tissue with RPMI 1640 containing penicillin-streptomycin (100 μg/ml), 25 mM Hepes, 50 mM β-ME, and 2 mM EDTA. Tissue was strained, minced, and further digested with serum-free RPMI 1640 containing penicillin-streptomycin (100 μg/ml), 25 mM Hepes, 50 mM β-ME, 2 mM l-glutamine, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 1× minimum essential medium (MEM) non-essential amino acids, DNase I (500 μg/ml; Sigma-Aldrich), and Liberase TL (100 μg/ml; Roche) for 25 min with continuous stirring in an incubator at 37°C/5% CO2. Cell suspensions were serially filtered through 70- and 40-μm cell strainers, spun down, and resuspended in complete RPMI 1640 medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum, penicillin-streptomycin (100 μg/ml), 25 mM Hepes, 50 mM β-ME, 2 mM l-glutamine, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, and 1× MEM nonessential amino acids before use. For IL-10 analysis, colon lamina propria lymphocytes were stimulated with LPS from E. coli O111:B4 (Sigma-Aldrich; 10 μg/ml), phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (50 ng/ml), and ionomycin (1 μg/ml) for 5 hours at 37°C; GolgiStop (BD) and GolgiPlug (BD) were added to cultures after 1 hour. Cells were incubated with viability dye in PBS, washed, incubated with Fc block, and then stained for surface markers as previously described. Intracellular staining for IL-10 was performed using BD Cytofix/Cytoperm reagents as per the manufacturer’s protocol, using antibody clones listed above.

Antibody ELISA

Total IgG concentrations in serum were detected using a mouse IgG ELISA kit (Invitrogen). For detection of C. rodentium–specific antibody in serum, 96-well plates were coated overnight with C. rodentium (108 CFU per well) at 37°C incubator, blocked for 2 hours, and then incubated with sera (serial dilutions starting at 1:100) for 3 hours. After washing, antibodies bound to C. rodentium were detected by horseradish peroxidase–labeled anti-IgG (RD Systems). Optical density (OD) value for 1:1000 dilution was used for each experiment because standards for anti–C. rodentium IgG are not commercially available. For fecal antibody, fecal pellets were collected from the indicated groups at specific time points. Fecal supernatant was prepared [10% (w/v)] with PBS, and fecal IgG was detected by the same protocol as C. rodentium–specific antibody in serum. Horseradish peroxidase–labeled anti-IgA (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used for detecting C. rodentium–specific IgA in feces with a similar protocol.

Statistics

Experimental results were analyzed for significance using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Tukey’s multiple comparisons test (for multiple groups) or unpaired Student’s t test. Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism. P values are shown as *P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, ***P < 0.001, and ****P < 0.0001 where statistical significance was found, and all data are represented as means ± SEM. Individual points in graphs represent biological replicates (i.e., individual mice) pooled from multiple experiments.
