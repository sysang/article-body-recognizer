STING and arthritis

John F. Foley
Science Signaling • 21 Dec 2021 • Vol 14, Issue 714 • DOI: 10.1126/scisignal.abn7607

Abstract

Recruitment of the kinase TBK1 to the adaptor STING mediates interferon-independent, autoinflammatory arthritis.
Failure to clear endogenous DNA from the cytosol leads to cell death and inflammation. Indeed, mutations in genes encoding DNases are associated with the autoinflammatory disease systemic lupus erythematosus. DNase II−/− mice exhibit embryonic lethality due to type I interferon (IFN)–dependent inflammation and cell death. Loss of the type I IFN receptor in DNase II−/− mice rescues lethality, but the mice develop chronic polyarthritis that has features of rheumatoid arthritis. Knockout of components of the cytosolic DNA–sensing, cGAS-STING pathway also rescues DNase II−/− mice from lethality. Thus, Li et al. investigated pathways downstream of STING that contributed to disease by generating DNase II−/− mice expressing various mutant STING proteins. Expression of the S365A STING mutant rescued DNase II−/− mice from lethality, but they still exhibited polyarthritis, which depended on the cytokines TNFα and IL-6. The S365A mutation prevents STING from recruiting the transcription factor IRF3, which is required for type I IFN production. However, S365A STING still recruits the kinase TBK1, which is required for the activation of both IRF3 and NF-κB, which induces proinflammatory cytokine production. In contrast, DNase II−/− mice that expressed the L373A STING mutant or a C-terminal truncation mutant of STING, both of which cannot recruit TBK1, survived into adulthood, but did not develop polyarthritis or exhibit proinflammatory cytokine production. Together, these data indicate that the recruitment of TBK1, but not IRF3, to STING drives polyarthritis due to defective DNA clearance, suggesting a therapeutic strategy for the treatment of autoinflammatory disorders that are caused by self-DNA.
