A day in the life of a technical sales representative

Henrik Fahlquist-Hagert’s experience doing lab work during his Ph.D. led to a job selling scientific equipment

  • 14 Dec 2021
  • 10:50 AM
  • ByElisabeth Pain

When materials scientist Henrik Fahlquist-Hagert saw a job ad for a technical sales representative position at Thermo Fisher Scientific, a scientific equipment provider, he immediately knew it was “a perfect match for me,” he says. “I’m a technology nerd by heart.”

During graduate school, Fahlquist-Hagert spent a lot of time working with lab equipment. He later used that experience to land a series of jobs as a field engineer and application specialist at scientific equipment companies in Sweden, Germany, and the United Kingdom. Although his main tasks involved going into labs to fix equipment and support customers, he also appreciated spending time collaborating with the sales team, he says. “I got a lot of insight in the sales processes and in the daily life of the sales representatives.”

This blend of skills and interests helped Fahlquist-Hagert convince Thermo Fisher that he would enjoy the job they posted, which entailed selling equipment to the cement and metal industry and to research labs in northern Europe. It’s been 9 months since he started that job and he acknowledges that the learning curve has been steep, particularly when it comes to learning the art of navigating legal issues and negotiating with clients. But it is rewarding for him to devise complex equipment solutions for people, Fahlquist-Hagert says—which gives him a chance to continue to work with technology, even though he no longer has “a screwdriver in … hand.”

Fahlquist-Hagert spoke with Science Careers about his career path and how he uses his scientific training today as a technical sales representative. The interview was edited for brevity and clarity.

Q: What specific tasks does your job involve?

A: My main task is to interact with customers to help them pinpoint exactly what equipment will solve their technical challenges. So, most of my effort is spent putting together quotes for new equipment, which can add up to anything between €40,000 and a couple million euros. For a quote to be accurate, you need to know the customer and their technical applications very well, and so a lot of my time is spent reading up on the customers’ proposed applications. Once a solution and configuration have been determined and agreed upon, a very important part of the job is to negotiate the terms and conditions of the purchase contract. These deals are very complex and include maintenance service agreements and liability clauses in case someone is injured.

Q: How is your work structured and your progress measured?

A: A lot of my work involves being proactive and reaching out to existing customers on a regular basis to see how things are going and if we can support them in any way. I also need to work reactively as I receive a lot of requests for quotes, which generally have a 10-day deadline. At any given time, I am usually working on 20 viable sales projects. The pace of those projects fluctuates a lot throughout the year, and it can take anywhere between 2 months and 2 years to close a deal. My performance is measured mainly in terms of how many systems I can sell, and for how much. I have milestones for each quarter and then also a target for the entire year.

Q: Who else do you interact with on a day-to-day basis?

A: I have regular contacts with our lab specialists in Switzerland who design the methods to be used with our equipment. I collaborate with them to determine the best product for a customer’s application. I also work closely with our service organization to figure out maintenance and repair agreements, our suppliers to work out technological solutions and delivery timelines, and our sales operations team—the people who create orders and handle invoices.

Q: Where do you work, and what are your working hours?

A: Due to the pandemic, I have not done a lot of customer visits so far, but normally I would have a couple of days traveling every week and spend the rest of the time at my home in Denmark. In terms of working hours, I am part of the Europe, Middle East, and Africa team, so I need to be able to interact with my colleagues across three time zones. I usually start early in the morning, go and pick up my 15-month-old son quite early in the afternoon, and then continue working in the evening. The flexibility of my schedule means that I can take a 2-hour lunch break during the week if I need to run some errands and then catch up on that work during the evening or weekend.

Q: What’s your favorite part of your job?

A: It’s the freedom, without a doubt—nobody is interested in my work hours, they’re only interested in my performance. And it’s nice to work with complex systems that are large both financially and technologically. I like that I can see how my hard work actually pays off, since all sales positions are commission based. I also enjoy traveling and meeting new people; the personal relationships with customers are very important.

Q: Did you need additional training or experiences—beyond your academic training—to do your job?

A: Before this job, during my time as a field service specialist, I gained quite a rigorous training in how to install scientific instruments. Still, it’s important to know the instrument and its capabilities and what the customer is talking about when they are struggling with a certain function. So, upon joining Thermo Fisher, I received technical hands-on training on our instruments and did a course on applications with our lab specialists. I also received formal sales training to better understand the sales process.

Q: Which tasks are you still learning to master today?

A: How to be a great sales representative. It’s very different from my previous role in customer service because I now have to negotiate what we can offer and do. What you want to reach is a win-win situation where the customers trust they have gotten exactly what they pay for. And where to find this sweet spot of negotiations, that’s something that I’m still working very hard to learn. And then, the legal issues are something that I was not aware of when I started this job.

Q: Are there elements of your job that some people might dislike?

A: People coming from a purely scientific background might not like to meticulously read terms and conditions and take part in negotiations. And then, all the paperwork and chasing customers. You need to keep track of what stage a potential business opportunity is in by frequently connecting with customers for updates on their schedule, budget, and expectations.

Q: What elements of your academic training are you using today?

A: I’m using all of it to a certain extent. For example, understanding the chemistry of different materials and the physics of sample measurement is important when helping customers find solutions. Also, my Ph.D. taught me how to find and drill down into very complex sources of information.

Q: What did you find most challenging when you took your position?

A: Reaching out to customers. Searching for the right people to talk to and the right things to say from a sales perspective was a bit of a hurdle at first. Sometimes you get to feel like a phony salesman, but after a while it comes quite naturally.

Q: Is there anything from your life as an academic that you miss?

A: Spending time in the lab and scribbling down notes on experiments is probably the thing I miss the most. I am always telling my wife, who is still an academic, that if we have a large enough house someday, I would love to invest in a science lab where I could have my own scientific equipment.

Q: What advice would you have for young scientists who are interested in following a career path like yours?

A: If you want to become a technical sales rep, keep in mind that these positions tend to be very seldom announced. So you have to be very perceptive of when they do show up—for example by following companies you’re interested in on LinkedIn. Then, be ready to demonstrate why you think a position is a perfect match for you, sometimes against employers’ expectations. Employers might think that you will get bored working directly with customers rather than machines. Show a genuine interest and leverage all the skills and experiences you have acquired to sell yourself.
