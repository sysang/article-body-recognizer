Peace Is the Prize

Personal Perspective: There is calm in all of us, the task is connecting with it

Posted December 9, 2021 | Reviewed by Tyler Woods

  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Email

Key points

  • Peace is a state of being.
  • Finding peace within is difficult for many people.
  • Learning to connect with our own inner peace is a process and a skill.
  • Peace exists in all of us.

“Peace cannot be kept by force; it can only be achieved by understanding.” Albert Einstein

I never considered myself and Donald Trump to have much in common, but there he was, the top image of my web search after typing in "peace is the prize." Apparently, he uttered those words and, in that moment, worlds collided and I found myself in agreement with a man whom I'd always believed to be the exact opposite of me. It was an odd moment, but, ironically, one that gave me hope. I have been thinking long and hard about peace and how I think it should be the goal for individuals, for communities, and for societies. Peace should be what we are all aiming for.

Source: Louise Taylor

“We must seek together to address the good aspirations of people everywhere, for we are bound together through great commonality.” Bryant McGill

I was born in Belfast in 1978. I spent most of my childhood growing up in a rural area in County Tyrone in a time that is often referred to regionally and internationally as "The Troubles," three decades marked by domestic terrorism and violence. I was aware that a threat existed and I knew there was a possibility that I could die, just as others had died, for simply being in the wrong place at the wrong time, for being affiliated with the wrong religion, or for being on the wrong side of the conflict. This was my childhood, this was my normal, this was daily life in Northern Ireland for many, many years and even though I was not living in the most affected areas during this time, all children were affected in one way or another.

I remember many nights lying awake as a young person feeling paralyzed by fear of death and being terrified of how it might feel and how it might come about. I now know that is anxiety. At the time, I thought it was reasonable, sensible, and mature to think intensely and in great detail about the multiple ways one may meet their own end. The more I thought about avoiding death, the more scared I became of death, though it did absolutely nothing in terms of reducing the likelihood of my dying.

The fear of death is not intrinsically unhealthy. To survive, we must be aware of risks and the potential for threat or attack. In my youth, the more I thought about the numerous, varied and sophisticated ways that I could die, the greater the risk I saw in everything. Now, I accept my own vulnerability and finiteness and I have found that acceptance is key rather than avoidance or hatching plans to escape the inevitable. Thankfully "The Troubles" are over and bombs and paramilitary murders and attacks are largely a thing of the past. Now, peace is prioritized in our society and our communities. The work for me as a therapist and mother is to help others and myself nurture that peace within. Now that the external threat of violence has changed, the hypervigilance can be changed.

This is going to sound a bit odd, but for years, peace, calm, and contentment were disconcerting and uncomfortable to me. For many people who have grown up in countries experiencing internal violence, being hypervigilant and hyper-aware becomes our normal. Excessive stress and pressure on our adrenal and nervous systems is what we are used to. We live within communities with high levels of the population experiencing and attempting to live with complex grief and trauma, and all of these factors and many more impact a person's ability to access inner calm and feelings of peacefulness and contentment.

“Many people think excitement is happiness... But when you are excited you are not peaceful. True happiness is based on peace.” Thich Nhat Hanh

I wish someone had sat me down and taught me about peace. I wish they had spelled it out to me. I think we should be taught how to be peaceful and how there are no negative consequences to being peaceful: individually, communally, or collectively.

Peace is good for our mental health, our relationships, and our future. I wish someone had shown me. I wish one of those to whom I used to look up to for guidance had whispered into my ear, "Peace is the prize, Louise. Euphoria and joy are fun and important, but peace is the prize."

Peace is not me dancing to Calvin Harris in the kitchen. Peace is not me throwing my head back and laughing loudly with abandon. Peace is the release of my stresses in a long bath. Peace is the deep breaths that I often take for granted, the sigh, the gentle smile, the surrender. It is the caress of a loved one and the last lulling, bobbing thought before a good night’s sleep. Peace is the moments of serenity and contended emptiness, but not emptiness that exists because of loneliness, emptiness that exists when our thoughts are gone and our soul rested and reassured.

Peace is, sadly, a privilege denied to many, but should be a basic human right. The longer I work in therapy and the more people I meet in my personal and professional life, the more I am convinced that peace is inside all of us. The more I see the more I believe there is an ocean of calm, an oasis of contentment, a galaxy of bliss and it is there always. The tragedy is that many of us barely notice it.

There is a prize within all of us. It is called peace but to find it we must believe it is there.

“Peaceful is the one who’s not concerned with having more or less. Unbound by name and fame, he is free from sorrow from the world and mostly from himself.” Rumi
