Do You Sometimes Feel Like a Fraud?

Ironically, many people who feel like frauds are high achievers.

Posted December 9, 2021 | Reviewed by Vanessa Lancaster

  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Email

Key points

  • Research has found that it is common for high achieving women to feel like frauds.
  • Researchers also found that men react with imposter-syndrome-like symptoms when put under pressure.
  • Realistically assessing goals and expectations can help ground a person coping with imposter syndrome.

“Sometimes I feel like a complete fraud,” Beth told me. She had just received an award for a project she had worked on for months. “I know I did the work,” she said, “but the thing is, I enjoyed it. It wasn’t hard for me to do.” I opened my mouth, and she stopped me before I could say anything, “I know, I know, I put plenty of overtime on it. It’s just, I don’t know, I don’t think I deserve the amount of praise that I got for it.”

Kirsten Weir (2013) wrote that,

Impostor phenomenon occurs among high achievers who are unable to internalize and accept their success. They often attribute their accomplishments to luck rather than to ability, and fear that others will eventually unmask them as a fraud.

Further, although not an official DSM diagnosis, it is a phrase that clinicians often use for what Weir called “a very real and specific form of intellectual self-doubt. Impostor feelings are generally accompanied by anxiety and, often, depression.”

Interestingly, so many people who suffer from this problem are, in fact, high achievers. When I’ve asked clients like Beth about how they explain their achievements concerning feeling like an imposter, they often say something about how hard they work.

Beth said, “I feel like I have to work harder to achieve success than someone else might. I’m always pushing myself, always trying harder, always looking to make sure that I give 150 percent in everything.”

“That sounds exhausting,” I told her.

She replied,

It is. And what’s worse, since I know I’ll never really be good enough. I never get a chance to stop and enjoy what I’m doing, or what I’ve done, or what I’ve accomplished. I’m always focused on the next task, whatever that is, to make sure I stay at the top of my game.

It’s hard to say who suffers the most from imposter syndrome. One study found 75 percent of the women executives interviewed had experienced imposter syndrome.

Another found that men do not necessarily identify themselves as having the syndrome but that the men they tested reacted with “imposter-syndrome-like symptoms when put under pressure.”

It’s clear that it’s not simply an internal psychological issue – that is, it’s not all in your mind. Culture, race, and gender expectations play important roles in imposter syndrome. Andrea Salazar Nuñez, a licensed psychologist at the University of Washington in Seattle, recently told an interviewer on NPR, “For people of color and especially women of color, impostor syndrome is ... influenced by the messaging that we've received from day one.”

Taking a slightly different perspective, Ruchika Tulshyan and Jodi-Ann Burey wrote that the concept itself is problematic because they said,

Imposter syndrome blames individuals, without accounting for the historical and cultural contexts that are foundational to how it manifests in both women of color and white women. Imposter syndrome directs our view toward fixing women at work instead of fixing the places where women work.

So what can you do if you suffer from feeling like a fraud at work or school?

I haven’t seen any literature on how many high achieving women have left the workforce, despite having moved up in their careers because of the syndrome, but I’d imagine it would be a large number.

  1. Recognize that you are not alone. If 75 percent of women executives interviewed for a single study described themselves as having the symptoms of this problem, you are in excellent company. And, if you’re a man struggling with self-doubt and low self-esteem despite successes in your field, you are probably one of many who have the same difficulty but have never named it.
  2. Pay attention to your actual successes. Then ask yourself: What did you do to achieve them? What do you think of as fraudulent? And would you consider a friend, a colleague, or an acquaintance who achieved the same success in the same way to be a fraud? If not, try to apply the same standards to yourself.
  3. Ask for help. It is not a sign of underachieving to ask for help from others. You do not have to do everything on your own. Working with a team can sometimes alleviate the feeling of being an imposter because you are not taking all of the responsibility–or all of the credit.
  4. Look at the cultural issues. We live in several intersecting cultures, including our families, religious groups, ethnic groups, work culture, and the culture of the society in which we live. What is going on in each culture to promote a sense that you really aren’t as good as your work would indicate? And what can you do to counteract that cultural belief, at least in yourself, if not in anyone else?
  5. Check out your goals. What do you want to achieve? Are you trying to please or impress someone in your family? Do you have a mentor you’re afraid of letting down? Did you set some goals for yourself when you were younger that you’re trying to attain? Take some time to revisit these goals. What would it mean if your dad wasn’t impressed with what you’d accomplished? How disappointed would that mentor be? What if you don’t meet a goal you established when you were younger and maybe more naïve? Will you not be a good person? Will you not be happy?

After Beth took these steps, she started to feel much better. She said,

I was setting up unrealistic expectations and trying to change impossible family beliefs. I come from a culture where women are supposed to stay home and take care of the house, children, and their husbands. Nothing I do will make anyone in that culture think I’m successful.

So I have to find a way to appreciate my success and surround myself with other people who see me for who I am and what I’ve achieved. For real. I can do that. I’ve done that at work. I just have to start appreciating the opinions of my colleagues and my bosses.

And stop imagining that they’re going to suddenly find out that I’m supposed to be home with my family, not out in the work world. They know that I’m where I belong. I have to know that too.
