Study Uncovers Brain Diversity in Octopuses

Brain imaging of four octopus species reveals links to habitats and behaviors.

Posted December 8, 2021 | Reviewed by Gary Drevitch

  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Email

Key points

  • Scientists performed MRI scans on the brains of four species of octopus with different lifestyles, behaviors, and habitats.
  • Enlargement, division, and folding in visual and learning areas of the brain are linked to ecological niches and lifestyles.
  • Diurnal species in shallow reefs have complex brains with more folds; nocturnal or deep-sea octopuses have less complex, smoother brains.
Day octopus (Octopus cyanea)
Source: National Marine Sanctuaries, via Wikimedia Commons. Public domain.

In a new study, researchers from the University of Queensland used advanced magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans to visualize the brains of several octopus species. The findings, published in Current Biology, shed new light on octopus brains and reveal previously unknown neuroanatomical diversity.

Outstanding Octopuses

Our current knowledge of octopus brain anatomy and associated behavior is mostly based on studies of one species, the nocturnal European octopus Octopus vulgaris. But octopus species vary greatly in their ecological niches, habitats, and behaviors. Some species live in visually complex and crowded coral reefs, while others inhabit deeper waters, which are relatively featureless and emptier. Some species are active at day, while others are nocturnal. And among octopuses, there are species that are masters of camouflage and some that have demonstrated complex cognitive abilities.

Yet, how these different ecologies and behaviors are reflected in the octopus central nervous system was largely unknown. The researchers used high-resolution MRI scans to create detailed, three-dimensional, digitized images of octopus brains and show how brain development is linked to the animals’ lifestyles and environments.

Blue-lined octopus (Hapalochlaena fasciata)
Source: Totti, via Wikimedia Commons. Distributed under a CC BY-SA 4.0 license.

Lead author Wen-Sung Chung and his colleagues chose four species to investigate, including one deep-sea octopus (Vampyroteuthis infernalis, the vampire squid), one solitary nocturnal species (Hap alochlaena fasciata, the blue-lined octopus) and two different reef dwellers active during the day (Abdopus capricornicus and Octopus cyanea, the day octopus).

Chung says that the latter two species of diurnal octopus are interesting for several reasons. In addition to being active during the day and possessing excellent camouflage, they regularly interact with other animals.

“We know most octopus are solitary and rarely interact with others outside of the mating season,” says Chung. “But these octopus often interact with others of the same species as well as animals of different species.”

The day octopus, Octopus cyanea, for instance, has been documented to hunt collaboratively with reef fishes and may even communicate with fishes using distinctive gestures.

Brain Complexity and Lifestyle

Overall, Chung and his colleagues found that enlargement, division, and folding in the visual and learning lobes of octopus brains are associated with their habits and habitats.

For instance, the vampire squid, Vampyroteuthis infernalis, lives in the deep sea and has a slow pace of life with limited behavioral interactions. It also has a less complex, smoother brain than the species that inhabit shallower waters and more complex environments. These diurnal, reef-dwelling octopuses have more complex brain structures with more folds.

Vampire squid (Vampyroteuthis infernalis)
Source: Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute (MBARI), via Wikimedia Commons. Distributed under a CC BY-SA 4.0 license.

“This brain folding had been hypothesized in octopuses because more complex brains with more folds can increase the ability to support advanced cognition or learning,” says Chung. Folding increases the brain’s surface area and volume, and among vertebrates, more brain folds are an indicator of increased size, complexity, and information processing capability.

In addition, Chung and his colleagues discovered that in reef octopuses, the vertical lobe of their brains (which plays a role in learning and memory) consists of seven subdivisions. Previous studies of the solitary and nocturnal Octopus vulgaris showed it only has five subdivisions. Chung says that these species may have more subdivisions and folding of the brain in order to support their behavioral complexity.

Cephalopod Secrets

These findings are intriguing but raise more questions for Chung. One is why octopuses invest so much energy in developing their brains when they only live for about a year or so.

Day octopus (Octopus cyanea)
Source: Totti, via Wikimedia Commons. Distributed under a CC BY-SA 4.0 license.

Another mystery is that they are color blind, yet they manage incredible feats of camouflage. Chung says that their ability to see polarized light might be a factor. His team has developed a polarization vision camera, enabling them to see what the octopus sees, and an ongoing project involves studying how octopuses use different properties of light, including polarization, in their camouflage.

“These are iconic marine animals, but we really know so little about them,” says Chung. “I hope our study can trigger more interest in these unique animals and spur more research on their brains and behavior. There are many things we can learn from cephalopods.”

References

Chung, W., Kurniawan, N.D., and Marshall, N.J. Comparative brain structure and visual processing in octopus from different habitats. Current Biology 18 November 2021. Doi: 10.1016/j.cub.2021.10.070.
