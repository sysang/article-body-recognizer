This Is Your Brain

Why We Should Teach Police to Think Like Brain Surgeons

Lessons from a neurosurgeon may help cool off police–civilian interactions.

Posted December 9, 2021 | Reviewed by Michelle Quirk

  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Email

Key points

  • In moments of extreme stress, the human brain is designed to prioritize survival in the face of a threat.
  • Certain professions need to be trained to overcome evolution and retrain their brains to deal with these moments rationally.
  • Thinking like a neurosurgeon may help police officers respond better to moments of high stress, when their brains are responding as programmed.
Dr. Phil Stieg in the OR
Source: Courtesy Weill Cornell Medicine

As the trial begins for former police officer Kimberly Potter, who shouted “Taser” as she fired what was actually a handgun, we are once again debating the deadly encounters that too often result from police–civilian interaction. As a neuroscientist watching the news through the lens of biology, I see the brain doing what it has evolved to do— prioritizing survival in the face of threat. In those moments of chaos, with human brains under unimaginable pressure, it is easy to understand how things can go so wrong. Understanding the brain’s primal response to stress may help prevent these deadly encounters and help first responders to act more like…neurosurgeons.

Need to Overcome Evolution

Neurosurgery may seem an unlikely model for policing, but what we have in common is the need to overcome evolution. Like air traffic controllers, Navy SEALs, and others in high-stakes fields, neurosurgeons need to train their brains to perform rationally, even when millions of years of evolution are directing those brains into panic mode.

Here’s what happens: When the brain is in its usual everyday state, we rely on the frontal lobe (specifically the prefrontal cortex) for good judgment. The frontal lobe manages emotional regulation, controls impulsive responses, and facilitates rational decision-making. It also enables us to anticipate and predict the consequences of our own actions, as well as the actions of others. In these moments of calm, the frontal lobe runs the show. Meanwhile, the limbic system—the emotional response center, where our most primitive reactions and urges live—remains on standby, awaiting incoming sensory information.

The Brain in Crisis Mode

We don’t fully understand what happens when that “lizard brain” senses a potential threat, but it can process and integrate that new information in the blink of an eye, rapidly sending out signals to other parts of the brain. These deep parts of the brain—the cingulate gyrus, which regulates emotion; the nucleus accumbens, which mediates the reward circuit; and the nerve cell networks, which process signals—are programmed to override our logical frontal lobe in times of crisis. Within nanoseconds of exposure to chaos and conflict, the brain starts buzzing with electrical impulses and chemical surges—the automatic human responses to fight-or-flight scenarios. The sympathetic nervous system rapidly supplies the bloodstream with the stress hormones adrenaline and noradrenaline. Although conscious awareness has barely begun to register what is happening, the biological response has already been deployed.

When we perceive a threat, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis (HPA axis) releases a cascade of stress hormones. The fear center of the brain, the amygdala, conveys this emotion-laden content to the adjacent hippocampus, the key structure for memory storage. Memories of previous threats inform how the brain will respond to its current situation, explaining why the stress response is so highly individual. That’s why a dog’s bark will elicit a stronger response from someone who has been bitten, and why celebratory Fourth of July fireworks may be terrifying for a veteran with memories of more threatening explosions.

When brain circuitry is under attack like this, the frontal lobe is unable to perform its own critical executive functions, let alone orchestrate the activities of other brain regions. During the stress response, hyperexcitability of neurotransmission and neural activation in the hippocampus and amygdala can override the brain’s executive center in the frontal lobe, compromising the ability to make rational decisions, anticipate consequences, and accurately predict others’ behavior. The brain in crisis mode has one goal: survive.

Intensive Brain Training

It is possible to move past these evolutionary impulses and not be held hostage by biology. Certain professions have already done just that, since they require optimal performance at all times and can’t afford to be governed by pure neurobiological and neurochemical reactions. They use intensive brain training to help them perform rationally under life-and-death pressure. These effective techniques are used by Navy SEALs, air traffic controllers, NASA flight engineers—and neurosurgeons.

For missions in which surrender to fear would be catastrophic, Navy SEALs tirelessly prepare their brains through mental rehearsal, self-talk, and arousal control to keep their frontal lobes in charge. In neurosurgery, which also leaves very little margin for error, we follow meticulous preparation outside of the surgery itself—including precise instrument organization, time-outs, and mental rehearsal—that keep patients safer. Mental rehearsal, in which we repeatedly walk through every step of a procedure in advance and anticipate possible complications, allows us to respond to any emergency with swift and clear reason instead of being propelled by emotional reactivity.

I have performed thousands of brain surgeries over my career and have faced crises in the OR—a sudden bleed, a drop in blood pressure, a cardiac arrest during surgery—that could have been catastrophic. Having mentally prepared for each potential development during that surgery is what allowed me to proceed with my carefully rehearsed solution. Today I train neurosurgical residents and fellows to do the same thing—they must learn to ignore the screaming of the HPA axis so that absolute calm and reason can prevail in the operating room.

Knowing that cognitive errors are more common when one is under stress, it is imperative to keep high-stakes situations as predictable as possible. The layout of the instrument tray, the patient’s position on the operating table, the location of the other surgeons—it is all exquisitely precise. I even make sure I know in advance if anyone who will be at the table with me is left-handed, so I can factor that into my mental rehearsal. Should anything unexpected happen, I can stay focused, with no cognitive distraction. The combination of exhaustive preparation and a controlled, predictable environment is key.

The profession of law enforcement carries life-and-death pressure with high-stakes outcomes for police and civilians alike. Continued mental preparedness through visualization, simulations, and drills delivered on a regular basis may be as important, if not more, than physical readiness or even firearms training. Like in my own profession where my mental preparation has saved lives, “brain training” for police officers and other first responders may hold the key to improving the outcomes of chaotic situations.
