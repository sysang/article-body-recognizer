Stereotyping Therapists Fuels Our Mental Health Epidemic

Discussions about therapy should be grounded and nuanced, to combat stigma.

Posted December 9, 2021 | Reviewed by Michelle Quirk

  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Email

Key points

  • Counselor educators emphasize that therapy is not a fit for all concerns or individuals, and do not pit therapists against other specialists.
  • Many therapists have personally experienced barriers to care, in addition to the limitations of treating structural trauma with therapy.
  • Not all therapists are therapreneurs—many are employed by public agencies with inadequate funding or space for their role.
  • Scapegoating therapists fails to address the disproportionate power that licensure and professional boards have in steering the profession.
Source: Alex Green/Pexels

Recently, a vocal mental health advocate posted a series of Tweets mischaracterizing licensed mental health professionals as elitists who weaponize credentialism against nonlicensed advocates and colleagues. These Tweets ranted about “snooty therapists” and implied that a majority of licensed counselors leverage their “pedigree” to brand themselves as more competent than crisis counselors and peer counselors, specifically.

While the name of the account’s owner is irrelevant, it is significant that the platform broadcasted generalizations and stereotypes to more than 10,000 followers. This is one reason why I am writing. Another reason is that the subtext—from my perspective—was that finding a good therapist is supposedly a lost cause. This premise is factually untrue, and carelessly misleading. In the U.S., there are about 106,000 psychologists, 120,000 counselors, and 188,401 clinical social workers—nearly half a million people.

To be clear, my sole intention is to present a portrait of therapy that is more grounded, informed, and nuanced. I am not interested in grandstanding or performative polemics.

And, for good measure, I am also not writing in defense of all therapists. There are plenty of therapists whom I consider colleagues, but not comrades. Furthermore, I do not deflect from owning my positionality in a discourse about the power dynamics between therapists and other mental health professionals. I admit that my professional credentials do indeed afford me presumed credibility and legitimacy more readily than for others in the field.

With all that said, I do take issue with the aforementioned thread, from the standpoint of a public health advocate for underserved communities, particularly LGBTQ+ and racially marginalized communities in rural regions that lack any mental health infrastructure or safety net.

Misconceptions

Now that my intentions are clear, let us delve into teasing apart some misconceptions I noticed.

Source: Tima Miroschnichenko/Pexels

First, counselor education is not a cult-like, fundamentalist hazing process that disparages other healing modalities, such as faith-based interventions, Indigenous wisdom, or peer-led groups like Alcoholics Anonymous. After a decade in the field, I have never met a therapist who advocated therapy to the point of proselytizing. In fact, one objective of clinical supervision is to mentor therapists into releasing guilt and shame about clients who drop out because therapy is just not for them. Whatever the case, therapists are trained to respect a client’s decision to not pursue therapy.

Moreover, research shows that the vast majority of counselors are not wedded to one theoretical orientation, and instead draw from multiple schools of thought. The most common rationales for adopting an eclectic or integrative approach are valuing the client’s needs above the therapist’s preferences, and viewing the client–therapist relationship as the single-most-important factor in the efficacy of therapy, across theories. This trend reflects the ultimate goal of individualizing the therapy experience for the sake of the client’s goals, even if tailoring treatment necessitates incorporating approaches that are less traditional than, say, cognitive-behavioral therapy.

Most counseling programs also teach that counseling predates the Western world's professionalization of it.

Second, therapists are as multidimensional as the next person. A therapist can be a farmer, gymnast, mountain-climber, pastor, pole-dancer, politician, or anything else. Moreover, identifying as a counselor or therapist—online or in person—is not necessarily a choice to be seen as professional-first. Nor should we assume that it is ploy to flex power over other advocates and colleagues. Yes, it is true that regulated professions tend to project an air of exclusive gatekeeping. If anything, though, the stereotype of therapists being serious and stoic is perpetuated by the media, not actual therapists. Play therapists, for example, use toys while working. And while therapists keep it professional in the therapy room, their full self is never not there.

Third, many therapists do understand, regrettably, the limitations of their profession. As the eloquent James Baldwin once said, “The price one pays for pursuing any profession or calling is an intimate knowledge of its ugly side.” Counselors-in-training, in the process of being pushed to innovate and carry the field forward, cannot help but realize therapy's inherent limitations. Some have even experienced these unfortunate realities firsthand. A good therapist is able to advocate for the healing power of therapy, while also holding in mind truths about shortcomings within the field.

Fourth, scapegoating therapists for the limitations of therapy does not actually challenge the disproportionate power that psychiatrists and accreditation boards have to steer profession. Psychiatrists more are overrepresented on the board that publishes the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders. Similarly, CACREP, the Council for Accreditation of Counseling and Related Educational Programs, is standardizes national curricula and licensure requirements. Many therapists take issue with both entities.

Moreover, the counseling field first emerged to offer an alternative, ​​egalitarian power dynamic to the patient-expert medical model, and to treat the whole person, not just symptoms alone. The intention was to affirm clients as the experts of their own lives, and to position the provider as a guide rather than an authority. This egalitarian dynamic necessitates relationship-building and trust-building, as well as a healthy skepticism toward pathologizing symptoms that can actually be traced back to sociopolitical structures, rather than the individual.

Therapists have advocated for both of these viewpoints, ever since the very initial shift from behaviorism and psychoanalysis during the 1950s.

For example, The Radical Therapist, a journal that emerged in response to the countercultural anti-war movement of the early 1970s, “voiced pointed criticism of psychiatrists during this period.” Led by a group of psychiatrists and activists, their motto was “Therapy means social, political and personal change, not adjustment,” reflecting the stance that social change redresses many societal roots of mental illness, to a degree that behavior modification was never intended to. One of The Radical Therapist’s 12 issues stated:

As powerful as therapy can be, it is seldom enough to offset the weight of the culture. If the political environment of the nation and the immediate subculture of the client is moving rightward, then the radical therapist’s job will be a tough one indeed…All of this (radical therapy) presumes a long, gradual building of a larger movement…

Finally, not all therapists are entrepreneurs. In fact, I would guesstimate that a majority of therapists work at community health or crisis prevention organizations, not in pricey private practices that reject insurance. This counters most media depictions, which tend to show therapists conducting sessions in stately home libraries or studies. If TV were actually realistic, movies and shows would depict therapy sessions in an unused classroom, an empty lounge, or the spare meeting room of a nonprofit center. Therapists, collectively speaking—exceptions exist—are just as undervalued as public school teachers. Institutions rarely ever offer counselors enough funding or space.

Reflections Before Sharing Social Commentary

Before public health advocates and officials speak authoritatively on public health matters, self-reflection is a necessary step. Especially when offering critiques publicly, it is essential for one ask themself whether their understanding of the issue at hand is credibly-sourced and well-rounded. To be sure, the advice here is not to avoid or discount lived experience. In the case of critiquing trends in therapy and among therapists, dismissing the personal narratives of non-clinicians would only stifle change, and further disempower and exclude minoritized groups. Their perspectives are certainly critical to repairing legacies of mistrust implanted in marginalized communities by mental health care institutions and social service professionals.

Source: NappyCo/Pexels

With that said, all of us in public health—whether clinicians, community organizers, outreach workers, or peer advocates—have a duty to fact-check our positions and public statements. Otherwise, we risk misinforming or misleading the public, the consequence of which impacts us all, especially in hard times.
