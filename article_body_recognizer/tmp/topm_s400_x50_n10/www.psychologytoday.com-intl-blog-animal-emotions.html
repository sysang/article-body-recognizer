Animal Emotions

Do animals think and feel?

Marc Bekoff Ph.D.

  • TWEET
  • EMAIL

Spain Joins Other Nations in Declaring Animals Are Sentient

While declaring nonhumans to be sentient is surely a move in the right direction, it hasn't yet mattered as much as it could on a large scale.

The Amazing Secret Visual Worlds of 8-Eyed Jumping Spiders

A "spider-centric" view of how these spiders see the world provides a window into their minds and the remarkable ways in which their eight eyes work independently and together.

Social Work Stresses Companionship in the Human-Animal Bond

A clinical social worker explains how an integrative biopsychosocial framework can help people in crisis care for their companion animals who are suffering with them.

How We Can Protect the Future of Whales

In a new book, a veterinarian and marine scientist with years of field experience argues that we're all responsible for saving the whales.

The Late Bernie Rollin: A Man for All Seasons and Reasons

Agree or disagree with "radvocate" Professor Rollin's views on a wide variety of topics, he made people think deeply about complicated issues.

Animals in Irish Society, Anti-Speciesism, and Globalization

A new book shows that more than a land of meat and potatoes, Ireland is a relevant, if overlooked, contributor to Western views on animal welfare.

Enriching the Cognitive, Social, and Emotional Lives of Cats

Companion cats aren't necessarily less social than domesticated dogs, and these remarkable felines also need a lot of love, attention, care, and stimulating social engagements.

Is Treating Abused Animals as Victims Good for the Animals?

An essay called "Animal Rights and the Victimhood Trap" makes the case that focusing on victimhood may distract attention away from the most important sources of animal suffering.

Ecological Reciprocity: A Gentle Mandate for Global Kindness

A new book offers a revolutionary study of the biology of global compassion and cooperation and the importance of tapping into our innate biophilia and ending ethnic disparities.

Should Sentient Insects Be Farmed for Food and Feed?

There is abundant scientific evidence for insect sentience, so is it wrong to farm them for food?

Standing Up to Aging: Science, Longevity, and Better Health

A new book called "Defying Aging" offers a fact-based and easy-to-read guide to empower us to do whatever we can to make aging healthier and easier to accept.

Veterinarians Can Help Promote Animal and Human Well-being

"One Welfare in Practice" stresses interdisciplinary collaboration and the important interconnections among animal welfare, human well-being, and the environment.

What Aesop's Fables Teach Us About Animals

Jo Wimpenny's "Aesop's Animals" asks if scientific facts on topics including tool use, self-recognition, cooperation, and future planning support Aesop's millennia-old fables.

Why Do Dogs Tilt Their Heads?

Head-tilting in dogs is a commonly observed behavior, but little is known about why they do it.

'Kindling' Asks Us to Think Deeply About Empathy and Apathy

In a non-judgmental way, Linnea Ryshke asks deep questions about our circle of moral concerns, our relationships with other animals, and how we deal with cognitive dissonance.

Science and Speculation Say Dogs Would Do Well Without Us

How would dogs find food, have children, and survive along with other animals on a posthuman planet of the dogs?

The Redemption of Yellowstone's Renegade Alpha Wolf 302

Rick McIntyre's new book, the third in a remarkable series about the wolves of Yellowstone, is a stunning true story of an unconventional elder male with a unique personality.

Conservation Dogs Offer Lessons of Hope and Resilience

Isabelle Groc's new kids book tells stories of conservation canines who came from shelters and had no hope of being adopted and will be of interest to adults including teachers.

What Wolves Tell Us about Our Relationship with Nature

Author John Vucetich discusses how his book, "Restoring the Balance," is braided with strands of natural history, memoir, and exposition on humanity’s relationship with nature.

Research Shows Brainy Parrots Have Unique Welfare Needs

A new comparative study offers the first empirical evidence that intelligent captive parrots suffer from poor welfare and that they need increased cognitive stimulation.

What Can Dogs, Coyotes, and Opossums Tell Us about Death?

Do nonhuman animals know "the end" is near or here, or is this human-centric view driven by narrow-minded human exceptionalism?

"Barking at the Moon" Shows Dogs Can Be Emotional Lifesavers

Life with Riley presented writer Tracy Beckerman with numerous challenges as she transitioned from a stay-at-home mom to an empty nester.

All Animals Must Be Safe and Sound During Disasters

A new book using on-the-ground research offers "a comprehensive study of what goes wrong in our disaster response that shows how people can better manage pets in emergencies."

The Broad Relevance of Vegan Ethics in Everyday Life

A new book shows that veganism isn't only about food, but rather a holistic perspective that informs choices we make in numerous situations.

How Animal Law Applies to Many Areas of Mainstream Practice

A new book shows how laws protecting animals intersect with family law, criminal law, wills and estates, and professional liability.

What Is It Like To Be an Acrobatic Arboreal Squirrel?

As squirrels assess travels while frenetically navigating intertwined tree branches, assessments of branch flexibility are six times more important than travel distance.

Pet Nation: The Transformational Power of Dog-Driven Culture

In 'Pet Nation,' we learn that sports, religion, music, schools, and civic engagement all play a role in building social capital, but dogs are the most powerful engine for change.

Puppy Dogs Are More Attracted to Humans Than Are Young Wolves

New research comparing young dogs and wolves shows "dog puppies are 30 times more interested in unknown humans than young wolves," confirming domestication affected dog cognition.

Animals Say "Hi" and "Bye" to Communicate What They Want

Research shows how bonobos and chimpanzees coordinate playing and grooming and form joint commitments so that they, like other animals, can agree on what they want to happen.

The Mind-Boggling, Fascinating Behavior of Insects

A new and beautifully illustrated book offers "an unforgettable tour of the insect world, presenting these amazing creatures as you have never seen them before."
