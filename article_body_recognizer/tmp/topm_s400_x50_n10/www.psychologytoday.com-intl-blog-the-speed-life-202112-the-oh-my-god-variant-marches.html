The Oh-My-God Variant Marches On

Omicron might turn out to be just what we need at this time.

Posted December 9, 2021 | Reviewed by Gary Drevitch

  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Email
Antibodies Attaching SARS-CoV-2
Source: Joseph Mazur

Here is a frightening news headline: “Spike in Omicron Variant Cases Puts Europe on Edge.” What Edge? In that context, the word means brink or threshold, but of what? It warns us of something ominous and lends fear to those who are easily uneasy over COVID-19. So in this post, I’ll bravely suggest that we should shackle the fear coming from media outlets that are not careful with words.

With almost every COVID news article I read, I am stunned to learn that viruses have an edge as if they are scheming to kill, or as if they know how to mutate to evade antibodies, or how to efficiently build spike proteins well enough to bind to ACE2 receptors to enter human cells. SARS-CoV-2 Doesn’t know anything. It has no brain. Accidental mutations could make it very good at spreading without increasing morbidity.

Aris Katzourakis, a viral evolution researcher at the University of Oxford, is quoted in the journal Nature as saying, “If you throw it into the mix in a highly vaccinated population that has given up on other control measures, [SARS-CoV-2] might have the edge there.” What is there about that sentence that should flag a misjudgment of wording? I’m sure Katzourakis did not mean to suggest that SARS-CoV-2 has a plan to gain an edge in the battle of human survival. It misrepresents what we know about mutations of viruses. Viruses have no will and certainly no plan of attack. They survive only by random replications of RNA. As with all such chemical replications, sometimes—though rarely—errors change what it is. It becomes what it is by total accident. Those viruses that survive longest could, by sheer accident, have an “edge,” but, again, only by accident.

What can we say about this new variant? Unfortunately, not much. I am neither a virologist nor epidemiologist but rather a mathematician who analyses risk. Here is what I do know: At least Omicron is not a variant of high consequence, which the CDC labels as having “clear evidence that prevention measures or medical countermeasures (MCMs) have significantly reduced effectiveness relative to previously circulating variants.”

At this moment, Delta is the dominant world variant of COVID-19 and will be for a relatively short time. When SARS-CoV-2 strikes as an Omicron virus (B.1.1.529), a variant of concern, it doesn’t strike as a Delta (B.1.617.2): It can’t be both, and it cannot become both.

Identified in Botswana and South Africa after infecting 10,000 people, it has spread to 50 countries on six continents in less than two weeks. The World Health Organization designated Omicron to be a variant of concern. What is that concern? Mostly, that the rapid rise in South Africa has been explosive. Two weeks ago, there were 3,402 reported cases. Last week there were 8,561 cases, a 152% increase over a relatively short period. That is the concern, because the Omicron variant likely came from 26 new mutations that have never been seen in any other SARS-CoV-2 variant. But it is not one of high consequence, a much, much, much higher worry, according to the CDC.

But hold on. Let’s consider where this alarming upturn is happening. In September, the R-value (epidemiologists' measure an epidemic’s growth) of Delta—the dominant variant—was far below 1. Now that R-value for the same region has almost doubled, which could mean serious concern that this new variant will cause a doubling spread throughout the world. And we know what that means from the mathematical chessboard example: A grain of rice on one square doubling to an adjacent one in continuation very quickly exhausts all the rice in the world long before it reaches the 64th square.

But hold on, once again. Let’s not ring the alarm bells too soon. We don’t know the number of Omicron cases in last week’s 8,561 cases. Computing the R-value for Omicron counts mixed cases. A case of Delta cannot be a case of Omicron and vice versa. It could be true that Omicron spreads much faster than Delta, but we cannot know for sure until we count using sequencing data to distinguish variants.

Omicron is highly contagious, but maybe not so symptomatic: One study suggests it might cause more breakthrough infections but not necessarily more severe illness.

This whole Omicron concern could be dangerous, not because of the power of the virus, but rather because it plays with the psyche to inadvertently promote fright. It could very well be that Omicron is the answer to our problems. As I said, SARS-CoV-2 does not come in two flavors. When one gets COVID-19, it would be of one variant, not two. So, what if Omicron has an R-value less than 1, and we are witnessing a spike in South Africa simply because South Africa is just 20% vaccinated? Or, to put it another way, what if Omicron’s R-value is greater than 1 but has a lower level of morbidity? Now we have a better situation. It means that Omicron’s spreading power will overcome Delta’s. And that is a good thing because it will lower COVID’s morbidity rate.

There could come a time when a variant (remember: mutations have no attack plans) accidentally gives immunity to the dominant variant (whatever that might be) through a build-up of antibodies and also has an R-value larger than 1. Let’s hope so. In that case, the dominant variant will disappear as long as it doesn’t have time and bodies for enough mutations to create a new, more virulent one. Let’s hope for that too.

Pfizer just released a preliminary laboratory study demonstrating that three doses of its Pfizer-BioNTech COVID-19 Vaccine neutralize Omicron. So, until we know much more about Omicron, we should practice good social behavior, following CDC guidelines, and not be so frightened by the new variant on the block.
