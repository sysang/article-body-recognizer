What to do in your 20s, 30s and 40s to retire as a millionaire

RETIREMENT planning is not everyone's favourite topic but retiring as a millionaire can be a lot easier when you know where to start.

When it comes to saving for retirement, the earlier one starts the easier it is for them to grow some considerable wealth in time for their old age. However, that doesn’t mean that starting retirement planning in one’s 30s or 40s dooms them to a less than comfortable pension income, here’s what to do in your 30s and 40s to retire as a millionaire.

The current economic climate is not exactly in the favour of savers as interest rates tank out at almost a quarter of the inflation rate. 

However, there has never been a better time to be in debt because of the very same interest rates, meaning that borrowers should take this opportunity to pay off as much of their debt as possible. 

Many financial experts will recommend that before looking to build substantial growth, one should look to pay off as much of their debt as possible as these will become more of a financial drain as time goes on. 

Once these debts have had a significant chunk repaid, one can begin looking to their savings and it is advisable for consumers to create an emergency savings fund before anything else.

This will ensure they are less likely to pinch from their pension pots in times of need.

Now technically, starting at the age of 30 with absolutely no savings and aiming to retire as a millionaire at the current state pension age will cost one roughly £17,000 per year until their retirement according to Monevator’s Millionaire Calculator. 

This increases dramatically to £26,000 in savings every year for those currently at age 40. 

For many, saving over £1,000 every month is simply not possible in their current circumstances, which is why CNBC shared five steps to help those in their 30s and 40s retire as millionaires. 

Multiple income streams 

Investors are often well-versed with the concept of diversification, an act which spreads their investment out into a range of products so as to mitigate the effects of a single market crash.

However, this can be translated outside of the investing realm too; by diversifying one’s income through multiple income streams they are not only receiving additional income but also ensuring that if one stream suddenly ends they won’t be financially distressed. 

Multiple income streams are usually seen as side hustles, freelancing gigs or side businesses that one takes on outside of their normal working hours. 

But asking for a raise, negotiating a higher salary when applying for one’s next job or starting investing are also examples of multiple income streams.

Increase contributions

Many financial experts agree that there is no point in getting extra money and creating multiple income streams if one uses the majority of it a few days after they are paid.

Earning more in one’s 30s and 40s should translate directly into saving more and increasing their retirement contributions. 

The earlier they do this the more likely they are to benefit from compounding interest, especially if they find themselves with a bonus or large paycheque that can be contributed as a lump sum. 

Be open with money conversations

Anyone in a serious relationship or a partnership where finances are shared or communal should set aside time to talk about money with the other parties concerned.

Learning each other’s financial habits and goals enables the entire group to create a financial plan or budget that will benefit all involved.

Although awkward, these conversations can go far in helping to relieve any financial tension or issues in the relationship as well.

Live within means

One commonality between the majority of the world’s richest is their frugality. Business mogul Warren Buffett is the prime example of this as despite having a multi billion dollar net worth, he still stays in the same $31,500 house that he bought in 1958. 

While it’s natural for living expenses to increase as one gets older due to big milestone purchases such as cars and houses, one should try their best to set and stay within a reasonable budget. 

Likewise, thinking twice about upgrading their living situation now is vital as it could cost their comfortable retirement in the future.

Teach financial education

Having children is one of the easiest ways to see money slipping through one’s fingers, but simply explaining the basics of money management and good financial habits can go a long way in not only saving one’s own finances but their children’s as well.
