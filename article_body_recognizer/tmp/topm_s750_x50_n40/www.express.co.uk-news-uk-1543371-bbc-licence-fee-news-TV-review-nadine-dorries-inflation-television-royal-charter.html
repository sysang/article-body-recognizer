BBC licence fee at risk as Nadine Dorries orders review on hated levy in weeks

BBC licence fees will be reviewed this month by Culture and Media Secretary Nadine Dorries as the corporation prepares for a reduction in funding from tax-payer money and is forced to tighten its belt.

The senior minister will be holding talks with directors from the BBC to determine the cost of the licence fee over the next five years, which currently stands at £159 per year. It is believed that any price increase will sit below inflation rates, but is still expected to rise prior to the royal charter in 2027. However, with the price rise likely to remain below inflation rates, this will essentially spell a budget cut for the corporation at a time where the BBC is under huge financial pressure.

Many consumers in the UK are frustrated at the licence fee, which is seen by many as a forced tax, and a recent poll produced worrying results for the corporation affectionately known as “Auntie.”

The poll by Savanta ComRes for the Defund the BBC campaign group found that two-thirds of people would support a nationwide vote on the corporation's funding, while only 13 percent said they would not support the plan.

Many were also appalled by the poor variety of listings over the Christmas period.

The poll also found that 49 percent of viewers say the BBC's Christmas Day schedule, with the inevitable screenings of Call The Midwife and Mrs Brown's Boys, is poor value for money, with only 23 percent saying it offered a good return on the £159 licence fee.

Voters were also asked their views on whether failing to pay the licence fee should be a criminal offence. Just over half of adults (53 percent) said it shouldn't, with only 38 percent supporting continued criminalisation.

With the cuts, the BBC will now be forced to make every penny count over the next five years.

Executives at the corporation believe they will need to make savings of around £1billion over the next five years.

With inflation set to rise to around 5 percent according to the Bank of England, BBC bosses suggest that this will add a further £1billion loss to the organisation.

Recent changes in TV viewing patterns by the public has also caused the BBC to feel the pinch.

Online giants Amazon Prime and Netflix have pushed up the cost of making their own shows by as much as 10 percent in order to provide big-budget dramas and serials to their audience, making the companies ever more popular with their audiences.

For the BBC, the inevitable cuts to funding will mean more repeats and less original content.

BBC News will also have to make bigger cuts after missing its £87million savings target by £12million.

Speaking to The Times, John Nicolson, an SNP MP who sits on the Commons culture select committee, said the Government must shoulder some responsibility for the BBC slashing spending as a result of the licence fee deal in 2015.

He said: "Conservatives tried to blame the BBC for the subsequent cuts, which is very dishonest because they knew at the time the deal was imposed what it would result in."

Pensioners have also shown huge discontent at the BBC after free licences were scrapped.

Conservative MP for Kettering, Philip Hollobone said the BBC “abolishing free TV licences show they cannot be trusted”.

He described the BBC’s treatment of pensioners as “disgraceful” - and called for the Government to use the 2022 funding review “to scrap the licence fee altogether”.

Ms Dorries said the future of the licence fee will be decided at the charter renewal in 2027 but said “those discussions … both from the editorial perspective, and a number of layers and recently highlighted during the response to the Serota review, are in all in consideration moving forward. His comments have been noted”.

In light of the poll which found many wanting a referendum on the licence fee, the BBC disagreed.

A BBC spokesperson said: “Our own research paints a very different picture from this snap poll, with the Licence Fee the preferred way of funding the BBC, over advertising and subscription.”

They added: “We offer great value to every Licence Fee payer and the last 12 months has seen the BBC deliver huge audiences and critical praise for a range of shows and content, including on most of today's front pages.”
