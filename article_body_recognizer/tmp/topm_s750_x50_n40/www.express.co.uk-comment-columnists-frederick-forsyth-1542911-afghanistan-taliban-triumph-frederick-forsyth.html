Taliban's speedy triumph indicates they are not just a minority, says FREDERICK FORSYTH

IT IS a sad conclusion and one I hesitate to make. But wandering the world and studying its countries and peoples it is hard to avoid.

There seem to be some people of whom one must say that no matter what the blessings a benign fate is able to pour upon them they will somehow always blow it, opting for futile war, hunger and poverty rather than accepting disappointment and getting on with life. One such people always seem to be the Palestinians. Back in 1948 it was the United Nations – not the British – who voted to allocate a small part of the former Ottoman Empire into a national home for the world’s Jews.

Taliban fighters walk toward journalists during a protest in Kabul on December 28, 2021 (Image: Getty)

The resident Arabs were outraged and following the urgings of the Grand Mufti of Jerusalem went to war. They lost resoundingly and have been losing ever since. Among the trendy Left there is huge sympathy for them which goes on and on. But nearly two million Arabs live in Israel with full civil rights and are well represented in the Knesset.

Scores of times since then there have been chances to make peace and develop Palestine into a viable, nay prosperous, repub-lic co-existing with Israel in peace and harmony. On each, usually following the leader-ship of the now-dead Yasser Arafat, they chose hopeless war.

EVERY dollar of the millions donated was spent not on invest-ments to build a state with a rich economy but on weapons to be fired pointlessly across the border into Israel. They launched three more wars and lost them all badly, forgoing more land each time. But why? The land is fertile, the rivers flow, the climate is glorious. A peaceful, tourist-rich Palestine could be so affluent.

Instead they live in poverty – and neighbouring Gaza is even worse – spending all they have on weapons, their diet hatred, fomented by one rotten leader after another. I only mention this dreary tale because Afghanistan is in the same tragic category. For 20 years an array of countries – fellow-Muslim nations as well as the Western alliance – has been trying to transform Afghanistan. But the blistering speed of the recent triumph of the Taliban in taking over indicates these merciless fanatics are not just a freak minority.

Now a land which could prosper in peace seems to be lapsing back into tyranny, violence and war. And these three have offspring – poverty, suffering and hunger. The reports do not lie. Famine seems to be sweeping the country. Up to a mil-lion children are threatened with death by malnutrition this winter unless aid pours in. And who will send it, if at all? Not Russia which, as under the Czars and in 1979 under the Soviets, invaded. Not China which carries out genocide on its own Uighur Muslims.

No, the aid will come from the West as ever. But why do indigenous peo-ples do this to themselves? Solve that and half the strife of the world would wither away. But not, one fears, in anyone’s lifetime. The cycle of self-inflicted misery just goes on and on. And yet in our own safe, wealthy island the stupid fret themselves into panic over a bronchial disease for which vaccinations are massively available. Only our papers and TV sets reveal to us how lucky we are. That said, we and our forefathers have earned it. Let us not now throw it away.

Putin must be laughing at our stupidity

RATHER as this page predicted, gas supplies from Russia have slowed to a trickle, sending prices through the roof with consequent damage to our western economies right across the board. There is nothing inevitable in this. Much of it comes from Russia via Germany. Years ago, Angela Merkel abolished her country’s nuclear-powered generation industry, making the nation dependent on Moscow for its prosperity.

Our shortages are the off-spin. It takes a special form of stupidity to create a chopping block, lay your neck on it and hand the axe to your worst enemy. But that’s what she did. Analysts think the shortages and unaffordable prices will go on until 2023 at least. Will we still have an economy by then? Thank you, Angela. We left the EU in order to become again fully sovereign, but no country can be that if its crucial energy source or sources depend on others, and never its enemies.

We have simply got to jettison costly white elephants like HS2 and make a massive effort, no matter what the price, to create energy generation that we completely control. It can be done but it will take drive and steely will. Alas, I perceive none at the moment.

Education seems to get worse, by degrees

AS ONE who never went to university – I actually chose the RAF instead – I must admit I have never missed it. Back then journalism was a career that never required it either. So the charms of three years boozing and puffing pot eluded me. Over the years it became plainer and plainer that a degree was becoming something you needed to be qualified to

shovel gravel. Then in 1992 John Major really hit the jackpot. He converted all our polytechnics into universities. Now it has become really weird. You can get a degree in golf course management and hairdressing, not to mention the post of a minor jobsworth in the town hall. My old 1954 A-levels are beginning to look seriously academic!

IT MUST be the passing years but much as the CO and I enjoyed the jollity and celebrations of the Christmas period it is such a relief to get back to normal. Normal as in the papers being on the mat when one gets up at the normal hour. To go down to the pub for lunch as normal, dressed like a normal human being rather than something that fell off a decorated pine tree.

In old age, routines are comforting, reassuring. So although we wish all and sundry a terrific time tonight, with wild cheering as the old unlamented 2021 departs, we will be turning in at the normal hour after a celebratory snifter of the good stuff for a normal night’s sleep. In departing, let me wish each and every one of you all that you wish for yourselves in what surely has to be a better year than last.

THE shortest day of the year may be behind us but real winter has hardly started, along with all the damage its frosts will do to our roads. But in my neck of the undergrowth the potholes are so deep that if you shout a greeting down one a voice replies: “G’day, cobber.” The only people who love it are the artisans who repair the suspension systems.
