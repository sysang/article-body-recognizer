U.K. Soccer Stadiums Design for Safer Standing at Games

Five British soccer clubs will test sections designed to safely satisfy spectators’ desire to stand and cheer

By

Katie Deighton

  • Biography
  • @DollyDeighton
  • katie.deighton@wsj.com
Nov. 19, 2021 2:08 pm ET

British soccer teams are poised to begin a test that could allow passionate fans to once again stand up during matches without breaking the rules. The U.K. government is backing a trial of “safe standing” areas, in which new railings separate rows to prevent fans from falling forward.

The government in 1990 mandated that top-level games be played in all-seated stadiums, a reaction to the 1989 Hillsborough disaster in which 97 soccer fans died as a result of a crush in standing-only pens at a stadium in Sheffield. Nearly every club in the top two leagues of English and Welsh soccer converted their stadiums’ standing areas, which often comprised nothing more than a set of concrete bleachers, into seated areas by the mid-1990s, and began telling fans to stay in those seats.

But adding seats and rules did not compel all fans to stop standing or eliminate the possibility that they could topple onto spectators below them, causing injury or even a phenomenon called progressive crowd collapse, whereby the forward fall of one person from the back leads to a crushing domino effect.

“The analogy we always use is, when you go to church and sing your hymns, you stand up,” said Peter Daykin, a board member of the Football Supporters’ Association and coordinator of its Safe Standing campaign. “There’s kind of a visceral enjoyment in that for some people.”

Now, just over 20 years since the so-called all-seater legislation was passed, the teams and authorities that regulate soccer in England and Wales have designed stadium sections that account for fans’ natural game day behavior, rather than trying to control it.

Five clubs—Cardiff City, Chelsea, Manchester United, Manchester City and Tottenham Hotspur—will begin formally testing the approach in January. Some teams in the trial have already installed safety railings in areas of their stadiums known for persistent standing.

There is no Hillsborough-style tragedy prompting the British test; so far, only minor incidents have occurred as a result of people falling from open seated areas, said Ken Scott, head of inspectorate at the Sports Grounds Safety Authority, or SGSA, the U.K. government’s official adviser on safety at sports grounds.

Approaches to stadium standing vary outside the U.K. German stadiums such as Borussia Dortmund’s Signal Iduna Park have operated standing zones for decades. In the U.S., a handful of Major League Soccer teams have recently built areas designed to encourage standing, seeking the energetic atmosphere generated by fans on their feet. The teams have installed railings for safety and some have either removed seats or locked them upright in these sections.

In addition to heading off potential accidents, the U.K.’s test also represents British soccer’s broader strategy to prioritize the fan experience more than it did in the past, Mr. Scott said.

“Going to football matches in the U.K. 20, 30 years ago wasn’t necessarily a pleasurable experience at many of these places because customer service didn’t really feature highly on the agenda,” Mr. Scott said. “Now we’re following very much the example of the American spectator experience, looking at things like the Super Bowl and seeing how it’s very much fan-driven.”

Unlike the standing sections of a previous era, the standing spaces being tested include fold-down seats, like those in movie theaters, to accommodate fans who want or need to sit during parts of a game. Computer modeling using virtual crash test dummies of differing sizes led the SGSA to recommend a rail height between 800 and 900 millimeters, equivalent to between 31.5 and 35.4 inches.

“It stops you from going over the top if you did suffer a force from behind, but equally it means that if you wish to sit and watch the match, the rail is still at a height that doesn’t obstruct your vision,” Mr. Scott said.

The SGSA said its priority will be to monitor the safety of fans during the trial period and work with the clubs involved to identify and resolve any issues. In addition to safety, Manchester United said it would assess fan feedback and the contribution to the stadium atmosphere, while Cardiff City said it would measure elements including compliance with the rules and the customer experience of those standing.

The test will finish at the end of the British soccer season in late May, when the SGSA will submit a report on its results to the government.

“I fully expect that following that report, they’ll get to roll this out to a wider base of clubs,” Mr. Scott said. “Safe standing is one of the first times I can remember that we’ve ever got something significant across the line that hasn’t required a tragedy to get us to that point.”
