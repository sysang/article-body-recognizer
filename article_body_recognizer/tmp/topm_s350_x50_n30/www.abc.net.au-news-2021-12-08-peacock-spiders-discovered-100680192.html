Two new species of peacock spiders discovered in Western Australia

Two new species of the tiny peacock spider have been discovered in the south-west of Western Australia.

Key points:

  • Two new species of peacock spider discovered in Western Australia in the last two months
  • The tiny maratus spiders are the size of a grain of rice.
  • Spider expert says it's changed views on how many species remain undiscovered

Spider-enthusiasts Flynn Prall and James McMulkin spend much of their free time combing through the bush, searching for a glimpse of these colourful little critters known as maratus spiders. 

"We found a few undescribed species this season and a few new colourmorphs… but we really wanted to find a brand new species of peacock spider," Mr Prall said.

The first new species was discovered two months ago in Capel, and the second just two weeks ago near Mt Romance, north of Denmark.

No larger than a grain of rice, these minuscule spiders are not easy to spot.

In order to get good footage of them, the pair started searching early in the morning, often camping in the area the night before.

"We look for changes in vegetation, anything different… scan the ground, anything we can see," Mr Prall said.

Colourful male display of 'crazy colours'

"They're these tiny four millimetre jumping spiders, and the males only live a year," Mr Prall said.

When they discovered the newest species, Mr Prall and Mr McMulkin sent their photos through to biologist and peacock spider expert, Jurgen Otto who confirmed the species had not been previously identified.

"I would say it is the least coloured species that I have come across and I wonder whether the female spider sees the same," said Dr Otto.

"Maybe she sees a lot more, so the question becomes what is it she sees and what are the mechanisms. Are there patterns that are visible in the UV spectrum, or polarised light?"

Until recently, Dr Otto believed that most species of peacock spiders had been discovered.

However, these new findings have prompted him to reconsider.

"This changes my opinion a bit… in particular the south-west seems to be a gift that keeps on giving… the diversity there seems almost endless," said Dr Otto.

It was sentiment echoed by Mr Prall, who said he felt incredibly lucky to live in the area, and was grateful to be able to show the softer side of spiders to the world.

"That's all it's about, understanding."
