Why is the world obsessed with Rebel Wilson's weight loss?

ABC Everyday
/ By Yasmin Jeffery

Warning: This article mentions eating disorders, disordered eating and restrictive eating practices.

Rebel Wilson revealed she wanted to "physically transform and change her life" last year.

In the months since, the actress has documented this on her Instagram, sharing active moments, "before" shots and beyond.

The tabloids and the glossy magazines have reported on it all as if by compulsion.

Headlines have described her weight loss as "stunning" and entire articles have been written about how much she weighs.

The glowing headlines about Wilson's body are new. The focus? Not so much

"It's fascinating, why are people so obsessed with it?" Wilson questioned in a recent chat with the BBC about her weight loss when she was named one of their 100 Women of 2021.

"With women in particular, about their looks?"

Wilson may have recently shared a great deal of this stuff herself, but the public obsession with her body and how it looks has been a thing pretty much since she became famous.

Throughout her career, she's been known as the "funny fat girl" (that's her own descriptor).

Her character on Pitch Perfect was literally called "Fat Amy", for starters.

So what's up with the long-running focus with Wilson's body and why is it so frenzied right now?

Why the world is obsessed with celebrities' bodies and the weight loss 'journey'

To Alex Cowen, The Butterfly Foundation's communications manager, the reason all of this is happening comes down to the fact "we live in a society that is underpinned by diet culture".

"Diet culture tells us that thin = 'good' and fat = 'bad'," she says.

Ms Cowen explains this medley is what prompts the social expectation of weight loss. She says it's also responsible for the negative perception of people in larger bodies, and explains the acceptance of this allows for things like fat jokes to be seen as "OK".

Celebrities can feel the need to change their bodies as a result of diet culture because they're "victims of it just as much as we are," Ms Cowen continues.

"They experience fatphobia and weight stigma as much as the next person — if not more, because of the amount of attention they receive."

What to remember when you come across this talk online

Of course, there are many reasons people may need to change their exercise or eating habits (like medical reasons), resulting in changes to their appearance. Diet culture is just one.

And there are many ways celebrities communicate some, all, or none of this, with the world. For now at least, Wilson is publicly documenting hers and talking about it in frequent interviews.

Jonah Hill, on the other hand, has said he doesn't want anyone to talk about his body, ever.

Loading

"I know you mean well but I kindly ask that you not comment on my body <3 good or bad I want to politely let you know it's not helpful and doesn't feel good," the actor recently posted on Instagram.

Adele, meanwhile, seems resigned to the fact people will talk about her body, telling Oprah Winfrey in an interview she isn't shocked or fazed by it because "my body has been objectified my entire career".

But the singer, who's recently made similar headlines to Wilson, made a conscious decision not to share her "journey".

"People are shocked because I didn't," she told Vogue in October.

"They're used to people documenting everything on Instagram … I did it for myself and not anyone else. So why would I ever share it? I don't find it fascinating. It's my body."

Regardless of the celeb whose weight-loss journey you find yourself following (because reporting of it is unavoidable and it's everywhere on social media), Ms Cowen suggests taking a beat to consider one thing.

"We need a level of literacy to understand that advice [celebrities] may be giving out — whether direct or indirect — isn't necessarily coming from a qualified person," Ms Cowen says.

It's OK if you feel impacted by any of this stuff.

Ms Cowen says the Butterfly Foundation would recommend reaching out to your GP or a mental health professional if you feel affected.

For help working out when it might be a good idea to do that, this piece has some expert advice.

And Ms Cowen has one nice thing to leave you with.

"You're perfect just the way you are — we don't need to change or improve ourselves because of what society says," she says.
