David Warner's no-ball dismissal by Ben Stokes at the Gabba probably could have been avoided

By Jon Healy

Ben Stokes wrecked David Warner's stumps with just his fourth ball of the first Ashes Test, only for the Australian opener to be called back to the crease.

Key points:

  • Warner was on just 17 when Stokes hit his stumps and reached lunch just two runs shy of his half-century
  • Warner has gone on to score four centuries after being dismissed by no balls in Tests
  • Former umpire Simon Taufel told Channel Seven the third umpire is supposed to check every delivery

Stokes had bowled a no ball. Rotten luck, but perhaps not just luck. 

Television cameras picked up that Stokes had overstepped on every one of his deliveries to that point.

If bowlers are called for no balls every time they overstep, they can adjust their run-up or delivery stride, but if they are not told, often the news is not broken to them until they have taken a wicket.

Former Australia fast bowler Mitchell Johnson acknowledged on ABC Sport Grandstand radio that getting called for a no ball on every one of those deliveries would be irritating for Stokes, but said it could have helped him fix his issue.

"I think through the 2013/14 [Ashes] series, every wicket that I got you had to wait for the no-ball call. And that gets really frustrating for the bowler."

The International Cricket Council (ICC) recently introduced shifted responsibility for calling front-foot no balls to the third umpire, but only one of Stokes's was caught.

"If it's someone upstairs who's supposed to be checking these, and they haven't decided that any of those are a no ball, it's pathetic officiating as far as I'm concerned," former Test captain Ricky Ponting said on Channel Seven.

"If he had been called for a no ball the first one he bowled, then of course he's going to drag his foot back."

Former umpire and member of the ICC's elite umpire panel Simon Taufel said the third umpire is supposed to be checking every ball.

"I really can't explain as to why they weren't checked," he said on Seven.

"They do have the support of an ICC technician in that third umpire's box. Together with that technician they're supposed to be looking at every ball and if it's close, they'll examine it and call it."

Cricket Australia revealed the answer later in the day, confirming the technology had been down all day and they had reverted to leaving the no-ball calls in the hands of the on-field umpires, with only specific deliveries able to be checked using the broadcast cameras.

The bad news for England, already on the canvas after being dismissed for 147 on day one, is Warner's track record after being dismissed by illegitimate deliveries.

Warner was clean bowled off no balls by India's Varun Aaron in 2014 and Pakistan's Wahab Riaz in 2016.

Umpires also broke the hearts of England's Tom Curran on Boxing Day 2017 and Pakistan's Naseem Shah in 2019 — both of whom thought they had Warner as their first Test scalps, only for the no ball to be belatedly called.

On all four of those instances, Warner went on to make three figures.

This time, he was bowled for 17. The wait continues to see just how many runs he scores.
