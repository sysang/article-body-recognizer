John Lennon died at height of the Cold War when a Soviet defector sought refuge in Sydney Harbour

By Jason Dasey

On the day John Lennon was shot dead in New York, a Soviet sailor jumped ship in Sydney Harbour, following in the footsteps of the so-called Red Bikini Girl the year before.

Key points:

  • December 8 is the 41st anniversary of John Lennon's murder in the US
  • The 40-year-old was shot dead on the same day that a Soviet seaman jumped ship in Sydney Harbour
  • While The Beatles never visited the Soviet Union, they wrote Back In The USSR at the height of the Cold War 

It was December 8, 1980, and the impact of the Cold War was being felt on both sides of the globe.

When Lennon was gunned down outside the Dakota Apartments in Manhattan, it came at the end of a year that saw his adopted nation boycott the 1980 Moscow Olympics, with Australia partially supporting the ban.

In Sydney, 43-year-old Soviet seaman Victor Dudko walked off his ship, Novopolotsk, when it was docked at Sydney Harbour and sought refuge in the city's Russian community after a blazing row with his captain.

The previous year, 18-year-old cruise ship worker Liliana Gasinskaya made an even more brazen escape, leaping through a porthole of the SS Leonid Sobinov — wearing nothing but a red bikini — and swimming ashore to Pyrmont.

Days after Dudko's escape, the two would meet and compare notes about leaving the Soviet Union behind for a new country they knew little about. Having been granted political asylum, Liliana became the first cover girl for Australian Penthouse magazine in October 1979 — posing without her red bikini.

I broke the story about the second Soviet defection as a teenage reporter for The Sydney Morning Herald, hearing whispers at the Maritime Services Board at Circular Quay that a Russian had risked it all to be with his secret Australian girlfriend. 

But, just weeks earlier, as a Beatles tragic, I had dreamt of an even bigger story: Flying to the US to interview Lennon about his acclaimed musical comeback after a five-year hiatus.

Lennon and defector share front page

Little did I know that my first front-page article would share column inches with news of the death of one of my musical heroes and I would have to fight back tears to write it.

The recent release of Peter Jackson's Get Back — re-editing previously unused footage from the Let It Be film to create a three-part series on Disney+ — is helping create a new generation of Beatles fans.

And, similarly, Lennon's Double Fantasy album, in collaboration with his wife Yoko Ono — and Paul McCartney's post-Wings release of the innovative McCartney II album, featuring Coming Up — brought the Beatles back into focus in 1980.

With the two main men of the group rediscovering their musical feet — and reportedly becoming friends again after years of antipathy — could there be the possibility of a Beatles' reunion?

Shot dead after studio session for Yoko

That dream was shattered on the evening of December 8, when Lennon and Ono returned in their limousine to the Dakota from the Record Plant studios where they had been mixing Ono's Walking on Thin Ice. 

Exiting the car on 72nd Street, Lennon noticed Beatles fan Mark David Chapman, to whom he had given an autograph earlier in the day. Seconds later, Chapman drew his gun and fired five shots into Lennon from 3 metres away.

Despite desperate efforts to revive him, Lennon was pronounced dead at 11:15pm at the nearby Roosevelt Hospital.

The news broke in Australia on the afternoon of December 9 — a Tuesday — just as I was wrapping up my interview with Mr Dudko, whom I found hiding with a Russian family in Sydney's south-western suburbs.

With the help of a translator, the divorcee explained he hadn't defected to be with an Australian girlfriend, but to escape the repression of the Soviet Union, tearfully leaving behind two school-age children in the port city of Saint Petersburg.

I was riding high on adrenaline as I headed back to the Sydney Morning Herald offices with notebook in hand, but my mood changed when I saw Brett, one of the newspaper's copy boys, sobbing face down on a desk of our Broadway office.

"Look at this," he said, holding up a special edition of our afternoon tabloid, The Sun, with the banner headline 'Beatle John Lennon Shot Dead'.

My immediate reaction was thinking it was one of those dummy newspapers, the kind you could buy at theme parks to create any kind of fictitious story. Surely, this couldn't be true?

I looked around the newsroom, and gauging the sombre mood, I quickly realised it was not a prank but confirmation of the most shocking and violent end for a musical legend. 

A growing appreciation for the Beatles

I enjoyed the Beatles in real-time as a child — my parents took me to the screening of the Yellow Submarine movie and Ob-La-Di Ob-La-Da was my favourite song in primary school.

But I'm too young to remember their only Australian tour in 1964. And the so-called summer of love of 1967 — when the Beatles released their influential Sgt. Pepper Lonely Hearts Club album — was spent in the kindergarten playground playing with Tonka toys and eating fairy bread sandwiches.

Like most late Boomers, my appreciation for the Beatles grew as a teenager in the 70s. Just weeks before Lennon's murder, I watched the Let It Be film in an arthouse cinema in Sydney's inner-west.

Lennon was found face down in the reception area of the Dakota and driven to hospital in the back of a New York police car.

One of Chapman's bullets lodged itself in Lennon's aorta, next to his heart, and medical staff at the Roosevelt Hospital unsuccessfully tried to resuscitate him for more than 20 minutes.

Typing up my defector story with senior colleague Ben Sandilands — this was the pre-computer area — I found it hard to concentrate. My emotions bubbled over as I almost refused to accept the reality of Lennon's death.

The defector, Mr Dudko, was starting a new life in Australia full of hope, freedom and optimism, just as the life of one of my musical heroes ended in the US.

Defector becomes model and soap star

The life of Liliana Gasinskaya — the Red Bikini Girl — had prospered since she started the trend of Australia's Soviet defectors in January 1979.

She was granted refugee status within four days of her defection and, apart from her high-profile Penthouse photoshoot — for which she was reportedly paid $15,000 — the Ukrainian appeared in episodes of the Young Doctors TV soap and was a professional disco dancer and DJ.

Eyebrows were raised during her meeting with Mr Dudko when she revealed that she had returned to the Soviet Union on holiday earlier that year, to the nation that had supposedly persecuted her. 

Today she lives in London after her marriage to an Australian property developer fell apart in the late 1980s, according to the UK's Daily Mail.

Back in 1980, the front page of the next day's Sydney Morning Herald — December 10 — had more on the Lennon assassination, "How an autograph-hunter killed Lennon'" above my story, "Exclusive: the defector talks'", with a photograph of Mr Dudko earnestly walking through the surf at Coogee Beach, hands in pockets and his jeans rolled up.

Like Ms Gasinskaya, Mr Dudko would be allowed to stay in Australia. In 1981, the then-minister of immigration and ethnic affairs, Ian McPhee, granted him permanent immigration status.

Mr Dudko would land a job as a sheet metal worker at the Peters Ice Cream factory in the Sydney suburb of Seven Hills.

As the so-called new Australians enjoyed an unfamiliar summer Christmas in 1980, the world ached from the Lennon loss, including his former bandmates.

A stunned McCartney called it "a drag", and guitarist George Harrison said it was "an outrage that people can take other people's lives when they obviously haven't got their own lives in order", before releasing a tribute song, All Those Years Ago.

Back in the Soviet Union, news of Lennon's murder warranted only a three-paragraph story in the December 10 edition of the daily newspaper, Trud.

However, around 200 to 300 fans quietly paid their respects to Lennon at Moscow State University, Radio Free Europe reported.

The Cold War had ensured that only snippets of the Beatles and Lennon's music made it through the Iron Curtain.

But, for me, Lennon's death will always be entwined with the former Soviet Union.

For that, the Beatles' 1968 composition, Back In The USSR — albeit mostly penned by McCartney — holds special significance whenever I think of the day that the genius of John Winston Ono Lennon left us.
