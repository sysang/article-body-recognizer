No, the ingredients in the Pfizer vaccine are not 'illegal' in Australia for kids under 16

CoronaCheck is RMIT ABC Fact Check's weekly email newsletter dedicated to fighting the misinformation infodemic surrounding the coronavirus outbreak.

You can read the latest edition below, and subscribe to have the next newsletter delivered straight to your inbox.

CoronaCheck #95

With the Pfizer COVID-19 vaccine having been approved for kids this week, we debunk claims that it contains an ingredient illegal for children under the age of 16.

We also revisit claims about athletes and the vaccine, and fact check Scott Morrison on emissions reductions.

No 'illegal' ingredients in Pfizer vaccine for kids

The Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) approved the Pfizer vaccine for Australian children aged five to 11 this week, a move backed by Health Minister Greg Hunt.

Loading

"They have made a careful, thorough assessment, [and have] determined it is safe and effective and it is in the interests of children, and Australians, for children five to 11 to be vaccinated," Mr Hunt said.

Following the announcement, some social media users posted claims concerning an ingredient in the Pfizer vaccine, tromethamine, saying it was "illegal" in Australia to give it to children under the age of 16 and that it had potential side-effects for young people.

"With the jabs about to be rolled out for 5-11yos I'm trying to raise awareness of the new ingredient Pfizer have included — tromethamine," one post, purporting to be authored by a critical care nurse, reads.

"It's a very strong NSAID [non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug] that we used to give adults for a severe flare up of back pain in [the Emergency Department], but we haven't used it in a few years. It's currently illegal in Aus[tralia] for kids under age 16."

However, while tromethamine is in the vaccines, neither is it an NSAID nor is it illegal in Australia.

Tromethamine, also known as trometamol and tris, is listed (as trometamol) as an ingredient in both the Moderna and Pfizer vaccines currently distributed to those aged 12 and over in Australia, as well as those set to be distributed to younger children.

According to the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, tromethamine is an ingredient which, along with other salt, sugar, acid stabilisers and acid, "work[s] together to help keep the vaccine molecules stable while the vaccine is manufactured, frozen, shipped, and stored until it is ready to be given to a vaccine recipient."

While the ingredient is also used as a blood acid reducer used in cardiac bypass surgical procedures, tromethamine was added to the vaccines as a means of ensuring it has a longer shelf life.

Colin Pouton, a professor at the Faculty of Pharmacy and Pharmaceutical Sciences at Monash University in Melbourne, told Fact Check in an email that tromethamine was a buffer salt used in the vaccine to maintain the product at physiological pH.

"It is used in many solution products, [like] injections," he wrote.

"Tromethamine is generally regarded as being inactive and safe. There could be very rare side-effects, as with all ingredients in medications."

As to whether it was illegal to give the ingredient to children under the age of 16, a spokeswoman for the TGA told Fact Check such claims were "both false and misleading".

"Tromethamine, otherwise known as trometamol or TRIS, is not illegal in Australia," the spokeswoman said in an email, adding that its use in vaccines did not "have a pharmacological effect as claimed in the social media posts".

"In the case of the Pfizer COVID-19 vaccine for 5-11 years, the TRIS buffer enables the vaccine to be more stable at refrigerated temperatures."

Professor Pouton theorised that the social media post could be confusing tromethamine with ketorolac trometamol, which was "an NSAID which is given as a salt of tromethamine" for which "the active ingredient is ketorolac".

Consumer information supplied by the makers of ketorolac trometamol warns against administering the medication to children.

"The safety and effectiveness in children under 16 have not been established," it reads.

Meanwhile, claims that tromethamine was added to COVID-19 vaccines in order to reduce the risk of heart attack have been found to be false by fact checkers at Reuters, Full Fact, the Associated Press and AAP.

Death of Scottish rugby player, AFL training collapse, spark fresh vaccine claims

The sudden death of a 26-year-old rugby player in Scotland and a fainting episode at an AFL training session have led to a new wave of inaccurate claims linking the deaths and illnesses of athletes to COVID-19 vaccines.

In Scotland, it was announced that Siobhan Cattigan had died on November 26. Neither the sport's national governing body, Scottish Rugby, nor Cattigan's club, Stirling County, cited a cause in statements announcing her death.

Anti-vaccine social media users, however, quickly and incorrectly linked the tragedy to COVID-19 vaccines.

In Australia, the Facebook page of Rod Culleton's Great Australian Party posted an article about Cattigan's passing alongside comments implying that the rugby player was "another sportsman… or sportswoman" to die in a "pandemic of the vaccinated".

Such claims have prompted Scottish Rugby to confirm to the UK Telegraph that Cattigan's death "had nothing to do with COVID-19 nor the vaccine", with the governing body reportedly working to have what it described as "despicable" posts removed from social media.

Meanwhile, in Australia, Melbourne AFL player Jake Lever was reported to have fainted at a training session on Monday during a "tough running drill".

Loading

On social media, posts about Lever's collapse were flooded with comments linking the incident to vaccines.

"Nothing to see here, just another super fit sports professional collapsing out of the blue," one user wrote.

"Football season [is] going to be crazy with all these vaxxed players!," another suggested.

Lever's own take on the incident was that he was simply the victim of "a little bit of bad luck" after pushing himself too hard.

"I am not the first person to faint after giving it their all and I don't think I'm going to be the last," he said on Wednesday having been checked by medical staff and sent home.

It's worth noting that footballers have been reported to have collapsed during training sessions and games well before COVID-19 vaccines were rolled out, such as Cam McCarthy in 2020, Dylan Roberton in 2018 and Hugh Goddard in 2015.

The notion that COVID-19 vaccines have led to an increase in the deaths of soccer players and other athletes has previously been debunked by Fact Check.

According to Andre La Gerche, the Baker Institute's head of sports cardiology, there has not been any increase in the number of athletes suffering heart problems following the introduction of COVID-19 vaccines, despite long lists of supposed vaccine-related deaths being circulated online.

"The inference that those cases are somehow due to the vaccine is wrong, because that has been happening well before [the pandemic]," Dr La Gerche told Fact Check previously.

The facts on Pfizer's vaccine trials

An article suggesting that Pfizer had "miscounted" or "publicly misreported" the number of deaths that occurred during clinical trials of its COVID-19 vaccine has gone viral online.

Written by former New York Times journalist Alex Berenson — who has been labelled by The Atlantic as "the pandemic's wrongest man" — and published on a website known to produce "misleading", "inaccurate" and "unsupported" claims, the article points to discrepancies between data released by Pfizer and that of the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA).

According to the article, while Pfizer had reported 15 deaths among vaccine recipients and 14 among those given a placebo during a clinical trial, data later released by the FDA put the figures at 21 and 17 for the data collection period of November 2020 to March 2021.

"Pfizer somehow miscounted — or publicly misreported, or both — the number of deaths in one of the most important clinical trials in the history of medicine," Berenson's article states.

But as the CSIRO's Rob Grenfell explained, while the Pfizer results cited in the article referred to a trial undertaken in the US, Argentina, Brazil, South Africa, Germany and Turkey, the FDA report included a second trial conducted in Germany.

"It appears that the death data comes from the combination of the two submitted studies," Dr Grenfell said, adding that data may have also been collected past the March cut-off date.

"Either way, it doesn't demonstrate irregularities, and the figures are pretty similar [between those who received the vaccine and those who received the placebo]."Elsewhere online, anti-vaxxers have seized on an article published by the British Medical Journal investigating a whistleblower's complaints that "poor practices" were undertaken by a contractor during Pfizer clinical trials.However, as fact checkers at Lead Stories found, while both Pfizer and the FDA were made aware of allegations surrounding poor clinical practices undertaken by the contractor in 2020, experts said the claims weren't "serious enough to discredit data from the clinical trials".

Meanwhile, a spokesperson for the Therapeutic Goods Administration told Fact Check they were aware of the claims made by the Pfizer whistleblower, but noted:

"The Pfizer COVID-19 vaccine clinical trials were conducted in over 150 sites in multiple countries. The allegations raised in the BMJ article pertain to three sites only (approximately 2 per cent of the trial population) in the United States and there is no suggestion that other sites may be implicated. The overall results are therefore not expected to be impacted."

The spokesperson added that "the safety and efficacy [of the Pfizer vaccine] demonstrated in clinical trials has been thoroughly substantiated in real world use".

In other news: Scott Morrison says there are only four countries in the G20 with a better emissions record than Australia's. Is that correct?

Following bruising appearances at the G20 in Rome and the UN climate change conference in Glasgow, Prime Minister Scott Morrison has been keen to spruik Australia's achievements in cutting greenhouse gas emissions.

In an interview with Sunrise on Channel Seven, Mr Morrison said that Australia had "reduced our emissions by more than 20 per cent".

"There are only four countries in the G20 that have actually done better than Australia on that front," he said.

Fact Check this week found that claim to be misleading.

Data from the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change shows four G20 countries ahead of Australia for emissions reductions on 2005 levels (Mr Morrison's chosen starting point) to 2019, including the land use, land-use change and forestry sector (LULUCF).

These parameters were the most advantageous to Australia when making international comparisons. Excluding LULUCF from the calculations or changing the starting year to 1990, when the data begins, would put Australia further behind the pack.

Also, the chosen dataset, though authoritative, didn't include several countries of the G20 listed as developing nations when the convention was signed. Using alternative datasets that include these countries would place Australia behind up to nine G20 countries for emissions reductions.

More problematic was the Prime Minister's use in his comparison of a 2020 figure ("more than 20 per cent") for Australia's emissions record, when data was only available to 2019 for other countries.

The 2020 figure includes the effect of the pandemic, which experts told Fact Check contributed to a large drop in emissions worldwide.

If a like-for-like comparison were able to be made using 2020 data for all countries, it is possible Australia's ranking could have slipped further.
