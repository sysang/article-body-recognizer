The 50 best albums of 2021

Thursday 9 December 2021 9:00am

The 50 best albums of the year, as voted by the Double J team

Never have we felt less entitled to great art.

For two years now, artists have been denied the opportunities they need to sustain their creative practices.

For many, 2021 was another year of solely working their non-musical day jobs as live music's pause became more and more pregnant, and record sales remained a shadow of what they once were.

But great music came anyway. Because great artists have no choice but to make it.

It's a compulsion. They won't stand still. They won't remain quiet. They'll continue to create amidst great personal, professional, and financial struggle, not because they're deluded, because they have no other choice.

The precarity of the music industry has been widely publicised of late, and for good reason. Because music improves our lives.

A great record won't fix your problems, but it makes those problems easier to process, easier to live with, and can offer perspective you didn't know you needed.

Music supports us. We must reciprocate that support if we want to continue experiencing the transcendent power of this art.

These are the 50 albums the Double J team voted as the best of the year. There are many more incredible records that wouldn't fit here that also deserve your attention.

Please, spend some time discovering a new album this summer. And then go and buy it.

- Dan Condon, Music Editor

50. James Blake – Friends That Break Your Heart

Partner Jameela Jamil apparently told him to not write about her again. So, with his fifth album, James Blake toned down the gushing and looked away from home base for inspiration.

"They know who they are", he told Double J about these titular 'friends'. It's a little less emotional than previous albums, but not by much. The closer, 'If I'm Insecure', is as open and heartfelt as Blake's ever been. I'll admit this isn't his strongest album, but because his vision is so unique, it still easily rates as one of the year's best.

His mastery of twisting sonics, exploring the ambient through to hip hop, and driving that voice into all corners of its range, is still a treat. – Richard Kingsmill

49. Gaspard Augé – Escapades

Gaspard Augé is one half of French duo Justice. Escapades, his first ever solo record, is masterful.

It's cleverly put together. It's exciting. It's a journey. It's epic. It's cinematic. It's from the future, but, because he's using some classic retro sounds, it ends up being a familiar future. One I'm quite happy to dwell in, on my robot future-horse with laser eyes and lightweight metal armour, riding away through the desert into the sunset, listening to 'Hey!'.

A perfect road trip record, or a headphone trip in your retro-futuristic egg chair. Or a robot future-horse ride, escaping. Escapades. It belongs in this list. – Dylan Lewis

48. Alice Skye – I Feel Better But I Don't Feel Good

Alice Skye shares a piece of her soul on I Feel Better But I Don't Feel Good.

The openhearted storytelling she showed on 2018 debut Friends With Feelings continues throughout this, her second album.

Bearing all with raw sensitivity, Alice takes us on her journey through the desperate depths of self-defeat in 'Stay In Bed' to the rocking peaks of 'Everything Is Great' to more reflective themes in final song 'Wurega Djalin'.

I connect with the light and shade of this album, I love the growth and maturity Alice displays as she develops her artistry with authenticity, spirit and hope. – Wendy Saunders

47. The Bronx – Bronx VI

I didn't realise how much I missed sticky carpets and sweaty band rooms until I put on 'Superbloom' from The Bronx's Bronx VI.

When Matt Caughthran screams 'Nobody LIVES, Nobody DIES!' I just want to be punched in the face by rock'n'roll. I want to scream it back at him, with a crowd of other horn-throwing punters.

The LA punks have toured here a lot, and they'll be back. When they next arrive, they'll be armed with blazing rockers like 'Superbloom', 'Watering The Well', the brain-melting 'Curb Feelers', and 'White Shadow', their best single in years.

I'm going to learn all the words, see you at the front soon. – Ryan Egan

46. Martha Marlow – Medicine Man

After this debut album came in May, I kept waiting for it to cross over to the masses. As we reach the end of 2021, although it has resonated for many, it's still one of the year's best kept secrets.

With some nods to Joni Mitchell's jazz-like delivery, and Nick Drake's introspection, there's a love here for the folk greats, as well contemporaries such as Weyes Blood and Phoebe Bridgers. The orchestration that supports her simple guitar is special and never saccharine.

Growing up in a musical household in Sydney, Martha Marlow's strength as a songwriter comes from her deep musical sensibility, those rich melodies she delivers with ease, and her ability to share life's important moments with a quietly confident power. – Richard Kingsmill

45. Holy Holy – Hello My Beautiful World

Timothy Carroll is one of the best lyricists in the country. With every Holy Holy release he expands his poetry, his emotional depth, and his inexplicable knack for distilling big emotions into beautiful words.

The sweeping '80s pop synths, expansive classical elements, and heart-wrenching guitar lines will embed themselves in your soul. Melbourne rapper Queen P's appearance on 'Port Rd' is straight-up fire: it's a hip hop-driven call for change that'll leave you breathless, which is then followed by a complete gear-shift in the symphonic, meditative spoken-word title track.

The first half of this album will open you up and let you expel the anger, confusion, and grief of the past two years. The second half will put you back together, and remind you that things might seem terrible, but there's still a lot to love about this big, beautiful world. – Gab Burke

44. Faye Webster – I Know I'm Funny haha

This may be a cliché, but my relationship with this record organically turned from a fascination into an obsession, eventually mellowing out into a firm and solid friendship as it embedded itself into the fabric of my 2021.

I already loved Faye Webster's energy and songwriting, and having heard its singles, I was excited to dive into the album. I listened through once and heard her strong but sly presence, earnest but wickedly funny lyricism, divine waves of slide guitar, a band sitting right in the pocket, understated. I just wanted to put it on again immediately. And then again.

Now it's the album I've listened to the most all year: a warm, sharp and thoughtful friend I can turn to any time. I love records like that. – Tim Shiel

43. Japanese Breakfast – Jubilee

When the mariachi-style horns started up in the opener 'Paprika', I knew this was going to be a joyful ride. And this album has remained that for me for much of 2021.

Led by Korean American Michelle Zauner, Japanese Breakfast's more focused third album has enjoyed deservingly glowing reviews, and even a couple of Grammy nominations.

The effortless 'Be Sweet' has some choice (yes, choice) '80s ingredients (anyone else hearing Cyndi Lauper?), while 'Kokomo, IN' nicks a little from The Beatles/Beach Boys' songbooks.

So, there's a bit of range, but at its core, the album simply showcases some clever and heartfelt indie songwriting. Even the chipmunk vocals in 'Savage Good Boy' can't annoy. Plenty to relish here. – Richard Kingsmill

42. Ross From Friends – Tread

To make Tread, British producer Ross From Friends built software that allowed him to avoid the faff of recording, saving, and archiving. This meant he had more time to live in the moment, lose himself in the jam, and stay in the rave pocket for hours.

You can hear that on this record. It's the sound of an artist at the top of his game. He has perfected his craft by simplifying his process. But what you hear isn't simple: Tread is hugely complex. It's vastly layered, the textures are emotive and vibrant, the beats are deep, and its piano and voice samples remove the fourth wall.

The most outstanding thing for me on this record is Ross' ability to compose some of the most euphoric builds and drops in electronic music. He is the master of the crescendo. – Phoebe Bennett

41. Lana Del Rey – Chemtrails Over The Country Club

The day before Lana Del Ray revealed the artwork for Chemtrails Over The Country Club, she wished Joan Baez a "Happiest of Birthdays". If the pic positions Del Ray as an artist breathing the same rarefied air, it's not too bold a claim.

Del Ray has produced a lowkey masterpiece in Chemtrails..., dialling back the grandiosity in favour of subdued Americana. Asserting her songwriting lineage, it closes with Joni Mitchell's 'For Free', after a heads-up on the penultimate track: 'I'm covering Joni and I'm dancing with Joan/ Stevie is calling on the telephone'.

A decade into Lizzy Grant's ongoing reinvention, she's truly earned a seat as a fellow lady of the Canyon. – Sam Wicks

40. King Gizzard the Lizard Wizard – Butterfly 3000

When I heard the latest LP from King Gizzard The Lizard Wizard, Butterfly 3000, I was confused as to how the band ended up creating such a sweet, warm album. What caused their shift from heavy metal? How'd the band deal with going from big touring schedules to being stuck in one of the most locked down cities in the world?

Lead singer Stu McKenzie has said the cosy, cocoon vibe was a deliberate choice. After seeing everyone cooped up, glued to terrifying news updates, he and the band wanted to create a "butterfly land" everyone could escape to.

Through the cosmic synths and whispered, hopeful lyrics I realised logging off and tuning in to a faraway world is just as valuable to our noggins and emotional states as constantly being "in the know". – Tanya Bunter

39. serpentwithfeet – DEACON

Currently, there is no shortage of strikingly emotive vocalists. Standing tall amongst those is the Baltimore-raised 32-year-old Josiah Wise, whose second album as serpentwithfeet confirms his special vision exploring gospel, RB, opera and pop.

As playful as it is poignant, as strong as it is soft, the success of this album is how it captures a universal beauty in celebrating black queer love. Leaving heartbreak at the door, DEACON is full of light and mesmerising warmth, and as an album benefits from being far more direct and concise than its predecessor.

Fans of kindred spirits like ANOHNI and Sampha (the latter of whom co-produces a handful of songs) will find much to treasure here. – Richard Kingsmill

38. Torres – Thirstier

I was practically fist-pumping the first time I listened to Thirstier. I fell for the power and moodiness of Torres with her second album Sprinter (2015). Finally, with album number five, Mackenzie Scott has made the album I (perhaps selfishly) wanted her to make.

It's dramatic, but with added exuberance and hope. She offers herself up with a full and open heart. She leans into melodies where, before, she'd actively subvert them. Her vocal range and guitar skills are there to savour. There's more synth than ever, ranging from semi-industrial to icy new wave, and feeling more natural in the mix.

While I'm not at peak Torres, it'll take a lot to top Thirstier. – Dorothy Markek

37. MOD CON – Modern Condition

MOD CON's unflinching second record Modern Condition is an incendiary slab of tracks spanning post-punk, rock, surf, garage, and pop. Giving us shades of a parallel universe where The B-52's ditched the dance floors and were playing on the streets to a climate rally.

Their songs hit out at selfishness and ignorance, and interrogate institutions, but find empathy, humour, and solace amidst our ongoing shit-fight.

"Our world is being chucked off the big trophy shelf in the sky and I'm ranting about it," Erica Dunn told Double J.

Equal parts frenetic, tense, and powerful, MOD CON have nailed the brief of our current Modern Condition. – Dylan Saville

36. Robbie Miller – Little Love

A lifetime ago – okay, 2013 – a shy folk singer from Brisbane won triple j Unearthed's NIMAs competition. Two EPs followed, then, finally, a debut album three years in the making.

For us, it's been a quiet test of patience. For Robbie Miller, it's been a whole lot of change and growth. The chance to find his voice, hone his songwriting and take the time he needed to learn who he is and – most importantly – who he isn't. With his rich, clear, soulful voice, Robbie shares relatable struggles with self-worth ('Show Your Skin', 'Catch Me') and relationships ('The Come Down', 'Two People').

It's a leap of faith that's definitely paid off. – Dorothy Markek

35. Julia Stone – Sixty Summers

Sixty Summers isn't Julia Stone's first solo album, but it might as well be. From the chirpy 'Break' through to the gentle 'I Am No One', the revered artist uses this new album to show sides to her creative brain we've not yet encountered, showcasing the versatility of her talent.

Stone has spoken of the freedom she felt when creating this work, and you can hear her relishing the chance to follow the ideas wherever they go, without succumbing to expectation. Her brand of art-pop isn't completely off-the-wall, but there are sounds and moods here that you won't have heard from her before.

Regardless of the status of her creative partnership with brother Angus, it's heartening to hear Julia Stone's solo career is heading in an interesting direction. – Dan Condon

34. Joy Crookes – Skin

Skin is Joy Crookes' debut record and it's one hell of an introduction to the South London singer.

The record tells the story of her Bengali Irish heritage, while touching on some of the larger socio-political issues affecting the world in past two years, looking at the importance of family, and the impact of heartbreak.

Joy's vocals mirror those of the greats: Nina Simone, Sarah Vaughan, Billie Holiday. But the classic sounds are meshed with the beats of London, a nod to the city which acts as the album's backdrop.

The record is an evocative snapshot of Joy Crookes' life right now as she celebrates her identity while trying to make sense of the world around her. – Phoebe Bennett

33. Nas – King's Disease II

When Nas released King's Disease in 2020, he rightly boasted an impressive batting average, and he's extended that record on the album's successor. King's Disease II sees Nas in rare form, taking a victory lap with esteemed guests like Ms Lauryn Hill, Eminem and EPMD riding shotgun.

Nas sounds more focused than he has in years, and part of that's down to his partnership with Hit-Boy. Given Nas previously found the pocket on tracks from heavy hitters like DJ Premier, Dr Dre and Kanye West, it's proof of the young beatmaker's nous that Nas manoeuvres so deftly across his crystalline production. 

Nas' career-best run doesn't look like slowing anytime soon. – Sam Wicks

32. Tori Amos – Ocean To Ocean

I fell in love with Tori Amos with 1994's 'Cornflake Girl'. I loved the drama and intensity of her music. 'Professional Widow' in 1996 solidified her as a mixtape favourite. Now, Ocean To Ocean reminds me why I admired her in the first place.

Its title sets the scene, a strong nature theme runs through songs like 'Speaking With Trees', 'Metal Water Wood' and 'Flowers Burn to Gold'. Tori wrote the album in Cornwall during the UK's long winter lockdown, so you might think it would be a dark and sober, but Ocean To Ocean is refreshingly bright.

Cornwall is known for its beautiful beaches, sheer cliffs and steep valleys; Ocean To Ocean captures that through Tori's unique vocals, the theatre of her music, and her whimsical charm. Strings, haunting piano and the quiver in Tori's voice brings the landscape to life. – Wendy Saunders

31. Brandi Carlile – In These Silent Days

The pressure of following up a Grammy winning album must be daunting. But, with In These Silent Days, Brandi Carlile delivers an album that not only fits the sentiment of the times, but does so with the impressive songwriting and artistic prowess she is globally known and loved for.

Woven carefully throughout the record are odes to her heroes like Joni Mitchell, Tanya Tucker and LGBTQIA+ pioneers Indigo Girls. There is a distinct and unique honesty and empathy in this record that is brutally raw and fragile yet incredibly brave and warm.

It will leave you with a renewed sense of hope and perhaps even a glimpse into a better version of yourself, as all incredible pieces of art should. – Kath Devaney

30. Tropical Fuck Storm – Deep States

There are moments in Tropical Fuck Storm's ARIA award winning third album when the band feels like its balancing on a knife edge, precariously walking the line between chaos and control.

'G.A.F.F' embraces both distortion and sweetness as Gareth Liddiard sings of 'Deep fakes, false flags, fires and famine'. Erica Dunn's 'Suburbiopia' is another standout, with samples and a skittish beat owing more to hip hop than rock. There's fractured funk, strange pop, gritty, angular rhythms, and some wild textural synth sounds and samples.

The pandemic, and political and social upheaval informs its lyrics, tackling conspiracy theories, media moguls, social media disfunction, cults and sci-fi and a whole lot more. It all adds up to an intense, inspired album with an off-kilter vibe perfectly in step with these weird times. – Karen Leng

29. NAO – And Then Life Was Beautiful

If you're navigating a difficult relationship, this album is for you. NAO channels joy, contemplation and crushing heartbreak in a record full of neo-soul pop jams and piano ballads.

'Messy Love' is a blueprint for the type of relationship you know you don't want, and it gives you the words and confidence to say it. 'Glad That You're Gone' is an empowering break-up anthem that holds nothing back. 'Burn Out' hits so close to the heart it hurts, and 'I Wait' will stop you in your tracks.

There's brilliant collaborations with Lianne La Havas and serpentwithfeet, and 'Antidote' with Adekunle Gold is pure radiance. Navigating a break-up is rough, this album is here to remind you that there is life, love and hope after a relationship ends. – Gab Burke

28. Jordan Rakei – What We Call Life

His first two albums hinted at a shy, introspective character. A run of five albums in five years – including via his techno alias Dan Kye – spoke to his work ethic. On What We Call Life, Jordan Rakei releases his most personal set of songs.

Having used therapy to help unlock the origins of thoughts and behaviours, he takes comfort in, and is comfortable with, sharing these revelations here. Before it all gets too heavy, skip to 'Illusion' and 'Runaway' for evidence of his heavenly voice and formidable skills. While songs like 'Send My Love' and 'Unguarded' have immediate impact.

As with therapy, repeat sessions are highly recommended for maximum benefit. – Dorothy Markek

27. Quivers – Golden Doubt

Golden Doubt is the sound of friendship. The sound of sunshine. The sound of coming to terms with how things are. The sound of appreciating what we have.

Melbourne's Quivers flawlessly blend colossally singable melodies, thoughtful and intimate lyricism, and impressive, tasteful musicianship on their second album. The big choir on opener 'Gutters Of Love', the perfect guitar riff that propels 'When It Breaks', the emotive, reflective beauty of 'Videostores' are just three of this record's many magical moments.

If you love The Go-Betweens, Paul Kelly, and feeling your feelings rather than pushing them down, you need Golden Doubt in your life. It's a beautiful record that's been written and performed so considerately and openheartedly that you just want to hug these songs and never let go of them. – Dan Condon

26. Femi Kuti – Stop The Hate

Femi Kuti has lived his life at the centre of the Afrobeat movement. He's shouldered his father Fela Kuti's legacy and continues to champion the sound and carry its torch.

Stop The Hate was released as a package alongside an album from his son Made, ensuring the continuation of a musical legacy.

The album is steeped in the classic Afrobeat sounds and Femi's playing is disciplined. His polyrhythmic drum patterns are hypnotic, and his lyrics are full of political commentary and the need for change.

Femi brings his own unique vision to the sound, stepping away from the structures his dad set and moving towards his own visions for the genre. Stop The Hate is an intergenerational celebration of Afrobeat and a symbol of a baton changing hands. – Phoebe Bennett

25. The Hold Steady – Open Door Policy

For a band formed as an excuse to drink beers a quarter of a century ago, The Hold Steady have had an extraordinary career. Open Door Policy felt like a proper rebirth, a consolidation of the band's importance now that the hype and potential for crossover superstardom has faded.

Craig Finn's stories are rich, as his characters navigate complex mental struggles and the reality of not being young anymore. The band's arrangements are both sympathetic and mighty, balancing the restrained beauty of their 2010s efforts with the giddying barroom rock'n'roll that made them stick out in the first place.

After all these years, we are all still The Hold Steady. – Dan Condon

24. Arlo Parks – Collapsed In Sunbeams

These past two years have been tough for many of us, and the debut record from South London based artist Arlo Parks came at exactly the right time. What I love about the record is how sincere it is. Arlo really makes you believe she's right there beside you, no matter what you're going through.

Her songwriting is easy to connect with, observational and vivid. It's full of comfort, introspection, thoughtful and soothing poetry, and she makes you feel like your emotions are not only safe and nurtured, but that you belong.

Songs like 'Collapsed In Sunbeams', 'Too Good' and 'Black Dog' are just a few of the highlights from such an intimate, powerfully vulnerable and perfect debut record that I'm extremely grateful to have been able to turn to throughout 2021. – Claire Mooney

23. You Am I – The Lives Of Others

'The Waterboy' kicks off The Lives of Others in effusive fashion, an ode to the power of music to bridge divides and distance. It sounds like You Am I, yet also like nothing they've done before. 'Rubbish Day' is a psychedelic wormhole of existential dread, 'Lookalikes' an irresistible stomp, whilst 'Manliness' delivers finely made observations.

Tim Rogers has penned wry and poignant tales that draw us into the lives of others for decades, and his comrades Hopkinson, Kent and Lane provide the swing, stomp and melodicism of '60s touchstones with a kind of freshness.

I don't know what I was expecting on this 11th You Am I record, but I do know that these songs leave me with a warmth deep in my belly, and a sense of joy filling my heart in all four places. – Caz Tran

22. Celeste – Not Your Muse

This is one hell of a debut album. The British artist appeared on many 'ones-to-watch' lists in 2019, and it looked like 2020 was going to be Celeste's year. And then COVID hit.

The album was pushed back, but when it finally landed in January, it did not disappoint. Celeste debuted as a fully-formed superstar.

Her voice is insanely good. She can do bouncy power anthems like 'Stop This Flame' (which earned a spot on the FIFA 21 soundtrack), and soulful lullabies like 'Ideal Woman'. There's horn blasts, neo-soul jams, and gentle guitar laments.

This is an album that'll fill the Amy Winehouse shaped hole in your heart. – Gab Burke

21. Courtney Barnett – Things Take Time, Take Time

The past few years have been a whirlwind of releases and touring for Courtney Barnett. Lockdown forced her back to where her songwriting first found its voice: from a place of stillness, at home in Melbourne, finding poetry and meaning in the mundane.

Taking a stripped back, homespun approach to production, and working one-on-one with drummer Stella Mozgawa (Warpaint), Things Take Time, Take Time sees Courtney more honest and optimistic than ever.

It's a pleasure to be in her company as she observes the world outside her window on 'Rae Street', digs deep into post-breakup vulnerability on 'Before You Gotta Go', and brims with the excitement of a new crush on 'If I Don't Hear From You Tonight'. – Caitlin Nienaber

20. Shihad – Old Gods

"At the age of 50, we shouldn't actually be making a rock'n'roll record this good." Jon Toogood has never been backward in coming forward, and he called it straight when telling Double J about his band's 10th studio album.

Old Gods bristles with Toogood's righteous anger at the state of the world since he started a family, converted to Islam, and witnessed the death throes of empires falling. His fury is matched by a sound as punishing as it is purifying, Shihad's unrelenting riffage equal to any of the meanest hits they've landed across 30-plus years.

This was never going to be a band that goes gentle into middle age, and on Old Gods the 'Had is raging harder than ever. – Sam Wicks

19. Bicep – Isles

Bicep's second album Isles is a tale of two cities. It was named for their time spent growing up in Belfast where they cut their teeth in the club scene, and their move to London, the place they've called home for the past decade.

The duo are crate diggers, and this album is an amalgamation of the music they've discovered in each city, with samples ranging from Malawian singers to The Bulgarian State Television Female Choir. You can hear the cities fuse together: through jungle, ambient soundscapes, deep bass beats, and Celtic melodies, their melting pot of cultures and genres feels carefully curated.

Isles is euphoric, moody, and introspective. Each build is deeply moving, every drop and beat feels devastatingly raw. It is industrial, minimal and icy, and its vocals and samples fuse it all together like honey. – Phoebe Bennett

18. The Bamboos – Hard Up

The Bamboos celebrated their 20th anniversary with a week-long lock-in in country Victoria to record songs for their tenth album. You can hear that 20-year connection on Hard Up, and feel the chemistry of the players on every track.

This album just wraps its smarts, positivity and warmth around you. In The Bamboos' hands (and mouths – oh, those horns!) songs of longing, like 'If Not Now (Then When)', fill you with hope. 'The Thing About You', makes you want to fall in love all over again – if you need a wedding song, look no further – and their cover of Black Box's 'Ride On Time' slays.

Hard Up is an irresistible ride from start to finish. – Meagan Loader

17. The Weather Station – Ignorance

Canada's Tamara Lindeman has been making intimate, acoustic-guitar-heavy records since the 2000s. But Ignorance, her fifth album as The Weather Station, is something very different: a richly orchestrated, jazzy, dark, beautiful collection of pop songs. This was partly in hope that a more approachable sound would help the album's climate activism themes across.

On 'Robber', heavy with percussion and violin stabs, Lindeman sings of corporate figures whose environmental destruction inspires resignation. 'Tried To Tell You' chugs like a Springsteen b-side, maudlin but upbeat enough to get you dancing.

While there's a focus on rhythm, it is offset by Lindeman's voice – sometimes velvety, other times cracking into a falsetto, always a calm, elegant presence amidst the drama of the music. – Paul Donoughue

16. For Those I Love – For Those I Love

David Balfe's For Those I Love project is uncomfortably personal and, at times, difficult to listen to. He brings the raw intensity of grief to life, with the full force of his love and his loss embedded in nearly every moment of every song.

The ecstatic catharsis of 'Birthday' acts as a Trojan horse into the album's themes of grief, loss and violence, with a darkened vision of Dublin serving as the backdrop. After interviewing David, we kept talking about art and life. I just wanted to get a read on whether he was doing alright now the album was in full view. He seemed shaken, but okay.

1. The Avalanches – We Will Always Love You

I've been thinking lately about how The Avalanches third LP, We Will Always Love You, is an album of messages. From the voicemail Orono Noguchi leaves in the album's opener, to the morse code that closes it.

Its artwork is an image of Ann Druyan, the creative director who sent messages of music into the great unknown with the Voyager Space Project.

Living between all of these touchstones is a collection of songs that speak to us, and to the duo themselves, buoying us all with the reminder of the connections that love, art, light, mystery, and music can create.

Robbie Chater and Tony DiBlasi couldn't have known how severed those connections would become as they made this record. Nor how We Will Always Love You would stitch so many of us back together, time and time again, throughout 2021.

When The Avalanches released their debut, it was like nothing else. It made our heads spin and our bodies move. A series of misfires, hiccups, and abandoned ideas led to a long break before its follow up, but their triumphant return here is nothing short of miraculous.

Rarely can an artist capture the wonder of a debut; you hear them try to find it, but seldom is it truly felt. However, Tony and Robbie manage to let the weight of the past 20 years settle, to draw on those hard times in order to be reminded of the good.

As Leonard Cohen put it; 'There is a crack, a crack in everything, that's how the light gets in'. And that dance of light and possibility lives again here. Better yet, they bring us along for the ride.

There is a poetic parallel in the macro and micro worlds of We Will Always Love You.

On the one hand, you feel a yearning for the purest kind of connection between humans.

On the other, the samples, sonics and singers invoke a galactic realm that reduces our biggest heartaches into a million insignificances.

It's a great leveller; the kind of joy I feel when I look out to the stars and am reminded that I am a speck, less than, passing in the split seconds of all experience.

But that incredible human family of friends that these two unashamed fan boys have gathered along the way makes this album what it is. More than ever before, this is a living album. Old samples still tell its story, but the guests – their heroes – dot the recordings.

It's a wild guestlist of legendary punks, iconic guitarists, trip hop royalty, and indie legends.

The contribution from Sananda Maitreya (formerly known as Terence Trent D'Arby) cuts deep. It's one of many significant moments where these voices sing to the duo's experience and translate it into something with a much greater resonance than anything The Avalanches have ever crafted before.

It's also, at times, a blissful space cadet.

Thousands of people bobbing at the same time, swaying side to side with their arms around much missed friends. I've never smiled so much.

Regardless of the huge volume of artists, ideas, and textures, We Will Always Love You manages to feel like one complete drift of beautiful music. It is a letter. A message. An astounding album. – Zan Rowe
