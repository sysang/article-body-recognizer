Matthew Davey sentenced for attempted murder of partner by setting her on fire in Hobart backyard

A Tasmanian man who set his girlfriend on fire in 2017, leaving her with "catastrophic burns" and lifelong injuries, has been sentenced to 18 years in prison for attempted murder.

Key points:

  • Matthew Davey can now be named after a suppression order lifted yesterday when he pleaded guilty to other crimes
  • Davey set his partner on fire in a backyard shed after she told him their relationship was over
  • The victim, Nicole Evans, spent more than five months in hospital

On Friday afternoon, Matthew John Davey was sentenced for the crimes of attempted murder and persistent family violence, committed against his then 20-year-old partner, Nicole Louise Evans. He was 34 years old at the time.

In the Supreme Court in Hobart, Justice Michael Brett described Davey's actions as "cruel" and "arrogant" and the final step in an escalating pattern of violence.

He said their relationship was "toxic and extremely volatile", characterised by "frequent arguments often related to mutual jealousy and lack of trust".

"You engaged in a continuous and marked pattern of coercive control over the complainant," he said.

This included constantly monitoring her whereabouts, making her believe he was tracking her through electronic surveillance, preventing her from leaving and demanding loyalty from her.

"In order to maintain this control you used threats of burning or use of fire against her or her property. On number of occasions you made clear if she ever left you you'd find and kill her."

Justice Brett said he found Ms Evans's evidence throughout the seven-week trial to be credible and reliable and he believed that on the night of April 24, 2017, she wanted to end the relationship.

"[That day] she clearly communicated to you that she wanted the relationship to be over," he said.

"Your response was to spit in her face, throw a soft drink can at her and cause damage in her bedroom."

"[Later that day] she again told you she wanted to end the relationship, begged you to let her go and told you she didn't love you anymore.

'You extended the lighter towards her and flicked it'

On the night of the incident, in a backyard shed in Hobart, Ms Evans "continued to make it clear" the relationship was over.

"You then threw accelerant from the fuel can on her, which you knew was capable of being ignited easily," he said.

"I have no doubt your purpose in doing this was to threaten and intimidate her and demonstrate the power you had over her … so she would back down and continue with the relationship."

But Ms Evans did not back down. Instead, she got angrier and insisted on going home.

Ms Evans "ran from the shed" as soon as the fire started, but within a relatively short time Davey took steps to assist her and put out the flames.

"After that, you were concerned to avoid responsibility for what you had done," he said.

"In the immediate aftermath of the fire, as you were waiting for emergency services to come, you asked the complainant to lie to others about how it started.

"You said if she did not you were going to go to prison for a very long time."

He described a phone call from Davey to Ms Evans, whilst she was in rehabilitation in August 2017, as a "final and desperate attempt" to save himself.

"It was a cruel and cynical act and undoubtedly caused her significant distress. It negates any remorse."

'She must have been in a great deal of terror'

Justice Brett said while Ms Evans had not made a victim impact statement, the impact of the crime could be seen in the medical evidence.

Ms Evans, whilst severely burnt, had been forced to walk from the backyard to the ambulance parked on the street and spoke about her long rehabilitation and lifelong injuries.

"She was aware her life was in jeopardy. She must have been in a great deal of terror, shock and pain," Justice Brett said.

"She was in intensive care under heavy sedation [at the Alfred Hospital in Melbourne for] approximately six weeks. Her risk of death was high for at least a month and gradually diminished thereafter."

Ms Evans was left with burns to 68 per cent of her body and was not discharged until the September 29, 2017, more than five months after the fire.

She will need to continue to have treatment throughout her life.

'Cruel and arrogant' act

Justice Brett said Davey's crime had left Ms Evans with "catastrophic life-threatening injuries" that had "permanent and devastating consequences".

"You treated her as your possession and when your ability to control her was strongly challenged you [punished her] and did so in a particularly cruel and arrogant way.

"It was a … shocking breach of trust inherent in the relationship and deserves severe condemnation."

He said Davey had shown no remorse.

A suppression order on Davey's and the victim's names was lifted yesterday after Davey pleaded guilty to separate charges of persistent family violence and trafficking in guns and methylamphetamine.

For the crimes of attempted murder and persistent family violence, Davey was sentenced to 18 years in prison with a non-parole period of 12 years, backdated to January 2019.

For trafficking in guns and methylamphetamine, he received four years, with a non-parole of two years. A unrelated suspended three-month sentence was also activated.

His total sentence in prison was 22 years and three months, with a non-parole of 14 years.
