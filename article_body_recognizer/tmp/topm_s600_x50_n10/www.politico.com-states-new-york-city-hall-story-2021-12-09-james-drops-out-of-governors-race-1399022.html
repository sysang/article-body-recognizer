Tish James drops out of New York governor's race

Share on Facebook Share on Twitter

ALBANY, N.Y. — State Attorney General Tish James has suspended her campaign for governor less than two months after a highly anticipated launch, saying that she will instead focus on reelection to her current job in 2022.

James had been viewed as the strongest potential primary challenger to Gov. Kathy Hochul, who succeeded Andrew Cuomo after James’ probe into allegations of sexual harassment forced his resignation in August. But even after she entered the race in October, she never launched a full-fledged campaign and regularly trailed Hochul by wide margins in early polls.

“I have come to the conclusion that I must continue my work as attorney general,” James said in a statement Thursday. “There are a number of important investigations and cases that are underway, and I intend to finish the job. I am running for re-election to complete the work New Yorkers elected me to do."

Just before she announced her withdrawal from the governor's race, several news outlets reported that she is continuing to pursue her office’s high-profile probes into former president Donald Trump. James is seeking a deposition from Trump on Jan. 7 at her New York offices as part of her investigation into potential fraud inside the Trump Organization, according to a source familiar with the matter.

A senior campaign adviser said the governor's race didn't seem to engage James as her previous campaigns did, something which became apparent to everyone on the campaign, including the candidate herself.

“She’s a phenomenal campaigner and very engaged and has won races with less money and resources," the adviser said. "I know what she’s capable of. And she just wasn’t that Tish. The question is why? I think she really felt she wasn’t feeling that campaign and was excited about some stuff she was doing in the [attorney general]’s office. If that’s where you end up, then it really doesn’t make sense to run.”

James hasn’t appeared north of Westchester County, which borders New York City, since entering the race two months ago. She did not have a policy platform and seemed to be trailing significantly in fundraising, especially in comparison to Hochul’s more than $10 million haul since August.

The adviser dismissed rumors that James' fundraising operation was dragging. She would have had enough money from local and national donors to be competitive, the adviser said, and would have continued the campaign for longer if money were the sole concern.

“I can tell you without a doubt that was not a factor at all, certainly, for her personally, and for us as a campaign,” the source said.

Her departure from the race changes the campaign's dynamics.

Hochul has regularly led in early polls — she topped James by 18 points in a Siena survey released earlier this week. But most observers believed that James was the best-positioned to challenge Hochul in a field that also includes New York City Public Advocate Jumaane Williams and Rep. Tom Suozzi of Long Island. Outgoing New York City Bill de Blasio could also enter the race soon.

Now, Hochul is well-positioned to win support from party leaders and top donors as she seeks a full term in her own right next year.

Williams, who lost a bid for lieutenant governor to Hochul in a 2018 Democratic primary, said he had a "good relationship and mutual admiration" with James. Williams now has a clear path to claim support from the party's progressive wing.

Suozzi, like Hochul considered a moderate Democrat, said of James in a statement that she "has tremendous responsibilities as the sitting [attorney general] I understand her desire to devote her energies to seeing through continuing the important matters before her."

James' exit from the governor's race will also complicate what was shaping up to be a volatile campaign to succeed her as attorney general.

Former Assistant U.S. Attorney Dan Goldman, Fordham University law professor Zephyr Teachout, state Sen. Shelley Mayer (D-Yonkers) and Queens Assembly member Clyde Vanel have already announced their candidacies for the Democratic nomination.

State Sen. Michael Gianaris (D-Queens), Brooklyn DA Eric Gonzalez and Queens DA Melinda Katz were also considering bids for the job, but several sources have noted in recent days that some potential candidates seemed to be holding off, waiting to see whether James would fully engage with the gubernatorial race.

Sally Goldenberg contributed to this report.
