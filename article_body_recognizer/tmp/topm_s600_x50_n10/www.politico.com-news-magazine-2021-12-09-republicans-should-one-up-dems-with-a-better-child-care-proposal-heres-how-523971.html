Opinion | Why Republicans Need a Childcare Proposal of Their Own

Build Back Better has good intentions, but it gets the solutions wrong.

Frank Augstein/AP Photo

Opinion by ABBY M. MCCLOSKEY

12/09/2021 11:16 AM EST

Abby M. McCloskey is the founder of McCloskey Policy LLC. She has served as policy director on presidential campaigns for Republican and independent candidates. Previously, she was program director of economics at the American Enterprise Institute.

There’s little question that child care in the U.S. needs attention. Child care costs exceed those of a mortgage or college in many states. Access to affordable child care is one of the biggest barriers to women’s work, and there’s increasing evidence that the cost of raising children is a barrier to having more kids as well, according to a New York Times survey. Low quality early childhood care situations have lifetime ramifications for children, including worsened health and economic trajectories and an increased likelihood of needing future government assistance.

The Build Back Better Act seeks to address the child care issue with a historic injection of child care funds. The House-passed legislation includes free universal preschool for all 3- and 4-year-olds, irrespective of income; a one-year expansion of the Child Tax Credit, which provides nearly all parents with $300 every month per child under age 6 and $250 for older children; four weeks of paid leave; and caps on child care costs for the vast majority of families, including making child care free or negligible for families earning up to 150 percent of state median income and providing support to families earning up to $400,000.

But Democrats are getting the solutions all wrong. While the administration should be applauded for its attention on working families, their plan is likely to make a bad problem worse over the long run. Republicans can propose a better deal, and maybe even given some lawmakers a reason to alter or abandon the Democrats’ bill.

The Democrats’ plan is flawed because spreading the money out over a broad population unnecessarily limits what can go to the most at-risk populations, which could limit the program’s overall effectiveness. The evidence of improved outcomes for children from universal preschool and universal child care is mixed at best. The preponderance of evidence shows the largest gains for at-risk kids and unclear results for everyone else, and state-based programs haven’t been around long enough to suss out long-term effects.

Moreover, providing generous subsidies to nearly all American families, irrespective of need, will make child care more expensive by increasing demand, which will necessitate larger subsidies over time. This is a recipe for spiraling costs; look no further than our experiments in health care or college to see how quickly costs inflate when the government makes something “affordable.” Exacerbating these dynamics, the administration’s proposal will also constrain child care supply by mandating higher wages and skill levels from providers who already have thin margins as well as potentially limiting religious providers. Faith leaders across religions (Catholic, Muslim, Christian and Jewish) have expressed concern that their ability to continue to provide care will be negatively impacted by BBB. Those providers make up a huge portion of child care providers: A Bipartisan Policy Center poll from last year found that 31 percent of working-parent households used center-based care, and over half, or 53 percent, of these families used one that was affiliated with a faith organization.

To be sure, most parents will be shielded from the effects of rising costs because of the generous subsidies they are receiving, making the policy seem like a win-win on the surface, though they might be affected by the reduced choice providers. But nothing is free. Taxes on the rich and corporations can only go so far, and at some point that money will also need to go toward the historic debt we’ve accumulated. Estimates from the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget and Moody’s suggest that the BBB child care provisions alone will cost nearly $1 trillion over 10 years once fully implemented, far exceeding the money to be provided by the tax increases that Democrats have proposed to fund the legislation. The people likely to pay for BBB and the runaway spending in Washington are the very children whom such policies are supposed to benefit.

Policymakers can do better. Republicans should up the ante on what Democrats have proposed with an alternative child care proposal — one that is more targeted, sustainable and also more transformative — by providing greater support and choice to parents.

1) Prioritize and increase support for the most vulnerable families.

The first step is defining the scope of the problem. No one likes paying for child care and it’s an uncomfortable expense for nearly every family to add to the budget. But offsetting a large and inconvenient expense is an insufficient reason for government intervention. The case for intervention is highest where child care is a barrier to work and thus financial independence for a family, or the cost of care is so prohibitively high that a child is in a low-quality care environment. Thus, instead of offering subsidies up the income ladder, support should be much more specifically targeted at low-income families. Inexcusably, these are the families that find themselves ineligible for our current maze of government support, which tends to accrue to families below the poverty line or wealthier families, leaving out the working poor.

Reducing the number of families receiving subsidies will limit the cost inflation of care. It also would allow policymakers to go bigger and bolder in their intervention (though attention would have to be paid to benefit cliffs, or the point at which support drops off precipitously as income rises, which has been shown to discourage work). Consider that the most successful early childhood interventions have been small, expensive, high-quality programs, starting from infancy and including wrap-around services such as parental coaching. Recent research by Nobel prize-winning economist, James Heckman, has shown that these programs have resulted in intergenerational benefits that far exceed their costs. Going big for at-risk families makes more sense than shaving $1,000 or $2,000 off a child care bill for a family earning up to $400,000, as Democrats have proposed.

2) Go bigger than Democrats on parental leave.

The entirety of the early childhood care section of BBB is anchored around early childhood being a unique time worthy of differentiated investment. But the provided amount of time — four weeks — is well before a maternal checkup following birth or the point at which a child care center would accept an infant, both of which tend to happen around six weeks at minimum. The short duration of leave offered in the BBB Act is an attempt to contain costs while providing leave for a wide variety of purposes — even though the vast majority of evidence shows the most significant benefits of paid leave accrue to new parents and their babies specifically, and parental leave is where the United States is the most pronounced international outlier.

Republicans have played on the edge with how to provide paid leave and how to rearrange existing benefits in a way that’s budget-neutral. They should go all in. They should lead on the cause that all parents should be able to be home with newborns by advancing a generous paid parental leave policy that goes well beyond the four weeks, and keep it focused on new parents rather than other kinds of leave. Some Republicans have expressed concern about the cost of a paid leave program. But paid parental leave is far from a budget leviathan. A six- to eight-week paid parental leave plan is estimated to cost under $10 billion a year, less than 1 percent of our entitlement spending.

3) Lower costs by increasing the supply and choice of providers.

Little about BBB addresses the core drivers of cost inflation and may exacerbate it by restricting supply. Mandating higher wages and benefits may inadvertently reduce the number of care providers, as would limiting parental options for faith-based care solutions, further contributing to the shortage of care. Increased costs are also likely to be passed on to parents, many of whom are low-wage earners themselves and already struggling with access and affordability of care (again, this can be masked with subsidies over the short run, but not the long run).

Republicans can lead on driving down costs by addressing overly burdensome regulations and increasing the supply of providers. A promising area to pursue is by increasing state-based apprenticeship programs. Multiple states — including West Virginia, Pennsylvania and Colorado — have begun to implement registered apprenticeship programs for early childhood educators. These programs provide early childhood educators with on-the-job training and a career pathway to improve their knowledge and skills. They create more opportunities for workers to move into roles of greater responsibility and pay, a virtuous cycle resulting in higher quality care. Other ideas, such as those from Patrick Brown, a fellow at the conservative think tank Ethics and Public Policy Center, have included states offering higher levels of support to smaller community-based or religious providers to encourage a proliferation of providers that best match parental preference. Notably, this is the exact opposite direction of what BBB is likely to do.

Being on the defensive may be rewarding politics, but it does little to change the status quo for American families and children, many of whom need support. The majority of Americans (88 percent) support more government investment in families and children according to BPC polling; the majority also support the strategic pause on BBB.

It’s time for Republicans to give working parents a more compelling alternative.
