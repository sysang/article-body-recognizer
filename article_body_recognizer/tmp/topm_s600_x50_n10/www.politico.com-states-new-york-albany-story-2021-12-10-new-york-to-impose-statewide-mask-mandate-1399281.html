New York to impose statewide mask mandate

Share on Facebook Share on Twitter

All New Yorkers must wear masks inside any business that does not implement a vaccine requirement, Gov. Kathy Hochul said Friday, announcing one of the most stringent mandates in the nation.

The new requirement, which takes effect Monday and will be reassessed on Jan. 15, comes amid a surge in Covid-19 cases throughout much of upstate New York, and as more cases of the Omicron variant are confirmed throughout the state.

Hochul framed the policy not only as a move meant to protect the public health, but also the state’s economy.

“I've been warning — I've been saying — if we can't get more people vaccinated or boosted, I have to protect people but also the economy,” she said at a press conference in New York City.

The city of New York already requires proof of vaccination when entering bars, restaurants and public venues.

The mandate marks the Democratic governor’s latest action to combat a winter spike in Covid-19 cases. On Thursday, more than 30 hospitals in upstate New York began restricting non-urgent elective surgeries — a measure intended to prevent the bed shortages seen at the height of the pandemic.

The details: Under the state’s new mask-or-vaccine requirement, everyone 2 and older must wear masks in indoor public settings — including offices.

The mask mandate will not apply to businesses and venues that require proof of full vaccination. But the state’s Department of Health strongly recommends mask-wearing in all public indoor settings, even when not required.

Unvaccinated individuals must continue wearing masks, including children age 2 to 5 who are ineligible for vaccination.

The state’s existing mask requirements will continue to be in effect for pre-K to grade 12 schools, public transit, homeless shelters, correctional facilities, nursing homes and health care settings, in accordance with federal guidelines.

Violators will be subject to civil and criminal penalties, including a maximum fine of $1,000 per violation. Local health departments will be tasked with enforcing the requirements.

What they said: Hochul, who hinted at the policy at a Thursday event, added that the mandate comes in direct response to three factors: “Increasing cases, reduced hospital capacity and insufficient vaccination rates in certain areas.”

Stephen Acquario, executive director of the New York State Association of Counties, said in an interview that local officials will need the public’s cooperation with the mandate.

“County public health and law enforcement are not in a position to police this policy,” he said. “It’s going to end up in the court of public opinion, and we hope that opinion is one of mutual respect to stop the spread and get vaccinated.”
