New York governor's race shakes up with James’ exit

Share on Facebook Share on Twitter

ALBANY, N.Y. — New York Attorney General Tish James presented Gov. Kathy Hochul with an early holiday gift on Thursday, bowing out of the coming Democratic gubernatorial primary and making the incumbent a clear favorite to win the party’s nomination next year.

It's not as though the field is totally clear for Hochul: New York City Public Advocate Jumaane Williams, Rep. Tom Suozzi and, probably, New York City Mayor Bill de Blasio will all be trying to get on June's primary ballot.

Still, James' departure is a significant moment in Hochul's quest to become the first woman to be elected governor of New York. It removes a candidate who was thought to be her toughest potential obstacle and means Democratic state leadership and top donors will no longer be split between the two frontrunners, potentially narrowing the path for everyone else. “The only way [the other candidates] have a chance is if it becomes a multiple-candidate race with very strong candidates,” state Democratic Committee Chair Jay Jacobs said in an interview. “Tish [was] the second-strongest candidate, and her departure from the race makes the odds much longer for someone like Suozzi or Williams.”

Jacobs, who endorsed Hochul in September, said he’s “encouraging other candidates to follow [James’] lead and put party over their own interests.” He said he was talking to labor unions, county chairs and other groups “to make sure we all coalesce behind Kathy Hochul.”

James’ withdrawal, coming just two months after she entered the race and after she'd nearly disappeared from the campaign trail, simplifies the coming months for other elected Democrats, particularly downstate, where James, Williams and de Blasio all share Brooklyn roots and would have been competing to pull support from the party’s progressive wing away from Hochul.

Just minutes after James’ statement, Assembly member Rodneyse Bichotte Hermelyn, the Brooklyn party chair, endorsed the governor, saying she was “confident Hochul will be the best choice to lead our state forward through the recovery and she will have the support of Brooklyn behind her as she continues to blaze a path as our first female governor.”

Two months ago, Bichotte Hermelyn welcomed James into the race with effusive praise, saying in a statement that “Brooklyn will at last be able to elect a Governor that will empower and uplift all Brooklynites.” It may not have been an official endorsement, but it sounded like one.

Bichotte Hermelyn’s decision to back Hochul speaks volumes about the perceived viability of de Blasio’s potential campaign. Bichotte Hermelyn was among a select few who endorsed the mayor’s 2020 presidential bid.

Even before Thursday, Hochul had been solidifying her status as the early frontrunner ahead of the June primary. The governor has regularly led in the polls, topping James by 18 points in a Siena College survey released earlier this week.

“There has to be a new opening that hasn’t happened yet — a new political opening created by circumstance or political misstep, and there’s no indication that’s going to happen,” Tusk Strategies’ Eric Soufer said of how competitive Democratic challengers could be against Hochul. “It’s really hard to see a path for them, especially layering on top of it huge cash advantage and incumbency.”

James’ departure transforms the 2022 gubernatorial race into something closely resembling the 2018 Democratic primary for lieutenant governor. In that contest, Hochul narrowly defeated Williams. Her odds could well improve this time around, as she’s an incumbent who will no longer be unknown to voters and will likely outraise her challengers by a logarithmic scale.

There may be a couple of complications, however: Suozzi’s candidacy could well compete for the moderate and suburban vote that Hochul relied on when defeating Williams four years ago.

The Long Island congressman has positioned himself as a centrist, calling himself a “common-sense Democrat.” He was one of only a few elected officials who backed Buffalo Mayor Byron Brown’s successful write-in campaign this fall, arguing that Democrats need to rally behind problem-solvers rather than ideological purists. Brown had lost June’s Democratic primary to a socialist and political unknown, India Walton, but went on to defeat her in the general election.

That message is likely to find a sympathetic audience among a swath of outer-borough New York City Democrats, especially in Queens and Staten Island. Many have “very suburban sensibilities” when it comes to issues like property taxes and law and order, said Larry Levy of the National Center for Suburban Studies at Hofstra University, including moderate voters who who, for whatever reason, don’t like Hochul or those who are “hard-core Cuomo supporters.”

“Suozzi is trying to carve out a bit of an unconventional lane for a Democratic primary,” Levy said. “It’s not a wide one, but it’s certainly not no lane.”

And even if de Blasio enters the race, Williams is now the clear favorite in the battle for the progressive left. That support was enough for a respectable, if not quite good enough, 47 percent of the vote in the 2018 lieutenant governor‘s race. If he manages to repeat that showing, it would likely be good enough for a win if there are two moderates on the ballot.

Some of the state’s political observers had suggested Williams’ candidacy is unlikely to gestate completely and he would bow out if he failed to gain steam in the coming months.

But Williams does not have to relinquish his job as public advocate to run for governor and therefore has little to lose. His campaign pushes back on the suggestion that he’s merely running for the sake of it, pointing to his lieutenant governor run that brought him within 7 points of Hochul despite miniscule campaign funds and his lack of party backing.

Polling “shows us not only in a strong position this early in the race, but with greater growth potential than the other top-tier candidates with statewide positions and recognition,” Williams communications director William Gerlich said on Tuesday.

In a statement on Thursday, Williams praised the work James has done in her current office and suggested they’ll be collaborating more in the future. "I’m glad to be able to continue that work together to bring bold, progressive, transformational change on behalf of the people of New York," he said.

James’ reentrance into the attorney general’s race will also shake up that primary. State Sen. Shelley Mayer (D-Westchester) ended her campaign soon after James’ announcement, and others are expected to follow suit soon.

But James won’t completely clear the field: “We have had an incredible response to the campaign since we launched just a few weeks ago and our momentum continues to build,” the campaign for former Department of Financial Services superintendent Maria Vullo said in a statement.

Even if Hochul prevails in the primary, she’ll still have to run in a general election. "As far as our campaign goes, everything stays the same as far as strategy and messaging," said GOP gubernatorial candidate Rep. Lee Zeldin, who has won the support of most Republican leaders as he seeks his party's nomination.

But the attorney general’s decision to exit the race does, in fact, change the dynamics for the general election. Zeldin had been planning on running against James in November.

The congressman, who has been portraying Democrats as dominated by their left wing, isn't assuming Hochul will be the nominee.

"It helps Jumaane Williams more than anyone else," he said. "The base of Democratic Party primary voters are so heavily concentrated inside of New York City. You have the further left base of voters and other constituencies where somebody like a Kathy Hochul has less appeal."

Hochul appears to be prepared for anything. She said on Thursday, speaking at an unrelated news conference, that she likes to “always run like an underdog.”

“Unless everyone drops out,“ she said, “we’ll have a primary.”

But this year’s election results also give reason to take the general elections just as seriously. New Jersey Democrat Phil Murphy, the only governor who faced a general election in 2021, narrowly beat out his relatively unknown Republican challenger in November. And Glenn Youngkin flipped the Virginia statehouse from blue to red, an outcome that seemed unlikely months earlier.

Hochul said she’s ready to fend off a potential Republican wave.

“My focus has to be on the November election, because we saw the warning signs just recently of what could happen in places like Virginia and places like New Jersey,” she said. “I have been heeding that warning and focused on that as the leader of the New York State Democratic Party. It is also on my shoulders to pull it together and have unity in our party because we have to make sure Republicans do not get a foothold anywhere.”
