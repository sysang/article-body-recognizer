Maryland lawmakers take up gerrymandering, veto overrides

General Assembly’s Democratic supermajorities weigh how much to adjust the state’s notoriously drawn congressional districts

Maryland lawmakers convened Monday in Annapolis for the messy, once-a-decade process of rejiggering the state’s congressional district boundaries, weighing how much the state should redraw its notoriously gerrymandered map.

After hours of public testimony, the House Rules and Executive Nominations Committee passed a map supported by Democratic lawmakers — which would make the state’s lone Republican district more competitive for Democrats — on an 18-6 party line vote with no discussion.

Maryland is among the few states with redistricting under Democratic control this cycle, as both parties seek to improve their electoral chances in 2022 and take control of the House of Representatives. The state’s current map includes serpentine districts that have been likened to blood spatter, a “broken-winged pterodactyl” and, on Monday, a “salamander having seizures.”

But Republicans argued that Democrats’ preferred alternative — drawn by the Maryland Legislative Redistricting Advisory Commission — is still just as gerrymandered and has made only limited progress toward more compact districts.

During the hearing, Republicans and other bipartisan critics of gerrymandering instead supported a map proposal drawn by Gov. Larry Hogan’s citizen commission, which would likely yield a map with two Republican districts and does not split as many counties and municipalities as the legislators’ proposal.

In addition, the General Assembly began its week-long special session by starting to override more than 20 of Hogan’s vetoes issued this spring, moving quickly to revoke the governor’s power to withhold parole from inmates serving life sentences and to impose more control on the governor’s emergency pandemic spending. Final override votes are expected by the end of the week.

The special session revived energy in a State House that hasn’t experienced it for nearly two years. The 2020 regular session ended abruptly due to the coronavirus, and the 2021 session was mostly virtual with the public prohibited to attend floor sessions.

Early Monday, advocates for paid family leave gathered at Lawyer’s Mall outside the State House with coffee and muffins, hoping for a moment to persuade lawmakers to take up that policy during the 2022 session.

The seven-foot-tall, plexiglass booths that surrounded senators’ desks that created a mouse-like maze on the Senate floor during the 2021 regular session were gone. All members of the House of Delegates were back in their chamber, none relegated to participating remotely.

Republican Senate leaders acknowledged Monday that Democrats’ supermajorities in both chambers essentially meant that the other party could “do anything they want, because they don’t need the minority’s voice.”

Advertisement

When it comes to redistricting, Sen. Bryan W. Simonaire (R-Anne Arundel) expected that “at the end of the day, the majority party is going to ram through their gerrymandered map, and I think that really hurts the people.”

The map backed by legislative Democrats received an F grade from the Princeton Gerrymandering Project for both partisan fairness and geographic compactness. It would likely create seven safe Democratic districts while making the sole Republican district competitive. The citizen commission’s map, by contrast, received an A grade from the Princeton project.

Story continues below advertisement

Democratic leaders, however, have defended the legislative commission’s map as more compact than the current one and the product of hours of public hearings statewide. Majority Leader Eric Luedtke (D-Montgomery) noted that some of the “bad grades” the map received were because the map doesn’t hew to county lines, but he argued county lines weren’t everything. It was more important, he said, that the map be representative of modern life.

Advertisement

“People don’t separate their lives based on county lines,” Luedtke said. “County lines drawn by colonial surveyors 300 years ago simply aren’t an accurate reflection of how people live their lives in modern day-Maryland.”

He pointed to the Eastern Shore-anchored 1st District jumping the Chesapeake Bay to include parts of Anne Arundel County as one example, arguing the Bay binds the communities. That change would make the Republican district more competitive for Democrats.

Story continues below advertisement

Several Eastern Shore residents supported the jump across the Bay Bridge, saying they’re more connected to that area than to northern counties that border Pennsylvania that are currently part of the 1st District. Chestertown resident Louise Miller says she crosses the bridge to visit doctors, businesses and friends. “Annapolis is very much part of my sphere, my social sphere,” she said. (Annapolis would be part of the 4th District in the map.)

Advertisement

But Republicans cast doubt on Democrats’ justifications of the convoluted lines, instead charging that they were necessary to maintain a Democratic advantage in Congress. The 3rd District, for example, spans six jurisdictions stretching from the D.C. suburbs in Montgomery County, dipping into Baltimore and then reaching out to the Susquehanna River. The 5th District has what one Republican described as an “umbilical cord” attaching rural southern Prince George’s County to College Park.

House Minority Leader Jason Buckel (R-Allegany) asked Luedtke if he would describe the districts as compact and contiguous. But Luedtke declined to answer the question and referred him back to his testimony.

A number of residents identifying as Republicans who spoke on redistricting said they were being denied fair representation, urging lawmakers to instead support the citizens’ commission map. Samuel Morse, a lawyer identifying as a Democrat, said he was “concerned about what the proliferation of partisan redistricting means for the health of our democracy.”

Advertisement

If Democrats pass the legislative map clearly advantaging Democrats, Morse said, “you’ll make clear that you’re committed to democratic ideals so long as they further your political goals.”

Hogan, who has made nonpartisan redistricting a signature issue, submitted written testimony in support of the map drawn by his advisory commission, which was composed of a mix of Democrats, Republicans and independents.

Story continues below advertisement

“Free and fair elections are the very foundation of American democracy and the most basic promise that those in power can pledge to citizens,” Hogan wrote. “Here in Maryland, nonpartisan redistricting reform has been overdue for decades and has saddled our state with the unfortunate distinction of being home to the most gerrymandered districts in the nation.”

Hogan has never personally testified before the legislature, a break from his predecessors who made personal appeals on their signature issues, such as repealing the death penalty. And while it is uncommon for Hogan to submitted personal written testimony, his spokesman Michael Ricci noted the governor has done it before during his campaign to start the school year after Labor Day.

Ricci said the governor was in Las Vegas until Tuesday, attending a real estate conference held by the shopping center industry group ICSC.

Simonaire said that if the committee advances the map proposal supported by Democrats, Republicans planned to put forth an amendment substituting the citizens’ map on the Senate floor. That way, he said, Democrats would have to go on the record supporting or opposing the citizens’ commission map.

“Quite frankly,” he said, “we know what the vote is going to be before we take it, so I think we should get the legislators on the record: Do they want to stand with the citizens’ map, or do they want to stand with the political map generated by politicians?”

Compared to a decade ago, the legislature’s redistricting process offered far more opportunities for the public to review its work and offer comments, but some told lawmakers they need to do better.

Advertisement

Joanne Antoine, executive director of the watchdog group Common Cause, praised the legislature for making this year’s process more transparent, inclusive and interactive. But she said it’s still “flawed” and said it was futile for public to weigh in.

“People are disengaged because they know their input will receive very little consideration,” she said, adding Common Cause couldn’t support either map. “We take no position today because the outcome is preordained.”

Hogan vowed to veto any maps he deems “unfair,” though the Democrats have enough votes to override him.
