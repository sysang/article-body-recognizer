How a new opening in Helsinki is reviving the joy of the railway hotel

The Finnish capital’s Grand Central harks back to a heyday of Art Nouveau, international bonhomie, champagne flutes and tinkling pianos

By Mark Stratton 6 December 2021 • 11:38am

There’s something silver-screen matinee about the experience of a railway hotel. Most of my rail journeys these days are utilitarian affairs, no passionate platform reunions amid the swirling vapour of a steam train, once a mainstay of celluloid reels from yesteryear. 

Yet I still yearn, when alighting a train after a long journey, for the convenience and romance of a station hotel. I imagine checking into a foyer packed with railway memorabilia, a bellhop wheeling my suitcase to my room. The brasserie’s open, of course, for a flute of champagne before dinner. Maybe a pianist tinkling away. Play it again, Sam. Although Casablanca never did have a station hotel. 

Few stations do, nowadays. Some linger on behind glorious edifices. Nashville’s beautiful Union Station Hotel, for one, a 19th-century neo-Romanesque jewel, and closer to home, the St Pancras Renaissance, an 1873 lovechild of Gothic revivalist Sir George Gilbert Scott.

It was thus welcome news when Scandic Hotels launched the Grand Central with little fanfare this year – within Helsinki’s cavernous Art Nouveau station, one of Europe’s finest. 

Before a two-year transformation to a 491-room hotel, the Grand Central was the national railway company’s head offices, located in the western bowels of a station designed by Finland’s answer to Frank Lloyd-Wright, Eliel Saarinen. Those offices were completed in 1909, followed by the main station – with a wondrous arched entrance flanked by four stone Teutonic-looking strongmen cradling globe lamps – in 1914. 

It wasn’t an exacting rail journey getting there – just 35 minutes from Helsinki-Vantaa Airport. No bellhop was waiting either. Yet I positively pleaded to haul my own luggage four floors just so I could sweep up a wondrous split marble staircase with smooth dark wood bannisters to my room – globe lamps and bays of tall rectangular windows on each floor illuminating a sullen Helsinki afternoon. 

My high-ceiling room was in the original part of the building where all the rooms were once pen pushers' offices. The room retained touches of early 20th-century furnishing – chunky iron radiators and a herringbone oak floor, while the view over the railway station square was an architectural sweep across the ages – Helsinki’s iconic Art Nouveau clock tower dressed in red granite and lit magenta at night, the 1887 Neo-Renaissance Art Museum, and Helsinki’s most popular department store, Stockmann, in 1920s Art Deco. 

A new annex has added 118 rooms behind the original building to create a courtyard into which tables flow from their new brasserie. There’s a sunning area, too, for those exiting the basement sauna. A few white-robed Finns braved the cold, displaying their legendary sisu. Yet the new wing is faithful in design to the four-storey original because this is one of Finland’s most protected buildings. It marks the transcendence from the rustic medieval motifs of modern romanticism (all bears and gargoyles), widespread in Helsinki, to the rationalised sleekness of Art Nouveau as local architects looked towards America and Central European Jugendstil. 

Eliel Saarinen won a competition to design the hotel in 1904. “His rivals were jealous, criticising his original modern romantic design proposal, saying it wasn’t forward-looking,” said Sari Saarinen, who showed me around. Eliel was her grandfather’s uncle. “He went away and travelled and came back with the revised plans, more modern.” 

That evening I sat in Bar G, which occupies former ticketing offices. This new contemporary space offers brighter upholstery, the windows of the bar stencilled with two giant peacocks, although this doesn’t supplant the spirit of Art Nouveau found throughout the hotel. 

After dinner in the emptyish brasserie, I sipped a Late Night in Helsinki cocktail (limoncello spritz) in a booth. Beneath globed lighting shaped like dangling cherries, I listened to three Spanish businessmen, their accents mixing with the otherwise Finnish crowd. 

The international bonhomie I so craved of a station hotel – bellhop or otherwise – suggested the delights of bygone rail travel were getting back on track.

Stay there
