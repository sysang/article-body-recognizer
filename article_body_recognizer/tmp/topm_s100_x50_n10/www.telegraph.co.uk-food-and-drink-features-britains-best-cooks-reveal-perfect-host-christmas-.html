Britain's best cooks reveal how to be the perfect host this Christmas

Dame Mary Berry, Nadiya Hussain and Ella Mills on how to survive the festive period, the best dishes to make and easy menu planning

By Mary Berry ; Nadiya Hussain and Deliciously Ella 5 December 2021 • 6:00am

Christmas can be a stressful period; juggling the cooking of the turkey, sides and dessert, while meeting and greeting family members and wrapping presents can be a little intimidating – not to mention the endless festive gatherings of family and friends. 

Don't fret, we've all been there. That's why we've talked to three of Britain’s best-loved cooks – Dame Mary Berry, Ella Mills of Deliciously Ella and Nadiya Hussain – for their take on how to be the perfect host at any time of year, along with stories of their most-cherished memories of family feasts and dinner parties. 

Dame Merry Berry

'I love a traditional roast at Christmas, including my mother’s lemon and thyme sausage-meat stuffing' 

My favourite dinner-party memory is from last year. I’ve hosted many meals over the years but this particular one, after lockdown, felt very special, as all the family came together for the first time in a while: my husband Paul and I, our two children and their other halves, and our five grandchildren.

What a happy time it was – we felt so grateful to all be together. Luckily everyone gets on well. The cousins hadn’t seen each for months – in children’s lives this can seem a long time – so it was an evening spent celebrating, but also remembering those who’d had a tough time.

I cooked and Paul sorted the booze (red wine and sauvignon blanc, as well as elderflower cordial for the children). Cooking is my passion and I love it; it’s never a chore. I find it relaxing to potter in the kitchen, thinking of the menu I am going to prepare.

We moved house a few years ago and in my new kitchen I have a large island where I arrange the ingredients and get organised. I write lists all the time and it’s the same when I’m cooking: I write down the meal plan and what I need to do – there is a particular satisfaction in crossing things off the list.

At that particular dinner, I laid the table before the family arrived with my best grey linen napkins, candles in antique stands and a jug of pink roses picked from the garden. Around that time we were testing recipes for my new book, Love to Cook, so I made little Thai veg rice wraps as canapés, which I prepared ahead, then we had roast fillet of beef – such a treat – with fresh horseradish sauce. 

I like to do a roast as it always feels like a special occasion. My granddaughter Abby is vegetarian (with a hint of vegan!), so I made her burgers with lentils and mushrooms.

I also made a platter of my ‘magical prepare-ahead vegetables’ – peas, sugar snap peas, roasted squash, potato and celeriac purée, and Tenderstem broccoli. It’s great to make vegetables in advance and reheat them at the last minute, without ending up in a sea of pots and pans to wash up.

To finish we had everyone’s favourite: lemon and lime meringue tranche. I make it in a long, slim tin, which is easier to portion than a round one. With pouring double cream it’s always a winner.

That night, the wine was flowing and it was so good to hear laughter and stories around the table. Our family loves games – the children decide which one to play, usually Who Am I?, where we all stick the name of someone famous on our foreheads and guess who we are. I think I was David Beckham – I was delighted!

I’ll be hosting again this Christmas. We love a traditional roast: a KellyBronze turkey with classic trimmings, including my mother’s lemon and thyme sausage-meat stuffing, which I now make.

The family all arrive on Christmas morning. I always say, the more the merrier as long as I have a rough idea of numbers in advance! We like to dress smartly and afterwards everyone has a turn at reciting a funny act or song, which is lovely. Then it’s time for a Christmas Day walk with all the dogs.

What Mary cooked

Thai veg rice wraps; roast fillet of beef, platter of vegetables (vegetarian option: lentil and mushroom burgers); lemon and lime meringue tranche with double cream.

Mary’s hosting tip

Prepare the food in the morning and get everything organised before the guests arrive.

Nadiya Hussain

'The words ‘dinner party’ could not be more alien to me – I can’t think of anything more uncomfortable'

The words ‘dinner party’ could not be more alien to me. Even the idea of a dinner party feels far too civilised and ordered – and it’s not something that I grew up with. My parents never hosted them when I was a child and now as an adult, I can’t think of anything more uncomfortable. I’m sure with the right food and perfect guests, it can be wonderful, but still I cannot bring myself to host one. 

Perhaps it’s just the words: there is a notion that a ‘dinner party’ must be about perfectly lit flower-dressed tables and social-media-polished food. That scares me a little bit. As a lover of food and people, restricting either of those two [to what fits on and around a table] is enough to make me run a mile.

Instead, my ideal home-cooked feast or get-together would be filled with people I adore and food that I have made with love, and movement. I don’t mean in terms of music and dancing, but using the whole house, to eat, to sit, to chat, to enjoy, not just the food but the company – in the safety and familiarity of the surroundings.

This is the kind of get-together I grew up with and it has been the pattern that has followed me into adulthood in my own home. In terms of the numbers, there was no way you could have had a sit-down dinner with 30-plus people. Even if we just invite family, there are more than 30 of us, with all the children and adults and elders, ages ranging from nine months to 90 years.

My fondest family-dinner memory dates back to childhood when my dad brought a whole butchered sheep (divided up into boxes) into our home in Luton. My brothers, sisters and I had only just come back from school, but we were instructed to dig the biggest hole in the only patch of garden that didn’t have exotic Bangladeshi vegetables growing. 

Then a fire was lit in it, and we all put an enormous pot on top and spent the night cooking this sheep, stirring in bags of chopped onions, fresh garlic and coriander, while Dad tended to the fire, which was at the mercy of the wind.

As the curry bubbled away, people started to drift in – kids from school, cousins from college, uncles from work, aunties bearing babies, and my granny sat in the corner watching.

If you could find a plate you could eat and it was piled high with warm rice and hot mutton curry. The party carried on through the night, until the pot was empty, but the fire was still burning enough to make a huge vat of milky sweet chai. 

I remember grown-ups laughed into the night, keeping warm around the embers, while us kids found spaces on couches or bunked together in beds to sleep, or else stayed awake watching television.

In recent years, I have replicated what my dad used to do, but with half the sheep, half the people and a much smaller hole in the ground. For me, these nights are all about the food, the people and the freedom to just spend time together and relax, just the way my family did it when I was a child.

What Nadiya cooked

Samosas, mutton korma, a vat of rice, cake and chai.

Nadiya’s hosting tip

Fill your home with people you love and food you adore, and just relax – no formalities (or tables) required.

Ella Mills (Deliciously Ella) 

'I’ve loved cooking for friends and family again – between toddlers and a pandemic I’ve not done much of it recently – and I can’t wait for Christmas' 

I’m a huge believer in the idea that food should be for sharing, a way of bringing us closer. When I switched to a plant-based diet 10 years ago, my friends and family were very sceptical and it felt a little isolating, so the first meal that won my closest girlfriends over was a huge moment… Luckily, they licked the plates clean and asked for the recipes.

I’m not a huge planner and love recipes that are relatively straightforward but bursting with flavour, colour and texture, which feels important when changing peoples’ preconceptions that plant-based cooking is bland. My favourite kind of group dinner normally involves sharing plates. 

I like to make a range of dishes, so that the table is filled to the brim and looks inviting. I always add a loaf of crusty sourdough with olive oil and sea salt when I’m doing a big salad spread, and pick recipes with different lead colours – this spread had dishes that were orange, yellow and green, and a super-creamy bowl with a purple/brown hue.

I’m not sure if I’m the best hostess now – juggling two toddlers and a busy business doesn’t leave a lot of time for extra prep and decoration – but I’ve learnt that simple can be brilliant, too. When I made this dinner, I was just getting Deliciously Ella off the ground, so was in a similar position time-wise and focused on simple things: little candles, colourful dishes, a bottle of red wine and long-overdue catch-ups, which you can’t beat.

The first dish, still a favourite now, was a bowl of spiced peanut sweet potatoes, cooked with ginger, cinnamon and cumin, then tossed in a smooth peanut dressing with sesame seeds and parsley. Next, I made a turmeric-spiced cauliflower, lentil and tahini salad, with garlic, sultanas and lemon. For a little greenery, I roasted Tenderstem broccoli with salt until crispy, then tossed it with garlic-infused olive oil and sea salt.

To bring it all together, I prepared a smoky aubergine dip – I love something creamy with salads. It’s made by roasting the aubergines until golden, then scooping out the flesh and pulsing it with peanut butter, sesame, smoked paprika, garlic and tahini. It’s so good!

In the past few months, I’ve loved cooking for friends and family again – between toddlers and a pandemic I’ve not done much of it recently – and I can’t wait for Christmas. This will be our first one spent with my family and our children: Skye is two and May is one. 

I’m planning a glazed wellington (carrots, mushrooms, thyme, garlic and onion) with rosemary roast potatoes, a rich gravy, balsamic roast carrots, crispy spouts and chestnut, sage and onion ‘sausage’ rolls. For pudding, perhaps a clementine and chocolate cake. I’m still pondering over that, but you can’t go wrong with something chocolatey.

What Ella cooked

Peanut sweet potatoes; spiced cauliflower, lentil and tahini salad; roasted Tenderstem broccoli; aubergine dip; sourdough.

Ella’s hosting tip

Prepare sharing plates to keep it sociable – and pick recipes with different lead colours.
