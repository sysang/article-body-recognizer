UK accused of 'travel apartheid' after Nigeria added to Covid red list

Nigeria’s high commissioner in London says Britain's decision in the wake of the omicron variant is 'wrong', 'panicky' and unscientific

By Charles Hymas, Home Affairs Editor 6 December 2021 • 9:41am

Nigeria has accused Britain of a “travel apartheid” by adding the African country to its red list, forcing all arrivals into quarantine.

Sarafa Tunji Isola, Nigeria’s high commissioner in London, said the decision to include Nigeria on the red list alongside 10 other African countries was “wrong”, “panicky”, unscientific and discriminatory.

He said Covid was a global pandemic and not restricted to Nigeria or other African countries, claiming most of the 67 passengers who tested positive for the omicron variant on arrival in the country were from Britain. “What is expected is a global approach, not a selective travel ban,” he said.

Mr Isola said it was on that basis that he “aligned” with the view of Antonio Guterres, the UN Secretary-General, that Britain’s decision amounted to “travel apartheid”.

Speaking on BBC Radio 4, he said that there had been 67 passengers who had tested positive on arrival in Nigeria, of which “the majority” were from the UK. There had been just three other cases within Nigeria, he said.

He added that omicron had also been classified as a mild variant. “There have been no hospitalisations, no deaths. It is quite different from the delta variant. This has to be based on scientific and empirical evidence, not a panicky situation,” he said.

The high commissioner said that while Nigeria was seeking to boost and deepen diplomatic relations with the UK, its travel ban would “hamper” that. “This position should be reviewed. We should not wait for three weeks to review it. To err is human and we must admit that it’s an error,” he said.

Kit Malthouse, the Policing Minister, said the use of language by the commissioner was “unfortunate”, but he said the measures were necessary to “buy time” for the UK to understand how difficult it might be to cope with the new variant.

He pledged that the restrictions would be reviewed after three weeks, but noted that they had been introduced in the interests of the “safety of the British people”. 

All arrivals from Nigeria and the 10 other countries on the red list including South Africa, Zimbabwe and Namibia are required to quarantine for 10 days and 11 nights at a cost of up to £2,285 per person.

It comes amid a growing backlash from the travel industry, Tory MPs and scientists at the reintroduction of pre-departure tests for all arrivals, on top of a requirement for all travellers to undergo a PCR test within two days of arrival.

Professor Mark Woolhouse, a government adviser, said on Sunday that the reintroduction of pre-travel Covid tests is like “shutting the stable door after the horse has bolted”.

He said that the rules had come “too late” because the omicron variant was “already spreading pretty rapidly” in Britain.

His warning came after the number of Omicron cases reported in the UK rose by 50 per cent in the last 24 hours, taking the total to 246.

Scientists in South Africa, where Omicron was first identified, said that although the variant appeared to be spreading faster, vaccines seemed to be protecting against it and the disease it caused was milder.

Boris Johnson is planning to decide by the end of next week whether further coronavirus restrictions are needed over Christmas.

The decision to reintroduce pre-departure tests, which will come into effect at 4am on Tuesday, was taken after the UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) found that the window between people being infected with the omicron variant and becoming infectious was smaller than with the delta variant.

However, the decision to bring back pre-travel tests has caused a fresh split among cabinet ministers. Dominic Raab, the Deputy Prime Minister, said on Times Radio on Sunday that the new rules would make only a “marginal difference” in stopping the spread of the new variant.

He acknowledged that the restrictions were “another burden” on the public, but insisted they were part of a package of “incremental” restrictions that would help to avoid a future lockdown.

Grant Shapps, the Transport Secretary, is understood to have opposed the reintroduction of the pre-departure test after telling The Telegraph it would “kill off the travel sector again”.
