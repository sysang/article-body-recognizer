Why every abandoned bundle will always find love with the Dogs Trust

At the Dogs Trust rehoming centre in Leeds, we meet the staff and volunteers who devote their lives to helping man’s best friend

By Jack Rear 5 December 2021 • 10:00am

On the morning I’m visiting the Dogs Trust rehoming centre just outside Leeds, there’s another arrival, a tiny two-day-old French bulldog puppy no bigger than a can of pop. Blind in both eyes, she was found in a park and taken to the local dog warden, who has brought her to the centre. Staff suspect she’d been dumped by a commercial dog breeder after they realised she was a “defective product” and couldn’t be sold for thousands of pounds, as her siblings will have been.

Emma Cooper, assistant manager of the centre, who lives on site, says that although this is by no means the first such case they have seen, they remain non-judgmental. “We try not to judge people,” she says. “If we start judging people, they won’t bring dogs to us, and if they don’t, we can’t help them. We just want to do our best for the dogs.”

For outsiders, it might be surprising to see the empathy that the staff afford to some of the former owners of dogs that end up in their care. Living at the centre, Cooper has experienced her share of “bundle on the doorstep” moments. One Valentine’s Day, she found a box at the door; she assumed it was a donation, but inside was a puppy with a broken leg.

“A couple of months ago, somebody buzzed the intercom and said: ‘There’s a dog here that needs your help’,” she continues. “I thought that was strange, so I walked up to the entrance and found they’d tied it to the gate and driven off. You have to think, although it’s not right to do that, it’s better than the alternative of dumping her somewhere where she was never going to be found or could have been run over.”

Thankfully, Dogs Trust’s policy is never to put a healthy dog down, and that means each and every one is given a chance.

By the morning’s end, Pamela Walker, a retired ICU nurse and dog foster carer, arrives to pick up Cassandra, the little Frenchie, who staff invited me to name. Walker has been working with the Leeds rehoming centre as a foster carer for the past 12 years.

“I tend to take puppies, and because of my nursing background, I’ve always been happy to take any dog that’s poorly or had operations,” she explains. “If there’s one they don’t think will ­survive, I’m happy to take it. Many won’t take those dogs, but with me it doesn’t matter. It doesn’t happen very often; I’ve had a couple and I knew what I was getting into. I can always say those dogs have had a lovely time, they were in a home and cared for, they had meals, they were able to curl up by the fire and have some comfort, have as nice a little life as they could have for as long as they could. I think that’s a good thing that’s worth doing. There are other carers who take the very elderly dogs. They know they’re not going to live that long, but they get to enjoy their last months.”

Fostering has become an increasingly important part of Dogs Trust’s work, especially since lockdowns have restricted the amount of staff and ­volunteers who can work on site. Besides, the benefits of living in a proper home are manifold. Older dogs and those wary of other canines are less stressed, those with medical conditions can be observed and cared for more closely, anxious dogs can learn to live with the comings and goings of life at home; people coming to the door, traffic outside, the sounds of washing machines and kettles; all of which helps them settle in more easily when they do find lifelong homes.

Amy Ripley, the home-from-home manager, chuckles as she recounts the regular headaches she faces when valued foster carers end up falling in love with their house guests and adopting them on a permanent basis: “It’s a nice thing, but it means we have to find more foster carers.”

The pandemic has been a boon to Ripley’s efforts. As carers are asked to be at home for the dogs as much as possible, traditionally this has meant most foster carers are retirees, but during lockdown younger people started signing up.

“Dogs tend to be in their foster homes for four to six weeks, so it’s almost a try-before-you-buy for a lot of foster carers,” Ripley explains. “We have some who have never owned a dog and they want to test the responsibility aspects. Until they have a dog, a lot of people don’t realise how much it entails in terms of not being able to go out whenever they want. We have some people who do a couple of months and decide it’s not for them, others who love it and continue fostering, and others who take the plunge and adopt dogs.”

All carers need is a relatively quiet home life, to be around for the dog, and ideally a car to take it to veterinary appointments. While the process is voluntary, foster carers are provided with all they need: food, equipment and medication, so it’s just a matter of treating the dog as though it’s their own until it finds a permanent home.

“Friends ask how I can bear to give them up, but there are a lot of others you can’t help if you don’t,” explains Walker. “When I was working on the wards, I never said to my husband ‘there’s a super little old lady, we’re going to keep her’. You do have a slight detachment.”

Walker is often called in to take care of litters of puppies. She’s become adept at hand-rearing them and is filled with pride when they are adopted.

“I do quite a bit of training with them as they get older. We do a bit on the lead, we get used to the noises outside, we go round the garden. That makes it much easier for a new owner who hasn’t had a puppy before. It sets them on a good path,” she explains.

Emma Cooper is one of the people in charge of pairing up the centre’s dogs with their future owners. As we chat, she keeps a watchful eye on Max, a five-month-old fluffy little Terrier-cross who has been living with her since he was brought to the centre after being abandoned at the vet’s after he fractured his elbow. Vet costs are often a factor in why dogs end up with Dogs Trust, she says. Thankfully, Max shouldn’t prove a problem to rehome: after just 24 hours, his profile has received upwards of 60 adoption applications.

“Covid has changed things,” says Cooper. “Before, people would come and look at the dogs, pick the one they liked, then we’d tell them a bit about it and see if they matched. Now, it’s all done online, which is working well: the dogs are a lot less stressed without ­having hordes of people looking at them all day. It also gives the dogs that aren’t on view a fairer chance of getting a home.”

People look at the website and find a dog they think would be a match, then they submit an application form. Cooper and the team go through the applications and find the most suitable. “You’d be surprised how many people apply because they like the look of a dog, but don’t match the criteria in the profile, so we discount those; then we narrow it down by whose lifestyle will suit it: have they owned dogs before, do they have small children, and so on.”

A suitable home was found for Max within the day and his potential adopters will be coming to meet him in a few days. “If they like him, we’ll put a reserve on him, then they have to go away and sleep on it for at least one night. After that, they’ll send us pictures and videos of their garden and home via ­WhatsApp, so we can do a virtual home visit, then they come again and take the dog home,” says Cooper.

For dogs with complicated needs, the process can take longer, with adopters having to visit more often so the animal can get to know them, or even go on temporary “sleepover” visits to their house before it goes home permanently.

Cooper’s office is festooned with pictures of the dogs she has helped rehome, and she gets visibly emotional when discussing the ones she’s most proud of, such as Wonky, a puppy whose deformed front legs made her a sensation in the local paper six years ago. After a week of doing basic physiotherapy and massaging Wonky’s legs in front of the TV each night, Cooper managed to heal the deformity and send her off to the home where she currently lives.

It’s dogs such as Wonky, those with more challenging needs and behaviours, who need more care and attention before they’re ready to be rehomed, that have a special place in the heart of the centre’s staff.

“Everyone says that you have to not get attached, but everyone does,” laughs Kevin Johnson, a canine carer of seven years, two of them as a volunteer, who takes me along to exercise Lucy, an excitable five-month-old lurcher-cross who zooms around the centre’s agility course.

Work starts first thing with the canine carers, who are responsible for the day-to-day welfare of the dogs on site. The morning begins with taking all of the on-site dogs out for a short walk to stretch their legs and do their business. While half the canine-care team handles that, the other half focuses on cleaning the kennels. Afterwards, it’s feeding time, with each dog having its own personal diet plan, focused around their likes and dislikes, and any medication they might need.

Then it’s down to doing sessions with the dogs, taking them out of the kennels. “We might take them for a big walk on site or even drive them to a nearby woodland or something,” says Johnson. “We might do some training or just spend time socialising by cuddling in their kennel. We vary it so they don’t get bored. Every dog will get different sessions. You might be working with a dog that is fine with everything. You can take them for a trot around and do whatever. Then you’ll put that dog back in the kennel and get your next one out and he might be reactive to other dogs and wary of new people. So you’d have to think where you can take that dog, what you can do to make sure it’s getting quality time out of its kennel. It’s no good taking a dog out of its kennel just to give it a session where it’s going to be stressed.”

I sat in on a session with Coco, a 10-year-old Lhasa Apso who has been at the centre a long time. His ongoing skin condition requires him to take twice-daily medicated baths, which has put a lot of adopters off, despite the dog’s easy temperament. If he’d ended up elsewhere, Coco may have been put down, but here he is, gamely wagging his tail and enjoying himself as his ­carers bathe him.

More recently, Johnson has begun working as the centre’s media assistant. On top of Dogs Trust Leeds’ social-media feeds, Johnson creates the dogs’ adoption profiles on the website, a tricky balancing act.

“We want to show the dog honestly, but in a positive light,” Johnson explains. “The public need to know if the dog has issues they should be aware of before they apply. We wouldn’t want to go down the route of introducing the dog to an owner who isn’t prepared to meet its needs. Equally, we also want to celebrate each dog’s personality and character, and let adopters imagine how that dog might fit into their lives.”

The more time the dogs spend with Dogs Trust, the better Johnson can get to know them and the richer their profiles become. He mentions Copper, a dog living with a foster carer, who wasn’t attracting much interest. After Johnson went to visit and photograph him in his “natural habitat”, feeling relaxed and at ease in his foster home, applications began pouring in. By the time this article goes out, he’ll be in his forever home.

As the day comes to a close, Cooper, Johnson and the rest of the team gather in the rehoming centre lobby to celebrate another success. They’re seeing off Walter, a two-year-old German Shepherd-cross who was handed into Dogs Trust when his boisterous ­behaviour became too much to manage for his elderly owners. After some work with the centre’s team of training and behaviour advisers, Walter is being picked up by Roy and Penny Kettlewell, who will be taking him home today. The couple’s last dog was from the same rehoming centre, and during the pandemic, the couple decided to open their home to another canine companion.

“I was helping my son and daughter look after their dogs, which satisfied my pang for one, then lockdown occurred and all that dog care stopped and I realised ‘I need my own dog’, so I’ve had to persuade Roy,” says Penny. Her husband gives a wry smile; it’s obvious he hasn’t needed too much persuasion.

“It’s not as easy as you think, getting a rescue dog,” adds Roy. “You can ring up a pedigree dog breeder one day and go and pick the dog up the next. They don’t care where the dog goes, but Dogs Trust insists on the environment being ideal. You have to qualify for it.”

While Walter’s training will continue at home with the Kettlewells (all pets adopted from Dogs Trust get access to free advice and training from behavioural experts for their entire lifetime), it’s clear how excited everyone is to get him on his way. Penny has a pocket full of slices of cheese, Walter’s favourite snack, to get him settled into the car. He repays the favour with an affectionate lick.

Everyone is smiling as they wave Walter off to his forever home in one of the moments that make the job joyous for everyone involved. It’s a high that lingers in the air as the car drives away, then it’s back to work; there are more dogs to walk, more towels to wash, more profiles to vet. The work seems never-ending, but for dogs like Walter, Copper, Coco, Lucy, Wonky, Max and Cassandra, there’s no denying the world is a better place for it.
