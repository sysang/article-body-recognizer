The best new novels and fiction books to buy for Christmas 2021

From Sally Rooney’s anxious millennials to Jonathan Franzen’s priapic pastor, the year’s best novels sought clarity amid the chaos

By Sameer Rahim 6 December 2021 • 12:23pm

Will technology save the novel? The pandemic might have closed bookshops for many months, but the demand for ebooks and especially audiobooks soared. Libraries started lending digitally, offering a lifeline to those reluctant to handle borrowed paperbacks. At the same time, the technological revolution has become a rich subject for some of the best novels of 2021.

In Kazuo Ishiguro’s Klara and the Sun (Faber, £20), the title character is a robot, an Artificial Friend purchased as a companion for an ill teenager named Josie. Events take a sinister turn when we realise that Josie’s family want to preserve her posthumous memory by uploading her personality on to Klara. While Ishiguro has written on similar themes before with more subtlety, this is still a fine effort coming four years after his Nobel Prize.

Similar sci-fi reincarnation takes place in Bewilderment by veteran US novelist Richard Powers (Cornerstone, £18.99), in which an astrophysicist mourning his dead wife uses an AI technology called Decoded Neurofeedback to recreate her emotions in their son, nine-year-old Robin. Set against the backdrop of environmental devastation and a Trump-like presidency, this novel sometimes feels weighed down by its own contemporary themes. But the ending packs a serious emotional punch.

Patricia Lockwood’s No One is Talking About This (Bloomsbury, £14.99) begins as a very funny satire on our obsession with the internet and especially social media: “She had become famous for a post that said simply, ‘Can a dog be twins?’ That was it. ‘Can a dog be twins?’” Written in short paragraphs that could almost be tweets themselves, the novel’s first half can be read – or should I say scrolled through? – with addictive ease. The darker second half asks tough questions about whether some things simply cannot be shared online.

Two entertaining satires of big tech and surveillance capitalism – The Startup Wife by Tahmima Anam (Canongate, £14.99) and The Every by Dave Eggers (Penguin, £12.99), the follow-up to 2013’s The Circle – could not, I’m afraid, keep up with the sheer weirdness of Facebook (sorry, Meta) head Mark Zuckerberg’s plan to colonise our minds with virtual reality.

Sally Rooney made her name by describing minutely the love – and sex – lives of digital natives, smartly incorporating texts and emojis into her fiction. Her third novel, Beautiful World, Where Are You (Faber, £16.99), once again tackles the on-off romances of overeducated millennials but in a more distant style, reflected in the long emails exchanged between her protagonists Alice and Eileen. You sense Rooney is dissatisfied with her own vertiginous success. Alice, a famous novelist, reflects: “Only afterwards, when I had received the money and acclaim which I believed I deserved, did I understand that it was not possible for anyone to deserve these things.” So where is the beautiful world? Perhaps surprisingly, Eileen looks to the Catholic Church and the figure of Jesus. Or maybe she’s just fallen hard for handsome, Mass-going Simon.

Gwendoline Riley published her acclaimed first novel Cold Water at the age of 22. Though never enjoying Rooney-level hype, in the two decades since she has produced pin-sharp novels that examine relationships with an unsparing eye. In My Phantoms (Granta, £12.99), Bridget has to deal with an awful father and a mother so irritating she can only bear to see her once a year. Though, as we come to realise, Bridget is hardly blameless for the family breakdown. Elizabeth Strout’s Oh William! (Viking, £14.99), told in a much cosier, chattier style, is no less insightful about the complications of parents and partners. In the third novel of the beguiling Lucy Barton sequence, the widower looks back on the foibles and faults of her two husbands, the recently deceased David and her first husband William, who cheated on her when they were married but is now asking for help to deal with a recently discovered family member. In the same vein, the Booker-longlisted A Town Called Solace (Chatto Windus, £14.99) by Mary Lawson, a drama about three damaged people living in 1970s Canada, provided old-fashioned narrative pleasures, sharp observation and no little humour. The kind of novel that’s often patronised, it’s actually much harder to pull off than Lawson makes it seem.

Lost children are a theme in Roddy Doyle’s story collection Life Without Children (Vintage, £14.99). In the most affecting, The Five Lamps, a man takes advantage of a Dublin emptied by lockdown to search for his teenage son, who went missing four years earlier. Wandering the streets like Leopold Bloom (who has visions of his own lost son in Dublin’s streets), the man shows a woman with a buggy a photo of the boy eating chips. “The chippers are all shut,” she exclaims in surprise. But that was a different time.

In Sarah Hall’s Burntcoat (Faber, £11.99), a Covid-like virus called AG3 turns the world upside-down: once you are infected, there is no chance of survival. Edith, a sculptor, embarks on a risky affair with a Turkish waiter whom she tends to as he fades. I was prepared to be sceptical about the first wave of Covid-lit to hit the shelves but both Doyle and Hall, in different ways, are movingly memorialising our experiences of a pandemic that may yet, like AG3, come back when we least expect it.

In a year when travelling abroad was tough, Maggie Shipstead’s epic Great Circle (Doubleday, £16.99) flew us to a different world. The novel tells two intersecting stories. The principal one is the life of Marian Graves, an aviatrix from the first half of the 20th century. A secondary plot follows Hadley, a Hollywood star playing Marian in a contemporary film. Though Shipstead makes clever points about how stories are twisted over the decades, she also provides a rollicking plot. It’s a book to devour the next time you get on a plane.

To win a literary prize requires you to have written a very good novel; but it also helps to illuminate a theme of the moment. The Promise by Damon Galgut (Chatto Windus, £16.99), which won the Booker, does both. The Swarts are a South African family who embody what is these days called “white privilege”. We meet them at four funerals held at four stages of the country’s recent past: during apartheid in 1986; among the rainbow nation hopes of 1995; in Thabo Mbeki’s uneasy continuation of Mandela’s legacy; and, in 2018, as the country descends into violence.

The promise of the title is made on her deathbed by Rachel Swart, who requests that their black servant Salome inherit her house. Of course that promise, like that of a harmonious post-apartheid South Africa, is not fulfilled. Technically ingenious, the novel is narrated by a boorish character who reflects the prejudices of the Swarts. Salome, throughout, remains a silent presence – waiting, as Galgut has said, for a different sort of writer to give her a voice.

The Zanzibari novelist Abdulrazak Gurnah, who has lived in Britain since 1968, was awarded 2021’s Nobel Prize in literature for telling just such different stories. In serious, well-researched and subtle fiction, he has been exploring the legacy of colonialism – Arab, British and German – in East Africa throughout his 10 novels. If you don’t know his work, start with Paradise (1994), a re-telling of the Joseph/Yusuf story that brilliantly evokes the sights and smells of the Swahili coast.

Born in 1980 in what is now Somaliland, Nadifa Mohamed moved to the UK six years later. Her Booker-shortlisted novel The Fortune Men (Viking, £14.99) fictionalises the true story of a terrible miscarriage of justice in 1950s Cardiff, when an innocent Somali man named Mahmood Mattan was convicted of killing a Jewish shop worker – and hanged. Mohamed’s novel, very much in the US genre of exposing racial injustice, is also an atmospheric account of Tiger Bay in 1952 and of the forgotten multiculturalism that allowed Mattan to marry a local girl, Laura, who for years campaigned to clear his name.

Having tackled suicide bombers and illegal immigrants in his previous books, British novelist Sunjeev Sahota is clearly unafraid of big subjects. His exquisitely written China Room (Harvil Secker, £16.99) has a more domestic focus: in India in 1929, three women marry three brothers on the same day. Teenage Mehar has to cope with a distinctly unromantic wedding night: “He smells strongly of grass and sweat, and of fenugreek… but beyond that she can detect soap, and is glad that he had thought to wash before coming to her tonight.” A slightly more familiar parallel plot follows her drug addict great-grandson.

Poland’s long history of Jewish life was snuffed out by the Nazis. Olga Tokarczuk miraculously recreates the mid-18th-century world of Jews, Christians and converts in The Books of Jacob, translated by Jennifer Croft (Fitzcarraldo, £25). Cited as the work that earned her the Nobel Prize in 2018, this 1,000-page novel follows charismatic sect (or cult?) leader Jacob Frank, and takes in persecution, faith and mysticism. Trust me, it’s worth the effort.

From the same terrific small publisher, Joshua Cohen takes on modern Jewish history in his comic novel The Netantyahus (Fitzcarraldo, £12.99). Inspired by an anecdote Harold Bloom told Cohen about meeting former Israeli prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu’s historian father, the book takes in Jewish cultural identity in the US, debates over Zionism and the minor humiliations of campus life with great panache. It’s perfect for anyone who misses Philip Roth.

Other historical novels worth your time include Pat Barker’s The Women of Troy (Hamish Hamilton, £18.99), which starts with a soldier packed into the Trojan horse desperate to empty his bowels. Where should they put the latrine buckets? “The a--- end… where else?” replies a pleasingly earthy Odysseus. Lauren Groff’s pitch-perfect Matrix (William Heinemann, £16.99), set in an English abbey in the 12th century, follows an unwilling nun called Marie de France (a real poet) who ends up as a power-hungry abbess.

The talented Ghanaian-American writer Yaa Gyasi, whose first novel Homegoing was a hit, this year produced Transcendent Kingdom (Viking, £14.99), which pits science against religion through the struggles of the narrator Gifty. Once a strict Christian, and now a rationalist scientist, Gifty must learn how to cope with the death of her brother from an overdose.

The search for faith in a chaotic world has been a prominent theme in 2021. But rather deeper than Rooney’s nostalgia for a well-ordered religious framework is the analysis offered in my novel of the year: Jonathan Franzen’s Crossroads (Fourth Estate, £20). Set at the cheerily named 1970s church youth group that provides the book’s title, this superb novel follows the complicated Hildebrandt family. The pastor father lusts after another woman, the mother is anguished by an abortion in her youth and their pretty teenage daughter Becky wants to stay a virgin. All are striving sincerely, if fallibly, to do the right thing.

Franzen’s marrying of moral debates with an addictive story calls to mind George Eliot, alluded to in the title of this proposed trilogy: A Key to all Mythologies. The next two novels will, we are told, reach present-day America and presumably take in the rise of the internet. Franzen, I suspect, will be more generous to tweeters who see in him everything wrong with privilege-soaked literary culture. He, for one, has never lost faith in the forgiving power of fiction.
