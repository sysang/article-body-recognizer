The best new history books to buy for Christmas 2021

From wartime Cabinets to crumbling stately homes, via assassins’ dens and medieval pews, 2021’s best histories gave us the inside scoop

By Simon Heffer 6 December 2021 • 12:22pm

Christmas is the time of year when people are most likely to attend divine service, and Going to Church in Medieval England by Nicholas Orme (Yale, £20), tells us how they did it 800 years ago: much as today, three masses were allowed (midnight, dawn, mid-morning) in buildings decorated with holly and ivy. Orme also describes how the churches that punctuate our landscape came about, and who ran them. He ends at the Reformation, which underpins Clare Jackson’s Devil-Land, (Allen Lane, £35). It begins as the Armada attempts to reverse the English Reformation and ends a century later with the Glorious Revolution, when James II, the last king to attempt Catholic rule, was driven out. Jackson looks at history from the viewpoint of the loser, painting England as a failed state – though the revolution turned out more glorious than she suggests.

The pivotal figure of the 17th century features in The Making of Oliver Cromwell, by Ronald Hutton (Yale, £25), which meticulously takes the Lord Protector’s story to 1645, showing how his gifts as a soldier equipped him for leadership.

A different type of 16th century is revealed in Antwerp: The Glory Years by Michael Pye (Allen Lane, £25). This was where, before the Dutch unsuccessfully rebelled against Habsburg rule, Bruegel painted The Tower of Babel, Tyndale issued copies of his Bible in English, Erasmus and Thomas More thought world-changing thoughts. Antwerp became a refuge for Jews fleeing a Portuguese pogrom; it was a model for what we call globalism.

Talking of old subjects newly relevant, in The Gun, the Ship and the Pen (Profile, £25), Linda Colley takes a fresh approach to nation-making, showing how the vogue for written constitutions from the 1750s onwards did not prevent nations waging wars on each other, or help them achieve many of the stated aims of the drafters.

In Time’s Witness (Allen Lane, £25), Rosemary Hill argues that after 1789 the way history was written, and its purposes, underwent profound change. Whereas before (to evoke Carlyle) it had been about the lives of great men, it now also looked at the common people – an inevitable result of the French Revolution – and sought to enlighten us about the objects and art of the past, a point Hill makes by studying the lives of various notable antiquarians. Such antiquarianism led on to archaeology, and Alexandria: The Quest for the Lost City by Edmund Richardson (Bloomsbury, £25) recounts the fashion for exploring the ancient world in the 19th century, from Persia to Afghanistan, and notably the adventures of one archaeologist, Charles Masson, who turned out to be more than met the eye.

The perfect hors d’oeuvre for the feast of warfare books is Allan Mallinson’s The Shape of Battle: Six Campaigns from Hastings to Helmand (Bantam, £25), which analyses why one side lost and another won, showing how battles fought by radically different armies with radically different weaponry over almost a millennium have nonetheless things in common – take the supreme sense of superiority over the enemy to be found in the proud British soldier, “nourished by meat and beer”, both in a modern fight in Afghanistan and in the middle ages.

But some causes of 20th-century conflicts are immaculately set out in Blood and Iron: The Rise and Fall of the German Empire 1871-1918, by Katja Hoyer (History Press, £14.99). She describes precisely the establishment and growth of Bismarck’s Second Reich from 39 individual Teutonic states, through to the errors of the Great War and the debacle of 1918, and is especially penetrating on the reasons for the Reich’s failure – not least the ineptitude of Kaiser Wilhelm II.

The Great War was mostly fought in northern France and southern Belgium, events outlined in detail and with immense narrative power by Nick Lloyd in The Western Front (Penguin, £12.99). It is a work of serious scholarship that reflects the author’s understanding not merely of the military campaigns, but of the men who fought them. Lloyd has written a remarkably authoritative overview of an expansive subject, and a perfect introduction to a more granular study of the conflict.

An unusual take on the war is found in Daniel Larsen’s Plotting for Peace (Cambridge, £29.99), a stunning piece of research whose author shows how Asquith sought to use President Wilson to conclude a negotiated end to the Great War: however, British codebreakers intercepted American communications traffic and stymied Asquith’s attempts, and the war was fought to its bloody finish after Asquith fell in December 1916, and the US entered the conflict the following April.

One of the war’s numerous legacies was the “unreturning army” of dead soldiers with no known grave. In one of the most fascinating and remarkable books of the year – The Searchers (Bloomsbury, £25) – Robert Sackville-West details both the efforts made to find and identify the bodies of missing soldiers while the conflict raged (and how some well-to-do families headed for the front themselves, to search), and then, after 1918, the efforts of the Imperial (later Commonwealth) War Graves Commission to continue the task. Several dozen of the Glorious Dead are still found each year, some identifiable thanks to DNA testing.

Another piece of unfinished business after 1918 was Irish Home Rule or, as it turned out, the partition of the island into the Free State, and Ulster as part of the United Kingdom. The Partition: Ireland Divided, by Charles Townshend (Allen Lane, £25) marks the centenary of this act in a drama that, regrettably, is probably far from over. The author tells the story with creditable objectivity, explaining much of what one needs to know about the tensions between these two parts of the British Isles today – and also why there was effectively no other way out of the problems of a century ago.

The 1918 Armistice was widely viewed as a pause in the struggle between Germany and its enemies – but in one of the year’s most impressive pieces of research, Jonathan Haslam describes the part that the growth of Bolshevism played in renewing the conflict in 1939. The Spectre of War (Princeton, £28) illustrates how it was not merely German aggression that led to the escalation of international tensions in the 1920s and 1930s. A widespread fear of Bolshevism (not least among Chamberlain and his colleagues) poisoned international relations in the period, and led to the clash of ideologies that was resolved in 1945 and then, again and more peacefully, in 1989.

For Richard Overy, in Blood and Ruins: The Great Imperial War 1931-1945 (Allen Lane, £40), the story of the war is about a last-ditch attempt to shore up or re-make empires – notably the German, Japanese and Italian – and he has produced one of the most stunning accounts of the Second World War and the events that led up to it. Ironically, it effectively ended the idea of empire altogether, though the Soviet bloc took until 1991 to go.

Nazis and Nobles by Stephan Malinowski (OUP, £30) tells one of the under-documented stories of Hitler’s war, how the royal and aristocratic families of Germany from the Hohenzollerns downwards offered support to this thuggish and brutal regime. Having failed to stop the creation of a republic in 1918, Germany’s nobles mobilised themselves to undo Weimar, with the consequences of putting Hitler into power. Malinowski shows how, in the form of the Stauffenberg Plot in 1944, this marriage of convenience did not end well.

Similarly under-noticed on the British side has been the role of the Special Boat Service, a matter rectified by Saul David in SBS: Silent Warriors (William Collins, £25), about the world’s first maritime special operations unit. David argues that the SBS – known as the “cockleshell heroes” – changed the course of the Second World War by making D-Day possible.

The recovery of the defeated Germans from the appalling “Hour Zero” of 1945 is related in fascinating detail in Aftermath by Harald Jähner (WH Allen, £20). Using diaries, memoirs and letters from those living in the ruins, Jähner describes not just the rebuilding but also the shame, demoralisation and ultimately sense of liberation among a people implicit in some of history’s worst crimes, and victims of some of its harshest punishments, and how they pulled themselves together and created a new society.

At the same time, rivalry was growing between the four occupying powers in the ruins of the German capital once the common cause of defeating Hitler had been achieved. Giles Milton’s Checkmate in Berlin (John Murray, £25) details the events that led up to the Berlin airlift of 1949, when the West asserted that it would not roll over in the face of Soviet aggression.

Britain had its postwar reckoning, too, an aspect of which is told in Adrian Tinniswood’s Noble Ambitions: The Fall and Rise of the Post-War Country House (Jonathan Cape, £30), a vastly entertaining account of the crisis that befell England’s stately homes in the decades immediately after the war, with many unsellable, decaying stately homes being demolished (in 1955, at a rate of one every five days). Tinniswood takes us inside the fight for their survival, and the wonderfully vulgar means to which the owners had to resort – the coachloads of day trippers, safari parks and even rock concerts. The arrival of the Beatles is one of the seismic moments in David Kynaston’s On The Cusp: Days of ’62, (Bloomsbury, £18.99), about four short months he identifies as pivotal, when the society shaped by the two world wars gave way to a new culture and Britain changed almost beyond recognition, physically and morally.

Finally, two books whose (sometimes morbid) entertainment value will keep any reader gripped over Christmas. Day of the Assassins by Michael Burleigh (Picador, £25) is a lively account of how political murders, from Julius Caesar onwards, have differed from most others – and he investigates the motivations of individuals and state-sponsored killers in asking whether they ever change anything, and whether they spread like a disease.

And the venerable tradition of writers and actors working (often with comic ineptitude) in intelligence, from Christopher Marlowe to John le Carré via Harpo Marx and Graham Greene, supplies the fascinating narrative of Stars and Spies by Christopher Andrew and Julius Green (Bodley Head, £20), which also traces the converse: how the espionage story has infiltrated 20th-century fiction. Perfect to read as you settle into James Bond on Christmas afternoon.
