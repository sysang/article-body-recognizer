Bob Dole, Republican politician from small-town Kansas who overcame agonising wartime injury to become a great legislator – obituary

He ran for vice president and president but though witty he was hopeless at campaigning or communicating anything resembling a ‘vision’

By Telegraph Obituaries 5 December 2021 • 6:29pm

Bob Dole, who has died aged 98, led the Republican majority in the American senate and ran for vice president in 1976 and for president in 1980, 1988 and in 1996, when he finally secured the Republican nomination, but lost to the incumbent Democratic president Bill Clinton.

In some ways Dole had the perfect resumé for an American presidential candidate. A poor boy from small-town Kansas, he had succeeded through bravery and determination in overcoming crippling injury in the Second World War to become the most successful legislator of his generation. Yet his legacy was to have been the only person in the history of the two major American political parties to have been his party’s nominee for both the greatest offices of state but never to have been elected to either.

During the 1996 primaries Dole told Republican supporters that he was “willing to be another Ronald Reagan, if that’s what you want”. They did, but he could not deliver. Instead, the Reagan folksiness, the Reagan optimism, the Reagan oratory and the Reagan empathy was effortlessly appropriated by Clinton.

Much as Dole advisers wanted to present the race as one between “Purple Heart Bob versus Billy Yellow Streak” or, as one analyst called it, “the codger versus the dodger”, it was Clinton who convinced people he could “feel yo’ pain”. The 72-year-old Dole had suffered real hardship and real pain, but with his Midwesterner’s distaste for emotional display, could not capitalise on it.

Clever, straightforward and not overawed by wealth, Dole was more like a British Tory than an American conservative. Politics for him was the art of the possible: he preferred the pragmatic nitty-gritty of committee work to broad brush notions such as “family values”, and he was suspicious of the “neocon” zealotry of his own party radicals.

He was not, therefore, unfit for office, he was just hopeless at campaigning for it or communicating anything vaguely resembling a “vision”. He ought to have won; poll after poll showed that, on almost every issue, the electorate’s views were closer to the Republicans than Bill Clinton’s Democrats. During the primaries, polls suggested that he could beat Clinton.

Yet on the stump Dole appeared shambling and lacklustre. His habit of lapsing into Beltway-speak made him seem out of touch, and the ironic wit, much appreciated in Washington (where he was known as a very funny man), sounded grouchy and inappropriate. He also made basic campaign errors. 

In New Hampshire he visited the Old Man Ale brewery as concern was mounting about his age. When panic gripped his minders, he scheduled a stop at a New Jersey manufacturer of life rafts.

Even when his managers provided him with a workable line – “A better man for a better America” – Dole invariably undermined it by adding “whatever”– the word almost became a catch phrase. He seemed to be incapable of sticking to a position. On tax cuts, abortion, assault weapons and tobacco, he infuriated both Left and Right in the party by saying one thing and then changing his mind.

“D’you think we should get tough on Clinton?” he asked a Republican audience. The crowd roared its approval. “Well, we’re thinking about it,” he muttered, retreating. His uncertain approach even affected his running mate, Jack Kemp. When berated by campaign staff for lacking aggression in televised debates with Al Gore, Kemp said he was following Dole’s orders to treat Bill Clinton as “our opponent, not our enemy”.

Various methods were tried to improve Dole’s image. A speechwriter was brought in and Dole was persuaded to use a teleprompter to help him avoid fluffing his lines. He wowed the Senate with an emotional farewell which, according to one observer, made him sound “like Demosthenes”. 

But the experiment lasted only two weeks; Dole disliked being told what to say. He was persuaded to appear more often with his wife and fellow politician Elizabeth, whose contribution was to convince everyone that she would have made a far better candidate.

“Dole’s weakness in politics”, a Republican ad-man was quoted as saying, “is that he tells the truth”. In the end the Republican party gave up trying and concentrated resources on the fight to maintain its majorities in Congress.

The crowning irony of the campaign was that it was Dole, rather than his serial philanderer opponent, who was damaged by a sex scandal. During the campaign Dole had refused, adamantly, to discuss Clinton’s well-documented sexual shenanigans over many years, despite pressure from his advisers to do so.

Then, 10 days before the election, the tabloid National Enquirer published claims by a woman named Meredith Roberts that she and Dole had conducted an affair in the late 1960s, when he was still married to his first wife. The story was catapulted on to the broadsheets when The Washington Post said its own “extensive inquiries” confirmed the claims.

Even in conceding defeat, Dole somehow missed the mark: “Tomorrow will be the first day of my life I won’t have anything to do,” he said, intending the words as a wry joke. Given that he had devoted his life to politics and was hardly likely, given his disabilities, to take up golf or gardening, the remark just seemed infinitely sad.

The second of four children, Robert Joseph Dole was born on July 22 1923 at Russell, a small town deep in the heart of Kansas wheat country. Dust Bowl erosion was already adding to the problems of the depression and his father Doran fed his four children by working at a succession of odd-jobs, later running a creamery, then a grain elevator. During the Depression the Dole family (his mother Bina was a seamstress) moved into the basement of their home and rented out the rest of the house.

Dole was educated at the local high school where he was a star athlete, and at Kansas University where he studied Law and earned a place on the Kansas Jayhawks basketball team.

War was the defining experience of his life. In 1942 he joined the US Army’s Enlisted Reserve Corps and became a second lieutenant in the 10th Mountain Division. In April 1945, two weeks before VE-Day, leading his men during a furious firefight in the Po Valley, Dole was hit by a German mortar blast while trying to rescue one of his men. 

When, after 10 hours waiting to be taken from the battlefield, he arrived in the field hospital, his right shoulder, upper arm and part of his spine were found to be shattered; his right kidney had to be removed. When he shipped back home in a full-length body cast he was paralysed below the neck. His patched-up right arm was withered and two inches shorter than his left.

He was finally cured, three years later, when the people from his home town of Russell scraped together their dimes and dollars and presented him with $1,800 in a cigar box to pay for his medical treatment. 

In Chicago he was treated by an Armenian immigrant called Kelikian, a noted neurosurgeon who performed three operations on the young man for no charge. “I do what I can for the country,” the doctor said afterwards. “Dole epitomised America for me. He had the faith to endure.”

With self-discipline and three years of physiotherapy, Dole learnt to walk again, but his right arm remained useless, his left impaired, and he suffered from constant pain for the rest of his life. He was three times decorated for heroism, receiving two Purple Hearts for his injuries, and the Bronze Star for valour for his attempt to assist a comrade.

Dole ran for office for the first time in 1950 and was elected to the Kansas House of Representatives, serving a two-year term. After graduating from law school at Washburn University in Topeka, he was admitted to the bar and began to practice in his home town, also becoming County Attorney of Russell County.

In 1960 he was elected to the House of Representatives from Kansas’s 6th Congressional District. Two years later, when his district was merged with another to form the 1st Congressional District, Dole was re-elected with a substantial majority.

In 1968, he defeated Kansas Governor William H. Avery for the Republican nomination for the Senate following the retirement of the incumbent Frank Carlson. He was elected to the seat then re-elected four more times. From 1985, until his resignation from the Senate in 1996, he was the leader of the Senate Republicans, serving as Majority Leader from 1985 until 1987 and again from 1995 to 1996.

A virulent anti-communist, Dole criticised President Kennedy’s sale of grain to the Soviet Union, was sceptical about arms control treaties and was hawkish on the Vietnam War and on crime. In the 1980s he would support Star Wars and embrace the Nicaraguan Contras and later, on Bosnia, he led the field in advocating an end to the arms embargo. But at the same time he used his first speech in the Senate in 1969 to plead for federal aid for the handicapped, was a firm supporter of civil rights legislation, and at one point joined forces with Senator George McGovern to expand the food-stamp programme.

On the party political stage, Dole made his reputation as an acolyte of President Nixon, who chose him in 1971 to be the Republican party chairman. Later he headed the Republican National Committee during Watergate. At the subsequent Congressional elections in 1974, he came within a few thousand votes of losing his seat.

In 1976 he was Gerald Ford’s running mate when Jimmy Carter won by just 2 per cent, and he ran hard, but briefly, for the presidential nomination against Ronald Reagan and the first George Bush. But having lost against each in the primaries, he threw himself faithfully into the service of both.

There were structural as well as personal reasons for the failure of Dole’s 1996 bid for the White House. First, the Republicans were pushed on to the defensive by a public backlash against the shutdown of chunks of government during the winter of 1995-6 when Republicans in Congress had tried to bully President Clinton into swallowing deep budget cuts. 

Secondly, as the electoral season was beginning in late 1995, much political energy was sucked away in the hope that General Colin Powell would stand for the nomination. 

Then, during the primary season, Dole bore the brunt of a $30 billion negative advertising campaign organised by his rival candidate, the publishing billionaire Steve Forbes. The fight forced Dole to spend his entire war chest in winning the southern states and the Midwest primaries.

With no money left, the campaign then deserted the airwaves from the end of April until mid-August, leaving the field to Clinton and to Democrats in Congress who used their position test popular ideas on focus groups, then table them in the Senate and force Dole to block them.

After his defeat, Dole returned to legal practice in a Washington law firm and assumed the mantle of elder statesman of his party, consulted by both sides of the political divide. He engaged in a career of writing, consulting, public speaking and television appearances, including becoming a television commercial spokesman for Viagra and Dunkin’ Donuts.

His books include a compendium of jokes told by the presidents of the United States, and a memoir of his experiences in the Second World War and his battle to survive his injuries.

Bob Dole was given the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1997, the American Patriot Award in 2004 and the Congressional Gold Medal in 2018.

He married first, in 1948, Phyllis Holden, an occupational therapist he had met during his stay in a Kansas veterans’ hospital, with whom he had a daughter. The marriage was dissolved in 1972 and Phyllis died in 2008. In 1975 he married Elizabeth Hanford, a graduate of Harvard and Oxford and a formidable politician in her own right.

As Elizabeth Dole she would serve under Reagan as secretary of transportation, under George Bush Sr as secretary of labour and would run, unsuccessfully, for the Republican Presidential nomination in 2000. Elizabeth Dole survives him, with his daughter.
