What England's batsmen must do to stand a chance in the Ashes

Captain is in peak form but the rest of the top seven all have something to prove against Australia's supreme quicks and unforgiving pitches

Sir Geoffrey Boycott 6 December 2021 • 7:30am

For me it has always been about whether England can make enough runs in Australia. Joe Root is our only batsman with a Test average above 37. If you can’t make runs, you can’t win Test matches. 

You don’t need huge totals - but you do need enough to give the bowlers a chance. I am aware that Australia aren’t a great batting unit either. But their seamers - Pat Cummins, Mitchell Starc and Josh Hazlewood - are better than ours on Australian pitches. Pace is usually the ace in Australia. They have more quality than England so our batsmen have got to win the battle against those three.

We all know that England’s batting is fragile so a lot depends on the experienced, big-name players in the team. If they do well it will lift the youngsters who are new to Ashes cricket. I would expect Australia to target Root, because he is the captain and best batsman. They will feel that if they can get him down and out cheaply it will have a psychological effect on the other players.

Fortunately Joe is in his prime and has been batting superbly this year. Four years ago in Australia he batted nicely, making five scores over 50 in his nine innings. But he never went on to stamp his authority on any match.

On the other hand Steve Smith dominated the series like Don Bradman used to. He made a series of huge scores batting England out of every Test. When Root gets in, it is vital that he goes big and really hurts the Aussies.

What a cricketer Ben Stokes is. But being out of cricket for so long nobody - not even himself - has any idea what effect that will have on his form. England need his runs more than his bowling. But can he recapture his batting against this top-class seam attack having had no cricket for six months and limited match practice? That’s tough.

It will take a monumental effort of skill, courage and mental toughness. But in the past he’s shown all these qualities a number of times.

Jos Buttler hasn’t done anything of note in Ashes Tests in England - he’s played 10 and is averaging only 20.5. And he hasn’t played a Test match in Australia either. He got his chance as wicketkeeper-batsman when the runs dried up for Jonny Bairstow.

Jos captivates us with his white-ball performances and he’s produced the occasional innings of note against other countries in Test cricket. But - and this is the big but - our Test batting careers are defined by how we play in the Ashes and how we play fast bowling and he has not done it yet.

I realise it’s fun smashing it around in one-day cricket with no pressure or criticism if you get out. Ashes matches are different. They’re meaningful. There’s pressure and expectation and millions who care. So now would be a good time for him to step up better than he has done.

I can’t think of anything more difficult for the England openers Rory Burns and Haseeb Hameed having to face the best seam attack in the world with no meaningful practice because of rain. Batting against that attack would be difficult with perfect preparation.

These two haven’t batted in a first-class match for nearly three months and neither have played in Australia before to gain experience of the extra pace and bounce. 

When he’s batting, Hameed plays a bit low and he would have seen people like me suggesting that he adjusts and be more upright. But it’s not an easy thing to make a change like that. 

I started my career like Haseeb playing a bit too low. But on my early tours to Australia and South Africa the team had plenty of warm-up matches. Not so these days. And if there is rain around like this time, practice is ruined. I would hate to go into a Test series, like these two, underprepared. I feel for them, especially as England teams have a poor record in Brisbane but I wish them luck because it will be tough.

Burns has experience of playing 29 Tests but he can’t fall back on a good technique. He’s always been an ugly duckling but made his runs through sheer determination and guts. I always take my hat off to him because it’s how many runs you get, not how you look. He will need to grit his teeth and fight like hell. 

I hope Dawid Malan plays at No 3. Of all the less established players I think he has the best chance of doing well in Australia because he stays back and lets the ball come to him. Early on against those fast bowlers, it’s not only about how you score, but how you leave. Leaving is an art form and he is good at it.

Pace men bowl a shorter length in Australia and try to intimidate batsmen but in how you defend and how you leave the ball you can make a statement to the bowlers. That’s important. So many people talk about great shots but against the new ball, you’ve got to show the bowlers that you can defend and make the judgments that matter about what to leave. Get the message to them that you’re here to stay.
