Money Makeover: ‘Taxes killed my buy-to-let empire – where should I invest my £600,000?’

One in four landlords have turned their backs on property – our experts have tips on how else to earn a living

By Harry Brennan 6 December 2021 • 6:00am

Almost a quarter of landlords plan to sell up over the next 12 months as buy-to-let loses its shine and becomes increasingly difficult to navigate.

That’s according to the National Residential Landlords Association, a trade body. Property investors face tougher tax rules, extra cost to make green upgrades and tighter restrictions on evicting problem tenants. This has resulted in some going for months without income.

For Anthony Crowther, a former Royal Air Force flight engineer, managing physical property has become too burdensome in retirement.

The 62-year-old from Oxfordshire has sold one of two buy-to-lets for £632,000, because of tax changes that squeezed his rental income. “I was on continuous duty with my squadron for over a decade,” he said. “This included the invasion of Kuwait, the first and second Gulf wars and a tour of Afghanistan. I lived in military accommodation, so we invested our savings in properties at home.

“They have been very good investments for us, but taking an hour-long drive to go and manage the property in our golden years has become a headache.”

Mr Crowther joined the forces not long after he turned 17 and served for some 32 years before he moved into teaching aviation and worked on aircraft including the C-130 Hercules and L-1011 TriStar.

He and his wife have finally settled in a self-built house in the Cotswolds and live on comfortable pensions. Still a keen advocate of the DIY ethos, Mr Crowther wants advice on where to invest and manage his buy-to-let profits, which are currently sitting in a cash account, even though interest rates are at record lows.

“I want the money to at least keep in line with rising inflation,” he said.

Rob Burgeman, investment manager at Brewin Dolphin, a wealth manager

Given that Mr Crowther’s income is fully covered by generous and reliable pensions, and he already has a cash buffer, he is able to invest all of his profits.

However, the sum he has to invest far exceeds the annual Isa limit of £20,000. Isas protect investments from taxes on income, dividends and capital gains, although they are not exempt from inheritance tax. Mr and Mrs Crowther should combine allowances, meaning they can move £40,000 now, and then every tax year. This will reduce their tax bill over time, though in the interim they will have to pay capital gains tax on any profits above the £12,300 annual allowance. CGT is paid at 10pc or 20pc of profits, depending on whether you pay basic-rate or higher-rate income tax.

For his portfolio, he should invest 20pc in bonds, 30pc in alternatives such as infrastructure and 50pc in stocks.

We favour bond funds such as Jupiter Strat­egic Bond, which yields around 3.6pc. Mr Crow­ther also needs ­inflation-linked government bonds, which offer protection against rising interest rates. The DBX Global Inflation-Linked Bond (GBP Hedged) ETF has a balanced approach. He should put a 10th of his savings into each.

For alternatives, I recommend 10pc in the JP Morgan Global Macro Opportunities fund, which invests in many different assets, including currencies and complex derivatives, with the aim of delivering returns no matter how stormy markets are.

I would also suggest 10pc in the Schroder Global Cities fund – a portfolio of property companies focused on the fastest-growing regions with strong demand for real estate. The same amount should go into the 3i Infrastructure investment trust – a fund that offers direct exposure to a wealth of global infrastructure projects.

For stocks Mr Crowther should put 12.5pc each into Scottish Mortgage, Fundsmith Equity, Lindsell Train Global Equity and the Fidelity Global Quality Income ETF.

This should be a starting point and more funds can be added over time, maintaining the balance between the different investments. Typically, we would use 40 to 50 investments for a pot this size, to reduce any single investment taking over and adding risk. Space precludes showing them all here.

Elaine McLachlan, director of private clients at 7IM, a wealth manager

Residential property is losing its appeal. Higher rental yields may be on offer in lower-value houses but the administrative hassle is putting many off. More expensive properties have seen their yields shrink.

What’s more, you cannot put a property into tax-protected savings accounts such as pensions or Isas, and tax rules have become less favourable.

Stocks are the closest asset type to property in terms of the risk-and-reward pay-off – and can be held in the tax-free accounts mentioned above, which should also shield them from future changes in government policy.

They too are “real assets” whose value should rise as inflation does, unlike bonds, which tend to fall in value or see value eaten away in real terms as inflation picks up.

A traditional “medium-risk” portfolio, which we would recommend, is made up of roughly 60pc shares and 40pc bonds.

With rampant inflation and central bank interest rates likely to rise at some point, the outlook for bonds is unattractive, but there are still some decent options.

The Barings Emerging Market Debt fund, for instance, invests largely in China, where the returns are far greater than in America, Britain and Europe. It is likely the Chinese government would intervene to avoid a debt crisis if companies were unable to service their debt, which is a benefit for bondholders.

The outlook for shares is far better, and investors should think about the big themes that companies can tap into to generate future profits. I would recommend the AB International Health Care portfolio.

This will benefit from ageing demographics and the developed world spending more on healthcare. Similarly, a world eventually waking up to the importance of decarbonisation should benefit funds such as Ninety One Global Environment.
