The best coffee machines of 2021, tried and tested

From big, barista-style coffee machines to compact espresso makers and pod and capsule systems, here's how to raise your coffee game

By Abigail Buchanan 2 November 2021 • 5:41pm

Everyone wants to be able to make a barista-standard coffee at home, but in reality it’s harder than it looks. Or at least that’s the conclusion I drew on my hunt for the best coffee machines. There were so many things to consider if I wanted to replicate my favourite coffee shop flat white. Where to even begin? 

“It’s a massive rabbit hole – I’ve been in the industry for 10 years, but I still have to play around with recipes to get it right,” says Jon Townsend, an authorised specialty coffee trainer and founder of the Institute of Coffee. “There are a lot of variables in coffee, and you can either take control of those and change them how you want, or you can make it easier for yourself.” 

I hadn’t previously given much these variables much consideration. It may sound obvious, but factors including the the coarseness of the grind and water filtration make a big difference to the taste of the cup you end up with. 

In fact, Ewelina Kania, General Manager of a personal favourite coffee shop of mine – Prufrock, in Farringdon, London – tells me you should never put tap water in any coffee machine. Yikes. There’s a gadget that can help called Peak Water, which is a specialist water filter jug for coffee (£49, Colonna Coffee). High-end coffee machines should have an inbuilt water filter. 

The other (perhaps unexpected) piece of kit you need for great coffee is a kitchen scale. "The two key things are water quality and the kitchen scale – one of the most under-appreciated pieces of equipment," Wilson Jimenez, founder of speciality coffee company Piqant, tells me. "Weighing your coffee will mean you get consistently good coffee – rather than relying on a tablespoon or eyeballing."

To make things more complicated still, the recipe – ie. the amount of ground coffee per shot – varies depending on who you ask. Baristas tend to use between 15 to 26 grams, depending on the machine, the roast of the coffee and the type of drink they're making. 

If this sounds like a lot of faff, that’s because it is – but the good news, Townsend says, is that when it comes to the machine “you don’t actually have to spend a crazy amount to get good results; you can, of course, spend upwards of £5,000, but if you’re a casual coffee drinker you’re not actually going to notice the difference.” 

Espresso machines start from £50, but start getting better around the £130 mark. Pod coffee machines use a pre-portioned capsule of espresso and tend to be cheaper – however, you do have to factor in the cost of buying replacement pods, and the potential environmental impact of disposing of them (although there are now some recyclable and compostable ones available). 

When choosing a machine, the type of coffee you drink is a good starting point, plus how much time you have. Manual espresso machines will be best suited to hobbyists keen to really get into coffee making, who are willing to invest time, money and energy, whereas a pod or capsule machine will be better suited to someone who wants a quick and convenient way to make coffee on the way out of the door to work. 

What else should you look for? If you opt for an espresso machine, if possible go for one that has PID [proportional integral derivative] temperature control, says Jimenez. "It's a more sophisticated thermostat which controls the temperature exactly, ensuring consistent results each time." See the explainer at the bottom of this feature for more.

A quick note on testing: for obvious reasons, the quality of the coffee you end up with depends as much on the pod/beans used as the machine. With the exception of the Nespresso Vertuo, I tested the below with either the delicious coffee capsules from award-winning roaster Colonna Coffee (from £24 for four boxes) or beans from Kent-based subscription service Lost Sheep Coffee. For machines that didn't have a built-in grinder, I used a Cuinsinart Burr Mill (£62, AO) to ensure continuity.

I’ve made my way through a constant rotation of coffee machines on a mission to replicate my favourite coffee-shop flat white from home. Here are the best, starting with my favourite... 

The best coffee machines to buy in 2021

1. Sage Barista Pro espresso machine

£699, AO

Best manual machine for coffee experts

This is, in my humble opinion, the king of home coffee machines. For anyone who has done even cursory research into the world of coffee geekery, it will come as no surprise to see this Sage machine in the top spot on this list. Similarly, Sage is Jimenez's top brand recommendation. Here’s why. 

This is as simple and intuitive as espresso can be. The Barista Pro takes care of every part of the process for you, as it has an inbuilt burr grinder and bean hopper, along with hands-free, automated grinding and an easy-to-use LCD display. In simple terms, this means it grinds exactly the right amount of coffee without you having to manual calibrate the dosage. It would probably most accurately be described as a 'semi auto' espresso machine. This means that adjusting the grind size (the trickiest part of the process, I’ve found) is made easier. 

It heats up in three seconds (thanks to Sage’s “Thermojet” heating system) and takes all of five minutes to set up from box to cappuccino. There’s clever tech under the bonnet that automates the correct pressure, time and temperature of water every time, so you get consistently delicious espresso without (too) much effort. 

Although performance is obviously more important than appearance, the Sage Barista Pro is one well-thought-out, stylish brushed stainless steel machine that anyone would be proud to have on display (as you’d hope, to justify its price tag). 

It comes with a wide range of premium accessories: a satisfyingly chunky tamper, milk jug and ‘razor’ tool for levelling coffee in the portafilter, along with four filters for both freshly ground and pre-ground coffee. The tools are stored in a handy inbuilt drawer.

I also tested the Sage Barista Express (£585, AO). It boasts pretty much all of the same features: most importantly, the digital temperature control (PID) for optimal espresso extraction and a precise built-in grinder. The only difference is the lack of LCD display. I found the coffee tasted just as good, so if £699 is a stretch, consider this (slightly) cheaper model.

2. Sage Bambino espresso machine

£329.95, Sage

Best manual machine for space-saving

Designed to demystify the process of making espresso at home, this machine offers value for money and proves that good things come in small packages. You might think it a bit rich to consider a machine that costs £329 good value, but it justifies the cost by punching well above cheaper competitors. 

Although it’s not quite as high-spec as the Sage machines above, and lacks a built-in grinder, the Bambino is the model I’d choose to have in my kitchen. Its pleasingly petite design and compact footprint means it’s more convenient to have in a flat than a larger coffee maker. 

It looks and feels a lot more premium than other mid-range espresso machines, with excellent build quality and a brushed stainless steel body. It also has the same PID digital temperature control as the models above, which you won't find on many (if any) cheaper coffee makers. 

When it comes to actually making a coffee, the Bambino doesn't disappoint. It heats up and is ready to go almost instantaneously and has many of the same premium features as the Sage Barista machines. 

It's intuitive to use and extracts the espresso evenly and consistently so you get a great-tasting coffee every time. It also has an especially powerful steam wand that made even my amateur latte art look passable.

3. De’Longhi Dedica pump manual espresso coffee machine 

£181.99, De’Longhi

Best manual small espresso machine

As espresso machines go, this is an affordable choice. Its slimmed-down silhouette is useful if, like me, your kitchen is on the smaller side, and it's the perfect entry-level espresso machine as you can use either freshly ground coffee or ESE (easy serve espresso) pods without compromising on taste and performance. 

If your latte art leaves a little to be desired, you may like that this has an easy-to-use adjustable steam wand with different options for frothy cappucinos or creamy lattes. Once I had it set up and had played around with the grind size, it was easy to use. 

It's controlled via three simple buttons on the front of the machine, but they're customisable, so you can adjust brew temperature and water hardness. It notifies you when the machine needs descaling, which is a handy extra feature. 

The portafilter was a little hard to load the first couple of times I used it, but that was quickly resolved with a bit of elbow grease. It can make two coffees at once and has "Thermoblock" technology designed to keep it at a consistent temperature, but I did find that on occasion this machine took a while to heat up and then re-heat between making drinks. It's also fairly loud. That's nitpicking, though – overall it's a sleek and stylish package at a great price.

4. Smeg ECF01BL coffee machine

£319, John Lewis

Best manual retro style coffee machine

This is the machine I would want for savouring a coffee on a weekend morning. The making, as well as the drinking, is part of the fun – it's the most stylish one I tested, with its curved edges and chrome accents, and it's very satisfying to use. It has a retro 50s-inspired design and comes in cream, red and a range of pastel hues as well as black. 

If you're prepared to spend some time tinkering with the quantities and grind size, this machine makes barista-standard drinks with lots of options for customisation. It comes fully assembled and is quick to heat up, but it took me a while to master making espressos with fresh ground coffee. I would hazard a guess that this comes down to my lack of coffee know-how rather than an issue with the machine itself. Luckily, it's also compatible with ESE pods. 

It has a "Thermoblock" system for fast heating, but it's not ready to go quite as quickly as my favourite machines from Sage. It's still speedy – I would say it heats up in around 30 seconds. Once it's ready to go, it pours coffee very quickly, consistently, and not too loudly, which is a plus.

There's space for a water filter in the 1l tank, but it doesn't come included. The only other thing to note is that, given its price point, I would have expected it to come with a milk jug, at the very least. The other accessories are a bit cheap-feeling – the tamper is only plastic, for example. But it more than makes up for it with quality build of the machine itself; it's sturdy as well as stylish and is definitely built to last. 

Buy now

5. Morphy Richards espresso machine

£109.99, Morphy Richards

Best manual budget espresso machine

My first impressions of this machine were very positive indeed, as it's a stylish-looking espresso machine for the same price as most pod or capsule models. 

It's a very decent budget buy. I found it simple to use via a panel of four buttons on a panel on the top of the machine, and suitable for beginners as you can use either ground coffee or ESE pods. However, it feels lighter and more plasticky than more expensive machines, and its performance can't quite keep up with the models above. 

It isn't that it doesn't make a decent coffee, as it definitely does – it just needs a little more time to heat up and produces slightly less consistent results than more expensive espresso machines. There are a few options for customisation – you can change the amount of coffee it produces by holding down the one or two cup buttons, as you would with a pod machine. The steam wand is decent, too, but note that it doesn't come with a stainless steel milk jug. 

Buy now

6. Nespresso Vertuo Next coffee machine 

£74.99, Currys 

Best (and best-known) pod coffee machine

It’s almost impossible to resist the ease of a Nespresso machine. They come ready to go out of the box and each time you want a coffee, you only need to pop a pod in the top and press a single button. It’s totally mess free. 

This version of the Nespresso machine uses larger versions of the original Nespresso pod, which gives you the versatility of producing longer coffees as well as espressos. It’s also packed with smart new features, including a barcode scanner that reads each pod as you put it in to deliver the correct volume of coffee and a movable drip tray for different coffee sizes.

It has a sleek, compact body that doesn’t take up too much space on my work surface. I found that it started up and heated up in seconds and was the easiest to use of any machine I tested. 

The coffee that comes out is rich and delicious, with a lovely crema. The only downside I can see is that unlike standard espresso pods, which are now produced by most retailers, the unusual shape of the ‘Vertuo’ pods would mean you’re limited to using Nespresso’s own. 

You’ll be spoilt for choice, as there are 30 types available, but with Nespresso pods comes a certain expense and you won’t be able to use cheaper ones from the supermarket. However, for delicious coffee at – quite literally – the touch of a button, it can’t be faulted. 

Buy now

7. Grind One coffee machine 

£275, Grind

Best alternative to Nespresso pod machine

This machine is a stylish shortcut for coffee snobs. London chain Grind has produced a capsule machine that’s essentially a Nespresso machine, but with an espresso aesthetic. 

The sleek stainless steel body is inspired by the manual espresso machines used in Grind’s shops and was designed in collaboration with the Swedish coffee brand Sjöstrand. 

Of all the machines I tested, this was the most satisfying to use, in part due to airplane-style toggles and manual lever. It was also fantastically quick and easy – just two buttons (for a single or double espresso) and on/off toggle, with the used pods deposited into an internal bin. It’s also surprisingly quiet. 

The coffee it produces is rich and delicious, but you don't get much customisation, and obviously there's no steam wand or milk frother. I would highly recommend it for style and ease, but if you want to become a true coffee aficionado, you're better off looking at one of the manual espresso machines below. 

Grind manufactures its own compostable coffee pods (although the machine is also compatible with Nespresso pods) and provides free repairs on this machine for two years from purchase, so although it’s expensive, it’s a more sustainable choice that’s built to last.

What type of coffee machine is best? 

Pod or capsule coffee machines

These tend to be looked down upon by espresso enthusiasts, but they are the easiest and most convenient way to make coffee at home. They use a capsule or pod of sealed espresso – all you have to do is put this in the machine and press one button. In the long run, pods can be expensive, and you’re limited as to what kind of coffee you can make. They’re also not always recyclable, although there are more and more eco-friendly pods popping up on the market. 

Manual espresso machines

The most versatile of the coffee machines, a manual espresso model requires a little bit more skill to use – but the results are worth it. They’re the kind you see in a coffee shop and use a scoop of ground espresso to produce a barista-standard coffee. The downside is that they can be tricky to get the hang of, are more expensive than pod machines and create a bit more mess. 

Bean-to-cup coffee machines

If you want the taste of fresh espresso without the faff of a manual espresso machine, it’s worth considering a bean-to-cup model. These handle every part of the process at the touch of a button – from grinding the beans, to making the coffee, to frothing the milk. The downside is that they tend to be expensive and more tricky to clean. 

Filter machines

The humble filter coffee machine offers ease and value for money. It simply presses water through ground coffee into a jug or receptacle, providing an easy and cost-effective way to make coffee for multiple people at once. As these are a different kettle of fish entirely – they just produce black filter coffee, not espresso – I haven’t included them in my list of the ‘best’ below. 

Cafetieres/French press

Although not technically a ‘machine’, a cafetiere is another affordable way to make great-tasting coffee at home. If you want to dip your toe into coffee making without investing in a machine, it’s worth reading the Telegraph guide to the best cafetieres.

Coffee glossary

  • Crema: The fine, aromatic froth that sits on top of a shot of espresso.
  • Portafilter: The handle and holder for the ground coffee filter that's used with an espresso machine.
  • Extraction: The process of drawing flavour from coffee grounds to make a shot of espresso.
  • Espresso: A coffee made by forcing hot water through coffee grounds at high pressure.
  • PID: Proportional-Integral-Derivative – a digital temperature control mechanism. Where a traditional thermostat simply turns the heat off when a set temperature is exceeded, a PID system uses a sophisticated algorithm to ensure coffee is extracted at the optimum temperature.
