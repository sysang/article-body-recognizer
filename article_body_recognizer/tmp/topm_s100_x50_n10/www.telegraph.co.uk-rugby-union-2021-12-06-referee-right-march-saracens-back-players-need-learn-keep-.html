The referee was right to march Saracens back - all players need to learn to keep their mouths shut

At every breakdown any number of players feel free to ‘advise’ the referee as to what they think happened - it's time it was stamped out

Brian Moore 6 December 2021 • 7:00am

"Moore, will you stop trying to referee this game". "Well Sir, one of us has to". That is the genuine exchange that occurred between me and the then top international referee, Tony Spreadbury. For the after-dinner version, I add on the comment "and you didn’t seem all that bothered".

The truth is that back then there was not a lot of chat between referees and players, or even captains and referees, because of the use of the law that allows referees to march a side 10 metres towards their own line after a penalty is given and there is any untoward comment from a player.

Saracen Billy Vunipola was marched back by referee Luke Pearce in Saturday’s game against Exeter and because of his profile, and the fact that this sanction has largely fallen by the wayside, this has drawn a lot of attention.

Let us say from the outset that those players are by no means the only or worst players for making unwanted or unwarranted comments to referees. When you commentate on international games you have an expanded version of the Ref-Link device sold at grounds and via which you can hear the referee’s comments. The amplified version allows you to hear not only the referee but also any surrounding players who chime in with their own observations. I can say with absolute certainty that the number and tenor of comments has changed markedly over the past few seasons, to the point that at every breakdown or flashpoint, any number of players feel free to ‘advise’ the referee as to what they think has occurred. Even if you do not have the advantage of having a Ref-Link, you can see this by the widespread pointing and arm waving from commenting players. This is particularly apparent when scrums collapse and nearly every player in both packs appeals to one or other of the nearby officials.

For some reason this has been largely accepted by officials who seem reluctant to apply what was once the common, and remarkably effective, sanction of advancing the penalty or free kick by the said 10 metres. Lots of referees I have spoken to about this claim they want to be able to have a dialogue with players and it is seen as part of game management. What they have inadvertently created is the belief that provided comments are not directly critical or abusive they are acceptable from any player, even in the said cases of collapsed scrums where nobody, other than the props involved can possibly know who is responsible for the collapse.

It seems that every few years, after condoning this or that practice, that World Rugby, or the RFU, finally understands what their referees have created by failing to apply current laws and an edict goes out to referees to take corrective measures. Something that would not be necessary if they did not allow things to get out of hand in the first place. They realise they have fallen into what I call the creeping trap. You allow A because it does not seem that bad, but then you get B, which you allow because it is not much worse than A. They you allow C, which is not much worse than B, and before you know it, you find you are at G, miles from where you started but you find it difficult to penalise because it is only marginally worse than the last example.

The solution is not to allow A in the first place and to be strict about this, knowing that if you do condone something, it will not end there. Players and coaches at professional level will push at a door that is only slightly ajar, and their amateur brethren will follow because they ape what they see. This sort of benign ignorance leads to coaches posing as water carriers and feeling able to get involved in on-field coaching. This has even extended to physiotherapists trying to instruct players and comment to referees about decisions.

What referees, and those who seek to and do try to manipulate them, have to understand is where this leads. Take a look at Premier League football and international rugby to see how far people will go, if allowed. Then see Sunday morning games involving kids and see the mimicry. From there go to the statistics about volunteer referees, without whom there is no game, and see how they are falling.

No good comes from not setting clear boundaries and the natural consequence of giving an inch is clear. The ten-metre rule is so effective because even your own players and supporters will not support you if you cannot keep your mouth shut. We need to see more of its use from those with the whistle.
