Hong Kong wages war on boars - drawing comparisons with crackdown on protesters

Hong Kong authorities are culling boars after one attacked a police officer

By Rachel Cheung 6 December 2021 • 6:00am

Armed with riot shields and bread, Hong Kong authorities have launched a new crackdown on wild boars rampaging through the hills of the city state - in a policy that has drawn comparisons to the region’s treatment of protesters.

Wild pigs are particularly prominent on the slopes of Mount Parker, the second highest peak in Hong Kong island. They have been a common sight for so long that the hiking trail takes its name from the animal, which is a native species. 

Families of pigs can be spotted diving underneath metal fences and scurrying towards a food market, or napping under trees.

“You just walk by them and they won’t do anything to you,” said one hiker holding trekking poles and going downhill as the sun set. He fondly counted all the places where wild pigs can be found, sometimes playing with monkeys, he added. “There’s no reason why humans and pigs cannot co-exist.”

But to the Hong Kong authorities, the pigs are a menace. Shortly after The Telegraph visited the site, wildlife officials conducted a capture-and-kill operation on the hillside.

Using bread as bait, officers armed with tranquillisers and shields battled for hours and emerged triumphant with two dead boar.

The two carcasses were in addition to seven pigs killed in an earlier operation.

After years spent neutering rather than killing pigs, the former colony last month returned to the controversial practice of mass culling, which was phased out in 2017.

The turning point came after an auxiliary police officer was attacked by a boar that later plunged down a slope to its death. The officer left the scene with a bite wound and bruising.

The new policy, which sparked public outcry, marked a dramatic U-turn in the administration’s strategy to control the wild pig population. And the anti-boar measures have reminded some of the ongoing police crackdown on pro-democracy protesters and the ensuing exodus of citizens. 

Some 90,000 people had left Hong Kong by August since the pro-Beijing government imposed sweeping measures clamping down on dissent. 

A cartoon in a social media group featured a boar and a piglet at an airport counter with the line, “Daddy, why are we leaving Hong Kong?”

“Many Hong Kong people are asking the same question,” a comment wrote.

Authorities insist such measures are needed.

Leung Siu-fai, director of Hong Kong’s Agriculture, Fisheries and Conservation Department, said: “Pigs that frequently wander into the city and are particularly large in size... are a serious threat to people’s safety.” He cited the surge in the number of injuries caused by wild pigs, which jumped from three last year to 19 in 2021.

Vincent Tse, a vet who had participated in the scheme to neuter the animals, disagreed.

He described them as gentle animals that would not barge or attack unless they felt threatened.

He was among more than 450 veterinary professionals and students that signed an open letter urging the agriculture department to suspend the policy.

Wildlife officials used to go to extreme lengths, such as abseiling down cliffs and diving into drainage tunnels to rescue trapped pigs, Mr Tse recalled.

During previous sterilisation operations, wildlife officials had respected his judgement and never put down a pig unless it was necessary, said Mr Tse, who once operated on a boar that broke its snout and dislocated its jaw at the scene to save its life.

Mr Tse believed the government was probably under pressure to deliver quick results after high-profile cases of injuries, including that of the police officer and another involving a pop star’s mother.

But over time, sterilisation could have achieved the same goals, Mr Tse argues.

“Collars were also placed on pigs to track their movements, and the collection of these valuable data about their distribution and activities will come to a halt,” the vet added.

“The policy of killing the boars is unnecessary,” said Roni Wong of the Wild Boar Concern Group. She had attempted to intervene and lead the pigs back uphill at the first operation, only to be detained by police officers.

Mr Wong criticised the government for taking a barbaric approach instead of tackling the root of the issue, people who feed the animals illegally and draw them to urban neighborhoods.

“By re-introducing the policy, the department has lost all its credibility and the public trust,” Mr Wong stressed.

On Mount Parker, an employee at a biodiversity education centre admitted the wild pigs could be a nuisance as they gnaw at plant roots, but shooing them away requires only a simple act of communication.

“You stand behind them and clap, and they would leave, knowing they are not welcome,” she said. “Otherwise, they are free to roam anywhere. This entire hill is their home.”
