'My teachers had given up on me but doing DofE helped me to turn my life around'

After a challenging childhood Emma Sammes found herself sofa surfing and getting into trouble at school. Then she was introduced to the DofE

By Anna Maxted 2 December 2021 • 11:30am

When Emma Sammes was thirteen, her 24-year old brother was killed in a car accident. Until then, despite growing up on “quite a rough council estate” in west London, she’d had a happy childhood. “It was just me and my Dad,” she says. “My Mum left when I was a very small baby. So Dad raised me alone, and did an amazing job.”

He worked Monday to Friday, getting up at 5am to take Emma to her childminder. “He had old-school moral values, he was a really good man. He gave me the best.” But following the tragedy, her father’s mental health deteriorated. “He started using drugs and drinking quite heavily, which meant that our relationship completely broke down,’ recalls Emma, now 31. “He found it really hard to cope with a 13-year old daughter, and losing his son.”

Eventually, she was “kicked out of home.” So for a couple of years, Emma “sofa-surfed,” sleeping at friends’ houses, being moved from place to place. She wasn’t the only teenager on the estate with problems that weren’t talked about. Getting up to mischief together was a distraction. “We could do whatever we wanted. No one checked on us. I started getting myself into trouble, staying out late. I didn’t really have any support. I was alone.” 

Her teachers were unsympathetic. “I was always told I was never going to make anything of myself.” She was expelled twice. And then, when she was 17, living in a hostel, her father took his own life. “I felt like that was, literally, the end.” She didn’t cry. She was in shock. “I lost my soul. I was just a body walking around.”

With a sense of having nothing more in the world to lose, she started putting herself in dangerous situations, and getting into trouble with the police. Then Emma was approached by a crime prevention project for young people, through Ealing Borough council. They put her on a youth work apprenticeship course. And, “as a part of my apprenticeship, I had to do my Duke of Edinburgh’s Bronze Award.” 

For the programme (which includes acquiring a skill, improving at a physical activity, volunteering, completing an expedition), Emma was packed off on a short trek in the wilds of Aylesbury - to her chagrin. “I didn’t want to do anything. I was kind of against it. I’d just been told I have to do this really long walk. All the guys I was in a group with were fit - they played football and went to the gym. From the get-go, I was moaning. It got to the point where they’d had enough of me. And I’d just had enough.”

Emma threw a tantrum, and sat down in the middle of a field. “I sat there for a couple of minutes - my surroundings were field after field after field.” The rest of her team kept walking. Usually, she recalls, “I’d run away from whatever I’d got myself into.” But this was different. “I had to get up. No one was coming to save me.” So she got up and caught up - crying with frustration all the way - until she reached base camp.

“That minute, all of those feelings disappeared, and I felt so happy. The happiest I’d felt in such a long time. I’d persevered, and finished something.” She says, “My life kind of changed from that moment.”

This bereaved young woman, who’d always been told “you’re this, you’re that,” at last was given the opportunity to see herself differently. “I felt really good about myself. From that point onwards, there really wasn’t anything I couldn’t do.” To be surrounded by people who believed in her was transformative.

“I was never a naughty child,” she says now. “I was very loud. I just wanted attention. I didn’t get it at home.” At school, they’d judged her behaviour, without understanding the confusion of emotions behind it. “It was easier for them to class me as a problem child, than to help me face the issues I was having,’ she says.

To block the pain, she’d always told herself she didn’t care. “That was my favourite thing - ‘I don’t care, I don’t care, I don’t care.’ Once I’d said that, I didn’t have to think about it. Not progressing wasn’t a problem for me, because, ‘so what?’ I had no direction, no focus. I felt unloved. I used to get angry all the time. I guess I felt misunderstood.”

But at the DofE, the approach was different. Staff and volunteers saw her for who she was - even before she saw it herself. “They take time to get to know you, how to work with you, how to speak to you,” she says now. “I wasn’t the easiest participant to deal with. But they never ever gave up.” No matter how challenging she was, the relationship wasn’t “broken,” her chance wasn’t “lost.” She says, “I feel like they see a light in you.” 

She achieved her Bronze Award, and Silver Award (for one of her skills she learned to play the saxophone, and for her volunteer work she helped in a group for young people with additional needs). Then, Emma was nominated by Ealing Borough, where she was doing her Gold, to be a member of the London Golds’ Forum - a project bringing together young DofE participants from across the city. 

For her Gold Award Emma had to complete a Residential Project - and the destination selected was The Gambia. “I feel so lucky that I was able to do it,” she says. Her group helped to renovate a school, and coached Gambian children in English and sport. They also worked with “young people in Gambia who do the President’s International Award” - the local DofE equivalent. 

This time, the expedition was tough. “We had to do 98K over two days. We had to wake up at two in the morning, so we didn’t have to walk at the height of the heat of the day,’ she says. “My shoes broke, so I walked twenty miles with no shoes on. But it was a life-changing experience.” 

While Emma did some fundraising herself, returning to The Gambia twice over the next few years to complete her Gold - her experience was also facilitated by a DoE bursary. Ten others in her group also received funding, including “young people who had never got to leave the country before. Some had never been to the countryside before. Sixteen, seventeen, eighteen, nineteen-year olds who’d never seen a sheep or a cow.” 

These days, Emma is happy and “loving life.” She works as a nanny, in London, and has a three-year old son. She feels that the DofE gave her drive (she hopes to train as a therapist) self-belief, and strengthened qualities that improve her parenting. “It’s made me more patient.” And she says, with a smile, “With a three-year old, you definitely have to have patience. I laugh sometimes to myself thinking, I can’t even imagine how I’d be otherwise. I have them to thank for how I am today.”

Emma remains a DofE ambassador, regularly giving speeches to potential patrons about how the charity and those within it changed her life. “You have to be a special person to work at the DofE,” she says. “They’re all so loving, empathetic and caring. That can do a lot. That can take a young person, who’s never ever had that, far places.”
