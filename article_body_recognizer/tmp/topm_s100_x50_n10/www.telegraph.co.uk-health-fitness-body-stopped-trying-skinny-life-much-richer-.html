'I stopped trying to be skinny – and life is so much richer for it'

A new breed of fitness fanatic is helping reverse the Instagram obsession with 'before and after' body transformations

By Jessica Salter 5 December 2021 • 5:00pm

As a marketing tactic aimed at those who are seduced by the idea of losing a bit of weight, it is hard to resist. So-called transformational photos – the “before and after” shots of people as they lose weight – are used widely by many fitness influencers, personal trainers and gyms to get new followers or customers. The hashtags #beforeandafter and #beforeandafterweightloss lead to more than 25 million posts on Instagram alone, while the habit of posting such pictures on #TransformationTuesday has garnered another 16 million posts.

They might be compulsive, but viewing pictures like these can make us unhappy. A 2015 study from Australia found that looking at 'fitspiration' posts on Instagram led to worse mood, body dissatisfaction and lower self-esteem in the women who viewed them.

“When we look at a before and after comparison, we are being given messages about acceptable and unacceptable bodies,” Dr Jenny Cole, senior lecturer in psychology at Manchester Metropolitan University, explains. “The ‘before’ is the ‘wrong’ body and the ‘after’ is the ‘fixed’ body. Social comparison is an automatic process, so it’s not something we can easily control.” 

But times are changing. Now, a new breed of fitness fanatics – including some reformed “before and after” snappers – are reversing the trend. Personal trainers such as Alice Liveing, who has nearly 700,000 followers, have eased up on their regimes and are posting before/ after shots to show they’re happier and healthier now that they are slightly heavier than they were in their super-slim days. 

In the United States, beauty blogger Taylor Rain posted before and after pictures of herself on Twitter after she put on weight due to taking mental health medication. In a post that has been liked more than 170,000 times, she says: “I posted this because I struggled with confidence when I gained weight.” Thousands of women replied to the thread with pictures of their own.

Chloe Hodgkinson, 25, is another who posts about her journey from super skinny to a more relaxed body size as she recovers from disordered eating. She says it was never particularly obvious that she had a problem – she was (and still is) slim, but not alarmingly skinny. But she spent every moment thinking about what she was or wasn’t eating. “I just wanted for someone to call me the skinny one. To not have armpit fat like my friends. To not have rolls when I sat in the bath. I wanted to belong somewhere, be known for something so I became ‘the one that went gym’,” she wrote on Instagram.

After splitting up with her boyfriend and coming off her birth control pills, her period didn’t restart – something known as amenorrhea. She was totally flummoxed: as a personal trainer, she thought she had a healthy lifestyle. “It was a shock,” she says. “I was a cocky young girl who prided herself on being ‘the fit one’. I judged others on what they ate and loved that I was so ‘disciplined’, so it was a big kick in the teeth that my healthy lifestyle wasn’t actually that healthy.”

But, she says, there was also an element of relief when she was told by doctors to ease up on her regime. “I was kind of glad. I remember thinking ‘is this my life now?’ as I got up at 5am for a workout. How do people do this with kids? Turned out that lifestyle wouldn’t have given me kids anyway.” 

Now, having switched jobs to working as a content producer and adapting her eating and exercising habits, she uses her social media feeds to promote her message that period health is an important marker of hormonal health. 

Part of the way she illustrates it is with before and after pictures of herself – often just head shots – which show a slightly gaunt, anxious girl as the before and a fuller-faced, happier-looking after. As she says in one post, “can’t decide which is the best thing about recovery – the better skin complexion, the fuller cheeks or thicker, longer hair [or] the freedom around food.”

It is not always an easy transition. In an honest post Liveing talks about how her identity was exclusively wrapped up in her small body size. “I found so much validation in people telling me I looked amazing, that I didn’t really feel I brought anything else to the table,” she writes. But, she says, now that she is slightly larger, she has found a different kind of freedom: “I’ve had to work so hard to love me for me, regardless of the body I inhabit.” She writes in another post: “It isn’t always easy to go from the girl on the left to the one on the right, but I can promise you that life is so much richer for it.”

The trend, which is linked with the denouncement of diet culture, has caught on with some big fitness corporations. The diet company Weight Watchers has phased out the use of “before and after” pictures in its advertising since 2018, saying it wanted members to adopt healthy lifestyles instead of aiming for a specific “after” body. 

Not that Coles is convinced that the big corporations really care about mental health benefits. “Diet culture is all about making money,” she says. “While money is to be made from before and after pictures, they will continue. Weight Watchers saw the trend towards ‘body positivity’ and took a risk to make more money by not using it.”

The reverse “before and after” pictures join a wave of body positivity movements on the internet. Tom Quinn, from eating disorders charity Beat, says that some women do find them helpful. “Engaging with content that encourages body positivity or self-acceptance can lead to improved feelings about body image and self-esteem,” he says. Hodgkinson says she “regularly gets comments saying it helps them continue on the hard journey of recovery”.

Cole is more damning. “It’s notable that these types of trends still tend to privilege a certain type of body. The new ‘after’ picture may be curvier, for example, but these images are usually of bodies that are still within an acceptable range. Anything where we are encouraged to judge the bodies of others as ‘more’ or ‘less’ desirable is problematic.”
