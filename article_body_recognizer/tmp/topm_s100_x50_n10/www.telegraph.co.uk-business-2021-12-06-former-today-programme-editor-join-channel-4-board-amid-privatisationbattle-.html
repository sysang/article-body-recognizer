Former Today programme editor Sarah Sands to join Channel 4 board amid privatisation battle

Journalist becomes a non-executive director at the publicly owned broadcaster as it seeks to avoid being privatised

By Ben Woods 6 December 2021 • 11:04am

The former editor of Radio 4's Today programme has been recruited to the board of Channel 4 as the broadcaster battles privatisation.

Sarah Sands, 60, will join as a non-executive director of the Bake Off broadcaster after leaving the BBC's flagship current affairs radio show in September last year, sources have said.

The former Evening Standard editor oversaw a period when the Today programme was at war with the Government after Number 10 banned ministers from appearing on the show. Ms Sands likened the move to Donald Trump's fight with the press during his US presidency.

Ms Sands is among a string of new non-executive appointments, including Dawn Airey, the former chief executive of Channel 5, who is currently chairman of the National Youth Theatre. At Channel 5 she became known for its "films, football and f******" programming policy.

David Kogan, the sports media rights adviser and Labour Party historian, and Tess Alps, the ex-chair of ThinkBox, the TV advertising industry body, are also joining the Channel 4 board.The appointments come against a backdrop of sustained uncertainty for Channel 4 as it awaits the outcome of a public consultation over whether it should be shifted into private hands.The Government has also fired up the search for the next chairman of Channel 4 to replace Charles Gurassa, who leaves at the end of January after six years in the role.Ofcom has hired headhunters at Russell Reynolds to pinpoint a successor. However, The Telegraph understands Channel 4's non-executive directors could also seek to apply for the role.Ministers have argued that a sale of Channel 4 would offer greater investment and financial security amid fears that its ad-funded business model would not survive the streaming age.Channel 4's board has defended the status quo, saying a new owner motivated by profit could close its regional offices or damage the UK's independent TV production sector by allowing the broadcaster to make its own shows.

Alex Mahon, Channel 4's chief executive, is hopeful that the new culture secretary Nadine Dorries will be sympathetic to the broadcaster's efforts to shift operations outside of the capital, which dovetail with the Government's "levelling up" agenda.She told The Telegraph on Saturday: "As I spend a lot of time talking to Conservatives, a lot of them believe that what we are doing in the nations and regions is spectacular."There have been concerns that the shake-up of the Channel 4 board could become the next battleground in Downing Street's attempts to install sympathetic figures into powerful public roles.Ministers vetoed the reappointment of TV producer Uzma Hasan and former ITV executive Fru Hazlitt as non-executive directors in April. When those roles were advertised, candidates with commercial experience managing an "organisation through structural market changes" were urged to apply.However, Boris Johnson failed in his attempt to recruit the former Daily Mail editor Paul Dacre as the next chair of Ofcom. The search for that position remains ongoing.
