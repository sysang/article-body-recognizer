The debauched history of 'Chiantishire', the playground for billionaires

The latest season of Succession is coming to Tuscany – a region that has seen more than its fair share of dynastic power struggles

By Tim Jepson, Destination expert 6 December 2021 • 5:00am

From the Medici to the Mafia, debauched emperors to decadent popes, Italy has a long history of wealth and power built on dynastic prowess, on long-lived family domination ultimately – and invariably – laid low by corruption and dysfunction.

No wonder, then, that the makers of the award-winning HBO series Succession have set the last two episodes of the show’s current run in Tuscany. If you’ve not seen the programme the gist is simple, and the historical echoes that inspired an Italian denouement easily understood. 

Waystar Royco is a fictional multi-billion-dollar media empire, the creation of Logan Roy, a grizzled patriarch surrounded by a seething dynastic mess of offspring, all seeking control of his corporation, and all, in the words of one critic, “terrible people with terrible ideas about how to save the family’s terrible business”.

Awful characters, then, but compelling ones, as hyper-wealthy individuals, fictional or actual, always have been, not least in Italy, a historical hotbed of dynastic squabble that provides a wealth of Logan-like patriarchs and failed families. 

Take ancient Rome’s first emperors, scions of the Julio-Claudian dynasty (27BC–AD 64), which not only has its roots in murder (the assassination of Julius Caesar) but a cast of characters – Augustus, Tiberius, Caligula, Claudius and Nero – whose diet of intrigue and mayhem makes the Roys’ antics in Succession look tame.

Then you have the great papal families of Rome, among whom the Borgias – powerful father (Pope Alexander VI), scheming son (Cesare), put-upon daughter (Lucrezia) – are the most notorious. Next come the dynasties of Italy’s medieval city states, the Visconti in Milan, the Gonzaga in Mantua, the Montefeltro in Urbino and, most famously of all, the Medici in Florence.

The Medici provide the perfect dynastic trajectory, from the Logan-like Cosimo, mastermind of the family’s banking business, and dazzling, if profligate offspring such as Lorenzo the Magnificent, through to wastrels like Gian Gaston de’ Medici, whose decadent ineptitude saw the extinction of both family and fortune. 

A Tuscan setting, then, provides Succession with an ideal backdrop for dynastic intrigue, but it also taps into another telling association, namely the long-standing love affair between Italy – Tuscany in particular – and a wealthy Anglophile elite.

Jesse Armstrong, the show’s British creator, explicitly plays up the link. “I don’t know how much of a social signifier it is to Americans,” he says, “but Tuscany has this particular flavour for the English upper class. Some call it Chiantishire, in a slightly sickening way.”

Armstrong is not quite right these days, as we shall see, but the point is still well made. If you are like the new-money Roys, and want to make your social mark, then you might imagine a family gathering and offensively extravagant party can have no better setting than a sumptuous villa in the Tuscan hills. 

It certainly worked for the Kardashians, a modern dynasty of a different, social media stamp, when they rented the Forte di Belvedere, a 16th-century castle above Florence – and a Medici castle at that – for Kim’s ultimately doomed, 600-guest 2014 wedding to Kanye West. The cost for the day was a reputed £315,000, marginally less than the cost of Kim’s dress.

Like dynastic dysfunction, the Anglo, and later Anglo-American love affair with Tuscany has deep roots. The collapse of the Medici’s male line in 1737 came during the heyday of the Grand Tour, a perambulation through Europe, with its climax in Italy, without which a young man’s (rarely woman’s) aristocratic education was considered incomplete.

Old Etonian Horace Walpole, the son of a British prime minister, and his companion Thomas Grey, were typical of many of the tour’s wealthily indolent participants. Describing a normal day in Florence (“one of the dullest cities in Italy”) Gray wrote in 1740 how: “We get up at twelve, breakfast till three, dine till four, sleep till six, drink cooling liqueurs till eight, go to the Ponte Vecchio till ten, sup till two, and so sleep again till twelve.”

For many, Tuscany’s cultural distractions took second place to sexual adventures, for as Lord Byron, the epitome of the aristocratic libertine, observed in Don Juan: “What men call gallantry, and gods adultery,/ Is more much common where the climate’s sultry.”

In 1611, English traveller Thomas Coryat praised the “pleasing dalliances” that might be had in Italy with “amorous Calypsoes so loose that they are said to open their quivers to every arrow.” Diarist James Boswell contracted venereal disease and crab-lice within a month of his arrival in Italy in 1765, yet still proceeded to cut a libidinous swathe through the country, bedding countess and chambermaid alike.

In time, Italy, and Tuscany in particular, came to represent a more general refuge, a “Paradise of exiles”, in the words of Shelley, who came, like Keats, “for a beaker full of the warm South”, and to escape “the shadows of his first marriage, the tumult of humankind, and the chilling fogs and rains of our own country”.

But even as Tuscany seduced more discerning visitors – Robert Browning, Tennyson, Trollope, E M Forster, Mark Twain, Henry James, D H Lawrence – its most compelling ex-pat residents continued to combine aristocratic roots with a whiff of difference or disgrace. 

After a supremely colourful life, the aesthete Sir Harold Acton, for example – Eton, Oxford, Bright Young Thing – spent 25 years with his companion, Alexander Zielcke, in Villa La Pietra, high in the Florentine hills, until his death in 1994.

And the writer Iris Origo, English-born to an Irish aristocrat mother and wealthy New York father, who in 1924 settled not in a fashionable villa near Florence, but, like some early denizen of Chianti doing up a ruined farmhouse, in La Foce, a rundown estate in the beautiful countryside of the Val d’Orcia south of Siena. Today, this countryside – the classic Tuscan medley of cypress, olives and villa-topped hills – has Unesco World Heritage status and the restored La Foce is a picture of the Tuscan dream. More to the point, Villa La Foce can be rented, and was rented, by Succession’s producers, as one of the series finale’s locations.

Another location, Villa Cetinale, west of Siena – the setting for a lavish 120-guest lunch hosted by the Roys – is even more appropriately chosen, not simply for the splendour of the villa and its gardens, but for the villa’s recent history of aristocratic exile and scandal.

Antony (Lord) Lambton, 6th Earl of Durham, was a Conservative MP until 1973, when he resigned after tabloid revelations involving drugs and prostitutes. In 1977 he bought and retired to Villa Cetinale, a 17th-century papal villa, where he played host to guests such as Mick Jagger, Princess Margaret, Tony Blair and Prince Charles.

Party house, party people – rock and actual royalty – perfect for Succession. But to argue that Lambton was Chiantishire’s upper-class archetype, or that Origo was among the first of the Chiantishire set, is not quite right, for two reasons. The first is that neither lived anywhere near Chianti, which is a tiny area of just 100 square miles (out of Tuscany’s 8,875 square miles), centred on the hills and a handful of towns between Florence and Siena. La Foce and Cetinale lie some way distant, along with other of Succession series three’s Tuscan locations: Pienza, Cortona, Bagno Vignoni and Montalcino. The second is that these days, visitors staying in Chianti’s villas are unlikely to be British aristocrats, but people like you and me, and, for that matter, not British at all, but Dutch, German or Swiss.

But this is to quibble, for Succession’s Tuscan sojourn draws on association, on Chianti as a state of mind. And in choosing locations away from Chianti itself, the producers are only doing what visitors should do, which is to explore the Val d’Orcia, or the Maremma, or the Garfagnana or the Pratomagno, or any other of Tuscany’s quieter and more captivating corners. 

And in doing so, there is no need for wealth or power, of course, just a desire for the simple – and priceless – pleasures of Italy’s most beautiful region.

Fit for a Medici: How to see Tuscany in style

You don’t have to spend like a billionaire, of course, to have a superb holiday in Tuscany, but if money were no object, and flamboyant outlay was the aim, here’s how you might go about it:

Villas

The 13-bedroom Villa Cetinale, a setting for Succession, can be rented on application (00 39 0577 311147; villacetinale.com) from around £22,000 to £48,000 a week. The main villa (sleeps 24) at La Foce (lafoce.com), which also features as a location in Succession, can be rented from around £15,735 weekly. 

Four smaller properties on the estate are also available, including Fontalgozzo (sleeps eight), with private pool, from £3,245 weekly. Other high-end Tuscan villas include rock star Sting’s Il Palagio, on Chianti’s eastern fringes, which sleeps 28. It can be rented direct (il-palagio.com) or via Abercrombie Kent Villas (01242 547705; akvillas.com) from £95,708 a week.

Abercrombie Kent Villas has a selection of other exclusive villas across Tuscany, with top-price Palazzo Prati (sleeps 18) in Chianti from £24,931 weekly and Villa Nereo (sleeps 14), from £19,548. It offers smaller villas in Tuscany such as Casetta nel Chianti (sleeps eight) from £4,731 weekly. Succession’s final episodes also briefly feature another cypress-framed Italian property, Villa La Cassinella (sleeps 17), but this is on the shores of Lake Como, possibly intended to double as Tuscany. It, too, is available for rent at £140,000 a week, making it one of Italy’s most exclusive and expensive villas.

Castles

It’s unlikely the Florentine authorities will rent you the Forte di Belvedere as they did the Kardashians, but you can always ask (comune.fi.it). Far older, more chic and from around £12,590 a day/night for the entire castle – far cheaper – is Gargonza (gargonza.it), a magical fortified hamlet between Siena and Arezzo. It sleeps up to 90 and can host/cater weddings and other events.

Hotels

If you prefer a high-end hotel to the rigours of a villa, then budget around £1,000 a night for a double room, give or take, at Borgo Santo Pietro (borgosantopietro.com), a grand country house hotel close to Villa Cetinale. 

In Florence, you’ll only need to spend about half that, £500, to overnight at the Four Seasons (fourseasons.com), a sumptuously converted Florentine palazzo. In both cases these prices are for the lowliest rooms: opt for a suite and you’ll spend much more; from £13,230 a night in the case of the Four Seasons’ Presidential Suite.

Dining

If you are in Pienza want to eat at the restaurant used in Succession, with its glorious views, book an outdoor table at La Terrazza del Chiostro (00 39 0578 748183; laterrazzadelchiostro.it). Given it may well become even more popular in the wake of screening, another nearby restaurant, Terrazza della Val d’Orcia (00 39 3288 965140; terrazzadellavaldorcia.it), offers an arguably better panorama from its garden terrace.

If you’re a billionaire, however, and cost and cachet are your ruling principles, then a Michelin star may well be your guiding light in choosing a Tuscan restaurant. In Florence this means the three-Michelin-star Enoteca Pinchiorri (enotecapinchiorri.it; five-course tasting menus from £128 per person) – among other things, Pinchiorri catered the Kim Kardashian-Kanye West Florentine wedding in 2014. 

Closer to Chianti, the choice is between the two-star Arnolfo (arnolfo.com) in Colle di Val d’Elsa and – suggesting the sort of money that’s around – no fewer than 10 one-star options. Near Lucca, where the smarter Russian money now resides, choose between a Terra (Land) and Mare (Sea) tasting menu (both £145 per person) at the two-star Il Piccolo Principe (ristoranteilpiccoloprincipe.it).

Sightseeing

Such a bore having to queue for Michelangelo’s David or the Uffizi art gallery in Florence. Or having to join the throng in Siena’s Piazza del Campo merely to glimpse a few seconds of the Palio, the town’s famous horse race. Much easier to pay a fixer to arrange private after-hours tours of Tuscany’s leading museums or, in the case of the Palio, a privileged position on a private palace balcony above the Campo. For these and other high-end Tuscan arrangements, contact Emily FitzRoy, a consultant for Succession, at Bellini Travel (bellinitravel.com).
