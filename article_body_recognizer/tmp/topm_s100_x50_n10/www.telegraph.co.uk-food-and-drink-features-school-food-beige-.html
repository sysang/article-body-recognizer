Why is school food all so... beige?

Instead of relying on external companies, head teachers are hiring specialists to give pupils healthy meals

By Claire Coleman 5 December 2021 • 11:00am

You probably remember the days of Jamie Oliver highlighting the poor standards of food in school canteens. But if you think that, more than 15 years later, things are any better, you might be disappointed. 

A recent report from Bite Back 2030, the youth-led charity founded by Oliver that campaigns for the food industry – including school dining rooms – to prioritise child health, revealed that we are, as Oliver said, risking “a return to the beige days” of chips, cheap chicken and white bread.

The report, based on interviews earlier this year with more than 30 secondary school pupils, is packed with damning evidence that many schools are ignoring the School Foods Standards and regularly serving up “pizza, sausage rolls or pasties, chicken wings even”, “11 different types of desserts: jellies, cakes, biscuits, flapjacks”, “loads of canned and bottled fizzy drinks, and subs and cookies”, “Skittles and Smarties”. 

Elsewhere, schools have reported that catering companies have either stopped providing fresh fruit entirely, or are providing poor quality, rotting produce. Some have complained that “main meals” are nothing more than cheap cheese and ham on sliced white bread. 

And this matters: not only do we know that nutritious meals improve behaviour and attainment in schools, but with rates of childhood obesity soaring, it’s more important than ever that children eat healthily in school. The evidence shows that what kids learn about food now will have an impact throughout the rest of their lives.

So what’s gone wrong?

For a start, there are still the fundamental problems associated with outsourcing catering to large private companies, and how much control schools can have over what’s served when multi-year deals have been signed. 

While it’s difficult to get a handle on how many schools employ external contractors, because the data isn’t collected centrally, best estimates by Chefs in Schools, one charity working within the sector, suggest that in England 20 per cent of schools use local authority caterers, a further 20 per cent have in-house provision, and 60 per cent use a private catering company. The largest of those external companies hold contracts worth millions. 

Earlier this year, it was reported that Chartwells, the school catering arm of Compass has, in the last five years won 70 contracts worth £443 million to provide school meals. Other big names include Caterlink, ISS and Aspens Services. “I spoke to a head who wanted to get rid of sugary drinks in the canteen,” says Nicki Whiteman of Bite Back 2030. 

“But the catering company was resistant on the grounds that those were some of the products with the biggest mark-up. When organisations with profit as their motive are involved, there seems to be a conflict between prioritising child health and filling the canteen with healthy options, or giving the starring role to whatever is going to make the profit.” 

Alongside this long-term sticking point, many attribute the backsliding to issues associated with Brexit and the pandemic. There have been reports that supply-chain problems have led to caterers either serving up mouldy fruit, or stopping providing it entirely. And the government advice to work from home over the last 18 months, in tandem with staff shortages, led to some schools scrapping hot meals in favour of sandwiches. 

But, according to Henry Dimbleby, the Government’s food tsar, whose recent national food strategy not only contains proposals to extend free school meals to more children but also to inspect the quality of school dinners, that’s not reason enough. 

“There will always be things that are tricky,” Dimbleby says. “There are always reasons, or excuses why you can’t do things properly, but there are thousands of schools that continue to serve fantastic food, who aren’t using these issues as an excuse, so Brexit and the pandemic are just not good reasons.” 

And he should know. Dimbleby is one of the founders of a charity called Chefs in Schools, which was launched after he posted a tweet asking whether any chefs would be interested in taking over the kitchen at his children’s state school. 

Nicole Pisani – then head chef at the acclaimed Soho restaurant Nopi – got in touch and was offered the job. She went on to train the school cooks as she would a restaurant brigade, teaching them to cook everything from scratch and to bake bread daily. 

Chefs in Schools was born and now uses several models to help thousands of children (to date they’re in 42 schools, in the process of training another 10, and have seven more due to start in the spring) eat healthier meals and engage with food – whether it’s helping to grow herbs and vegetables that subsequently end up in their school lunches, being taught cookery classes by the school chef or simply being more informed about where their food comes from. 

“A school can hire a head chef who becomes not just the head chef but also a food educator – we can train that head chef, who goes on to train the existing team,” explains Pisani, who along with Dimbleby and Louise Nichols, the head who offered her the job, founded the charity. 

“Or, we can train the existing team in an intensive two-week training programme where we might take the previous menu that they were using and do all the exact same items but made from scratch. We’ll also introduce them to our recommended suppliers, look at costings, help them do surveys and get parents involved for tasting sessions so they understand the food journey that the school is about to go on.” 

The benefits of employing a head chef rather than an external contractor are legion – not only are kids eating better, and learning about good nutrition, but if there are supply chain issues, chefs can be more adaptable and, in the long run, it’s also a more cost-effective way of catering than outsourcing to an external company. 

“It’s always cheaper to buy a bag of onions rather than buying pre-chopped, and when you’re buying direct from a supplier, you’re not paying a middle man,” says Lisa Markwell, a patron of Chefs in Schools and The Telegraph’s acting food editor.

 “One of the schools that worked with Chefs in Schools actually found they saved enough money cooking from scratch that they were able to employ more teaching assistants.” 

That’s certainly the experience of Kirstie Barrett, head teacher at Coppetts Wood Primary School and Children’s Centre in Barnet. She had worked with Chefs in Schools at her last school in Hackney and so when she moved to her new role last year, made revamping the catering a priority. 

Chefs in Schools came in and trained the existing team, who were subsequently employed directly by the school. “It’s too early to see the financial impact here,” she says. “But I know in my previous school, I was surprised by how much additional money we seemed to have.” 

But for Barrett, there were other advantages. “When you employ the kitchen staff directly, it has a really positive impact. They feel part of the team, part of the community, they’re not just in the dining room, they are a part of the school and a part of the education process.”

 She also says she’s seen a real change in the way that the staff relate to their work. “When stuff comes in vacuum packed, you’re very removed from the raw ingredients and I think you lose some of the pleasure you get from serving food that you’ve cooked from scratch yourself. Now, you can see they take pride in what they’re doing, that they have a real love for the food that they’re making for the children.” 

Chefs in Schools aren’t the only ones doing great work in this area. Food consultancy Eagle Catering Solutions and charities such as School Food Matters help support schools to bring catering in-house, while other schools have independently made inspirational changes to their canteens. 

Greenside Primary in Hammersmith boasts vegetarian menus cooked in-house that have featured on the TV show Jamie’s Meat-Free Meals and Washingborough Academy in Lincoln has instigated food education strategies so admired, they’re being used as a case study by the Department of Health on their Childhood Obesity Plan.

So if there are only upsides, why aren’t more schools doing it?

“Sometimes I think it’s easier to stick with the status quo,” says Barrett. “Often you might be part of a group of schools, or a local authority that all use the same external catering company, so maybe you feel that that gives you some sort of protection. But also it’s just something you often don’t have to think about when there are so many other demands on your time. It’s just a payment that you make to one of those big companies and then you don’t have to think about it.” 

She also points out that while in the long term it’s more financially viable, “it is a bit of an HR task to employ catering staff directly, and sometimes there might be outlay in terms of equipment too”. 

None of that seems like it should be a barrier to entry, but for too many people who have the power to effect these simple, yet seismic changes, it is. And if the head doesn’t do it, nobody else will, as Prue Leith, another of the Chefs in Schools patrons tells me. 

“The tragedy of school food is that it depends on the attitude of the head teacher. If they are not prepared to put time and resources into kitchens and teaching a child about food and diet it doesn’t happen,” she says. 

Maybe if there was more pressure on schools to be accountable for what they’re serving to our children this would change, but despite the importance of healthy school meals being widely acknowledged, there’s a huge amount of buck-passing.

 “Everyone in politics realises just how important nutrition is for youngsters in schools, not just in terms of school performance and long-term health, but because it’s a critical part of tackling the obesity epidemic. Yet even though this is recognised, for the Secretary of State for Education, it’s just never on the top 20 list of things to do,” says Dimbleby.
