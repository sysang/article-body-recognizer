Supply chain crisis hits first-time buyers

The number of new homes built this autumn fell 11pc as shortages hit builders

By Melissa Lawford 6 December 2021 • 10:00am

The supply chain crisis will crush the hopes of first-time buyers as it arrests the construction of new-build homes.

Buyers face delays and shortages as housebuilders grapple with a scarcity of building materials and rapidly rising costs that have dramatically hit the supply of new homes.

What impact will this have on house prices, especially for those trying to get on the ladder before the Help to Buy scheme closes in 2023?

Builders cannot meet demand

The cost of construction materials for new housing jumped 22.6pc year-on-year in October, according to government data. Nearly two thirds of respondents to estate agent Knight Frank's industry survey said they were affected by the supply chain crisis.

This has hit the number of homes being built, which has fallen sharply in recent months. From September to October this year, the number of completed new homes was down 11pc on the same period in 2019, analysis by Knight Frank estate agents found.

This drop in housebuilding is remarkable in the context of the housing market boom. Developers construct new homes at the “absorption rate”, said Andrew Wishart, of Capital Economics, a consultancy.

This is the maximum number of homes that they can produce without inflating supply to such an extent that prices start to decline. When sales rise, builders scale up production accordingly, not worried that values will be affected.

New-build starts are normally equivalent to 13pc of housing transactions. In the first half of 2021, however, that fell to 9pc.

Transactions across this period jumped by 50pc compared to the first six months of 2019, driven by a massive jump in demand. Yet housebuilders did not ramp up production.

Small builders have been worst hit

Small housebuilders and specialist subcontractors have been worst hit by the supply chain crisis and rising materials costs, said Noble Francis, of the Construction Products Association, an industry body. This is because larger builders have a greater certainty of demand and much better economies of scale.

Katy Jordan, of Story Homes, a medium-sized housebuilder, said: “We’re at the bottom of the food chain.”

The cost of timber, which is needed for roofs and joists, has jumped at least 50pc, said Ms Jordan. On average, across every project, Story Homes’ entire build costs have jumped by 5pc. These materials costs will add to the overall price that buyers must pay.

Buyers face delays

Major and smaller builders alike cannot escape the problems of scarcity of labour and materials, said Mr Francis. “The availability issues have slowed build out rates and are likely to do so over the next six months.” Bricks, cement, roofing products, paints, resins and timber are all in short supply.

“Looking ahead, we have a project in planning and we are trying to determine now what bricks we can get delivered in time. We could be forced to wait six months or longer before we can start work,” said Ms Jordan.

Buyers who have purchased off-plan in affected developments could be left in the lurch. If builders can't deliver on time, some could be pushed into rental accommodation while they wait, said Ms Jordan. 

What will happen to house prices?

For now, double-digit house price growth will protect housebuilders’ margins, said Mr Francis. In turn, this will protect buyers from having to shoulder the additional costs.

But longer term, if the supply of new homes drops significantly, prices will increase for buyers, said Ms Jordan.

“Materials shortages, the end of the Help to Buy equity loan scheme, and a cooling market suggest that the high water mark for new housing supply has already been and gone,” added Mr Wishart.

Bigger blows ahead

When supply chain problems ease, there will be no increase in building because developers will have been dealt an even bigger blow. The end of the Help to Buy equity loan scheme will coincide with a forecast dip in transactions as the post-pandemic boom fades and interest rates rise. 

Without the scheme, new-build housing construction will fall from 219,000 homes in the 2019-20 tax year, to 190,000 in 2023-24, forecast Mr Wishart. This is a fall of 13pc, and 110,000 short of the Government’s target of 300,000 homes per year by the mid 2020s.

The Government has billed its affordable housing scheme First Homes as the replacement for Help to Buy, but as yet builders have no information on how the logistics of this scheme will work.

“We’re still waiting to see how it will play out in practical terms. It would be helpful to talk about the details, but I don’t have any,” said Ms Jordan.
