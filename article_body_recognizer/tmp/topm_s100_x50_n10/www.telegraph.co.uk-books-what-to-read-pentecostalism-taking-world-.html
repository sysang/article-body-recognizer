Why Pentecostalism is taking over the world

3/5

Murder, mega-churches and speaking in tongues – Elle Hardy's Beyond Belief explores the dark side of Christainity's fastest-growing sect

By Colin Freeman 5 December 2021 • 5:00pm

As social influencers for God, Flordelis dos Santos de Souza and her husband Anderson were Christianity’s answer to the Kardashians – she with her model good looks, he with his televangelist grin. The pair ran a chain of Pentecostal mega-churches in Brazil, financed by sales of gospel records and worshippers’ donations.

For years, their heavily Instagrammed, jet-set lifestyle mesmerised their followers and Brazil’s tabloids, but it wasn’t all good news. In 2019, after a night making love on Copacabana Beach, Anderson was shot dead while parking the car back home. It looked like just another shocking Rio street crime – until Flordelis herself was charged with ordering the hit.

Worthy of a Brazilian soap opera, the rise and fall of this preaching power couple is among many vivid tales told by journalist Elle Hardy in Beyond Belief: How Pentecostal Christianity is taking over the World. Often associated with shiny-suited pastors sporting a Bible in one hand and a Rolex on the other, much of Pentecostalism may seem like serpent-oil from the Garden of Eden. Yet today it’s Christianity’s fastest-growing strand: a brimstone-scented alternative to wishy-washier branches of the faith, such as the Church of England, and with catchier tunes than To Be a Pilgrim.

In just a century, it has gained 600 million followers worldwide, from the favellas of Rio and Trump strongholds in Texas through to Nigeria and North Korea. It wields political as well as spiritual clout, Hardy argues, anointing hard-Right figures like Donald Trump as Christian soldiers in our culture wars.

“In spite of its fundamentalist beliefs, the movement has already shown itself to be the future of Christian faith, with its uncanny ability to stay in lockstep with the social zeitgeist,” she concludes.

So how did all this start? Hardy takes us back to the American Midwest in the early 20th century, painting a picture reminiscent of the “Prophets’ Corner” scene in Monty Python’s Life of Brian. Self-styled holy men were everywhere, denouncing sinners and each other – among them one Charles Fox Parham, a slick-tongued preacher said to rant at 250 words per minute. It was the tongue of one of his congregationers, however, that sprang him to fame. While in the throes of religious ecstasy at his church in Bethel, Kansas, a woman called Agnes Ozman began speaking in a “tongue” said to be Chinese.

Parham hailed it as a sign that the Holy Spirit was coursing through her – just as it had done on Pentecost, the 50th day after the Resurrection, when Christ’s disciples are likewise said to have spoken a multitude of languages. So-called “glossolalia” – or tongue-speaking – is said to be a divine gift to allow the conversion of souls in foreign lands.

However, one flaw of Hardy’s otherwise elegant account is that it fails to unpick this key miracle. Ozman was not, by all accounts, speaking real Chinese. Nor did any other early Pentecostalists, some of whom only discovered this after heading off for missionary work in Asia. Hardy doesn’t really examine this – or say why folk believed it in the first place.

What does seem clear, though, is that Pentecostalism thrives where other churches – or the state – fail to deliver. Partly, it’s because it embraces a so-called “Prosperity Gospel”, whereby donations to the church encourage God’s financial blessings in return, like a heavenly investment bank. While Hardy says this can sometimes just be a “sacred shakedown”, it can also mean a social safety net in countries where there isn’t one, like Brazil. Couples like Flordelis and Anderson – who grew up in the favellas – can also connect with their congregants in a way that lofty, theologically-trained bishops do not. “The appeal of the Pentecostal pastor is that they have bubbled up from below, just as you aspire to,” writes Hardy. “They grew up navigating the same streets you did... They’re the first person to come around to give your idiot husband a talking to when he’s been drinking and gambling and womanising again.”

Pentecostalism also seems uniquely adaptable to local needs. In Nigeria, Pentacostalist megachurches have opened on motorways, making them handy for commuters. In South Korea, Pentecostalists help North Koreans escape Kim Jong-un’s regime – a process comparable to a religious conversion. And in Britain, the Light and Life Gypsy Church has 20,000 members – including the boxer Tyson Fury – who credit it with encouraging literacy and self-discipline.

It may be no surprise that Pentecostalists are also keen Donald Trump supporters. Like many Pentecostal pastors, he is as flawed as he is flashy, but still manages to come across as an ordinary citizen rather than a Beltway high priest. That can make for very loyal followers – and very forgiving ones.

While Hardy is an engaging usher round the Pentecostal world, I feel she has underestimated lay readers’ curiosity on certain basics, like the mysterious finances of Prosperity Gospel. How much of the money that Pentecostal worshippers give to their churches actually comes back to them? And given that some preachers turn out to be lining their own pockets, how does the movement manage to stay credible? The faithful may be too busy speaking in ecstatic tongues to care much. To me, though, this seems like Pentecostalism’s biggest miracle of all.
