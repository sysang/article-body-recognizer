How Pearl Harbor answered the prayers of Allied and Axis leaders alike

On the 80th anniversary of the surprise attack, two books trace how it shaped the war – and why it delighted Roosevelt, Churchill and Hitler

By Christopher Harding 6 December 2021 • 5:00am

Churchill slept like a baby. Chiang Kai-shek danced a jig. Hitler saw his future come into murderous focus. And Roosevelt at last had his country’s anti-interventionists where he wanted them. One of the most remarkable things about Japan’s surprise attack on Pearl Harbor, on December 7 1941, is the way that – with the sad exception of the thousands who were killed or injured – it appeared at the time to be the answer to entirely contradictory sets of geostrategic prayers.

So deep and dense is our understanding now of what came next, thanks to decades of research, that we easily lose sight of just how open to interpretation was this infamous air raid in its immediate aftermath. Two dramatic and highly readable accounts take us back there, to a time when information was partial, rumour was rife, and a still largely European war stood on the brink of going truly global.

Takuma Melber’s brisk account Pearl Harbor draws on Japanese-language sources, revealing uncertainty and division within Japan’s leadership in the autumn of 1941. The country was clearly in a corner. Conflict with China was depleting precious resources – fuel most of all – that American embargoes (intended to punish Japanese aggression) were making harder to replenish. Should Japan seek peace or prepare for war? In the end it did both: a shrewd policy muddied by poor coordination – sometimes intentional – between branches of government.

Japan’s ambassador to the United States, Kichisaburō Nomura, went to see US Secretary of State Cordell Hull in the early afternoon of December 7 bearing a diplomatic missive declaring negotiations over. Surprised by the frosty treatment he received – he was only the messenger, after all – on his return to the Japanese embassy he realised why: an attack was under way against American ships and planes on the Hawaiian island of Oahu, including at Pearl Harbor. Nomura knew of no such plan. Hull had received an initial report just minutes before their meeting.

Those who planned the attack – a fallback option if talks were to fail – regarded the Americans as merchants at heart, rather than warriors, bound to return to the negotiating table after a demonstration of Japanese resolve. The attack was, as Melber points out, well-supported by espionage in Hawaii. The Imperial Navy found out just before the attack that two American aircraft carriers – the biggest prizes on offer – would be at sea that day. Had the plan allowed for the pursuit of such vessels in open water, Pearl Harbor might have been an even greater success for Japan.

This is an impressively balanced survey, but the flipside to Melber’s clear and economical storytelling is a relative dearth of interpretation. Pearl Harbor was probably inevitable once America’s final negotiating position, known as the “Hull note”, was interpreted in Japan “not [as] a compromise but a veritable affront”. Given that an earlier draft had been far more conciliatory, Melber might have asked why the position hardened – and whether there is weight to the claim that the US effectively manoeuvred Japan into striking first, as a means of overcoming isolationist public opinion and allowing America to enter the wider war.

In Hitler’s American Gamble, Brendan Simms and Charlie Laderman bring us the global picture, arguing that the real turning point during that dramatic December was not the attack itself but Hitler’s declaration of war on the United States four days later.

Hitler was under no immediate treaty obligation to make that move, since his ally Japan had launched an attack rather than suffered one. Nor could anyone be entirely sure, as news of Pearl Harbor rolled in, quite what Hitler would make of it. As the book makes clear, it was highly likely by this point that the US would end up at war with Japan and Germany – an undeclared naval war between the US and Germany was already under way in the Atlantic.

The value of this book lies less in the making of a new argument than in the real-time insights it offers into reactions around the world to Japan’s audacious assault, switching – sometimes hour by hour, paragraph by paragraph – between countries. Race emerges as critically important. Many Japanese worried that Hitler might reconcile with white America and Britain rather than stick with Japan. Europeans and Americans, meanwhile, held such a low view of the Japanese that many believed Hitler must have had a hand in Pearl Harbor. “Japan is an avid pupil of Germany,” wrote the anti-Nazi diarist Friedrich Kellner.

Rumours did the rounds in the United States that swastikas had been spotted on the planes over Pearl Harbor – here was an aerial blitzkrieg of which only the Germans were racially capable. “There is nothing to worry about… they are Japanese,” said an officer aboard HMS Repulse, shortly before the Japanese sank his ship and one other off Malaya on December 10.

There was politics here, too, of course. When Churchill declared in Parliament on December 8 that “Hitler’s madness has infected the Japanese mind”, he was seeking to establish the strongest possible connection between Japan and Germany. Both he and Roosevelt realised they had work to do here: Pearl Harbor did not immediately convince Americans of the need for war with the Nazis. “The world… held its breath”, as Simms and Laderman put it, while the isolationist pressure group America First remained in business and Churchill and Stalin began to fear that Pearl Harbor might conceivably set them back.

Lend-Lease arrangements, under which the United States funnelled war supplies to Britain, China and the Soviets, might be curtailed to help the Americans fight Japan. And even if the US joined the war in Europe, their troops might nevertheless prioritise punishment for Japan. Churchill’s claim to have “slept the sleep of the saved and thankful” after Pearl Harbor must be weighed against such worries (Chiang’s relief was purer perhaps, though the story of his joyful dance may – sadly – be apocryphal). Hitler’s December 11 declaration of war against the US helped put an end to some of this uncertainty. A decision that historians have tended to regard as evidence of Hitler gradually losing his grip – on the war and reality itself – is seen by Simms and Laderman as a strategic move not unlike Japan’s attack on Pearl Harbor: faced with a stronger opponent, be sure to throw the first punch.

There is a great deal of detail in this account, and some readers may feel that the desire to present a multi-focal global picture at times wins out over the demands of pertinence or coherent narrative. Still, it is fascinating to see news of Pearl Harbor ripple outwards and reactions build, from rumours in the US of an imminent Japanese attack on San Francisco to commuters in Japan moving from quiet disbelief to patriotic extemporising for the benefit of fellow train travellers.

Both of these books leave unanswered questions about Hirohito and Roosevelt’s thinking – the latter’s “internal deliberations”, Simms and Laderman concede, “remain as elusive as ever”. Perhaps these questions are simply unanswerable. What we are left with is a renewed respect for those who, 80 years ago, had to take such big decisions on the basis of uncertain evidence, and a deeper appreciation for Churchill’s jubilant response to one of his chiefs of staff, who advised him after America’s entry into the war against Hitler to continue treating his Atlantic ally with care and caution: “Oh that is the way we talked to her while we were wooing her. Now that she is in the harem, we talk to her quite differently!”
