Our job is to wipe smiles off Australian faces during these Ashes

Plus: Why England's players are watching the Azeem Rafiq case with interest

James Anderson 6 December 2021 • 8:03am

There is Test cricket and there is an Ashes series. There is Ashes cricket and then there is an Ashes series in Australia. It is another level again and to win over here for the second time in my career would be a very special feeling.

There is just something about the Ashes as an England cricketer that lights a fire inside you. You know the history, and how much it means to Australians to beat England in front of their own crowds. You are here to be walloped and laughed at, your purpose is to make Australians feel good about themselves and take national pride in their team. It is our job to make sure that does not happen.

This is my fifth Ashes tour and it has a very different vibe to what I have experienced in the past. We have gone out for dinner a few times and the nice welcome, barring the odd one or two individuals, shows the Australian public are genuinely happy to see us this time. They have recently emerged from tough, long lockdowns and are just glad the Ashes series is going ahead.

One guy in a restaurant tried to taunt us but it fell a bit flat when he warned us to get ready for a green seamer at the Gabba. I looked at Stuart Broad and we said ‘great, can’t wait then’.

There is normally a lot of noise around an Ashes, plenty of verbal bashing, but this feels like the quietest build up to a series I have experienced over here.

But since arriving on this tour I have tried to impress on people what it means to win in Australia, and achieve great things at historic grounds like the Gabba, Adelaide Oval, MCG and SCG. I have also tried to tell the lads what it takes as well. One of the big things that sticks out for me from 2010-11 is how much hard work we put into that trip.

We were all absolutely knackered at the end of it. When the Tests come thick and fast it can be brutal but if you get on a role and play good cricket, which we did then, it can help as well.

But I have also had some tough tours to Australia as well and winning here would make those fade into distant memories, so that is extra motivation.

On the last couple of tours to Australia we have not been sure of our best XI, had players who have struggled and little incidents happen that distracted us too much.

To win here you need absolute sole focus on the cricket and to throw yourself into it. The challenge this time is for individuals to flick the switch mentally after so little cricket. Everyone will be feeling their way in for the first Test match.

In terms of bowling overs out in the middle, I have never been so underprepared. With the weather and quarantine it has been a very different preparation to anything I’ve experienced before, let alone an Ashes.

I feel fortunate that with my expeience I will be able to switch on when that Test match comes around. It might be a little more difficult for the younger guys who have not played in an Ashes before but it is up to the more experienced players to help them find the right headspace.

It is about trying to tell them what to expect at the Gabba. The changing rooms feel like a dungeon down below the field of play and when you walk out you are hit by a wall of noise as you emerge from the tunnel. It can feel daunting when you are out there and you have to help each other as much as you can.

If batting you need to use your partner so it does not feel like 11 versus one. Similarly with bowling, sometimes things can go badly for you as a bowler. If you feel events are spiraling out of control, use the people around you to slow things down and help you through that.

I heard a rumour there might be some ex-pats forming a Brisbane version of the Barmy Army. There might be up to 1,000 of them in amongst 30 odd thousand Aussies so they will need to make a lot of noise to be heard.

Personally, I would be delighted to play three of the five Tests in this series. Anymore would be a great bonus. I was going to get through all five against India in the summer before the last was cancelled but this will be more intense and the conditions draining so we are going to have to manage our way through it. It is the same for Australia.

We are hearing conditions might be different to what we have experienced before at the Gabba. The weather is very average and more rain is forecast. But the challenge will be the same. The Ashes first morning in Brisbane is a fabulous experience. We just have to embrace it, and remember we can win.

Azeem Rafiq feeling alienated in a cricket dressing room is upsetting

We are thousands of miles from home but watching the Azeem Rafiq situation with close interest.

We have obviously discussed it a lot as a group. It has been upsetting to see the game of cricket being talked about in this way. We know we have got a job as international cricketers to set an example for people, to try and show people cricket is an inclusive sport.

What has upset me is hearing that Rafiq felt alienated and unhappy in the dressing room because it has been my second home for 20 years now. It is somewhere I have felt safe and happy so to hear someone feel like he did is is absolutely awful.

My job is to make everyone feel welcome especially as I have grown into being a senior player. In my experience we have tried to be as inclusive as possible in the England set up to anyone who comes in.

I have spent time thinking back and asking myself whether I have heard stuff and should have stepped in but I can’t think of any occasion when something was out of order or unnecessary. Perhaps I have just been lucky but I hope most others feel the same way.
