Calls for tougher fines to stop water companies dumping sewage

Telegraph analysis reveals fines levied against England's main nine water firms barely made a dent in their profits between 2010 and 2021

By Olivia Rudgard, Environment Correspondent and Ben Butcher 6 December 2021 • 10:33am

The courts are failing to reflect the cost of pollution in fines levelled on water companies, campaigners say, as figures show they were fined less than one per cent of their profits over the past decade.

England's main nine water firms were cautioned or punished hundreds of times between 2010 and 2021, but Telegraph analysis suggests the fines levied barely made a dent in their profits.

The nine companies were fined 0.7 per cent of their post-tax profits in Environment Agency cases over the past decade, with the average fine less than £200,000, based on data published by US transparency group Good Jobs First.

Earlier this year Southern Water was fined a record £90m for deliberate sewage dumps between 2010 and 2015, but the data shows this case is a significant outlier.

Sentencing the company in that case judge Jeremy Johnson, at Canterbury Crown Court, said it "had not learnt" from being fined £2.7 million in three separate but similar cases since August 2013, and "its offending simply continued".

'A real deterrent is desperately needed'

Penelope Gane, head of practice at Fish Legal, said a "real deterrent" was "desperately needed".

She added: "If you think of the savings that Southern Water must have made each year on their treatment costs, you may conclude on a purely financial basis it was still cheaper to pollute the water environment."

Sentencing guidelines for environmental offences were updated in 2014 to allow for higher fines, and suggest penalties of up to £3m for the most serious, deliberate offences by large companies, though judges can go beyond this for the largest businesses with turnovers of over £50m.

In 2018, a £33,600 fine was handed to Northumbrian Water for three separate pollution incidents which covered a stream with a "thick sewage fungus" for 100m and killed freshwater shrimp and midge larvae.

In 2019, Anglian Water was ordered to pay £156,000 for polluting a brook with sewage because of negligence and a lack of maintenance at its plant.

'Businesses can absorb the financial impact of fines'

Mark Lloyd, chief executive of The Rivers Trust, said: "We are in a position where businesses can absorb the financial impact of fines in their operating costs and many pollution incidents are not even investigated, let alone prosecuted.

"Since the Farming Rules for Water were introduced three years ago, there have been no prosecutions but there are widespread breaches of the rules.

"The Environment Agency needs more resources and support from the government to play its unique role as a regulator more rigorously.

"The courts need to recognise the impact of pollution on the rest of the society with more significant fines that are linked to business turnover."

Environment Agency cases are not the only form of enforcement for pollution offences. Financial regulator Ofwat also has the power to discipline water companies for failing to treat sewage correctly, including financial sanctions.

Fines levied for pollution can be court-imposed or come directly from the EA, using a measure called an enforcement undertaking.

'We would like fines to grow higher'

A spokesman for the Environment Agency said: "Since 2015 the Environment Agency has brought 48 prosecutions against water companies, securing fines of over £136 million."We are pleased to see that courts are imposing higher fines but we would like to see them grow higher still.

"We would also like to see the criminal courts applying penalties consistently and proportionately, and we would welcome the most serious breaches by very large companies attracting sanctions based on a percentage of turnover."

A spokesman for industry group Water UK said there had been "£160 billion of investment since the 1990s - achieved despite bills remaining about the same for over a decade, with some of the biggest water companies foregoing all investor dividends for several years.

"Those dividends that are being paid will often go to pension funds and other institutions with a long-term stake, and allow companies to efficiently raise finance - part of the reason why productivity has increased by 60 per cent over the period."

A spokeswoman for Anglian Water said it did not recognise the figures, adding: "Any profit generated during this timeframe should be seen alongside the huge capital investment of £4.5billion made in the same period."

A spokesman for Northumbrian Water said: "Our regulators, Ofwat and the Environment Agency, have assessed Northumbrian Water Limited as industry-leading on pollution over the last three years."
