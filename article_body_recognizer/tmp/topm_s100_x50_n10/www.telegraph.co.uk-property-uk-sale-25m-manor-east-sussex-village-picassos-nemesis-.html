For sale: a £2.5m manor in the East Sussex village that was Picasso’s nemesis

Chiddingly has been home to a disproportionate number of celebrities

By Melissa Lawford 6 December 2021 • 6:00am

According to local lore, when Pablo Picasso visited the Six Bells pub in the East Sussex village of Chiddingly, he wholly misjudged the landlord.

It was November 1950 and he was staying with his friends, the photojournalist Lee Miller and her husband the surrealist artist Roland Penrose, who lived in Farley Farm House on the edge of the village. Picasso had strolled to the Six Bells for a drink, but on arrival realised that he had empty pockets.

The walk was nearly a mile and Picasso really wanted a beer. He made an unconventional proposal to the proprietor: could he get a drink in exchange for a sketch?

The landlord was having none of it and told him to use money like everybody else. Picasso was spurned. One suspects the landlord would have been a staunch supporter of Telegraph Money’s Keep Cash campaign.

Picasso should have known that star power would have little clout in Chiddingly. Despite its unassuming population of 1,247, the village has seen a disproportionately large share of celebrities over the years. 

Penrose and Miller, who was reportedly particularly accomplished at cooking roast suckling pig, entertained a steady stream of artistic visitors that included Henry Moore, Dorothea Tanning and Max Ernst. But Chiddingly’s roster of past residents also included Peter Pan author J.M. Barrie, the composer Neil Emerson and Sir John Jefferay.

Sir John was one of Queen Elizabeth I’s Chief Barons of the Exchequer – a judicial officer role below the Chancellor of the Exchequer. In other words, roughly speaking, he was a kind of junior, Tudor Rishi Sunak. 

He lived like a prince. In 1574, Sir John rebuilt a local house to create a grand six-bedroom manor with high ceilings and stone mullioned, diamond leadlight windows.

The property, which is for sale for £2.5m with Jackson-Stops estate agents, has been restored with an open-plan family room that has doors opening onto the gardens. 

The flag-stoned kitchen is serviced by a large cellar. But Picasso wouldn't have needed it if he had stayed, because he could have quickly popped back from the pub for his wallet. This house is just 300m from the Six Bells.
