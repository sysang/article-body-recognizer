The tricky politics of inheritance – who should you leave your money to?

When someone dies, leaving very little paperwork, it can open up a whole quagmire of unknowns

By Jenny Tucker 6 December 2021 • 5:00am

A close friend of mine has always thrived in the warm and nurturing bond of her close siblings. But after their father’s death, a bitter row evolved over a fairground sign. It was a thing of quirky beauty and an emotional link to their childhood – their father had displayed it in his kitchen, and it was literally at the core of many family gatherings. The problem arose because three of the five siblings announced that their dad had intended to pass it on to them. Unfortunately, nothing was in writing; a will had never been made. So it was one sister’s word against a brother’s. Eventually the dispute was settled when the more bolshy sister claimed she’d never forgive them if they didn’t agree she could take it. The fairground sign is now positioned over the fireplace in her living room, and rarely do the rest of her clan choose to sit in there.

It’s little surprise that most of us find the conversation about end of existence wishes a prickly and uncomfortable ordeal. Often divisive and scary, the topic stays veiled in silence until the omnipresence of death thrusts it disturbingly into the open. But like most of life’s thorny issues, avoidance is the more harmful option.

Through discussion and transparency – the earlier the better – families can circumnavigate all manner of heartache. As the money saving expert Martin Lewis said on ITV’s This Morning, “Yes, it’s morbid talking about this stuff, but we are grown-ups!” In fact, involving potential recipients of the will in the planning of how the inheritance may unfold post death (or even during life) can be a chance to iron out the complexities thoughtfully and successfully.

Georgia, 32, recently visited her parents for Sunday lunch and over the apple crumble was told they wanted to discuss their will. “My initial reaction was ‘Oh God, no!’,” she admits, “They’re only in their mid-60s, and thinking about their death feels far too soon.” But Georgia’s parents were worried that their daughter, as an only child, would carry the burden of decision-making as they moved towards their declining years. What’s more, beside the family home, there was another substantial rental property to consider too. “Even though my parents had been reliant on this second house as a form of income, they wanted to gift it to me now to try to avoid me paying the 40 per cent inheritance tax once they were both gone,” says Georgia. “It was such a caring gesture, and I was overwhelmed at first, but, for my benefit, it made financial sense.” This decision also meant that Georgia’s future security was protected. Once the property was in her name, the state would no longer regard it as an asset of the parents but that also meant mum and dad could not receive any benefit from it either (ie claim the rental money). Obviously, transparent discussion about the impact of these decisions is essential to make it work for all involved.

Mark Lindley, a solicitor and partner at the top London law practice Boodle Hatfield, says: “When a husband, wife or civil partner inherits from their spouse, there is no inheritance tax due. Thereafter, their children also might not pay inheritance tax because the nil band rate (tax-free threshold) is currently £325,000 per person. So, parents can potentially leave their estates with a combined value of up to £650,000 to their children without any inheritance tax being due. Obviously, if the estate exceeds that amount, then inheritance tax is payable on that additional value. If parents are considering gifting property or money before death, as in Georgia’s case, then the value of the gift does not escape inheritance tax immediately. If the parent making the gift dies within seven years, then the value of the gift will be brought back into account for tax purposes. If parents survive for seven years after the initial date of the gift, there should be no tax to pay on the gift. Helpfully, the amount of tax payable also starts to reduce after three years, with an increasing reduction every year after that.”

Inheritance can be a quagmire of complexity, and while you can buy basic legal wills from the post office or organisations like Citizens Advice, if your estate is anything more complicated than a single home, you will probably want to talk to the experts. A particular safeguard Mark feels strongly about is Lasting Power of Attorney (you appoint someone to manage your affairs if you lose capacity). “Sometimes these can be abused, of course. There was a famous case where someone was given Lasting Power of Attorney and unwisely invested a large amount of their aunt’s money in an unsuccessful reptile-breeding business. But, on the whole, these documents are a very good idea. If you put one in place and you don’t need it, fine; but if you haven’t got it and you lose your mental capacity, all kinds of problems can ensue,” he says.

The Alzheimer’s Society states that more than 40,000 people under 65 in the UK have dementia. I have my own experience of this with my late mother who was ravaged by dementia at the end of her life. As she disappeared both physically and mentally, myself and my two siblings were eternally grateful that she gave our eldest sister Lasting Power of Attorney in the months before she started to disappear from us. There were many occasions when drawing money from her bank account felt uncomfortable and somehow dishonest. I remember one shopping trip when we wanted to buy new nighties for her. We spent an exorbitant amount on a selection of luxurious and slightly ridiculous items, but as my big sis reminded me, “Mum does little other than sleep, why not allow her to feel as comfortable and as special as we can? The rest of her existence is pretty terrible.” We cried at that point.

When our mother eventually left us forever, we were grateful for an extensive will which stated her wishes and specified the finer details around sentimental goods like jewellery. She’d had the emotional integrity to leave instructions to share her gold between us, but to remember that big sis (who carried the lion’s share of our mum’s end-of-life care) would want to claim our mother’s engagement ring. There was never any question between us that this was the proper thing to do.

A will or testament can be as detailed as you want. There is no guarantee the recipients will agree with it – many families fall into feuding misery about preordained decisions – and so you might not be able to please all the beneficiaries all the time. When actress Dame Diana Rigg died in September, she left £5,000 to her nail technician who had carried out her manicures and pedicures for 20 years. The beautician, Jessica Zhu, decided to give the money back to the family, claiming she performed the treatments as an act of love.

At the other end of the scale, when people die intestate (no will), the aftermath can be disastrous. Amy Winehouse, Robin Williams and Prince are some of the many celebrities whose sudden deaths meant nothing was made legal. “In an intestacy, the law says that a surviving spouse and any children will usually inherit the estate between them,” says Mark Lindley. “This may work well with straightforward, relatively small estates, but obviously for the larger estates or more complicated families, including situations with unmarried partners, the intestacy rules are unlikely to be satisfactory. It also means there is a chance that no thought has been given to the care of younger children. Thinking about who steps into your empty parenting shoes is a life-changing decision.”

Just a few months ago, my cousin suddenly announced that she has decided to leave me something special in her will. She won’t tell me what it is and now I am intrigued. She does have an incredibly rare photograph of David Bowie, taken by a trendy photographer in the 1980s. It’s probably worth a fair bit of cash, but it is also an emotive reminder of our nights dancing together at The Wag club in Soho to Modern Love. Every time I go to her home these days, I can’t help scrutinising the photo on her wall and wondering if it will be my heirloom?

I hope it doesn’t impact our relationship... Or feed into the disappointment I might feel when the lawyer tells me that instead I’ve acquired her cast-iron doorstop. But beside the speculation of where her flotsam and jetsam might ultimately end up, I deeply love my cousin and hope there’s plenty more dancing to do before the mystery of her generosity is finally revealed.

Some of the finer detail worth knowing...

Probate is the process in England and Wales for administering someone’s estate. A Grant of Probate obtained from the Probate Registry is the document that third parties (ie banks) will accept as authority for the executor (named responsible person) of the will to deal with the deceased’s property.

A Lasting Power of Attorney gives a person the authority to look after your financial affairs and/or make decisions about your health and welfare if you lose mental capacity during your lifetime. You register these with the Government’s Office of the Public Guardian.

You can decrease the rate of inheritance tax payable on your estate if you leave a certain portion of your assets to charity. If you leave everything to charity, there is no inheritance tax.

It can take a period of time for banks to release funds after a death. This can be because of delays in valuing the estate, paying tax or due to disputes. The larger the inheritance, often the more complicated and time-consuming the process.

There is the option of setting up a trust fund. This is a legal arrangement where a trustee holds the assets of trusts for the beneficiary. Once you have established a trust, the assets no longer belong to you and you have no control over them. This is a complicated process and needs specialist advice.

A person has an annual gift allowance of up to £3,000 each year, tax-free. This amount is called the annual exemption. You can also make use of the small gift exemption, which means you can gift up to £250 a year to as many people as you like.

Most jurisdictions have different rules when it comes to succession. For example, in Scotland, you are required by law to leave part of your estate to your spouse and children.

Wills can be contested. Someone could claim it is not valid, or certain people (including your spouse or children) can bring a claim to court with the Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975.
