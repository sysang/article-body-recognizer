Electric car sales double to claim one in five purchases

Almost a third of new cars sold were either pure electric or hybrid

By Howard Mustoe 6 December 2021 • 11:15am

Electric car sales doubled in November to account for one in every five purchases as the switch away from petrol and diesel vehicles gathers pace. 

Almost a third of new cars sold were either pure electric or a petrol/electric hybrid, according to figures for the Society of Motor Manufacturers and Traders.

The trade body said 115,706 new cars were registered last month, up 1.7pc from last year, but still 31pc lower than the pre-pandemic average.

Meanwhile the inexorable rise of home delivery for online shopping orders and the recovery in construction has helped fuel a surge in the van market, with record sales for new light commercial vehicles. 

Almost a third of the 31,320 vans sold were Ford Transits and the smaller Transit Custom.

The swell of electric car sales comes as the industry is still recovering from the production line shutdowns of the early days of the pandemic as well as an ongoing shortage of computer chips.

While customers forge ahead with buying electric vehicles, which for some drivers are now cheaper than petrol cars despite their higher initial expense, the charging infrastructure is still lacking.

There were 16 electric or hybrid vehicles for every public charging point in 2020, up from 11 the previous year. While many owners have driveway and can charge them at home, those in terraced homes or living in cities must rely on public chargers or lamp post conversions.

While charging is getting faster and battery capacity improves each year, easy charging on the hoof is thought to be needed to win over more buyers who want to combine short commutes with the occasional long journey.

Mike Hawes, SMMT chief executive, said rising sales of electric cars masked the underlying weakness of the overall market: “Demand is there, with a slew of new, increasingly electrified, models launched but the global shortage of semiconductors continues to bedevil production and therefore new car registrations.”

Disruption is likely to continue into next year, he said, which is causing some curious distortions in the second-hand car market.

Auto Trader estimates that the constrained supply of new cars has led to 1.5m “lost” sales. That has forced some potential buyers to keep their cars for longer, or to opt for a second-hand model, pushing up prices.

The Seat Alhambra people carrier has jumped by almost 50pc to £18,900 second-hand compared with this time last year, while a used Renault Grand Scenic is up 44pc to £9,425, according to Auto Trader.

One in four used cars less than a year old are now more expensive than their new equivalents, Auto Trader says, and this is especially evident in electric cars.

The Smart fourfour retails for £16,700 but a nearly-new model will set buyers back £22,490, while a Mercedes-Benz EQA sports utility vehicle can sell for almost £10,000 above its £45,200 list price.

The increases helped push the average cost of a used car to £17,366 last month, 29pc higher than November last year. Prices have jumped by almost £3,500 in the past six months alone.

Factory shutdowns halted computer chip production in the early days of the pandemic. While chipmakers are gearing up production, they are focusing on higher-margin chips for phones and computers rather than the cheaper silicon examples used in cars. 

This has cut supply and slowed vehicle manufacture, with only high-end car makers swallowing the extra cost.
