What Is the Cheapest Car Insurance Company?

USAA is tough to beat for cheap car insurance, but you need a connection to the military to qualify for coverage. For that reason, we kept USAA’s rates for the sake of comparison, but omitted the company from the bulk of our analysis of the cheapest car insurance providers. USAA earns top ratings from its customers, and it has the lowest annual rates for drivers with a clean driving record or poor credit history. USAA also has the lowest car insurance premiums if you have a speeding ticket or an accident on your driving record.

Geico is the cheapest car insurance company we found for good drivers that have a clean driving record, but lack a military affiliation. If your credit rating needs work, Geico also offers cheaper car insurance than most of the providers in our study.

State Farm is an excellent choice if you need affordable car insurance, but have a less-than-perfect driving record. Our study found that State Farm has the lowest average cost of car insurance after a DUI. Its rates are also among the most affordable if you have a speeding ticket or an accident on your record.

How to Get the Cheapest Car Insurance Quotes

We’ve done the heavy lifting to find out which major car insurance companies are likely to have the best rates for you, but you won’t know exactly how much you’ll pay until you start getting quotes.

Insurance companies base rates, in part, on data that shows older drivers and married drivers make fewer claims. You shouldn’t get married just to save on insurance and you can’t change your age. However, you can consider these steps to save on car insurance and get the cheapest rate possible:

Shop Around

Comparing quotes from multiple companies is the first step toward getting cheap car insurance. Be sure to ask each insurer about how to adjust your level of coverage to fit your current needs.

Ask for Discounts

Nearly every insurance provider has discounts available for its customers. These can include lower rates for customer loyalty, maintaining a clean driving record and good students.

Increase Your Deductible

The rates that you pay are directly related to your deductible, which is the amount that you agree to pay if you need to file a claim. Opting for a higher deductible is one way that you can lower your insurance premiums.

Look for Local Insurers

It’s possible that a large insurer might not be the best or cheapest match for your coverage needs. Check with smaller insurers or with a local agent to see if you can find a lower rate.

Improve Your Driving Record

Insurance companies reward safe drivers with clean records by giving them lower rates. This is because good drivers are less likely to file a claim.

Switch to Pay-Per-Mile Insurance

This type of coverage replaces your traditional car insurance policy, and the main difference is that you pay for auto insurance by the mile. If you put only a few miles on your car every year, the savings can be worth making the switch.

Review Coverage Annually

Take a look at your policy when it’s time to renew. Adjust your coverage, such as your deductible or the number of miles that you drive, to fit your current needs.

Pay Premiums Annually

Most insurance companies will allow you to pay for coverage monthly, biannually or on a yearly basis. However, paying your premiums annually will almost always save you money.

Improve Credit Rating

In most states, drivers with poor credit scores are penalized with higher insurance premiums. Maintain a good credit score, or take steps to improve your credit, and you’ll likely secure a better rate.

Bundle Your Insurance

Most companies offer car insurance discounts if you buy two or more policies. This could include adding a renter’s or homeowner’s policy to your coverage, as well as insuring multiple vehicles together.

Methodology - How We Collected Our Car Insurance Rates

To get comparative insurance rates for this study, U.S. News worked with Quadrant Information Services to analyze a report of insurance rates in all 50 states from most of the largest national car insurance companies, though not every company operates in every state. Quadrant obtained publicly available rate data that car insurers file with state regulators. Our study rates are based on profiles for both male and female drivers aged 17, 25, 35, and 60. Vehicles used include the 2015 Honda Civic, 2015 Toyota RAV4, and 2015 Ford F-150, with annual mileage ranging from 6,000 and 12,000. Three car insurance coverage levels were used, as were credit tiers of good, fair, and poor. Clean driving records, as well as records with one accident, one speeding violation, or one DUI were also used in the calculations of certain driver archetypes.

To get the study rates shown here, we computed the mean rate for male and female drivers ages 17, 25, 35, and 60 who drive 12,000 miles per year, have medium coverage, good credit, and a clean driving record. The rates shown here are for comparative purposes only and should not be considered “average” rates available by individual insurers. Because car insurance rates are based on individual factors, your car insurance rates will differ from the rates shown here.
