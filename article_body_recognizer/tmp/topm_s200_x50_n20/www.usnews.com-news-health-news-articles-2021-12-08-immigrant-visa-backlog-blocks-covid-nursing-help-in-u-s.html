Visa Backlog Blocks Nursing Help in U.S., but Progress Seen

As the country grapples with COVID-19 and a need for more nurses, many foreign health care workers have been waiting for permission to enter the U.S.

Observers say they’ve started to see progress on efforts to streamline the immigration visa process to allow more internationally trained health professionals to work in the U.S. and help address widespread nursing shortages amid the COVID-19 pandemic. But they also say more needs to be done.

The pandemic has slowed the pace at which U.S. officials have conducted interviews with visa seekers, which is one of the final steps needed to gain permanent residency. As of November, only 28,964 out of more than 490,000 qualified immigrant visa applicants were scheduled for interview, according to figures from the Department of State’s Bureau of Consular Affairs.

An estimated 10,000 foreign-trained nurses have been on the waiting list for an interview, with the average wait time anywhere from 12 to 18 months, according to Sinead Carbery, brand president of O’Grady Peyton International, the international division of leading health care staffing recruitment firm AMN Healthcare.

Carbery and others contend a contributing factor to the delays in processing immigration visas for nurses has been a four-tier system the U.S. Department of State established late last year to prioritize cases. While the system was intended to work through the backlog by granting higher priority to petitions filed by immediate family members, for example, it placed employment-based visa petitions, which includes visas sought by health care professionals, among the last in line to be processed.

“These health care workers already have an employer that needs them who is ready to hire them as soon as they enter into the U.S.,” says Sherry Neal, an immigration attorney with the law firm Hammond Neal Moore in Cincinnati. Neal says nurses waiting for visa interviews have already been matched with an employer, and have met both nurse licensing and English-language proficiency requirements.

“That’s why employers are screaming because they’re saying, ‘We have these people who are willing to come and work, we have already identified them and we have already interviewed them – these are the people we have selected,’” Neal says. “Yet the only thing that’s preventing them from coming in is this hurdle of the consulate interviews.”

Health care trade groups have called on the State Department to expedite the visa application process for foreign-trained nurses in light of the ongoing public health crisis caused by the pandemic. In June, the American Hospital Association sent a letter to Secretary of State Antony Blinken requesting higher priority for the processing of visa applications by international nurses.

“There has never been a more urgent need for the care that foreign-trained nurses provide than during the current COVID-19 pandemic and its looming aftermath,” AHA President and CEO Richard J. Pollack wrote. “These professionals play critical roles in ensuring the health of the communities we serve.”

In August, a coalition of about a dozen long-term care organizations led by the American Health Care Association and the National Center for Assisted Living sent a letter to Blinken asking that the immigrant visas of international nurses and health care workers be given higher prioritization. Also in August, the State Department announced it would allow consulates to choose whether to expedite visa cases involving certain health care workers who would be working directly in the COVID-19 relief effort.

“The problem with that is that when they did that, it basically was a permissive situation,” Neal says. “So everybody started asking for expedites, but the consulates were saying no.”

The coalition sent a second letter to Blinken on Nov. 10, recommending that the department allow for virtual interviews and waive the immigrant visa interview process for nurses altogether.

“We need to prioritize health care workers who wish to live and work in the United States, especially those who are willing to care for our nation’s most vulnerable,” the American Health Care Association/National Center for Assisted Living tells U.S. News in an email. “Expediting the visa authorization process for these heroes would help us build back our workforce and expand the number of seniors we can serve.”

On Nov. 19, the State Department announced it was effectively rescinding the tier-based prioritization system, stating embassies and consulates have “broad discretion” to determine how to prioritize visa appointments. And Neal, the immigration attorney, says she has started to see some progress in the processing of visas for nurses in recent weeks.

Carbery, at O’Grady Peyton, says she also has become more optimistic. She says several U.S. embassies located in the countries of origin for a majority of international nurses who come to the U.S., such as the Philippines, have begun to put nurses higher up on the prioritization list for interviews.

Both Carbery and Neal contend, however, more can and should be done to expedite the visa process.

“It’s a good sign that they have started to move,” Carbery says. “They need to continue the momentum because the shortage is almost feeling like a crisis at this point.”

A State Department official tells U.S. News that the department remains focused on “reducing wait times for all consular services at our embassies and consulates overseas while also protecting the health and safety of our staff and applicants.”

Some 500,000 immigrant nurses are believed to have worked in the U.S. in 2018, making up 15.5% of the country’s nursing workforce, according to data tabulated by the Migration Policy Institute. Approximately 86% of international nurses providing care in the United States have treated COVID-19 patients, based on a recent survey of nearly 600 nurses conducted by O’Grady Peyton International. The survey also found 17% of respondents reported having contracted COVID-19.

Carbery warns solving the country’s nursing woes will take more than bringing in more international nurses. What the pandemic has helped make clear, she says, is that such an approach is unsustainable to meet both current demand and the need for health care professionals moving forward.

“The root of the problem is we’re not producing enough U.S. domestic supply,” Carbery says. “As long as we’re not paying nursing educators enough money to be able to produce U.S. domestic supply, we’re going to continue to be in this crisis.”

The health care sector has lost more than 400,000 workers since February 2020, according to an employment report released last week by the Bureau of Labor Statistics. Despite an increase in applications to nursing programs, many prospective students are still turned away due to faculty and resource constraints. The pace at which nurses have either quit or left the field through retirement has contributed to what the Bureau of Labor Statistics projects will be a demand for more than 194,000 registered nurses each year over the next decade.

Meanwhile, the pandemic has fueled an exodus of nursing professionals. An estimated 30% of nurses have either quit or been laid off from their jobs since February 2020, according to a poll in September by Morning Consult of 1,000 workers, with 54% reporting COVID-19 as the primary reason for their departure.

Such issues have prompted calls and efforts to increase the capacity of nursing schools so they can train more students.

Bob Riney, president of health care operations and chief operating officer for Detroit-based Henry Ford Health System, says part of his organization’s recruiting strategy includes recruiting international nurses. He says the health system has begun to file work visa applications for nurses from the Philippines, with the hope of adding the first group of approximately 100 nurses to their staff within a year.

Riney says Henry Ford is also working to increase the number of students that can be trained and potentially enter the workforce. In January, Henry Ford launched a 30-year partnership with Michigan State University that’s to include efforts aimed at removing barriers to medical education for members of marginalized communities, in the hope of improving diversity among health care providers.

“We have an aggressive short- and long-term recruitment strategy in place to navigate the ongoing national nursing shortage,” Riney says.
