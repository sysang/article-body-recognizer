Instagram CEO Faces Questions in U.S. Senate Hearing on Child Safety and Cites New Protection Measures

Published: Dec. 8, 2021 at 4:23 p.m. ET
By

Connor Smith
comments

Referenced Symbols

    FB
    +2.40%

U.S. senators on Wednesday grilled Adam Mosseri, head of Meta Platforms ‘ Instagram unit, during a hearing focused on protecting children online. Lawmakers applied renewed pressure on the rebranded corporation, raising concerns about how children and teens interact with Instagram’s services.

The hearing followed testimony earlier this fall by whistleblower Frances Haugen, who provided internal documents to lawmakers, regulators and journalists. The whistleblower highlighted internal research that seemed to show the Instagram app amplified body image concerns among teenage users.

Ahead of the hearing, the company (ticker: FB) on Tuesday announced new teen safety features and parental controls. Amid negative news coverage, the company said in September it was pausing its planned “Instagram for Kids” feature that would eventually open a limited window into the app for children.

“This pause looks like more like a public relations tactic brought on by our hearings, just as these announced changes seem to be brought on by these proceedings,” Sen. Richard Blumenthal (D.-Conn.), the chair of the Subcommittee on Consumer Protection, Product Safety, and Data Security, said as he kicked off the hearing.

Blumenthal pointed to concerns expressed by employees, including Haugen, that the company prioritized advertising dollars. He cited “horrifying stories from countless parents whose lives and their children’s lives have been changed forever” such as a teenage girl who attempted suicide following severe anxiety following negative experiences on Instagram.

“Much has been said recently about Instagram and its impact on young people,” Mosseri said in his prepared testimony. “As a parent and as the head of Instagram, this is an issue I care deeply about. It’s an area our company has been focused on for many years, and I’m proud of our work to help keep young people safe, to support young people who are struggling, and to empower parents with tools to help their teenagers develop healthy and safe online habits.”

Mosseri pointed to efforts the company is making to verify users’ ages so that only people age 13 or older can use the social media platform. He also said the company is putting in place parental controls and protections to ensure content displayed for teenagers are age-appropriate and limit who can message teens on the app.

“When it comes to advertising, we’ve long restricted certain kinds of ads from being served to minors, and we recently limited advertisers’ options for serving ads to people under 18,” Mosseri added. “Now, advertisers can only serve ads to people under 18 based on age, gender, and location but not interests or activity. This means that previously available targeting options, like those based on interests or on their activity on other apps and websites, are no longer available to advertisers.”

Sen. Marsha Blackburn (R.-Tenn.) said the American public is frustrated, believing the company has turned a blind eye toward taking responsibility, and accepting accountability for its platform, how it’s structured, and how the company uses data on children. She also suggested the company’s proposed changes, which were released at 3 a.m. ET on Tuesday, could have been timed keep members of the subcommittee from seeing it right away.

“Where I’m from, the middle of the night is when you drop news when you don’t want people to see,” Blackburn said.

Mosseri called for an industry body that could determine best practices on how to verify a user’s age, how to design age-appropriate experiences, and how to build parental controls. He also committed to provide access to data so that third party researchers can design studies on the impact of young people’s well-being.

Blumenthal pushed back, suggesting a need for an independent body rather than one created by industry players. Mosseri later clarified lawmakers could have input in such a body and enforce standards.

Mosseri argued Instragram’s products aren’t addictive. Blumenthal retorted that teens find it difficult to stop using the app, while content is driven to them to keep them on the site.

Asked if he would halt plans for an Instagram for Kids, Mosseri declined to do so, but said no child between ages 10 and 12 would have access to such a service without explicit parental consent. He said it was his decision to pause Instagram for Kids.

Sen. Amy Klobuchar (D.-Minn.) asked if Instagram for Kids is viewed as a feeder to get kids using Meta’s products, rather than losing them to other platforms.

“We try and make Instagram as relevant as possible for people of all ages, including teens,” Mosseri said. “Teens do amazing things on Instagram every day. But we also invest, I believe more than anyone else, in keeping people, including teens, safe. We will spend around $5 billion this year alone, and we have over 40,000 people working on safety integrity at the company.

Klobuchar also asked Mosseri if he thought Meta has invested enough in identifying underage users. He responded that the company has invested more than other companies, adding that it’s a challenging issue for the industry. He said the company plans to invest heavily in improving such efforts.
