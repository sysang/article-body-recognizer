The highly anticipated video conference began with a friendly wave from the American president in the Situation Room to the Russian leader, ensconced in his Black Sea mansion.

According to the readout issued by the White House, President Joe Biden called on Russian President Vladimir Putin for “de-escalation and a return to diplomacy,” and they both agreed their respective administrations will follow up.(MANDEL NGAN,MIKHAIL METZEL/SPUTNIK/AFP/Getty Images)

President Joe Biden and Russian President Vladimir Putin spoke for slightly more than two hours Tuesday morning at a time of a credible likelihood of imminent war in Ukraine, though it was not immediately clear whether the two leaders made any significant breakthroughs.

Russian media drew immediate attention to footage of the first few moments of the highly anticipated video call, which the Kremlin released publicly, showing Biden waving amiably to Putin as the Russian leader sat alone behind a desk at his residence in Sochi. Biden, also appearing to sit alone at a table in the White House Situation Room, stated at the onset he would have preferred to have gathered in person and said he was sorry not to have met Putin at the G20 summit, which took place in Rome in October.

The remainder of the call took place behind closed doors. The White House later released a picture from inside the Situation Room, showing members of Biden’s staff including Secretary of State Antony Blinken sitting in on the call.

The White House issued a readout of the call roughly an hour after it concluded, confirming many of the talking points it previously said Biden would raise.

“President Biden voiced the deep concerns of the United States and our European Allies about Russia’s escalation of forces surrounding Ukraine and made clear that the U.S. and our Allies would respond with strong economic and other measures in the event of military escalation,” it read.

Biden called on Putin for “de-escalation and a return to diplomacy,” and they both agreed their respective administrations will follow up. The readout added, “the U.S. will do so in close coordination with allies and partners.”

Russian state news reported that Biden also threatened Putin with sanctions if he does not deescalate the situation involving Ukraine.

The Kremlin had set low expectations going into the exchange – the fifth interaction between the two leaders since Biden took office – with Putin spokesman Dmitry Peskov telling reporters they should not anticipate “any breakthroughs from this conversation.” He described it as a “working conversation” carried out during “a difficult period” of high tensions in Europe.The White House, however, characterized Biden’s plan going into the call as one of straight-forward focus on the many problems facing the U.S.-Russia relationship – “without any kind of flourish or finger-wagging” as a U.S. official told reporters in advance of the call.

At the center of the new tension lies Putin’s apparent outrage that Ukraine, the former Soviet republic with deep political and cultural connections to Russia, has indicated its intention to join NATO, the Cold War-era alliance designed to offset the Soviet Union. He has subsequently deployed tens of thousands of troops to Ukraine’s east – adjacent to the ongoing conflict zone there – as well as to the Crimean Peninsula, which Russia controls, as well as to Belarus, to Ukraine’s north.

Though the U.S. has stated it’s willing to defend Ukraine’s sovereignty and threatened employing devastating sanctions were Russia to invade, reports emerged Monday during the call that Washington’s position is relatively limited. State Department policy chief Victoria Nuland reportedly briefed members of Congress late Monday on American options to deter Russia, which some lawmakers interpreted as “gloomy,” CNN reports.

National security analysts also observe that an ultimatum on the issue would come down to whether the U.S. or Russia were willing to go to war over Ukraine. Putin has indicated he is, despite the blowback he would likely experience domestically for doing so.

Putin expressed before the call that he seeks legal assurances from the U.S. that Ukraine would not join NATO – a pledge the Biden administration indicated it was unlikely to yield.

“We don’t think talk of red lines is helpful,” the official said. “And, as the president has said, we’re not going to operate according to that logic of accepting anyone’s red lines.”

Biden planned to call his counterparts in France, Germany, Italy and the U.K. once the virtual meeting with Putin concluded, Agence France-Presse reported, citing officials at the White House. In addition to briefing them on the contents of the call, the move also matches the Biden administration’s signature priority of working with allies to achieve America’s goals abroad – a break from the unilateral approach embraced by former President Donald Trump.

Those with experience in the Washington-Moscow relationship who have followed its fallout said that regardless of the contents of Tuesday’s call, little will likely change until Putin decides it will.

“No matter what the outcome of the Biden-Putin virtual summit, please remember that Putin invented this ‘crisis’ single-handedly,” Michael McFaul, a former U.S. ambassador to Moscow during the Obama administration, wrote on Twitter. “Nothing changed in Ukraine. Nothing changed regarding NATO policy.”
