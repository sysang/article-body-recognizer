Paying for Nursing Homes

What does a nursing home cost?

There are two primary types of nursing home care:

  • Short term rehabilitation following an accident, stroke or other event requiring physical rehabilitation.
  • Long term care for more chronic illnesses.

The cost of a nursing home is tied to the type of care you need.

Medicare often covers most of a short term stay, so most people are more focussed on long term care costs. Costs vary greatly by region but the national average daily cost for a shared room was $255 in 2020.

Does Medicare pay for nursing homes?

The Medicare program provides coverage for rehabilitation – but not long-term care. Medicare will pick up the tab for up to 100 days of care in a skilled nursing facility each benefit period. A benefit period is how Medicare measures the length of stay in a hospital or skilled nursing facility.

How can you pay for a nursing home?

Depending on your circumstances, payment for long term nursing home care typically starts as “private pay” - meaning that payment comes from personal savings, investments, assets or private long-term care insurance. Public assistance in the form of Medicaid may be available if a senior has low income and few financial assets. State Medicaid programs are required to cover nursing home care but eligibility is complex.

Depending on the state and the eligibility criteria, seniors may still be required to pay some of the costs. Seniors who own their home also need to consider that while under federal law a home is exempt from certain asset calculations, if the homeowner dies while receiving Medicaid nursing home benefits, the government can take the house as payment, unless some advanced planning is put in place. For a valued home, the family may choose to transfer the home ownership to another family member or pay out of pocket instead.
