What to Know About Omicron

Experts are rushing to learn more about omicron as the globe anxiously awaits answers surrounding the latest COVID-19 variant of concern – one that poses a “very high” global risk, according to an assessment from the World Health Organization.

“The emergence of the omicron variant has understandably captured global attention,” WHO Director General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus said this week. “The emergence of any new variant should capture our attention, especially one as highly mutated as omicron.”

Noting that omicron has over 30 mutations, WHO last week named it a variant of concern. Such a status means that the strain likely has certain characteristics that pose a threat to global public health.

While public health officials are urging caution and calm in response to the variant, the globe remains on edge over the COVID-19 pandemic that has cost over 5.2 million lives.

Much remains unknown about the variant, and experts expect to have a clearer picture of the strain in the near future. Still, if someone is concerned about the variant, they say there are steps a person can take.

“The important thing that an individual should do is be vaccinated and boosted and wear a mask when you're indoors in public,” says Nancy Nielsen of the University of Buffalo. “It's that simple.”

Why Are Experts Concerned About Omicron?

With so many changes from the original COVID-19 strain, the variant poses many uncertainties to experts.

The biggest uncertainties surrounding the variant are whether it is more contagious than the delta strain, causes more severe disease and whether current vaccines are effective against it.

WHO says that preliminary evidence suggests the variant has higher transmissibility – meaning it passes more easily from person to person – and can more readily reinfect people who have recovered from COVID-19.

These characteristics could “lead to further surges with severe consequences,” WHO said in an assessment, qualifying that “considerable uncertainty” still surrounds the data.

Some reports out of South Africa say physicians are seeing mild omicron cases. However, the observation came from cases in mostly young people, who tend to fare better with COVID-19 than older populations.

Leading infectious disease expert Anthony Fauci cautioned against leaning into the reports too much, saying “it is too soon to tell what the level of severity is.”

“With the small number of cases, it is very difficult to know whether or not this particular variant is going to result in severe disease,” Fauci said this week. “Although some preliminary information from South Africa suggests no unusual symptoms associated with the variant, we do not know and it is too early to tell.”

It will take two to four weeks to have a better understanding of the variant, Fauci said. Anything being reported right now “remains speculative,” he said.

Will Vaccines Work Against Omicron?

Fauci says there’s reason to believe the vaccine will have at least some degree of protection against the variant, “particularly against severe disease.”

“We are fairly sure that the vaccines that we've been given do provide protection against severe disease,” says Nielsen, who serves as the senior associate death for health policy at the University of Buffalo’s Jacobs School of Medicine and Biomedical Sciences. “So if there's one message, it’s get vaccinated. And if you're vaccinated, get boosted because that is the best way to prepare.”

But in case the effectiveness of vaccines against omicron significantly wanes, the U.S. is working on a contingency plan with vaccine developers that would involve a vaccine that specifically targets the variant and receives expedited consideration from the Food and Drug Administration and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.

“We believe the current vaccines provide at least some protection against this variant and that boosters strengthen that protection significantly,” White House COVID-19 response coordinator Jeff Zients said. “In the event that additional measures are needed, we will be prepared. We're working with Pfizer, Moderna and [Johnson Johnson] to develop contingency plans for modifications to vaccines or boosters, if they're needed. And we will ensure that the FDA and CDC review them as fast as possible while maintaining their rigorous scientific protocols.”

Zients said these shots would take a “few months” to develop, test, authorize and manufacture.

But the best case scenario is that such a shot wouldn’t be needed.

“What we really are hoping is that it isn't necessary,” Nielsen says. “That the vaccines that people have already been given give them enough protection that even if they get infected, they're not going to get severely ill.”

Where Has Omicron Been Found?

The variant has been documented in over 20 countries, and the list is only expected to grow. The first cases were identified in South Africa and Botswana in November.

U.S. health officials announced the country’s first case of the variant on Wednesday. The individual in California recently returned from travel to South Africa. The person was fully vaccinated against COVID-19 and was experiencing mild symptoms, according to Fauci.

CDC is sequencing 80,000 samples every week to see which variants are circulating. Still, the overwhelming majority of cases in the U.S. are currently from the delta variant.

For many, the variant illustrates the global inequities the pandemic has underscored.

When asked if the U.S. is doing enough to combat vaccine inequity across the world, Fauci responded that the country is donating more shots than any other nation. He added that “many of the doses that have been shipped have not been used.”

“Other African countries on the African continent have actually told us not to ship any more vaccine because they have not been able to adequately utilize it,” he said.

But the continuation of the pandemic allows variants like omicron to keep developing, WHO’s Tedros said.

“And as I have said many times, the longer we allow the pandemic to drag on – by failing to address vaccine inequity or to implement public health and social measures in a tailored and consistent way – the more opportunity we give this virus to mutate in ways we cannot predict or prevent,” Tedros said on Tuesday.

How Might International Travel Be Affected? 

WHO on Tuesday updated its travel recommendations to say that people at risk of severe COVID-19 who are not vaccinated or recovered from previous infection should not travel to areas where the virus is spreading.

“Persons who have not been fully vaccinated or do not have proof of previous SARS-CoV-2 infection and are at increased risk of developing severe disease and dying, including people 60 years of age or older or those with comorbidities that present increased risk of severe COVID-19 (e.g. heart disease, cancer and diabetes) should be advised to postpone travel to areas with community transmission,” the organization said in its advice.

Countries, like Israel, Japan and Morocco, banned foreign travelers altogether.

The U.S. has not gone that far, instead working to update its rules for international travelers to require a negative test result taken within 24 hours of their departure. But it did promptly restrict travel from South Africa and several other countries in the region after the variant was reported – a move that some praised but others questioned.

Tedros said that it is “deeply concerning” that the countries that rapidly detected and reported the variant, like South Africa and Botswana, “are now being penalized by others for doing the right thing.”

“I well understand the concern of all countries to protect their citizens against a variant that we don’t yet fully understand,” Tedros said. “But I am equally concerned that several member states are introducing blunt, blanket measures that are not evidence-based or effective on their own, and which will only worsen inequities.”
