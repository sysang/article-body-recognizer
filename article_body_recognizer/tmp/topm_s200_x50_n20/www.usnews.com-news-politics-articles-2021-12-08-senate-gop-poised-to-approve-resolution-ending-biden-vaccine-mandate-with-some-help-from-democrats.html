Senate GOP Poised to Approve Resolution Ending Biden Vaccine Mandate – With Some Help From Democrats

Two Democrats are expected to join all GOP senators in a largely symbolic vote to overturn the vaccine and testing mandate for private companies with more than 100 employees.

By Lisa Hagen
|

Senate to Vote on Ending Vaccine Mandate

Sen. Joe Manchin (D-WV) speaks to reporters as he walks to the Capitol Building on December 07, 2021 in Washington, DC.(Anna Moneymaker/Getty Images)

The Senate is poised to adopt a resolution on Wednesday night to overturn President Joe Biden’s vaccine mandate in what would be a victory for Republicans on a major point of contention with the administration. While it faces little chance of becoming law, the symbolic win also exposes some Democrats’ frustration with their party when it comes to imposing rules on private business.

All 50 Republicans and at least two Democrats are expected to back a disapproval resolution aimed at the federal government’s requirement for private-sector workers at large companies to receive a vaccine or get frequently tested. Democratic Sen. Joe Manchin of West Virginia and Sen. Jon Tester of Montana have said they’ll support the measure, which needs 51 votes to pass.

While Senate Republicans don’t hold a majority, they can force votes on resolutions seeking to nix federal regulations under the Congressional Review Act. Such measures only need a simple majority in both chambers to pass Congress but can be vetoed by a president.

But the effort faces an uphill battle once it makes its way to the House. Republicans are working to get enough support from Democrats in order to force a vote in the lower chamber. If it ultimately comes up for a vote, Democrats can only afford a few defections since they hold a slim majority. Regardless, Biden would be able to veto the resolution and a two-thirds majority in both chambers would be needed to override it – a tough feat with Democrats fully in control.

GOP resistance to vaccine and mask mandates has become a major part of their agenda. The resolution from Sen. Mike Braun of Indiana, a former businessman, is their best chance at escalating the issue. Many in the party are straddling the line of encouraging Americans to get fully vaccinated – and receive booster shots – while opposing the government forcing them to do so, including Senate GOP Leader Mitch McConnell of Kentucky.

Republicans tried to make waves last week by tying mandate opposition to government funding. Conservatives threatened to gum up the Senate process on a must-pass bill if they didn’t get a vote on an amendment to defund those work requirements. Democrats ultimately gave into the demand, knowing it wouldn’t pass, and Congress averted a shutdown last Thursday.

The GOP opposes mandates especially when it comes to private businesses and linking them to employment. In addition to requirements for federal workers, the Department of Labor’s Occupational Safety and Health Administration has issued an emergency rule for companies with more than 100 employees. Employers can choose to implement a vaccine-only mandate or a policy that allows workers to receive a shot or opt out and undergo testing on a weekly basis plus mask wearing.

“It’s got Main Street America scared,” Braun told reporters Wednesday. “That is the heavy hand of government. That is overreach. ... That’s why for me it was easy to do. We quickly got all Republicans on board and now it’s bipartisan.”

Republicans are touting the fact that Manchin and Tester, two moderates who have at times expressed skepticism of their own party’s legislation and proposals, are likely to join them. The Democratic senators took specific issue with a requirement for private companies but not for mandatory policies for the public sector and the military.

Biden, meanwhile, stands behind his requirements – especially as the omicron variant quickly spreads through the U.S. and globally. His administration argued that the rule isn’t burdensome for many Americans since a majority of adults are already fully vaccinated and small businesses are exempt from the pandemic protocol.

“At a time when COVID is on the rise, a new variant is on the loose, and more Americans are choosing to be vaccinated, it makes no sense for Congress to reverse this much-needed protection of our workforce,” the White House said in a statement opposing the GOP resolution. “It puts our recovery in danger, and a vote for this resolution risks a return to shutdowns, layoffs, and closures that result from allowing COVID to spread more easily in the workplace.”

The mandates for private companies – as well as ones for federal contractors and health care workers who participate in Medicare and Medicaid – are currently tied up in court and temporarily halted. They are scheduled to go into effect on Jan. 4.
