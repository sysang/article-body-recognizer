'We Show Up for Each Other': After Reform Defeat, Minneapolis Residents Seek Their Own Change

A ballot question to scrap the city's police department failed, but volunteers near the site of George Floyd’s murder are taking their own public health-related approach to public safety.

Minneapolis Residents Seek Their Own Change

More

A composite image shows Remi Douah, a volunteer counselor, juxtaposed with George Floyd Square in Minneapolis on Nov. 30. As the divided city grapples with the future of its troubled police department, efforts to bring about change have already been happening in the neighborhood where Floyd was killed.(Caroline Yang for USNWR)

MINNEAPOLIS – In late October, driving near her home by the spot where George Floyd was murdered, Jeanelle Austin heard a crash behind her. Glancing in her side mirror, she saw the accident: Two cars collided at an intersection – one vehicle, she says, was still spinning.

Last year, Austin was among thousands of demonstrators in the streets demanding justice for Floyd, and she was a big supporter of a ballot question to abolish the Minneapolis Police Department, an initiative that went down in flames last month. At the scene of the accident, Austin pulled over, and she didn’t wait for a patrol car to show up.

“I got out of my car and I ran” to the crash scene at 38th Street and Park Avenue, says Austin, 37, a south Minneapolis native who lives at 38th and Oakland, just blocks from the scene where Floyd was killed. “Then another neighbor came out of their house” and one thing – checking for injuries, talking to witnesses – led to another, even directing traffic away from the crash. Everyone out on the street pitched in: three adults and two kids.

Jeanelle Austin, 37, an activist and caretaker of George Floyd Square, poses for a portrait outside the Hennepin County Courthouse in Minneapolis.(Caroline Yang for USNWR)

When police arrived later, “the officer said, “‘You guys have done my job,’” Austin recalls. But while some might view her actions simply as good citizenry, Austin tells the anecdote to make a sharper, more salient point: Sometimes, her neighborhood would be just fine without cops.

In November, Minneapolis voters soundly defeated Question 2 – a proposal that sought to effectively replace the MPD with a Department of Public Safety focused on lowering crime through “a comprehensive public health approach.” Seen as a proxy for the nation’s mood on reform after last year’s protests against racial injustice, police cheered the outcome, while disappointed pro-reformers insisted it was a win to simply get the question on the ballot.

Yet as a divided city grapples with the future of its troubled police department – a force with a long history of racial bias accusations and use-of-force complaints – efforts to bring about the change Austin and 44% of voters sought through the ballot box have already been happening in the neighborhood where Floyd was killed, face to the pavement, a police officer’s knee on his neck.

A small group of people visit the area where George Floyd was murdered, outside Cup Foods in Minneapolis.(Caroline Yang for USNWR)

Amid the hundreds of flowers, stuffed animals and other tributes to Floyd arrayed at the intersection of 38th and Chicago in south Minneapolis, Austin and an ad hoc group of local activists and neighborhood volunteers have been addressing the health and social needs of poorer residents.

Across the street from Cup Foods – the corner market where an employee, phoning police, set into motion Floyd’s murder by former MPD officer Derek Chauvin and the racial reckoning that sparked a global uprising – a bus shelter has been transformed into a closet with free clothing for anyone who needs it.

Remi Douah, a volunteer counselor, the father of a young protester and a neighborhood fixture, has been holding court outdoors around the corner, offering an ear to stressed-out activists, residents and gang members alike. Douah, who lives just outside the city, also helped arrange free yoga classes in an empty space at a nearby building, and plans are in the works for a safe, quiet space for neighborhood schoolchildren to do homework while their moms wash clothes at a nearby laundromat.

An abandoned Speedway gas station – destroyed during the protests over Floyd’s murder – has benches arrayed around an iron fire pit near the deactivated pumps, a de facto community meeting space. As she talks, Austin and another volunteer use a nail gun to attach sheet plastic over the frame of a small greenhouse erected near Cup Foods – winter shelter for the plants left in tribute at what’s now known as George Floyd Square.

Much of this DIY approach predates the vote on Question 2. Austin says it’s “the work of neighbors being neighbors – this is the work of being community,” the only constant in a fractured city.

The police “are not going to do their job correctly, but there's still work to be done,” she says. “So we show up for each other. And we're present for each other. And that doesn't stop, because we're not waiting on government entities” to fix the community’s problems.

A local bus shelter is seen transformed into a closet of free clothing in George Floyd Square in Minneapolis.(Caroline Yang for USNWR)

Samuel L. Myers, Jr., an economist and head of the University of Minnesota’s Roy Wilkins Center for Human Relations and Social Justice, isn’t surprised that the neighborhood where Floyd was killed is plotting its own course despite the defeat of Question 2, a political campaign that splintered the Black community and polarized a reputedly progressive city.

Although Minneapolis’ top cop, MPD Chief Medaria Arradondo, is an African American and crime has been on the rise, “the vast majority of whites in Minnesota trust the police, while Blacks and other people of color are divided,” Myers says. Black activists were the driving force behind Question 2, yet there were influential Black ministers who wanted a “no” vote: While their parishioners have issues with the MPD, they argued, it’s a bad idea to cut back on armed officers in places where gunfire is all too common.

Though the public safety department proposed by Question 2 could have focused on health and economic equity rather than get-tough crackdowns on crime, Myers says “many Blacks understandably feared that the referendum did not solve the problems” of gun violence, criminal gangs, police brutality and racial discrimination.

The Rev. Ian Bethel Sr., a Minneapolis pastor and opponent of Question 2, believes the community made the right choice in rejecting the initiative, but argues that the police force can no longer conduct business as usual.

“If anybody needs the police, it is the Black community and also our Native and our Latino friends and the LBGTQ-plus community,” says Bethel, co-pastor of New Beginnings Baptist Ministries. He notes that the campaign to dissolve the MPD drew a wave of outside cash even as he and others on the city’s Unity Community Mediation Team held ongoing talks with police about reform.

Indeed, Bethel says, soon after the election, leaders on both sides of Question 2, along with the UCMT, convened to talk about the way forward.

“We said to them, ‘Let's come together and let's work together now,’” he says. “We gave them a palm branch: ‘Tell us what you want.’ Now let's move forward with transformation, reform, transparency – and also consequences” for officers accused of wrongdoing.

While the community rejected wholesale dissolution of the Minneapolis police force – and Bethel says the subsequent meeting resulted in a “road map” to hold police accountable – Myers, the UM professor, says true reform must take place from the ground up.

“Change comes not just from legislation or mandates,” Myers says. “Change comes from communities working together. And the Minneapolis community wants change.”

That yearning for change comes after more than a year of impassioned debate over policing in America, fueled by intense street protests in Minneapolis after Chauvin killed Floyd. During the subsequent weeks and months, demonstrations against police use of deadly force against African Americans swept across the country and around the world.

Activists from Washington, D.C., to Los Angeles also demanded a new model of law enforcement, urging cities to “defund the police”: a call to drastically cut police budgets and use the money to fund social programs. In Minneapolis, such demands culminated in Question 2, a proposal that would eliminate the police department in favor of a public health-focused public safety agency under broader city control, although armed “peace officers” almost assuredly would be available.

Although the ballot question needed 51% of the vote to become law, it fell short by about 7 percentage points – a definitive result, but far from a landslide. While Arradondo and Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey opposed the question – though the newly reelected Frey has called for “deep, structural change to policing in America” – proponents of the measure say the election put both city officials on notice: After all, more than 4 in 10 residents voted for sweeping change.

Remi Douah, 60, poses for a portrait at the Say Their Names Cemetery art installation near George Floyd Square in the city.(Caroline Yang for USNWR)

Even though Question 2 failed, “the police force will never be the same, the city will never be the same,” says Douah, the volunteer therapist and an affiliate researcher in the University of Minnesota’s College of Design. Douah is also executive director of 846s.org, a nonprofit focused on improving the mental health of residents in south Minneapolis; “846” represents the length of time Chauvin was believed to have knelt on Floyd’s neck during the murder.

For Douah, an immigrant from Ivory Coast who has an advanced degree in the public health arena, change came not long after Floyd’s killing, and hit especially close to home.

His son, Isak Douah, 22, had participated in a number of street protests after two other police killings of Black men: Jamar Clark in 2015 and Philando Castile in 2016. Things took a darker turn when Chauvin killed Floyd: After days of demonstrating, Isak Douah told his father that he and his friends were having thoughts of exacting revenge.

“They felt like they could kill without having any remorse,” Remi Douah says, standing on the sidewalk of Chicago Avenue near a mural of Emmett Till, across the street from Cup Foods. “He said, ‘Dad, I need to go to counseling.’ He had so much anger in him.”

After entering therapy, Isak Douah urged his father to help the young men in south Minneapolis who were just as angry. So Remi Douah created an impromptu outdoor therapist’s office on the street – free, no copay – and founded 846s.

“I decided to bring two chairs” to a nearby green-space memorial dedicated to Black people who died while under police control, says Douah, a compact, gregarious man who nods, smiles at or glad-hands passersby as he talks. “I was seeing people coming to this place, all shattered. And they wanted to talk to someone and I was there. So I started offering my services for free for about a year now.”

Remi Douah sits at the Say Their Names Cemetery, an art installation near George Floyd Square. Artists Anna Barber and Connor Wright created the installation in honor of Black people killed by law enforcement.(Caroline Yang for USNWR)

Not long afterward, Douah obtained permission from the owners of an empty art studio in a residential building on Chicago Avenue to offer free yoga classes for neighborhood residents. He also has plans to set up a wellness center featuring a homework space for school children near the Giant Express Laundromat, and is contemplating how to feed his hungry clientele.

“They tell me, ‘We can’t come to therapy on an empty belly,’” he says.

With a public health background, Douah says the problems that led to Floyd’s killing and the unrest that followed are “within the social determinants of health.” People turn to crime, he says, usually because of pressing issues: “No health insurance, food insecurity, housing insecurity. It’s difficult to solve these problems” with just one ballot initiative.

Still, voters chose to keep the MPD intact, and that’s a problem for the city, Douah says – particularly since many voters wanted to dissolve the department. It was a lot closer margin, he says, than opponents of Question 2 may care to admit.

If the police force were a business, “forty-five percent of people have said, ‘Your product sucks,’” Douah says. “Don't go and celebrate your victory. You'd be a fool. It's not a win.”

Austin, the neighborhood activist and a caretaker of George Floyd Square, says Question 2 was moot for some: After Floyd’s killing drew so much condemnation, she says police all but stopped responding to her neighborhood and other poor parts of the city.

“The police haven't been responding to this neighborhood, because of George Floyd Square,” she says, noting that gun violence in the area seems to go unchecked.

Numbers also may play a role: Since the unrest of 2020, the MPD has been hit by a wave of retirements, resignations and disability claims. And since the election, the path forward for a department that’s under federal investigation for a “pattern or practice” of misconduct is unclear.

Ultimately, the question is if grassroots attempts to deliver public safety through volunteer and neighborhood connections are sustainable, says Myers, the University of Minnesota professor.

Messages of support to Daunte Wright's family hang at the Solidarity Station, an art installation outside the Hennepin County Courthouse in Minneapolis on Nov. 30. Protesters gathered there on the first day of the trial against Kim Potter, a former police officer who fatally shot Wright in a Minneapolis suburb on April 11 and has been charged with manslaughter.(Caroline Yang for USNWR)

The initiatives “are testimony about the resilience of the community members,” Myers says. “Long after the camera crews and journalists have left town, these community gardens, wellness classes and clinics will thrive and grow.”

It’s evidence “that sometimes communities will solve their own problems without the interference of politicians,” he says. “Hopefully, the nation will learn from these local initiatives and see them as a beacon of hope for resolving many of the continuing problems our communities of color face.”
