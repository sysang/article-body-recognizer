WHO: Boosters Probably Not Answer to Omicron

More

A health professional holds a Pfizer COVID-19 vaccine booster shot, Nov. 30, 2021, in Freeport, N.Y.(Steve Pfost/Newsday RM/Getty Images)

The leader of the World Health Organization on Wednesday warned that the omicron variant could have a “major” impact on the COVID-19 pandemic while the group stood by its stance against booster shots for the general population.

“Certain features of omicron, including its global spread and large number of mutations, suggest it could have a major impact on the course of the pandemic,” WHO Director General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus said at a press briefing. “Exactly what that impact will be is still difficult to know. We are now starting to see a consistent picture of rapid increase in transmission, although for now the exact rate of increase relative to other variants remains difficult to quantify.”

The organization was asked whether it is reconsidering its stance on booster shots in light of news from Pfizer that a third dose of its vaccine offers protection against omicron. WHO has called for a booster shot moratorium in order to increase vaccine access in low-income countries.

“The boosters are, unfortunately, probably not the solution to this,” WHO chief scientist Soumya Swaminathan said, adding that the data from “country after country” shows that most people in intensive care units who are getting severely ill or dying are unvaccinated.

While she said WHO isn’t ruling out that boosters could be needed in the future, she said that the benefits of expanding coverage with primary shots are higher right now. She noted that even in countries with ample vaccine supply, large proportions of people are still unvaccinated.

Swaminathan acknowledged that preliminary reports mostly looking at the Pfizer vaccine have found that “there is a reduction in the neutralizing activity” against omicron. But she said that this is just one element of the immune system’s response.

“I think it’s premature to conclude that this reduction-neutralizing activity would result in a significant reduction in vaccine effectiveness,” Swaminathan said. “We do not know that.”

She said the results were expected given the omicron’s combination of mutations but noted that there is a wide variation in the reported reduction and that the study numbers are very small. She urged people to refrain from “jumping to conclusions on study by study.”

Pfizer on Wednesday said that preliminary data showed that just two doses of the vaccine “may not be sufficient to protect against infection with the omicron variant,” but it added that two doses could still offer protection against severe disease.

Three doses, on the other hand, increased virus-fighting antibodies 25-fold in blood samples tested in a lab.

The results were announced just a day after a preliminary report out of South Africa suggested that omicron can partially evade protection from two doses of Pfizer’s vaccine.
