Lawmakers Demand Accountability From Instagram CEO

Lawmakers concerned about teen mental health are working on bipartisan standards for social media platforms that include oversight of algorithms used to target adolescents.

By Lauren Camera
|
Dec. 8, 2021
By Lauren Camera
|
Dec. 8, 2021, at 6:52 p.m.

Dems, GOP Find Common Enemy in Social Media

More

Instagram CEO Adam Mosseri testifies before a Senate Commerce, Science and Transportation Committee's Consumer Protection, Product Safety, and Data Security Subcommittee hearing.Reuters

Democrats and Republicans appear in lockstep as they draft legislation to better police major social media companies to blunt the mounting mental health challenges among school-aged children. But it’s unclear whether they can act quickly enough to contain a crisis that’s been exacerbated by the pandemic and boiling over in real time.

“Our nation is in the midst of a teen mental health crisis. Social media didn't create it, but it certainly fanned the flames and it fueled it,'' said Sen. Richard Blumenthal, Connecticut Democrat who led a Commerce Committee hearing Tuesday featuring the head of Instagram. “The magnitude of these problems requires bold, broad accountability.”

In a political quagmire where partisanship rules, Blumnethal and Sen. Marsha Blackburn, Tennessee Republican who acted as ranking member for the hearing, are collaborating on a bipartisan framework for how Congress could impose standards and checks and balances on Instagram and other social media platforms, including oversight of the algorithms used to target adolescents.

The hearing took aim at Instagram – an offshoot of Facebook, which was recently rebranded as Meta.

“I’m frustrated because this is now the fourth time in the past two years that we have spoken with someone from Meta, and I feel like the conversation continues to repeat itself ad nauseum,” Blackburn said about the pair’s efforts, for which they anticipate introducing legislation in the new year. “Nothing changes. Nothing.”

Adam Mosseri, the head of Instagram leaned on changes his company announced early Wednesday that include better time management options and more parental control, which are set to go into effect next year.

“I believe it's important that companies like ours are accountable to high standards and I believe the most effective way to do so is to define best practices and best standards at the federal level, ideally, and to seek enforcement,” Mosseri said.But members of Congress from both sides characterized the safety announcement as too little, too late and far from robust enough to address the growing mental health crisis among adolescents. They also criticized what they deemed as vague commitments by Mosseri and said social media behemoths like Instagram could no longer be trusted.

Blumenthal said his staff created a fake Instagram account on Monday, posing as a teenage girl, and within an hour was inundated with recommendations to follow accounts that promote anorexia and eating disorders.

Sen. Amy Klobuchar, Minnesota Democrat, said Instagram’s priorities and the priorities of parents are “diametrically opposed.”

But what Congress lacks in its current authority and ability to effect any type of substantial change in the near term, school districts across the country are grappling with in real time.

Indeed, earlier this week Surgeon General Vivek Murthy issued a rare warning about a rapid and concerning decline in mental health among children, underscoring that the crisis was emerging prior to the onset of the pandemic, which has only served to worsen the issue.

“It would be a tragedy if we beat back one public health crisis only to allow another to grow in its place,” he said. "This is a critical issue that we have to do something about now. We can't wait until after the pandemic is over."

In a 53-page report Murthy released Tuesday – a clarion call to action – he noted that 1 in 3 high school students reported persistent feelings of sadness or hopelessness, a 40% increase from 2009 to 2019, and that suicide rates also increased during that time by 57% among youth ages 10 to 24.

The surgeon general also cited soaring rates of anxiety and depression, worsened by the pandemic as well as concerns over gun violence, climate change, racism and social and political conflict.

"We also have to recognize that kids increasingly are experiencing bullying, not just in school but online, that they're growing up in a popular culture and a media culture that reminds kids often that they aren't good-looking enough, thin enough, popular enough, rich enough – frankly, just not enough," Murthy told NPR on Tuesday.

His warnings come on the heels of a report from the nonpartisan watchdog arm of Congress, the Government Accountability Office, which found an alarming increase in what it calls “hostile environments” in schools, including bullying, cyberbullying, harassment, hate speech, hate crimes, physical attacks, rape, sexual assault and victimization.

In recent weeks, Education Secretary Miguel Cardona has promoted ways for school districts to use federal coronavirus aid to provide additional social and emotional support to students, especially those from hardest hit communities who experienced the most remote learning and often the most loss of family and friends from COVID-19.

Until recently, school closures this academic year have been driven almost entirely by COVID-19 outbreaks. But in an effort to address mental health of both students and staff, many school districts are adding early dismissals to the school calendar or closing early for planned holiday breaks.

Public schools in Richmond, Virginia, for example, are set to close Dec. 20 and 21 to give students and staff “a full two weeks of Winter Break to rest and recharge – physically, mentally, and emotionally,” Superintendent Jason Kamras said in a letter to the school community last week.

“This has been a particularly challenging year,” he said. “The pandemic has profoundly affected our students and staff, and we're only now seeing the full impact. Though physical health has been our focus for the past 20 months, I'm now particularly concerned about everyone's mental and emotional health.”

The school district recently reallocated $3 million in federal coronavirus aid to fund mental health supports.

"Please know that I do not take this action lightly,” Kamras said. “But I believe we face an emotional and mental health emergency right now that requires us to think differently about this year."

As for Congress and what it can do, Blumenthal said to expect bipartisan legislation in the near future.

“Parents are asking, ‘What is Congress doing to protect our kids?’” he said. “The resounding, bipartisan message is legislation is coming.”
