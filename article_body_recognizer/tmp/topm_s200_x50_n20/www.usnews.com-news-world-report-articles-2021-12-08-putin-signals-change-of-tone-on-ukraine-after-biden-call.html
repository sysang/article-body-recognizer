Putin Signals Change of Tone on Ukraine After Biden Call

Despite concerns in recent days that a Russian invasion of Ukraine appeared imminent, the Russian leader a day after a high-profile summit offered new cooperation with the White House.

Russia President Vladimir Putin on Wednesday characterized his virtual meeting with President Joe Biden the day before as open, substantive and constructive, and he announced that the Kremlin would within a week – perhaps even days – present the U.S. with new proposals on joint security issues.

Putin detailed his take on the call at a press conference Wednesday morning, acknowledging the principal topic of Ukraine but also the importance of discussions about cybersecurity – amid U.S. accusations of a Russian-backed hacking campaign – and the value of keeping an open dialogue.

“We agreed that an appropriate structure will be created that will be able to do this professionally,” the Russian leader said, according to a translation of his remarks.

The announcement, though seemingly bureaucratic, indicates the Biden administration has succeeded in at least one of its goals for the high-profile encounter: moving heightened tensions in the Washington-Moscow relationship away from Putin’s provocative military action around Ukraine and instead toward matters of broader mutual concern.

Several senior officials within the administration this week have, at times awkwardly, attempted to push bold rhetoric about the strong military response the U.S. is willing to employ were Russia to follow through on its threats to undermine or even invade Ukraine, a former Soviet republic. But they are also quick to shift the conversation to the economic and diplomatic measures they appear more comfortable in wielding – not in the least because those don’t center on ultimately going to war over Ukraine, which Russia, at least, appears willing to do.

Sen. Tim Kaine, Virginia Democrat, suggested to the State Department’s top policy officer shortly after Tuesday’s Biden-Putin call ended that a failure for the U.S. to respond militarily to the Russian threat in Ukraine could embolden other adversaries with similar designs on contested territory, specifically China against Taiwan.

“In this setting, I would simply say that this is a moment of testing,” Victoria Nuland, undersecretary of state for political affairs, said in response during the Senate Foreign Relations Committee hearing, indicating she would rather continue the conversation in a closed setting. “I believe that both autocrats around the world and our friends around the world will watch extremely carefully what we do, and it will have implications for generations.”

Many close observers agree that America’s traditional methods of deterrence against other global powers appear to be changing.

“Strong deterrence plus a willingness to have a strategic dialogue on broader European security issues (rather than a Ukraine-focused negotiation) seems to be the approach,” Martin Indyk, a distinguished fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations, wrote on Twitter. “Let’s hope it works.”

Indyk was responding to a press briefing by national security adviser Jake Sullivan held shortly after the virtual summit ended. And there, too, Sullivan demurred when asked specifically if the U.S. is willing to send new troops to the region if Russia invaded Ukraine.

“Obviously many of our partners on the eastern front, our Baltic allies — Romania, Poland, other countries — will be increasingly concerned about the security and territorial integrity of their countries,” Sullivan said. “They will be seeking, we expect, additional capabilities and potentially additional deployments, and the United States will be looking to respond positively to those things in the event that there is a further incursion into Ukraine.”

Biden eventually appeared to confirm this approach. When asked on Wednesday about sending U.S. troops to Ukraine, he responded, “That is not on the table.”

“We have a moral obligation and a legal obligation to our NATO allies,” Biden told reporters before leaving for a trip to Kansas City. “It’s a sacred obligation. That obligation does not extend to Ukraine.”

Samuel Charap of the RAND Corporation think tank observes the Biden administration believes it can coerce Russia into backing down from its military provocations against Kyiv through its own threats in addition to bolstering the defense of Ukraine itself.

“But Moscow’s current military buildup has been accompanied by dramatically tougher rhetoric in recent months, suggesting that this time is different,” Charap wrote in an op-ed published in Politico. “It may not be too late for the Biden administration to seize the diplomatic initiative and broker arrangements to avert a war. But to do this, it should rethink the basic carrot-and-stick formula that has characterized America’s approach thus far.”

“Russia has demonstrated that it is prepared to go to extreme lengths in Ukraine — way farther than the United States or the EU,” he concluded, adding that if the U.S. is unwilling to also push Ukraine to live up to its diplomatic obligations to cease hostilities with Russia, “the current policy of threatening consequences to Moscow and bolstering support for Kyiv may be insufficient to stop a war.”
