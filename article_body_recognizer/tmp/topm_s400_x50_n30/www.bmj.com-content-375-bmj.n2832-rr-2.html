Rapid Response:

Re: Partha Kar: Holding up a mirror to racism in the NHS

Dear Editor,

I read Partha Kar's article about racism within the NHS with dismay. I am disappointed that the BMJ continues to publish such one-sided angry rhetoric.

I have no doubt that racism exists to some extent in many organisations and I am not so naive as to think that the NHS is any different in this. But this article paints a picture of an entire NHS culture that is racist.

I work in the frontline of a busy teaching hospital and this is not my experience. I have the privilege of working with high performing surgical and critical care teams which are culturally, ethnically, and racially diverse. Our theatre matrons, team leaders, and clinical managers are drawn from all ethnic backgrounds and represent a multitude of different nationalities. Our clinical teams thrive as a result. I find the sweeping generalisation in Partha Kar’s article, that NHS staff are ‘blighted by racism, sexism, homophobia' deeply insulting. My experience is that race doesn’t enter the equation or the conversation. We are far too busy at the coalface to consider anything beyond the hideous waiting list backlog, the intense emergency department pressures, and the crisis in our staffing and bed capacity. These are the issues which occupy frontline clinicians on a day to day basis, not worries about ‘racial slurs’. This article feels at best misleading and out of touch, at worst highly inflammatory.

I would ask that Partha Kar considers the negative impact of his comments on our working relationships. I worry that his views fuel suspicion and mistrust amongst colleagues, create division where we have cohesion, threaten collaborative working, and risk undermining the high performing multi-cultural teams that currently exist at every level within the NHS.

Competing interests: No competing interests

08 December 2021
Jenny K McLachlan
Consultant Anaesthetist
Shackleton Department of Anaesthetics, University Hospital Southampton
