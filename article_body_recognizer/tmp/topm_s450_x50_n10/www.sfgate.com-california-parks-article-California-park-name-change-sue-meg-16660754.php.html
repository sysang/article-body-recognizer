Why it cost California tens of thousands of dollars to rename a small state park

Ashley Harrell, SFGATE
Dec. 1, 2021Updated: Dec. 1, 2021 11:54 a.m.
Facebook Twitter Email

Members of the Yurok Tribe had been asking for a change to the name of Patrick’s Point State Park — now called Sue-meg State Park — since the turn of the century.

The former name came from a landmark named after Patrick Beegan, an Irish immigrant who briefly claimed a peninsula on ancestral Yurok lands, and was accused of murdering numerous Native Americans, including a young Yurok boy.

So why did it take two decades to reinstate the Yurok name for the land?

It’s complicated. But because changing a park’s name is time-consuming, costly and confusing for visitors, the agency is typically very protective of park names, said California State Parks planning chief Alex Stehl, who shepherds all park naming projects through to completion.

For a perpetually cash-strapped state agency, financing name changes can be a tall order, particularly because the process is so extensive. When there’s a request, state employees must research where the name came from to determine whether the park or feature should be evaluated for a name change.

If so, discussions among the agency and executive staff, stakeholders and partners commence. “We might decide no, we're not going to change this name, because we feel that the name is appropriate,” Stehl said. “Then we would message that out and hope that our initial evaluation and our decision would be accepted.”

If the agency decides that the name does need to be changed — as was eventually the case with Sue-meg — the agency works with the public, historians and stakeholders to research an appropriate name. “Working with the public can take months to years,” Stehl said, “depending on how involved they want to be. And our stakeholders can really take some time.”

That’s the hardest part of the whole process, she said. Figuring out the new name, and making sure that everybody is OK with it.Once that step is complete, State Parks presents the name to the executive staff, who evaluate it. They can either approve it or ask for more research. Once they approve the new name, it is sent to the California State Park and Recreation Commission for final approval. Or not.

Years ago, when the name of Annadel State Park — now Trione-Annadel State Park — was being reconsidered to honor a generous donor, the commission asked State Parks to go back and re-evaluate, then eventually approved the change.

In the case of Sue-meg, the commission voted unanimously to accept the name change. The cost of that entire process, Stehl said, was in the tens of thousands of dollars. But the length of the planning stage can run the gamut, she said, and in other cases could creep into the hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Should a name change be approved, there are then costs with switching out signage and brochures. With Sue-meg, those changes totaled $3,900, said California State Parks spokeswoman Adeline Yee, and in fact, the materials were due for an update regardless of the name change. For other, larger parks, though, that process could cost much more, she said.

Meanwhile, in step with a wider racial reckoning, the Reexamining Our Past Initiative has California State Parks re-evaluating all 280 of its park names, along with thousands of features within those parks, Stehl said. The publicity for the initiative has “gotten people’s brain fluid flowing,” she adds, which has resulted in a number of new name change requests for parks and features.

In addition, earlier this month U.S. Interior Secretary Deb Haaland issued an executive order declaring the word "squaw," which has been used as a slur toward Indigenous women, a derogatory term. Within California's state park system, one facility and several geographic features include the word and will likely need to be renamed, according to the San Francisco Chronicle (SFGATE and the Chronicle are both owned by Hearst but operate independently of one another).

Another park feature in question is Negro Bar, a day-use area within Folsom Lake State Recreation Area. State Parks is asking for public input on a culturally appropriate name for the historic site, which honors African American miners. Suggested new names have included Black Miners Bar, African American Bar, Black Freedom Bar, Eagle Bar, Freedom Bar, Historic Negro Bar, Leidesdorff Bar, Main Bar and Miners Bar. Officials hope to present a recommendation for a new name "in the new year," according to California State Parks’ website.

Certain aspects of the interpretative programs at Humboldt Redwoods State Park, Prairie Creek Redwoods State Park, Santa Cruz Mission State Historic Park and Sutter’s Fort State Historic Park are also under construction.

In the Humboldt Redwoods, new interpretive signs highlight the racist legacy of some of the founding members, and in Prairie Creek Redwoods, a 1948 memorial honoring Madison Grant — who promoted racist, anti-Asian, eugenicist and anti-miscegenation policies — has been removed.

Santa Cruz Mission State Historic Park issued a statement acknowledging the negative impact of the mission on Native Americans and committed to creating a more inclusive space. Sutter’s Fort State Historic Park also issued a statement acknowledging a previously "unbalanced perspective" with regard to Native Americans and promised to work with local tribes to tell a more complete story.

Many more names and features may yet come under scrutiny. And while these processes may be time-consuming and expensive, Stehl said, it’s important.

“We've recognized that as the times change, what is and isn’t appropriate changes,” she said. “And so we're hoping that we establish a process where these names are regularly reviewed.”
