This historic neighborhood in Fremont could have been Hollywood. Now it's a retreat from Bay Area life.

Susana Guerrero, SFGATE
Dec. 8, 2021Updated: Dec. 8, 2021 12:40 p.m.
Facebook Twitter Email

On the four-block stretch of Fremont’s Niles Boulevard, no major grocery store, coffee chains or corporations can be seen. Instead, there’s a mix of antique stores, tiny auto shops and a dash of hole-in-the-wall restaurants housed within worn-out buildings. If you didn’t know any better, you might take the sequestered corridor, tucked between residential homes and Highway 84, as a forgotten part of town. Yet the historic district is anything but desolate.

The quaint promenade is unlike any other neighborhood in Fremont or maybe any town around the Bay Area. Time seems to slow down in Niles, where locals meander down sidewalks to browse inside antique shops with a cup of coffee in hand. And on a recent morning, it’s exactly what I needed.

Niles just so happens to be a short commute from my East Bay home, but the drive there never ceases to amaze me. Just as I make a right turn onto Alvarado-Niles Road to head towards Niles Boulevard, I take in the typical small-town businesses nearby. A Taco Bell on the corner, a Citi Bank on another, and the veterinarian where my family used to take our pets for checkups. Then after a few minutes, the traffic clears, and the setting transforms from retro strip malls to what appears to be the set of an old Western movie.

Devout Coffee, a cafe and coffee roaster, is at one corner of Niles Boulevard, and its location gives way to the lush green hills in the distance. The hills change in appearance with the seasons, but the bold “Niles” signage that resides near the peak is a constant that never fades.

When owners Jon Fisher and Steven Pape opened Devout nine years ago, there weren’t many specialty coffee roasters near Fremont, so they decided to take a leap of faith and open their own. They started off with just a coffee cart and half the space they currently have, but as third wave coffee began to gain traction, Devout has since expanded to include an indoor sitting area (which is currently put away during the pandemic), an outdoor patio and a roasting facility that’s still in the construction phase.

“We built everything in our coffee shop,” said Kindsey Pape, marketing manager at Devout Coffee. “Something that was always really cool to us was that Niles was the first Hollywood. It was a place where people dreamed of doing something new and different to make moving pictures. We just liked that for Devout because we thought that we were going to be these pioneer people that introduced specialty coffee to our community.”

On my visit, I order a cup of coffee to-go and continue my exploration of the area. Next to Devout is The Florence, a local watering hole that describes itself on Facebook as “very biker friendly.” On most days you’ll catch a cluster of motorcycles parked out front along with a short Charlie Chaplin statue that proudly stands just outside the door — a subtle nod to Niles’ silent movie filming days, before Niles and other neighboring towns became part of Fremont.

Gilbert M. “Broncho Billy” Anderson, an actor and film director who helped run the Essanay Film Manufacturing Company based in Chicago, opened the California branch in Niles in about 1912, and more than 350 silent films were shot over the years in the area, including “The Tramp,” which stars Chaplin himself.

“Anderson had been traveling around the United States for three years, looking for the perfect weather and filming location for the Westerns that he was making," David Kiehn, a historian for the Niles Essanay Silent Film Museum, told KQED.

"Chaplin had been working at the Keystone Film Co. for $150 a week, and his contract was almost up," Kiehn added. "And Broncho Billy's righthand man, Jess Robbins, signed him at Essanay for $1,250 a week — and a $10,000 signing bonus.”

Chaplin, the area’s unofficial mascot, can be spotted throughout Niles, including on a mural just past the Niles Essanay Silent Film Museum, but Anderson is also celebrated at a popular nearby pizzeria. Bronco Billy’s Pizza Palace, at 37651 Niles Blvd., bears the silent film director’s name and has been a fixture of Niles for at least 30 years. By 11 a.m., there’s already a steady group of customers sitting inside the restaurant waiting for cheesy pies, while others place orders outside at the takeout window by the entrance.

About a block away is Tyme for Tea, a popular Victorian-style tea service and antique shop, also known for drawing lines. Tyme for Tea reminds me of stepping into your grandmother’s home. Ornate floral tea sets are stacked on shelves, while porcelain figurines with bonnets and expressive eyes observe guests who bite into scones under bright chandeliers. The wooden floors squeak slightly as the staff whoosh by with tiered trays stacked with cucumber sandwiches and puff pastries gently placed on doilies.

The room hums with the sound of private group conversations until I notice a young waiter slip away into a shadowed employee only section. Then a moment later there’s a boisterous “Ho ho ho, Merry Christmas!” that rouses guests who are too busy nibbling on dessert. Suddenly Eartha Kitt’s cheeky and classic Christmas hit, “Santa Baby,” begins to play. Getting into full character mode, the waiter joyfully makes his way to each table as he cradles a red bag filled with miniature candy canes.

Between the suggestive lyrics and the theatrics, I can’t decide whether to feel awkward as I sit among a room of mostly older women or if I should join in on the merriment and get into the holiday spirit. I decide to get my head out of the gutter and do the latter.

One Santa photo op and too much gingerbread flavored tea later, I’m feeling quite cozy and head over to the Niles Depot Museum. It's just a short drive from the Niles Canyon Railway station that offers rides to Sunol throughout the year, but during the holiday season, each train car is decked out in Christmas streamers for its annual Train of Lights. There's little movement by The Niles Depot Museum but the building is still enjoyable to see up close.

After a day of exploring, I can’t help but wonder about the businesses that operate along Niles Boulevard and whether the owners ever feel isolated from other major streets in Fremont. When I ask Pape about Niles, she says the neighborhood offered a slower pace of life that just made sense for Devout.

“We hand brew pour over coffee and you slow down for a minute,” Pape said. “You enjoy your experience of having a cup of coffee. We're not trying to push you out the door. I remember people were like, ‘Why did you guys pick Niles? It's such a weird part of town.’ And years later they'd be like, ‘Oh my gosh, Niles. Of course! It's like the perfect spot.’ And there's so many people that hang out here now and weekends are so busy.”

“When people say Devout is a hidden gem, I feel like that counts for the whole neighborhood," she added. "It's just this little street that you never really think about, but when you do, you're like, ‘Oh my gosh, I really love this place.'"

Editor’s note: An earlier version of this story misidentified the Niles Depot Museum for Niles Canyon Railway station. The story has been updated to reflect the change.
