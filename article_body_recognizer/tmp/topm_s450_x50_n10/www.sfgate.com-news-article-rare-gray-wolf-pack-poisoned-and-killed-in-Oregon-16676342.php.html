'Horrific': Oregon police ask for the public's help after 8 rare wolves poisoned, killed

Amanda Bartlett, SFGATE
Dec. 5, 2021
Facebook Twitter Email

There was reason for concern last February when the Fish and Wildlife Division of the Oregon State Police received an alert that a collared wolf from the state’s already scarce population was possibly dead. 

Troopers and ODFW personnel responded to the scene and discovered that not one, but five wolves — three males and two females — had been killed, and that the wolves had comprised every last member of the Catherine Pack. 

A helicopter circled the area where their bodies were located southeast of Mount Harris in Union County and spotted a deceased magpie but nothing else of evidentiary value. The animals were transported to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service Forensics Lab, where it was determined that their deaths were caused by poisoning.

Now, after months of investigation and exhausting all leads in the case, police are seeking assistance from the public in locating the person or persons responsible for killing eight wolves total, according to a press release from the department.

“To my knowledge this is the first wolf pack to be killed by poison in Oregon,” Oregon State Police Capt. Stephanie Bigman told the Associated Press. 

Three additional wolves from other packs were also found deceased in subsequent months. A female from the Keating Pack turned up in the same vicinity as the Catherine Pack on March 11, as well as a skunk and another magpie. A month later, officers located a deceased male wolf from the Five Points Pack just over 12 miles away near Elgin, and in July a young female wolf from the Clark Creek Pack was found dead northeast of La Grande. Toxicology reports confirmed the presence of “differing types of poison” in all three of these wolves.

“This is horrific,” Sristi Kamal, of Defenders of Wildlife in Portland, told the Associated Press. At last count, Oregon had a population of 173 wolves spanning 22 packs, according to a 2020 report from the Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife. Kamal called the loss of eight “so egregious.”

“This is quite clearly an intentional and repeat offense,” Kamal told the AP. 

The news of the wolves' deaths comes just weeks after OR-93, the gray wolf who made a record-setting journey from Oregon to California, was found deceased as a result of a vehicular strike. The initial sighting of OR-93 near Yosemite in February was the first of its kind in 100 years — the species was absent in California for nearly a century until 2011, and now the state has three officially recognized packs.

Organizations including Defenders of Wildlife, the Center for Biological Diversity, Cascadia Wildlands, the Humane Society of the United States, Northeast Oregon Ecosystems, Oregon Wild, Predator Defense and WildEarth Guardians are offering a combined reward of $26,000 for information leading to an arrest of the party responsible for the deaths of the eight wolves in Oregon. 

Gray wolves are protected under the Endangered Species Act. Unlawfully killing an endangered species carries a maximum sentence of one year in prison, a $100,000 fine and one year of supervised release, according to the U.S. Attorney’s Office.

The Oregon State Police asks that any person with information related to the investigation call their tip line at 1-800-452-7888 or *OSP (677) or via email at TIP@state.or.us.
