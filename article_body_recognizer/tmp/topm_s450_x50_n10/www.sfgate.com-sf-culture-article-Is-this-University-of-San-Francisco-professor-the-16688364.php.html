Is this University of San Francisco professor the next Bay Area 'Jeopardy!' champion?

Fiona Lee
Dec. 10, 2021
Facebook Twitter Email

The Bay Area is having a moment on “Jeopardy!” 

First, there was champion Andrew He, a software engineer from San Francisco, who won five straight games, earning him a spot on the Tournament of Champions. He was defeated by the current champ, Amy Schneider, an engineering manager from Oakland, who made headlines for her groundbreaking run. Schneider is still undefeated after 13 games, putting her in the top echelon of “Jeopardy!” contestants. 

Now, there could be another Bay Area “Jeopardy!” champion in University of San Francisco professor J.P. Allen, who is competing in the game show’s first Professors Tournament. 

Allen — who teaches classes on entrepreneurship and digital business — has lived in San Francisco for nearly 20 years, after growing up in the Oakland Hills. (Between Oakland and San Francisco, he has also lived in Saudi Arabia, Switzerland, Greece and Santa Cruz.) Like He and Schneider, Allen’s background is in technology: He holds a Ph.D. in computer science. 

The digital business professor’s appearance is the culmination of a long-held dream, and it came during a time that has been particularly difficult for educators with the pandemic. When Allen got the call, he laughingly likened it to “getting the Nobel prize ... you get crazy.” 

He has been attempting to be on “Jeopardy!” since 1999, he told SFGATE over a video chat, and back then, would-be contestants had to physically go for an in-person interview. (Today, contestants start with an online test and move to online tryouts.)

“I qualified then 20 years ago, but I never, never got the call,” he explains. “So of course, since then, it’s been my white whale every year.” 

To prepare for his stint, Allen says that he called upon a lifetime of intellectual curiosity, since “‘Jeopardy!’ demands you have a sort of surface knowledge of everything,” rather than a deep knowledge of specialist trivia. He did brush up on some of his weaker categories, which he says included literature, Shakespeare, opera and the Bible, and he also reviewed and played older games through the J! Archive, a fan-run archive of the show that includes clues and answers. 

“[‘Jeopardy!’] taps into the geek’s secret of happiness, which is: It’s fun to know things,” he says. 

For Allen, the hardest part of the tournament was figuring out the buzzer’s timing. Contestants face a tricky and fast-paced decision-making process at that moment, because they can’t buzz in until the reading of the clue is finished, yet they must also beat the other contestants to the buzzer.

“There’s different schools,” Allen says, “People have the pens, people have the toilet roll plungers, but until you get on that stage, it’s really difficult to try it out and prepare for it.” He pointed out that while viewers at home see the clue as it takes over their television screen, the contestants see the clue on a board “that’s 30 feet away, it’s got all these other colors and there are blue LEDs around it when you’re allowed to buzz in.” 

Despite the intensity of the tournament, he says that the best part was meeting and developing a camaraderie with the other contestants, perhaps because they were sequestered away without electronics in a darkened “Wheel of Fortune” stage to await their turns, where there’s “this huge sign [that] says don’t touch the wheel.”

And despite the long-running controversy over who should be the next “Jeopardy!” host, he also had high praise for host Mayim Bialik. “She’s a pro, man. She is really good at delivering those lines,” he says. He pointed out that when they had to shoot retakes to cover for mispronunciations at the end of each show, Bialik needed very few. 

Allen wasn’t able to tell us how he performed in the tournament — that would be a spoiler that the show won’t allow before it airs — but when SFGATE asked if he thought contestants with a STEM or computer science background, like himself (as well as He and Schneider), had an advantage on “Jeopardy!,” he laughed and gave a very professorial answer.

“It's interesting, but of course, we're academics, so we can't just identify a correlation,” he said. “We need to have causality. Right. So we need to identify the causal mechanism and is it, they just know more? They're quicker on the buzzer? More research is needed.”

J.P. Allen will appear on the “Jeopardy!” Tournament of Champions tonight at 7 p.m. on ABC 7.
