It’s 10:31 a.m., I just found out about the Rittenhouse verdict and I took a shot of vodka

Rod Benson
Nov. 19, 2021Updated: Nov. 19, 2021 1:47 p.m.
Facebook Twitter Email

It’s 10:31 a.m. and I just took a shot of vodka. 

I was about to go to the gym. After that I was going to go to the art store to pick up some canvases for a commission I'm working on. I was wondering if I had time to hit the driving range later. Nope. I read the news and hit that moment of grief where it felt like there was no other recourse. So I took a shot of vodka.

I found out from my group chat, via a text from a friend: “Bro I'm so f—king angry right now. They couldn't even give this dude 1 CHARGE!” I immediately knew he was talking about Kyle Rittenhouse. 

I’ve reacted this way before. When George Zimmerman got off I remember I took a shot of vodka. When Tamir Rice was killed I walked out of my Hollywood apartment aimlessly until I found a bar I felt comfortable crying in, and then I took a shot of vodka. When I found out Donald Trump won the presidency, I was in my apartment in South Korea. It was the morning there and I took a shot of vodka. 

All of the above led to a familiar cycle: people I knew, or watched on TV/read on social media, expressed being upset, questioned how something like this could happen. But in each of those situations, every Black person I knew was shattered inside. Time after time we see the justice system work radically differently for one set of people than it does for us. For whatever reason my coping mechanism is to take a shot of vodka. For others it's to cry, to hug their families, or to consult their friends. It’s a frantic, desperate search for a long-extinguished sentiment: hope. Hope that it has to get better than this. 

Our group chat is off and running. One guy says, “Not even minor in possession bro come on.” Another says, “f—king trash.” We discuss the judge’s bizarre behavior, how there has to be a mistrial. Rittenhouse crossed state lines and used an AR-15-style rifle to kill two people. We, as a group, are all we have to try and cope with the news, and the reality. A hopeless one.

We just watched the judge act like a f—king asshole the entire trial. We watched this kid’s fake tears fly in the face of real tears from the Central Park Five, who did nothing wrong. Kyle Rittenhouse cried fake tears for killings he committed on camera, and a jury of his peers let him walk. Where was my help, I wonder, when I cried real tears in real handcuffs screaming out, “Why is this happening to me? I've done nothing wrong!”

I'd love to make some big, grand point here. I can’t. There isn’t one. There never has been. It's just the way life works; the way justice works in America has never worked for us. No need to turn this piece into a lengthy article detailing the history of this, the history of that. 

The Black community has always looked to each other in these moments. When Trayvon Martin was killed, I was at the protest. There must have been 60 people at the one in Hollywood. And when Tamir Rice was killed, I was at that protest too. There might have been 60 people in downtown Los Angeles. It never felt like we had allies or support. 

After George Floyd was murdered, more non-Black folks started listening to people like me. I have to imagine that since Floyd was murdered, more of you are really coming to grips with our sh—ty criminal justice system. 

All I can ask is that you grieve with us. No, not every Black person is going to have a conversation with you about it. Not everyone's going to want to explain how we’re processing things. Some of us — myself included — don’t want to feel alone, is all. Just be there for us and be there with us and be better.
