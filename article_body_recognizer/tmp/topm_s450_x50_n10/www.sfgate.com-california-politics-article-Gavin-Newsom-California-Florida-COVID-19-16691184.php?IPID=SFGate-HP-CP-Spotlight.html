Is Gavin Newsom telling the truth about California and Florida's handling of COVID?

Eric Ting, SFGATE
Dec. 10, 2021
Facebook Twitter Email

While on a book tour this week, California Gov. Gavin Newsom continued to draw contrasts between his state and the state of Florida, the latter of which took a much more hands-off approach to the COVID-19 pandemic.

"It's just interesting, there's just so much attention that's been given to Florida as of late," Newsom said while on ABC's "The View" on Wednesday. "And I don't mean this in a cheap shot, but it's interesting. Since the beginning of this pandemic, 53% more deaths per capita in the state of Florida. Fifty-three percent more in the state of Florida compared to California."

He continued, "The state of California did better economically than Florida, Texas, Indiana and the U.S. as a whole," echoing past shots he's taken at Florida in recent months.

It is true that Florida has almost 53% more COVID-19 deaths per capita than California does (though it is worth pointing out that Florida's population is considerably older than California's). The two states actually had similar death rates in March of this year after California saw a devastating winter surge amid a second stay-at-home order, but Florida experienced a much worse delta variant-driven wave over the summer. COVID-19 vaccines are highly protective against severe illness and became widely available in the spring, and California has maintained a higher vaccination rate than Florida since at least early summer.

While Newsom is telling the truth on death rates, his claim that California "did better economically" than Florida is more dubious. 

The governor's office has a fact sheet pointing to the raw number of jobs created, a budget surplus and other metrics as evidence of a strong economic recovery. Omitted from that fact sheet is the state's unemployment rate.

The most recent estimates from the United States Bureau of Labor Statistics found that California is tied with Nevada for the highest unemployment rate in the country at 7.3%. By contrast, Florida is middle-of-the-pack with an unemployment rate of 4.6%.

Furthermore, a Bureau of Labor Statistics report from October found that California's job growth had dramatically slowed and that the state makes up 31% of the nation's unemployment claims despite having 11.7% of the country's labor force. In addition, a recent report from the California Center for Jobs the Economy found that California's "recovery of jobs lost during the pandemic" lags most other states.

Using the number of job openings divided by the number of unemployed individuals by state, the report concludes that "jobs potential remains higher elsewhere," and that "California remains poised to continue in its recovery mode for the foreseeable future under current policies" while "a great many other states — those near or above the one job per unemployed — are instead poised to move into a renewed growth phase." Florida is just above one job per unemployed, and will presumably move into a "renewed growth phase."

Beyond employment numbers, California has beaten Florida in GDP growth over the course of the past year. According to the most recent quarterly estimates provided by the United States Bureau of Economic Analysis, Florida had a real GDP growth rate of 12% between Q2 2020 and Q2 2021, while California had a rate of 13% in that time frame.

If you believe that employment and GDP growth are the best measures of economic performance, it's not quite clear that one state is doing "better economically" than the other.
