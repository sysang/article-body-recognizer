Current Mortgage Rates Increase Slightly Over 3% For First Time Since June

(Ad) The average rate for a 30-year fixed-rate loan dropped to 2.98% this week, according to Freddie Mac’s weekly survey.

Rates are decreased by 0.11 percentage points from a week ago. Even with the decline the 30-year rate has been above 3% since June 24 and the biggest rate increase since the middle of January. Rates had been hovering between 2.86% and 2.88% since mid-August.

Some forecasts indicate that mortgage rates may remain below 3% for the time being. For potential buyers considering whether to buy or refinance a home, these historic lows may push them to act fast and lock in a rate with a pre-approval.

The direction of rates for other types of loans also trended upward this week:

      • The current rate for a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage is 2.98% with 0.7 points paid, an decrease of 0.11 percentage points week-over-week. The interest rate during the same week last year was 2.84%.
      • The current rate for a 15-year fixed-rate mortgage is 2.27% with 0.6 points paid, down 0.08 percentage points week-over week. The reate averaged 2.34% during the same week last year. 
      • The current rate on a 5/1 adjustable-rate mortgage is 2.53% with 0.4 points paid, down by 0.01 percentage points from the previous week. A year ago, the 5 - year ARM rate averaged 3.11%.

Mortgage Rate Trends

Will current mortgage rates last?

Mortgage rates have been in a holding pattern for a little over a month. The rise of the Delta variant has put a damper on the economic recovery.

This may soon change, however, as COVID infections seem to be plateauing. On the economic front, inflation was a little lower than expected last month.

Why does this matter?

The Federal Reserve has based decisions around tapering its accommodative monetary policy on the strength of the labor market and the economic recovery. The Fed’s position has been that inflation, which is currently over 5% and above the central bank’s target rate of 2%, is temporary. 

If inflation continues to slow down and other economic indicators, such as employment and retail sales, continue to improve, it could push the Fed to a more aggressive stance on its policy, slowing down its asset purchases.

This may result in higher mortgage rates. While that will be good news for sellers, potential homebuyers will have to dole out more money to cover the interest. Again, the time to buy or refinance at a low rate is now.

Current mortgage rates and your monthly payment

More than other factors, the annual percentage rate (APR) on your real estate purchase will affect your monthly payments — whether you’re refinancing or buying a new home.

On a $200,000 home loan with a fixed rate for 30 years:

      • 3% interest rate = $843 in monthly payments
      • 4% interest rate = $955 in monthly payments
      • 6% interest rate = $1,199 in monthly payments
      • 8% interest rate = $1,468 in monthly payments

These numbers don’t include taxes, insurance, or HOA fees.

Refinancing to a lower interest rate could save hundreds of dollars a month if you kept the same loan terms. Shortening the loan term could negate your monthly savings but save thousands over the life of the loan. You can experiment with a mortgage calculator to find out how much a lower rate could save you.

Other factors affect how much you’ll pay in mortgage payments:

      • Mortgage Insurance: Costs up to 1% of your home loan’s value to your payment each year.
      • Closing Costs: Some buyers finance their new home’s closing costs into the loan, which adds to the debt and increases monthly payments.
      • Loan Term: Choosing a 15-year mortgage instead of a 30-year mortgage will increase monthly mortgage payments but reduce the amount of interest.
      • Fixed vs. ARM: An adjustable-rate mortgage’s monthly payment could change from year to year after the loan’s introductory period expires. A fixed-rate loan’s payments remain the same throughout the life of the loan.
      • Taxes, HOA Fees, Insurance: A monthly mortgage payment could also include homeowners insurance premiums, city or county property taxes, and Homeowners Association fees.
