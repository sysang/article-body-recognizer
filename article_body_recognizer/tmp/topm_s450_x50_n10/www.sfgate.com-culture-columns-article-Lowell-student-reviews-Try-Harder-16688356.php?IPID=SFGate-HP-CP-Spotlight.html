A Lowell High School student reviews the new documentary about San Francisco’s most competitive public school

Lowell student Astrid Utting on what the film 'Try Harder!' got right, and what it didn't

Astrid Utting
Dec. 10, 2021Updated: Dec. 10, 2021 9:42 a.m.
Facebook Twitter Email

As a Lowell High School junior, I was eager to watch “Try Harder!” The documentary follows five high school students as they navigate the notoriously stressful college admissions process at Lowell, a top-tier San Francisco public high school. The film begins with a teenager giving a dry and comical tour of the campus, arriving at a trophy case and bluntly stating, “This is all our awards and stuff. This is what alumni get to brag about.” 

“Try Harder!” is an exploration of the extreme pressure Lowell High School students put on themselves. Lowell High School, ranked seventh in best California high schools, is known for its ambitious students, rigorous standards, high number of AP (Advanced Placement) course offerings and majority Asian American student population. Additionally, Lowell has been in the headlines because of controversy over its merit-based admissions (on pause due to the pandemic and the subject of multiple lawsuits). Despite often being characterized as an “elite” institution, roughly 40% of Lowell students qualify for free/reduced lunch and a large portion are the children of immigrants. For many public school families, Lowell is their best opportunity to get an education that matches or even rivals that of a private school. 

Throughout the film, viewers get to know a core cast of students.

Alvan, one of the most endearing characters, is a class clown who loves to bust out his dance moves, but also struggles under the high expectations of his loving, but controlling Taiwanese “tiger parents.”

Sophia, an extremely independent senior, juggles an ice cream parlor job, works as the school newspaper editor and captains the tennis team.

Rachael is biracial and gets strong guidance, and pressure, from her single African American mother. Being a minority at Lowell, she winces as she recounts experiences of casual racism, such as a student saying to her, “I didn’t expect for Black people to actually care about their grades.”

And Shea, an economically disadvantaged Lowell junior, chooses to live with his absent father with a drug use problem so that he can stay in San Francisco and attend this school of proudly nerdy (and more privileged) kids. 

The documentary itself is charmingly filmed and edited, featuring lighter comical moments like the senior class’s obsession with the almost-mythical Jonathan Chu (a top student who once purposefully flunked a chemistry test to lower the grading curve) or an awkward prom montage. These candid moments captured by director Debbie Lum show the audience that Lowell students aren’t just “machines” or “AP robots” — terms students and teachers frequently use to describe how Ivy League admissions officers view them — but instead are thoughtful and very real individuals. 

As a Lowell junior myself, I frequently witness students fretting over test scores, worrying about extracurriculars and agonizing over B’s. I know I do it, too. Watching these developing teens under so much pressure to make a $200,000 college decision felt like witnessing emotional abuse. Not necessarily from parents, teachers or fellow students, but from the college system itself. It is painful to watch bright, charming students judge themselves so harshly.

However, Lum works hard to show that there is so much more to these students. She successfully paints a rich portrait of high school quirkiness, portraying how creative, diverse and unique each individual student is, especially the Asian American students. There is just as much range in personalities at Lowell as at any other school. Lum humanizes Lowell students, without shying away from their ambition. 

Additionally, through the focus on Mr. Richard Shapiro, a beloved physics teacher, you see that the relationship between the teachers and the students can be richly personal. That is the Lowell I know: hard-working high schoolers, but high schoolers nonetheless. However, while the film explored the endearing personalities of dedicated students, it didn’t portray why I believe all of us students are so engaged in the first place. 

Yes, we are all ambitious and want to get into good schools, but we are also interested and curious learners. We are proud of our hard work. We don’t do everything for the test scores. I glimpse a hint of this in Shea, who, deeply passionate about climate change, relishes being surrounded by students also passionate about their education. 

After watching the film, I felt that the title “Try Harder!” took on new meaning: not only referring to the students’ motto, but the fact that today’s high schoolers must try much harder to get into college than their parents. Lowell students face a ruthlessly competitive system, higher expectations and higher pressure than the generation before. This is what makes “Try Harder!” an important film for people of all ages: It gives viewers, especially those without teenagers, a window into the motivations, ambitions and even insecurities of today’s teenagers. Most importantly, it offers insight into high school culture and experiences that are not only off-limits to adults, but starkly different from the experiences of previous generations. 

The film does not come to a definite conclusion; instead, “Try Harder!” leaves the viewer with thought-provoking questions. Adults who watch the film may reflect on their own high school and college experiences and have their own opinions on how much it defined their future. However, students like me, who have yet to embark on this college minefield, are left wondering was it worth it? 

How can we be reassured that life will go on if we don’t get into the school of our dreams, or our parents’ dreams? I still find myself trying to answer one question: Was what these students went through good or bad? Did they learn a life lesson from all that hard work, did they develop grit that they’ll apply in the future or are they burnt out and exhausted? 

At one point, Shea comes to the thoughtful conclusion that he can succeed regardless of which college he attends. If this film implies any benefits of trying hard, it's that hopefully Lowell’s senior class will emerge from the admissions process proud of the work they’ve done and prepared for the world to come, regardless of where they continue their studies.

Astrid Utting is a Lowell High student who has been published in the New York Times and Noe Valley Voice.
