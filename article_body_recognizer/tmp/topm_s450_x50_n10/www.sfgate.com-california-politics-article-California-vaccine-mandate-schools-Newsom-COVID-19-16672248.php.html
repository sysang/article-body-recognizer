Why have no other states joined California in issuing a schoolchildren COVID vaccine mandate?

Eric Ting, SFGATE
Dec. 6, 2021
Facebook Twitter Email

In early October, Gov. Gavin Newsom triumphantly announced that California would become the first state in the nation to mandate the COVID-19 vaccine for children attending school, once the Food and Drug Administration fully approves the shot for the 5-11 and 12-16 age groups.

"Vaccines work," Newsom said at the time. "It’s why California leads the country in preventing school closures and has the lowest case rates. We encourage other states to follow our lead to keep our kids safe and prevent the spread of COVID-19."

Two months later, no states have followed Newsom's lead — not even states that have been similarly aggressive in implementing COVID-19 restrictions, such as Washington, New York, Oregon and Hawaii.

Why is that? Dr. Elena Conis, a historian of medicine and public health at UC Berkeley, believes that California's breakneck pace can be attributed to momentum on childhood vaccinations that predates the pandemic.

"Before COVID, California switched from being one of the states with the nation's most lenient childhood mandatory vaccination laws to one of the states with the strictest," she said. "That happened in 2015 and 2016 in response to a measles outbreak that started at Disneyland in late 2014, where there was then scrutiny placed on the increased use of personal belief exemptions."

That outbreak spawned pockets of infection in Utah, Washington, Colorado and Oregon. As Conis noted, the state then received bad press over personal belief exemptions. She said that parents in Marin County and certain portions of Southern California routinely used those exemptions — which could be claimed for any reason — until the state then revoked them in 2015. 

Because the state had already recently tightened the screws on childhood vaccination laws with minimal backlash, Conis believes that state health officials felt comfortable moving forward with the first-in-the-nation mandate. As for why other states have yet to follow suit, Conis is less sure.

The governor's office also did not have a direct response when asked if they had a reaction to being the only state in the country to announce a mandate.

"California, like many other states, already requires students to be vaccinated against viruses like measles, mumps, and rubella — there’s no reason COVID-19 should be treated any differently," a spokesperson from Newsom's office said in a statement. "California’s school safety measures are keeping kids safe and in the classroom, and we’re leading national trends in preventing school closures due to COVID-19, as evidenced by the fact that we educate 12% of the nation’s public school students, but accounted for less than 1% of the nation’s school closures."

Dr. Amy Beck, an expert in pediatrics at UCSF, noted that California's mandate is only a "plan to make a plan," as it will not take effect until full FDA approval is given, and the governor's office said it plans to consult several pediatrics groups "prior to implementing a requirement." 

Given the squishiness of the plan, Beck hypothesized the mandate may be more about politics than anything (Newsom previously said Democrats should "lean into" COVID-19 restrictions), and that other states may be taking a "wait and see approach" where they encourage childhood vaccination before moving to outright mandates.

Under the mandate, vaccination against COVID-19 would be required for in-person learning, and Beck questioned whether having unvaccinated children in schools would be a worse societal outcome than banishing kids — whose vaccination status is largely determined by their parents and out of their control — from the classroom when they were already out of school for over a year.

"We need to be careful to not have policies with unintended consequences," she said. "I would not want any policy that would result in kids being denied in-person education over not having the COVID vaccine. We’re currently seeing the impacts of being out of school for a long time, with those cumulative impacts having long-term effects on children in respect to their weight, to their mental health, to so many health impacts that go beyond COVID. Keeping them in school should be the number one priority."

Beck also noted that some childhood vaccinations are voluntary in California, including immunizations against some forms of hepatitis and HPV. She said it's possible other like-minded states see COVID-19 as posing a risk to children more akin to those illnesses than to the risk posed by measles, mumps and rubella. COVID-19 rarely leads to severe illness among children.

"Ideally, we'd have both kids in school and kids that are vaccinated against COVID," she said. "But I think if it takes more time for parents to feel comfortable vaccinating their children, we should do what we can to encourage them but I don’t think we should be keeping them out of school."
