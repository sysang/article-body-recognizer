Caregivers led the way when the pandemic hit. Now they need help

By StoryStudio December 7, 2021 1:59 pm
  • Print

Gabriella* didn’t choose her occupation — it chose her. She had gone to college and earned two associate degrees, one in graphic design and the other in criminology. Her dream had been to become either a designer or a forensic scientist. But when her son was diagnosed with Cerebral Palsy, Gabriella stayed home to care for him while her husband worked to pay the bills. As dear as Gabriella’s professional goals were to her, family was more important.

That sentiment was tested again three years ago when Gabriella’s husband suddenly fell ill. Overnight, Gabriella was taking care of two people. With her husband unable to dress himself, much less work, the family needed income from somewhere. And when Gabriella applied for In-Home Supportive Services (IHSS), she soon learned that a caregiver’s paycheck wasn’t going to be nearly enough to cover basic expenses. So, Gabriella got a job at McDonald’s.

Now Gabriella spends her days caring for her son and husband; juggling errands, chores, and the school and social calendars of her other children; and then working the late shift until midnight five nights a week at the restaurant.

“I want to be home caring for my husband and my son. They really need me,” says Gabriella. “But we simply can’t survive.”

Gabriella is far from alone. As roughly 10,000 Americans turn 65 each day, home care has become the nation’s fastest-growing job sector. The U.S. will need to fill an estimated 4.7 million home care jobs, one million of them new jobs, by 2028. And yet our current caregivers are in crisis. The average hourly wage for long-term care providers is far below what experts calculate it would take to cover basic living expenses in Alameda County. Nationally, 53% of home care workers are forced to rely on some form of public assistance to feed their families. They commonly lack health benefits, paid sick days, hazard pay, or training opportunities that would enable them to advance in their careers. Eighty-seven percent of them are women, 62% are people of color, and 31% are immigrants — populations that have long been left behind in terms of workers’ rights and fair pay.

This is more than just an emergency for these workers and their families — it’s quickly creating a dire situation for entire communities. The pandemic has only re-emphasized how essential these frontline workers are, as many of them were facing Covid-19 head-on, working extra hours to save lives and comfort patients and families, often without proper PPE. And yet because of the low wages, lack of benefits, and risk of illness to themselves, their families, and their care recipients, many of these caregivers were forced to leave their jobs, creating widespread staffing shortages at a critical time.

One possible answer to this emerging public health crisis in Alameda County is to provide these IHSS workers with a living wage. Currently, the Service Employees International Union Local 2015 (SEIU 2015) is negotiating with the county to set a $20 per hour living wage floor for caregivers. Our research shows that investment like this can add millions of dollars to the local economy, create thousands of jobs and generate substantial increases in state and federal tax revenue.

And perhaps more importantly, a $20 per hour living wage would help caregivers like Gabriella feed their families and keep a roof over their heads while they provide their loved ones and their communities with the care they need.

At the moment, Gabriella is scraping by. Despite working two jobs, her family has been forced to move in with Gabriella’s parents. Because Gabriella’s sister also lives there with her children, eight people share one bathroom in the cramped house.

“We’re a close-knit family, and I’m grateful,” says Gabriella. “But it’s affecting my family’s mental health, and it’s very challenging to deal with how crowded we all are, along with everything else going on.”

To voice your support for caregivers like Gabriella and help them get the wages they desperately need, sign the petition to call on Alameda County leadership to establish a $20 per hour living wage floor. The health of their families and our community depends on it.
