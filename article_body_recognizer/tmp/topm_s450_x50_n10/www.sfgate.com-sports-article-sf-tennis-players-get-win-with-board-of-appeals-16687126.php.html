Real estate developer Alexandria reneged on rebuilding the San Francisco Tennis Club. Tennis players are fighting back.

Alex Shultz, SFGATE
Dec. 9, 2021
Facebook Twitter Email

In April 2021, Alexandria Real Estate did what many local tennis players feared it would do: It reneged on a voluntary agreement from 2016 to rebuild the San Francisco Tennis Club, a SoMa staple demolished last November to make way for the massive, multi-purpose 88 Bluxome building.

The tennis club was supposed to be rebuilt at the same plot of land, just multiple levels underground. Alexandria was never going along with that plan out of the goodness of its heart — this was a hard-fought, hard-won concession for the city’s tennis community, who’ve garnered the support of local figures including former San Francisco Mayor Art Agnos over the past few years.

In announcing its about-face, Alexandria argued that the tennis club never constituted a “significant” portion of the 88 Bluxome project, and therefore, its removal from construction plans does not require the approval of the city Planning Commission. Zoning administrator Corey Teague agreed, though he noted that his ruling was a narrow one that did not reflect whether Alexandria’s actions were “appropriate.”

SF Tennis Club dissenters were incredulous. Seth Socolow, who’s been leading the effort to have Alexandria stick to its word, told SFGATE that Teague’s definition of the term “significant” defies logic. “We feel that the removal of 134,000 square feet of indoor recreation space in a city that has so little of it, specifically in SoMa that has almost no recreational space of any kind, is indeed a significant change,” he said.

On Wednesday night, eight months after Alexandria tried to pull out of its side of the deal, Socolow’s side attempted its rebuttal in front of San Francisco’s Board of Appeals. And the board, swayed by hours of public comments and presentations from the city’s tennis community, handed Alexandria (and multi-billion dollar developers) a rare defeat, ruling that Alexandria’s sleight of hand does require a Planning Commission meeting — and furthermore, that zoning administrator Teague erred in his judgment.

The SF Tennis Club’s winding journey to this point is a story in itself. The SparkNotes version is such: SoMa’s 24-court facility, constructed in 1974, was considered a world-class venue that hosted greats like Arthur Ashe and Venus Williams. Chock-full of ritzy amenities, it required a membership, and it wasn’t cheap: about $200 a month. Its current members, who’ve migrated to a temporary facility at the Cow Palace, say their SoMa-based club is nevertheless a community benefit worthy of a proper second life; members were allowed to bring guests, countless public tournaments were held at the venue, and the club has a close relationship with Youth Tennis Advantage, which provides tennis lessons and tutoring to hundreds of disadvantaged children. SF Tennis Club’s advocacy extends outside, too, members say: When they originally negotiated a deal with Alexandria to rebuild their indoor facilities, they also secured $864,000 to resurface 20 courts at eight different outdoor locations in the city.

“This is not just about their own tennis membership,” Agnos, who’s been advising SF Tennis Club members since 2016, told SFGATE. “It’s about contributing to the city, not only with physical upgrades to all these facilities, but also providing programs to give poor kids a chance to play tennis.”

Wednesday’s Board of Appeals meeting demonstrated the strength of SF Tennis Club’s growing coalition. A 9-year-old child spoke about meeting her best friends at the SoMa location. An 80-year-old woman said the club has been an essential part of her life for decades. Brad Gilbert, a former pro who coached Andre Agassi and Andy Roddick, gave the club glowing reviews. Multiple speakers broke down in tears describing the relationships they’ve cultivated at the club over the years. It was an impressive showing, save for one speaker, who identified themselves by the name #SavePengShuai and argued that the Chinese Communist Party’s treatment of Peng Shuai, a pro tennis player who accused a party official of sexual assault and has since only been sparingly heard from, was akin to Alexandria’s treatment of San Francisco tennis players.

Alexandria’s defense relied on its own questionable assertions. John Kevlin, the lawyer representing the real estate developer, argued that ditching the tennis club rebuild was no different than moving on from an underground parking garage. During public comments, a high school student responded by countering that “we are not a parking garage.”

Under questioning from the Board of Appeals, Kevlin said that Alexandria’s decision was made as a result of the pandemic and a significant shift in office attendance, a key aspect of the original idea behind 88 Bluxome. “Absolutely, financial considerations came into play,” Kevlin said. He pointed out that anchor tenant Pinterest, which originally signed a long-term lease for 50% of 88 Bluxome, backed out of the deal in August 2020, a sign of the “market moving forward.”

The Board of Appeals wasn’t swayed by that argument. For one, Pinterest paid Alexandria handsomely — almost $90 million — to break its lease. SF Tennis Club’s lawyer, Anthony Giles, separately showed that Alexandria’s stock market valuation is close to an all-time high. The developer isn’t mitigating massive losses;, it’s just worried about its profit margins, club advocates argue.

“Corporate boards measure success by how much profit you make on a deal,” Agnos told SFGATE. “They have made the cold-blooded calculation that it’s worth it to them [to remove the courts] for the profit they foresee making at this facility.”

Ultimately, the board ruled 4-0 in favor of kicking this battle back to the Planning Commission at a yet-to-be-determined date. Their ruling doesn’t change a slew of realities for San Francisco tennis players: After S.F. Tennis Club’s demolition, there are no indoor tennis courts in the city, and there won’t be for quite a while. There’s no guarantee the Planning Commission will side with tennis proponents, and if the commission does, it’ll only be after a lengthy battle over far more legalese and technicalities.

But the board’s ruling does help make the playing field a little bit more even. 

“We’re negotiating with a multi-billion dollar company, and these guys are notorious for renegotiating and not honoring their commitments,” Socolow said. “But any significant change requires them to go back for full authorization of the project. Basically, we’re starting at square one.”

For now, that’s good enough.
