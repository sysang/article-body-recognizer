The best way to end a night out in San Francisco

SFGATE culture editor Dan Gentile explains why he loves a midnight bike ride home

Dan Gentile, SFGATE
Dec. 10, 2021
Facebook Twitter Email

In the immortal words of Queen: “Bicycle, bicycle, bicycle ... I want to ride my bicycle!” 

Those were my thoughts as I biked out of Golden Gate Park on the last night of Outside Lands, past throngs of attendees hailing hundred dollar Ubers and the seductive smell of bacon-wrapped hot dogs sizzling on grills. No disrespect to Lizzo’s “Squid Game” costume, but the memory of swerving down slow streets on the 30-minute ride home to Lower Haight might’ve been the happiest I felt all weekend.

Unfortunately not every ride in San Francisco is as breezy as that downhill cruise back to Lower Haight. Of all the places I’ve ridden — including New York City — San Francisco is the most challenging. Sure, it earns accolades for most bike-friendly city, the iconic Wiggle route looks like a spandex fashion show on weekends and the city boasts statistics like 1.8 million bikeshare trips in 2019. We also probably have the highest per capital of electric unicycles, which is another topic entirely. But even the most data-driven study can’t take into account that some bike lanes are blocked by a vehicle every minute, or how many of my elbows have been broken from wheels skidding in spiderwebbed Muni tracks (one).

So even for a lifelong urban biker, riding in San Francisco can be stressful. Pre-pandemic, riding to work in SOMA bike lanes is a gauntlet of pedestrians and potholes (though it made up 4.2% of San Francisco commutes as of 2018). On leisurely mid-pandemic rides up to North Beach for pizza, I’ve been cursed at by Stockton Street drivers who wouldn’t give me a pool noodle’s worth of space. But now that it’s safe (for me) to re-engage with the city’s nightlife, I’ve been able to resume the most relaxing type of bike ride there is: the midnight ride home. 

San Francisco turns into a different city after dark. Inside bars and dance clubs, inhibitions fade as people explore the possibilities that indulgence can bring. But outside is where the city really looks naked. Stripped of traffic and pedestrians, sepia streetlight mutes the Painted Ladies, changing even the brightest San Francisco street into a dark noir set. And if you’re riding a bike, it’s all yours.

Sure, I am a cheapskate who doesn’t want to pay for Ubers and I’m happy to log an extra half hour of cardio, but this feeling of ownership is the real reason I love biking home at night. On quiet neighborhood streets, it feels like you’re the only person in San Francisco still awake. Instead of riding inches from a curb through litter-filled bike lanes, I swerve in wide arches, like a downhill skier. It’s not a time to be careless, but it is the most carefree you’ll feel on two wheels (as long as you remember your light, which you should definitely remove when you park).

For those who associate biking with exercise, and associate exercise as something they definitely don’t want to do after a night out, this might all seem a bit counterintuitive. I’m not a particularly fit cyclist, and my bicycle does not cost as much as a used Prius (more like a 1995 Volvo with a broken engine). You will not see me powering up hills in Marin on Saturday mornings. But once you’ve acclimated to urban biking, a 20-minute ride home is like a refreshing digestif. The slow drip of endorphins reactivates your body and warms your soul (a nice pair of gloves is recommended).

Most of all, a nighttime ride serves as time to reflect on the city and your place in it. For all of San Francisco’s image problems, it’s objectively one of the most beautiful places in the world. That’s often lost given how competitive it is to thrive here, and how easily the pace of the city can make someone feel insignificant, like their personal story is the least interesting one in the room. But at the end of a night out while pedaling down empty city blocks, your story is the only one in sight.
