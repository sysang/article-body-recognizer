    West End landlord Shaftesbury signs up 60 new brands as footfall recovers

    Commercial landlord Shaftesbury has hailed London's 'enduring success' after adding more than 60 brands to its portfolio in the past couple of months.

    The group, whose properties span the capital’s West End and various locations in Covent Garden, said it had signed 62 new retail, hospitality and leisure brands in Carnaby, Seven Dials, Chinatown, Soho and Fitzrovia since the start of October.

    It comes as weekend footfall in these central locations has now recovered back to or above pre-Covid levels, while during the week it is at about 80 per cent of 2019 levels.

    The group has welcomed 33 new retailers, with 23 of those making their debut in the UK market.

    Of the new signings, six were from the US, with LIDS, NBA, American Eagle and Aerie, Third Man Records and Gilly Hicks all opening their first stores in the UK.

    A further nine were online brands making the move into bricks-and-mortar for the first time, including Bloobloom, the French eyewear brand.

    Meanwhile, it signed up 29 new restaurants, including Istanbul-inspired Zahter in Carnaby and Japanese robata concept Yatay in Chinatown.

    It said:

    The strength of demand from operators also coincides with the rapid recovery in footfall and sales across its locations and represents a vote of confidence in their long-term appeal and prospects.

    Activist investor Elliott criticises SSE over green energy plans

    Activist hedge fund Elliott Management has publicly called for Scottish energy giant SSE to be broken up, a move which it claims could add more than £5billion to the firm's value.

    Elliott said that it was unimpressed by a plan that SSE announced last month that would pour an extra £1billion a year into wind farms and other investments.

    But SSE has rebuffed calls to break itself up, with SSE chief executive Alistair Phillips-Davies saying: 

    Separation risks valuable growth options across the clean energy value chain, would jeopardise our ability to finance and deliver the major infrastructure the UK needs to create jobs and achieve net zero, and would lose shared skills that benefit the group.

    In its letter Elliott said that the board of SSE is 'inadequate': 

    Not one of SSE's independent board members has any meaningful operating experience in growing a large renewables business.

    This lack of expertise puts the company at a strong disadvantage to its competitors.

    Grocery inflation hits 17-month high

    Grocery inflation hit a 17-month high in November, with the prices of crisps, savoury snacks and cat food increasing the most, according to new figures.

    Market researcher Kantar said grocery inflation leapt 3.2 per cent year on year in the four weeks to November 28 – the highest rate since June last year.

    The average cost of a meal for four is now £27.48, which is an increase of 3.4 per cent compared with last year, driven by pricier turkeys, sprouts and parsnips.

    Despite the rise in prices, Britons are not yet reining in their spending for the festive season, Kantar said.

    Grocery spending in the last 12 weeks was 7 per cent higher compared with the same period in 2019, before the pandemic, although it fell 3.8 per cent compared to last year.

    Fraser McKevitt, Kantar’s head of retail and consumer insight, said things could change though:

    Recent concerns over the next stage of the pandemic may see consumers change the way they shop in the next few weeks. Our excitement about Christmas this year has been slightly tempered as news of the Omicron COVID-19 variant has emerged.

    Online grocery sales fell by 12.5% in the four weeks to late November, as we compare against more orders last year during the second lockdown. As concerns grow over rising case numbers, we expect some people will prefer to shop online again to limit their visits to stores.
