Retail sales are 4% up on pre-pandemic levels: Scramble to secure Christmas shopping amid supply chain fears drives sales of toys, jewellery and fashion - but now Omicron is set to ruin the festive cheer

  • British Retail Consortium trade body says total sales in November 2021 were 5% higher than November 2020
  • Report added that total retail sales grew 4.1% during November 2021 compared with the same month in 2019 
  • Separate Barclaycard data shows total consumer spending was 16% higher last month than in November 2019
  • But New West End Company reveal footfall in London's West End is flatlining and down 30% on pre-pandemic 

By Mark Duell for MailOnline

Published: 08:32 GMT, 7 December 2021 | Updated: 09:03 GMT, 7 December 2021

    British consumers have boosted the economy by starting their Christmas shopping earlier than usual - with big increases in spending on fashion, jewellery, gifts and toys as high streets enjoyed a Black Friday boost.

    The British Retail Consortium trade body said total sales in November were 5 per cent higher than a year earlier, which was the biggest annual increase since July and up from a 1.3 per cent year-on-year increase in October. It added that total retail sales grew 4.1 per cent during November 2021 compared with the same month in 2019.

    But the BRC said online sales of non-food products fell by 18 per cent during last month, compared to November 2020 when the UK was in lockdown. However they were up by 18 per cent when compared to November 2019.

    Separate credit and debit card data from Barclaycard showed total consumer spending - which includes eating out and travel, as well as shopping - was 16 per cent higher last month than in November 2019, pre-pandemic.

    However there are already signs that the sales boom may be over amid concerns over the spread of the new Omicron variant, with New West End Company revealing that footfall in London's West End is now flatlining.

    Footfall in the country’s main shopping district remained down 30 per cent on pre-pandemic levels in the last week beginning November 29, although Saturday saw a spike of 23 per cent compared with the previous week.

    Consumer demand has been aided during the pandemic by Government measures such as the furlough scheme, but a large chunk of retail spending moved online and only partly reversed when shops reopened in April.

    Four in ten consumers surveyed by Barclaycard said they were doing their Christmas shopping earlier than before, which analysts said came amid the background of industry warnings about shortages due to supply chain issues.

    Clothing saw its largest growth level (11 per cent) since before Covid-19 as shoppers stocked up on warmer items for winter, while 'specialist retailers' such as gift shops, toy shops and jewellers saw an increase of 21 per cent.

    Colder and darker evenings also encouraged Britons to spend more on at-home activities in front of the TV with takeaways (up 63 per cent), subscriptions (up 43 per cent) and electronics (up 17 per cent) also seeing big growth.

    But the travel industry remains badly affected, with spending on plane tickets 22.1 per cent below its level two years ago, the data showed – and this was before travel restrictions were brought in amid Omicron.

    Overall, non-essential spending grew at its fastest rate since the start of the pandemic, up 17.7 per cent from two years before, which was driven by uplifts across the retail, hospitality and leisure sectors.

    Nearly 80% of City of London workers were back at their desks last week despite rising Omicron cases - the BUSIEST offices have been since March 2020, mobility data reveals 

    By JAMES GANT FOR MAILONLINE 

    City of London workers have flocked back to the 'Square Mile' despite rising cases of the Covid variant Omicron.

    More staff were at their desks last week than at any point during the pandemic as the festive party season kicked off. 

    It comes as traffic data for the capital showed rush hour traffic last week had rocketed above average pre-pandemic levels.

    Meanwhile mobility figures showed more people were walking or driving around the city than at any point this year.

    The City of London, which employs 540,000 people. appeared to be back in the swing of things as almost 80 per cent of workers were at their desks last week.

    More staff flocked back to the district on Tuesday and Thursday than at any point since Covid struck in March 2020, according to Google data seen by Bloomberg. 

    This is despite the surging Omicron variant of coronavirus, with 246 official cases in the UK so far.

    Christmas parties may have been the cause as bars and restaurants were packed with punters last week. But many are now scrapping plans amid fears of the new strain, which the government is monitoring cautiously.

    TomTom traffic data showed London as a whole was busy throughout last week as the country gears up for the festive season. 

    Rush hour on Tuesday saw congestion levels at 79 per cent, compared to 67 per cent before the pandemic. On Wednesday this shot up to 81 per cent, in comparison with 67 per cent pre-Covid-19. 

    Thursday's figure remained high on 73 per cent - as opposed to 65 per cent two years ago - but Friday dipped to 51 per cent as many worked from home.

    Meanwhile Apple mobility data for the capital also showed Londoners were bouncing back from the pandemic and heading out. 

    November and December have seen figures shoot up compared to earlier in the year and last year. Transit is up 47 per cent, walking surged by 31 per cent while drivers also increased by 15 per cent.

    The graph only ticked above 0 per cent in July when the country opened up again and has stayed high since.

    However, economists forecast that households' disposable income will become increasingly squeezed in coming months as inflation picks up further and payroll taxes rise from April, complicating the task of the Bank of England which is due to consider whether to raise interest rates next week.

    Barclaycard executive Jose Carvalho said: 'The arrival of Christmas markets and lights across the UK brought some festive cheer to the retail, leisure and hospitality sectors in November, while the return of darker nights and colder evenings saw Brits spend more on at-home pursuits.'

    The Barclaycard report also found 88 per cent of Britons are concerned about rising household and energy bills, and a similar proportion of 87 per cent are worried about rising inflation.

    Environmental concerns are also weighing on consumers' minds after the Cop26 conference with 15 per cent of Britons either having or considering a meat-free Christmas - which increases to 28 per cent for 18-34-year-olds.

    And 38 per cent of Britons who celebrate Christmas are trying to make this year's celebrations more sustainable than they have in the past, for example by buying recyclable wrapping paper and second-hand gifts.

    Mr Carvalho added: 'As we head into final preparations for the holiday season, it's unclear how the new Covid variant and potential further restrictions will impact socialising, shopping and consumer confidence.

    'In light of recent changes, we will need to wait and see whether this has an impact on how Brits choose to finish their Christmas shopping this year.'

    BRC chief executive Helen Dickinson said November's boost in spending partly reflected Black Friday sales promotions beginning earlier in the month than previous years.

    She said: 'While Christmas may or may not be getting earlier every year, Black Friday certainly is. The American holiday has now become a month-long affair in the UK, with deals spread over a longer period than ever before.

    'As people prepared their wardrobes for the cold weather this winter, consumers took advantage of discounted clothing, shifting the focus of Black Friday from just electronics and household appliances.

    'While e-commerce was significantly down on last year, when lockdown pushed more consumers online, it still remains almost one-fifth up on pre-pandemic levels, accounting for almost half of all non-food spend.'

    Like-for-like sales, a measure which adjusts for changes in retailers' floor space, were 1.8 per cent higher than a year before, compared with a 0.2 per cent drop in October.

    The BRC data, which covers the four weeks of October 31 to November 27, found that on a total basis, sales increased by 5 per cent in November, against a growth of 0.9 per cent in November 2020.

    This was above the three-month average growth of 2.2 per cent and the 12-month average growth of 9.9 per cent.

    On a two-year basis, total retail sales grew 4.1 per cent during November compared with the same month in 2019.

    And UK retail sales increased 1.8 per cent on a like-for-like basis from November 2020, when they had increased 7.7 per cent. 

    This was above the three-month average growth of 0.2 per cent and below the 12-month average growth of 9.3 per cent.

    Over the three months to November, Food sales increased 0.1 per cent on a total basis and decreased 0.5 per cent on a like-for-like basis. This is below the 12-month total average growth of 3.7 per cent. For the single month of November, food was in decline year-on-year.

    Over the three-months to November, non-food retail sales 3.9 per cent on a total basis and increased by 0.9 per cent on a like-for-like basis. This is below the 12-month total average growth of 15.1 per cent. For the single month of November, non-food was in growth year-on-year.

    Ms Dickinson added: 'Looking forward to Christmas, spending patterns suggest that sales could be more spread out than in previous years.

    'Consumers, erring on the side of caution, are shopping for gifts earlier to get ahead of issues relating to shipping and transport.

    'Meanwhile, retailers are doing everything they can to prepare stores, warehouses and deliveries ahead of Christmas, prioritising all the food and gifts that customers will need to enjoy the festive season.'

    And Paul Martin, UK head of retail at KPMG, said: 'Consumers determined to enjoy Christmas hit the high street in November with sales up 5 per cent compared to last year.

    'Apparel and jewellery dominated Christmas gift buying at the tills, whilst limited Black Friday promotions saw big purchases for the home and on technology put on hold as sales continued to fall in these categories.

    'The high street dominated sales growth during November as consumers buoyed by the continued roll out of the vaccine programme, and eager to ensure they secured the gifts they want for Christmas, headed for physical stores to do their festive shopping. Online sales fell yet again in November, with only clothing sales seeing minor positive growth.'
