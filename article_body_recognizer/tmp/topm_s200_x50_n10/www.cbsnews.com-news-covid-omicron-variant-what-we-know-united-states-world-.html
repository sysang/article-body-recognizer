Omicron: What we know about the COVID variant seen in a growing number of states

By Alexander Tin

Updated on: December 8, 2021 / 10:02 AM / CBS News

As cases of the Omicron variant of the coronavirus have been spotted in a growing number of states and countries, researchers are now racing to measure the impact of the fast-spreading strain's unusual combination of mutations on the protection offered by vaccines, treatments, and immunity from a prior infection. 

Though the Delta variant is still estimated to be causing the vast majority of current cases in the U.S., more than 20 states have confirmed least one case of Omicron. Multiple local health officials now say they believe the variant is beginning to spread in their communities, citing cases with no links to international travel and data collected from wastewater indicating infections are here.

Early data suggests a vaccine booster shot could be needed to restore protection against infection from Omicron, though the initial doses might still be enough to fend off severe disease. Those who survived a prior case of COVID-19 may be at heightened risk of reinfection from Omicron.

Will current vaccines need to be changed?

Moderna, Johnson Johnson, and Pfizer and BioNTech all say they are already ramping up development of potential new versions of their vaccine that may be needed, targeted to Omicron's mutations.

Early data suggests a swap could be needed, though it may take weeks to know for sure. Scientists are still gathering data from lab experiments and from countries already facing surges driven by the variant to determine whether they will be needed. After that, drugmakers will need to show regulators that new versions of their vaccine can significantly outperform their original shots.

On December 8, Pfizer and its German partner BioNTech announced early findings that suggest only two doses "may not be sufficient to protect against infection with the Omicron variant." 

That came after early data shared by scientists at the Africa Health Research Institute and a German virologist also found "much more extensive escape" from the protection of two vaccine doses against Omicron compared to the Beta variant, which had previously raised fears over its ability to evade the body's defenses.

However, Pfizer and BioNTech also believe that protection offered by a different part of the immune system, vaccine-induced T-cells, may still be enough to fend off "severe forms of the disease." Boosters might also be enough to restore "a similar level of neutralizing antibodies to Omicron" as against earlier strains, the companies said.

Pfizer and BioNTech have said they could be ready to file for emergency use authorization from the Food and Drug Administration to ship a modified vaccine within 100 days. Moderna has laid out a similar timeline to advance their Omicron-specific booster for testing.

On November 29, President Biden said he was directing the FDA and CDC "to use the fastest process available, without cutting any corners for safety," to get new vaccines greenlighted if necessary. 

FDA officials have said that the agency would not need "to go back to square one and do large scale clinical trials" to greenlight a modified shot.

"I actually think we're in a really good place with the technologies we have, because if a new variant came along, it would probably be a matter of very few months before we would be mass-producing a vaccine against that variant," Dr. Peter Marks, the FDA's top vaccines official, told an American Medical Association webinar in mid-November. 

Will current medicines still work?

Spokespeople for Merck and Pfizer both say they believe their antiviral pills, which have been hailed as potential game-changers in the fight against the virus, will likely remain effective against Omicron. 

On December 7, GlaxoSmithKline and Vir Biotechnologies announced preliminary data suggesting their monoclonal antibody drug sotrovimab "retains activity" against Omicron's mutations. 

Regeneron and Eli Lilly say they are still working to study the impact of Omicron on their antibody treatments. Eli Lilly's treatment, bamlanivimab and etesevimab, had previously faced pauses by the Biden administration over concerns it might not be effective against another variant of concern.

On December 4, Dr. John Brooks, the CDC COVID-19 response team's chief medical officer, said federal health officials were weighing whether to switch to a monoclonal antibody that "might be more pan-sensitive, so it gets all variants." 

"We're watching closely to see where Omicron crops out, how quickly it spreads, and if there's a need to switch," Dr. Brooks told a webinar hosted by the Infectious Diseases Society of America. 

How dangerous is the new variant?

While few of the recent Omicron cases spotted outside Africa have been severe so far, health authorities say it will take weeks to assess the true risk of transmission, hospitalization, or death. 

In Europe, officials cautioned that younger vaccinated travelers — who have made up the majority of early cases there — tend to be healthier than the general population and may have more protection against severe disease. If severe cases occur at the same rate with Omicron as with Delta, hundreds of cases would need to be identified before seeing patients with complications.

"Severity outcomes often take several weeks to accumulate and longer to be evident at population level, impacting hospital rates," the European CDC said on December 2.

The Biden administration's top doctors say they are also looking for clues in the spread of the virus abroad, as they prepare for more cases in the U.S.

Out of Hong Kong, the CDC published a study on December 3 suggesting Omicron may be transmissible enough to evade already strict local quarantine measures. 

In South Africa, where the country has reported an unprecedented surge of infections driven by the variant, officials are investigating a "sharp increase" of cases in hospitalized younger children not seen in previous waves. Cases have largely been in younger South Africans, though officials say hospitalizations could climb as infections spread to older adults.

People also appear to be at greater risk of reinfection from Omicron, scientists concluded from early data of the variant's spread in a recent study that has yet to be peer-reviewed.

Dr. Anthony Fauci, the president's chief medical adviser, said on December 7 that early data from South Africa on hospitalizations appeared promising, suggesting that Omicron might not be more severe than the Delta variant.

"However, this could be influenced by the fact that many in this particular cohort are young individuals. The hospital stays seem to be less and the use of supplemental oxygen needs to be less," cautioned Dr. Fauci. 

Where did Omicron come from?

The sample of the first announced Omicron case was collected in South Africa. It has since been identified by the country's health authorities in samples there dating back to November 8, the CDC said on December 2. It has been confirmed the most widely so far across several African countries, and is dominant in regions of South Africa. 

Omicron appears to have evolved separately from the Delta variant, descending from another strain that appeared in mid-2020. Some scientists speculate it may have accumulated so many changes while evolving for months in animals or an immunocompromised person.

"The Omicron variant is the most divergent variant that has been detected in significant numbers during the pandemic so far which raises serious concerns that it may be associated with significant reduction in vaccine effectiveness and increased risk for reinfections," European health officials said on November 26.

Can current tests detect Omicron?

Test manufacturers and the FDA say widely used rapid and lab-based PCR tests for COVID-19 "show low likelihood of being impacted and continue to work" to detect whether people are infected by SARS-CoV-2. 

And some tests may even help accelerate the ability of health officials to track and investigate cases of Omicron. Unlike the Delta variant, cases of Omicron can lead to the same "S-gene target failure" seen in positive tests for infections by the Alpha variant earlier in the pandemic. 

Some states and countries have been using this quirk in some COVID-19 tests to prioritize which samples collected from recent positive tests should be sequenced, speeding their investigations into whether Omicron caused the infection. The FDA recently reported 26 different test kits that labs can use to screen for potential cases.

"You can get a sense before you do the whole genome sequencing of what proportion of the samples that are testing positive might be this variant," Richard Lessells, an infectious diseases specialist at the University of KwaZulu-Natal said at a press conference with South African health officials on November 25.
