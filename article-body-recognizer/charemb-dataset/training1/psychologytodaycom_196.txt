<p>Delirium’s primary symptoms involve disturbances in attention or awareness. In order to qualify as delirium, according to the DSM-5, these disturbances must:</p>
<li>Develop in a short time period (usually over the course of hours or a few days)</li>
<li>Represent a change from typical levels of attention and awareness</li>
<li>Tend to fluctuate in severity of the course of a day</li>
<li>Appear alongside an additional disturbance in cognition (such as in memory, language, perception, or orientation to time or place)</li>
<p>How these disturbances in attention or awareness manifest can vary between patients. Possible symptoms of delirium include, but aren’t limited to:</p>
<li>Difficulty concentrating on a specific topic of conversation or activity—or, conversely, becoming abnormally fixated on a topic or activity, even as others try to redirect</li>
<li>Increased susceptibility to distraction</li>
<li>Appearing disoriented or confused</li>
<li>Difficulty recalling recent events</li>
<li>Sudden anxiety, euphoria, or anger</li>
<li>Sudden or unexplained drowsiness</li>
<p>Delirium often lasts about one week in a hospital setting, but symptoms may persist for longer. Delirium can progress to coma or death, especially if the underlying cause is not treated.</p>

<p>Common symptoms of Alzheimer’s disease include:</p>
<li>Forgetting appointments or events</li>
<li>Feeling confused or overwhelmed by routine tasks, like paying bills or going grocery shopping</li>
<li>Difficulty performing basic hygiene or self-care tasks, such as getting dressed or taking a shower</li>
<li>Poor short-term memory for conversations; repeating statements, stories, or questions over and over</li>
<li>Routinely losing possessions; may put them in strange locations without realizing</li>
<li>Struggling to express thoughts or ideas; may forget frequently used words or names for objects</li>
<li>Forgetting names of loved ones, especially in the later stages of the disease</li>
<li>Poor judgment—for example, ventures outdoors in bad weather without adequate clothing</li>
<li>Inappropriate social behavior</li>
<li>Sudden personality changes, such as uncharacteristic anger </li>
<li> Depression, apathy or social withdrawal</li>
<li>An increasing tendency to be distrustful or paranoid</li>
<li>Hallucinations or delusions, particularly in the later stages of the disease</li>
<p>To qualify as a major neurocognitive disorder, there must be:</p>
<li>Clear evidence of a decline in memory and learning</li>
<li>A steady progression of symptoms</li>
<li>A gradual decline in cognition</li>
<li>An inability to live independently</li>
<li>The absence of other neurodegenerative, cerebrovascular, or neurological or mental diseases</li>
<p>To qualify as a mild neurocognitive disorder, there may or may not be evidence of genetic mutation from family history or testing, but the following symptoms should be present:</p>
<ul><li>Clear evidence of a decline in memory and learning</li> <li>A steady progression of symptoms</li> <li>A gradual decline in cognition</li> <li>The absence of other neurodegenerative, cerebrovascular, or neurological or mental diseases</li> </ul>
<p>The deficits must cause significant impairment in social or occupational functioning and represent a notable decline from a previous level of functioning.</p>

<p>Dear Primary Care and Family Doctors,</p>
<p>As T.S. Eliot has said: “We shall not cease from exploration, and the end of all our exploring will be to arrive where we started, and know the place for the first time.”</p>
<p>Alzheimer’s has been around a long time, longer than you might think, and few have known it the first time.</p>
<p>About 2,400 years ago, Plato described an illness that “gives rise to all manners of forgetfulness, as well as stupidity.”</p>
<p>Scripture, unwittingly, may have put an exclamation point on Alzheimer’s and other forms of <a>dementia</a> in the Gospel of John: </p>
<p>“When you were younger,</p>
<p>And walked wherever you wanted,</p>
<p>You will stretch out your hands,</p>
<p>Will dress you, and carry you to places</p>
<p>You do not want to go.”</p>
<p>There’s a sign on my writing desk: “When your heart speaks, take good notes.” And I have.</p>
<p>First of all, I have great respect for your profession, though I think nurses deserve far more credit than they receive.</p>
<p>Secondly, all of us, in all professions—medical, business, ministers, scientists, teachers, engineers, journalists, and the like, can do better at times. There are few exceptions.</p>
<p>Shortcuts and drive-bys get one home quicker, but the stop signs in life can make one think—a lost art at times today of multitasking.</p>
