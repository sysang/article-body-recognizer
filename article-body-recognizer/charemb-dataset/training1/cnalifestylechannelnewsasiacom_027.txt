<h1> ‘Autos are so mundane’: These women share their love of manual cars </h1>
<div> <p>From getting a kick out of changing gears to feeling more in control, they reveal the personal rewards of sticking to old-school driving.</p> </div>
<div> <div> <a> Jamie Nonis </a> </div> </div>
<p>You can scarcely find manual cars on Singapore roads these days, let alone people who still drive them. </p>
<p>So what makes these women choose to drive a manual when a car with auto transmission is manifestly more practical and much less cumbersome in Singapore’s stop-and-go traffic? </p>
<p>They settled on a second-hand silver Toyota Yaris, a COE car that was about to be scrapped, and renewed it for another five years.</p>
<p>“There is so much to appreciate about a car when you drive a manual. You feel the car very differently and you never get bored,” she said.</p>
<p>“It’s also way more engaging and fun to drive a manual car that even when we rent a car overseas, we pick manual cars over automatic ones.”</p>
<p>Lee’s first car was also a manual drive: A 1970 classic Volkswagen Beetle. </p>
<p>“I wish I could’ve kept her but it’s not cheap to upkeep vintage cars in Singapore so I had to let go. But I hope to own another one again one day,” said the 38-year-old.</p>
<p>How was Lee’s interest in manual cars first kindled? Watching her parents drive as a precocious kid of 10.</p>
<p>“I started asking my mum to explain how a manual drive worked. She would let me hold the gear stick while she took the lead with her hand over my hand and showed me how to change gears while she drove around the carpark. </p>
<p>“I think having a taste of that made it even more exciting for me. So as soon as I could, I signed up to take manual drive lessons,” Lee recalled.</p>
<p>It’s a similar story for Serena Chin, Legal Director at Carousell, whose love affair with manual drives began the moment she climbed into a Land Rover Defender when she was also of primary school age.</p>
<p>Her father, who was in the construction business, had driven the Defender home from the office one day and the rest is history.</p>
<p>“I loved the ruggedness of the vehicle and I fell in love with it immediately,” the 30-year-old said.</p>
<p>In 2018, Chin snagged a reproduction Defender 2.5 3-Door TD5 90 Hard-Top that was listed on Carousell for S$47,000.</p>
<p>She named it Leaf after its grasmere green hue befitting the vintage styling of the almost 20-year-old vehicle, and still proudly drives the impeccably maintained classic car today. </p>
<p>Chin had driven another manual car, an Opel box van, for a good decade before that, which was great for ferrying friends for picnics by the bay, replete with a bean bag in the back.</p>
<p>“Drives in a manual car tend to be less boring," she said. </p>
<p>“IT’S THE FEELING OF BEING IN FULL CONTROL” </p>
<p>For Carolyn Theng, it’s the feeling of being in full control of the vehicle that she misses most about driving a manual car. </p>
<p>She had driven a manual black Honda Civic Type R FN, which she even modified, for a decade before it had to be scrapped in 2018 after its COE ran out. </p>
<p>“I used to get a kick out of gear changing. The short shifter and the gear ratios on the 6-speed Type R were amazing; smooth and easy to manoeuvre. </p>
<p>"And I enjoyed listening to the engine rev as I changed from first gear all the way to sixth; it was music to my ears,” said Theng, who works as a marketing manager in the automotive industry.</p>
<p>These days, Theng, who is in her 40s, drives an automatic Audi A5 Sportback, a shared family car.</p>
<p>“I miss my manual drive a lot. Autos are so mundane. There’s no excitement; it’s just Point A to B for me,” she said. </p>
<p>For fellow manual enthusiast Annalisa Yeo it’s all about the clutch. </p>
<p>“I love being able to coordinate the clutch, gears and pedals all at once. I find it much more satisfying than driving an automatic car. </p>
<p>"The ease of automatic cars makes it easy for the mind to wander, sometimes even feel a little dull,” said the 29-year-old freelance actress, writer and performer. </p>
<p>“So I really appreciate the focus manual cars require. It feels cool to control multiple parts of a complex machine instead of letting it do all the work for you.”</p>

<h1> Singaporean mums win US award for English book series that teaches kids Arabic </h1>
<div> <p>When sisters Hafeezah Alsagoff and Hanan Alsagoff couldn't find any fun English books to help their kids learn the Arabic alphabet, they took matters into their own hands.</p> </div>
<div> <div> <a> Hidayah Salamat </a> </div> </div>
<p>When Hafeezah Alsagoff and Hanan Alsagoff couldn't find fun and interactive books about the Arabic language written in English for their kids, they published their own series – and won an award for it.</p>
<p>The Arabic Alphabet Of Huruf Island series, which comprises seven books, was first released in 2017 and recently won the Best Book Series 2021 award from US publishing company Daybreak Press.</p>
<p>The award was presented virtually as part of the company's Book Festival held from Oct 22 to 24.</p>
<p>It features characters "that children of all religions and backgrounds can relate to", "helping to lower barriers between cultures and encourage unity in our society", according to a press release.</p>
<p>Hafeezah, a 39-year-old mother of two, said the duo are "very grateful" for the award.</p>
<p>Though Singaporean, the sisters said they were exposed to "different forms of education" in their early years, having attended Australian, American and British international schools in different parts of the world. </p>
<p>The series has proved popular with readers across the world, with parents creating home schooling activities based on the books, leading to the pair releasing spin-offs that include an interactive finger trace book and card game. </p>
<p>The Arabic Alphabet of Huruf Island series is available in Singapore, Indonesia, Malaysia, Australia, New Zealand, the United Arab Emirates and the United States.</p>
<p>Those in Singapore can purchase the books at retailers like Darul Arqam, Kinokuniya and Wardah Books or via the <a>Homely Hammock website</a>.</p>

<div> <div> <p> <a> Commentary </a> </p> <h5> Commentary: Trying to live more sustainably? It’s impossible to make a difference alone </h5> </div> </div>
<h1> Commentary: Trying to live more sustainably? It’s impossible to make a difference alone </h1>
<div> <p>In combating climate change, our individual efforts are statistically insignificant – especially when compared to what businesses and government can achieve, says climate activist Ho Xiang Tian.</p> </div>
<div> <div> <a> Ho Xiang Tian </a> </div> </div>
<p>SINGAPORE: We’re all familiar with the threat of climate change.</p>
<p>We also know what we must do: Reduce our emissions to net zero by 2050 to have a 50 per cent chance of limiting global temperature rise to 1.5 degrees Celsius.</p>
<p>Yet, a common solution among individuals is frantically accounting for every gram of their carbon footprint, hoping that every little action counts when it comes to climate change.</p>
<p>This is also encouraged by governments and corporations, with messages to practise the 3Rs and switch off lights when not in use.</p>
<p>But if we stop to think about it, our individual efforts are statistically insignificant, especially when compared to what businesses and governments can achieve.</p>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: S$5 tank tops can end up costing too much</a></h3>
<p>Take cooling as an example.</p>
<p>To lower emissions from cooling in Singapore, many things can be done. Alternative cooling methods can be developed. Buildings can be painted a lighter shade. Plants can be used to reduce the ambient temperature.</p>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: Singapore could be a model for cooler cities in a world heating up</a></h3>
<p>None of these can be done by individuals. Try painting your HDB flat’s exterior walls white to reduce heat gain of the building, or try planting many trees around your estate and you’ll likely be fined or asked to undo your changes.</p>
<a>recent guidelines</a> on improving ventilation in buildings to prevent the spread of COVID-19 has shown.
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: Shorts and slippers aren’t sloppy – they’re sensible attire for Singapore’s weather</a></h3>
<p>REDUCE, REUSE AND RECYCLE?</p>
<p>Waste production is another example. Individuals in Singapore generated 915g of waste per day on average in 2019, about 334kg per person that year, and collectively all individuals in Singapore generated 1.87 million tonnes of waste.</p>
<p>It is a staggeringly huge amount but is only about a quarter of total waste generated in Singapore. The rest comes from the non-domestic sector.</p>
<p>A lot of effort is then spent on trying to recycle rather than reduce our waste, but recycling is another area where individual action could easily be negated.</p>
<p>If you throw a clean plastic bottle into the blue recycling bin, but the bin gets contaminated, all its contents would have to be incinerated. Your efforts would have literally gone to waste.</p>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: Why does Singapore still lack a recycling ethos?</a></h3>
<p>Unfortunately, 40 per cent of recyclables collected in Singapore’s blue bins are contaminated and sent for incineration.</p>
<p>There might be an argument that education would help prevent that, but realistically the number of people who know how to recycle properly and actually do so will never hit 100 per cent.</p>
<p>This shows that the existing system is flawed and needs to be changed to achieve the aim of increasing Singapore’s recycling rates, but no amount of proper recycling from an individual can change that.</p>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: Taking Singapore’s green ambitions to new heights with a circular economy</a></h3>
<p>DISTRACTIONS FROM TRUE PROGRESS</p>
<p>Focusing solely on individual actions could also distract from metrics that are able to measure if true progress exists.</p>
<p>In this context, it might seem like individual efforts to reduce meat consumption might be better used to engage in systemic change instead.</p>
<p>This could include encouraging eateries to introduce more plant-based options or getting event organisers to make plant-based meals the default.</p>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: Going meatless doesn’t have to be a dilemma for the Singaporean foodie</a></h3>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: Is lab-grown meat a new frontier or a passing fad?</a></h3>
<p>Individual efforts might even end up doing harm. Case in point being the plastic alternatives currently available in the market.</p>
<p>A 2017 lifecycle analysis commissioned by NEA shows that in Singapore’s context, biodegradable disposables might not be better for the environment. They’re made of plastic and additives to catalyse degradation.</p>
<p>But because waste is incinerated in Singapore, rather than buried in landfills, these products cannot degrade, so are not different from plastic in terms of environmental impact.</p>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: Paying for a plastic bag isn't enough to change our dependence on it</a></h3>
<p>This is not to discourage individual action. Despite all I’ve written, I try to do my part – reduce my use of disposables, recycle, use a fan instead of an air-conditioner and take public transport instead of private transport.</p>
<p>These actions might be statistically insignificant, but they help me live my life in line with my values.</p>
<p>It is important to do things that make us feel good about ourselves, but we must remember it is not enough to take individual action.</p>
<p>We need to learn why things are the way they are now, rethink the assumptions we might have about how things are supposed to be, and work at changing the systems that shape the way we behave.</p>
<p>After all, individual efforts can have an outsized impact. Such efforts could be joining a local environmental group, participating in citizen’s workgroups organised by the government, or getting businesses to change their current practices.</p>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: What good will Coldplay’s vessel cleaning up Malaysia rivers do?</a></h3>
<h3><a>READ: Commentary: Work of Singapore climate activists has only just begun</a></h3>
<p>Efforts by the People’s Movement to Stop Haze managed to get several eateries to switch to using RSPO-certified oil, which helps to reduce haze from forest fires.</p>
<p>These efforts were made by a relatively small group of people, but the impact is far greater than if they just took individual action and nothing else.</p>
<p>Our lifestyle choices do have a place in raising awareness and helping us live with our conscience. But if we truly care about any issue, we need to think bigger to really make a difference.</p>
<p>Ho Xiang Tian is the co-founder of informal environmental group LepakInSG.</p>
